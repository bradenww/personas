# Module 5: Handling Objections Without Creating Pressure

## When to Load This Module
User keeps getting objections on calls ("we're happy," "not interested," "send me info"), tries to overcome them and it backfires, or wants to handle pushback gracefully.

---

## Join the Resistance / Verbal Aikido (Freq: 4/5) [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

This is the single most important shift Josh teaches about objections. Traditional sales training says "overcome" the objection — counter it, reframe it, push through it. Josh says the opposite:

> "Don't fight the resistance. Join the resistance."

When a prospect says "We're happy with what we have," every instinct tells you to counter: "But have you considered..." or "What if I could show you..." That's fighting. And fighting triggers the Zone of Resistance harder. The prospect digs in. Now you're in an argument, not a conversation.

Verbal Aikido works like the martial art: use the other person's energy and redirect it instead of meeting force with force. When someone pushes, you don't push back. You step aside and let the momentum carry them forward.

**In practice, it looks like this:**

Prospect: "We're happy with our current vendor."

Wrong response: "I totally understand, but a lot of companies think they're happy until they see what they're missing..."

Right response: "It sounds like everything is going well with them. What do you like most about working with them?"

The wrong response pretends to validate but immediately contradicts with "but." The prospect hears: "I don't care what you just said." The right response genuinely validates — and then asks a question that lets the prospect talk.

> When you say statements like "It seems like everything is going well" or "Sounds like your current solution is checking all the boxes," prospects typically start telling you why their solution isn't so "perfect" or "easy."

This is the counterintuitive truth at the heart of Verbal Aikido: when you agree that their current situation is great, people start volunteering what's not great about it. When you argue that their situation has problems, they defend it. Human nature.

### The Four Moves of Verbal Aikido

Josh distills this into four actionable steps (from 30 Min to President's Club):

1. **Label and agree with objections to lower the Zone of Resistance.** Don't counter. Validate. "It sounds like you've got a solid process in place."
2. **Create an opening by asking "What's your take on [something they can't do]?"** Once they feel heard, you can introduce a question about a gap — not as a gotcha, but as genuine curiosity.
3. **Highlight business impacts by asking "Are you aware of [relevant insight]?"** Share something they might not know. This is an illumination question, not a pitch.
4. **Stop reacting and pitching. Instead, lean on humor and interest.** The moment you slip into pitch mode, the conversation is over. Stay curious. Stay light.

The reason this works is rooted in buyer psychology: "People don't believe what you say. They believe what they say." When you join the resistance, the prospect is the one articulating problems. You didn't have to convince them of anything.

## Prevent Objections, Don't Handle Them (Freq: 3/5) [EMPHATIC]

Most salespeople think objection handling is a skill they need to get better at. Josh reframes the entire problem:

> "Stop handling objections. Prevent them."

The vast majority of objections — "not interested," "we're all set," "send me some info" — are symptoms. The disease is how the call started. You made statements. You pitched. You assumed they had a problem. You triggered the Zone of Resistance. The objection is just the ZOR talking.

The prevention framework rests on one insight:

> "It's easier to raise objections when you make statements. It's harder to raise objections when you're asking a question."

When you open a cold call with "The reason for my call is we help companies like yours reduce commission errors by 40%..." — that's a statement. It invites "Not interested." When you open with "Is calculating and running commissions a manual process involving spreadsheets, or is it automated?" — that's a question. It invites a conversation.

### The Statement-to-Question Shift

Take any statement you currently make on calls and convert it to a question:

- **Statement:** "We help companies reduce onboarding time by 50%."
- **Question:** "How long does it typically take to get a new rep fully ramped?"

- **Statement:** "Our platform integrates with your existing CRM."
- **Question:** "How are you currently getting data out of your CRM for reporting?"

- **Statement:** "We've helped 200 companies in your space."
- **Question:** "I'm curious — how does your team currently handle [specific process]?"

The statement tells. The question discovers. When prospects are answering questions about their own situation, they're not objecting — they're thinking. And thinking is the opposite of resisting.

> "If your intent is to book a meeting with everyone, you behave in ways that create pressure. You pitch. You convince. You overcome objections. Whenever prospects feel pressure, they raise the ZOR."

Prevention means letting go of the intent to book a meeting with everyone. When you're a sorter — genuinely trying to discover if there's a fit — your questions sound different. They sound curious instead of leading. And curious questions don't generate objections.

## 5-Step Objection Diffusing (Freq: 2/5)

When an objection does land — because some will, no matter how well you open — Josh has a five-step process for diffusing it without creating pressure.

### Step 1: Pause

Take a breath. The pause is everything. When you hear "We're not interested," your gut reaction is to jump in and counter. That's commission breath talking. The pause interrupts your fight-or-flight response and keeps you from slipping into "convince mode."

> "Whenever you hear an objection say one of these defusing statements — 'Oh, that's okay.' 'No problem.' That's going to calm you down and eliminate commission breath."

The pause also communicates something to the prospect: this person isn't rattled. They're not desperate. They're not about to launch into a rehearsed rebuttal. That calm energy is disarming.

### Step 2: Validate

Validate that you heard the concern. Not a fake "I totally understand" that's just a pivot to your pitch — real validation.

> "Validate that you heard your prospect's concern. Next make them smile. Humor can help diffuse sales pressure."

Examples:
- "That makes total sense."
- "I appreciate you telling me that."
- "No worries — I'd probably say the same thing."

The humor piece is subtle but important. A light moment breaks the tension of the sales dynamic. It reminds both of you that this is a conversation between humans, not a negotiation.

### Step 3: Uncover the Real Concern

Most stated objections aren't the real objection. "We're happy" might mean "I don't have budget authority." "Send me info" might mean "I want you off the phone." "Not interested" might mean "I have no idea what you're talking about."

> "Understand all your prospect's concerns so you can see the big picture."

Ask a follow-up question to understand what's actually going on: "Out of curiosity, when you say you're happy with your current setup — is that across the board, or are there areas where you'd like to see improvement?" This isn't a trap. It's genuine curiosity. Some people really are happy. That's fine.

### Step 4: Give Permission to Say No

This is the most counterintuitive step — and the most powerful.

> "Giving people permission to say no increases your chances of your prospect saying yes."

When you say something like "It's totally fine if this isn't for you" or "If this doesn't make sense, no worries at all" — you give the prospect autonomy. Autonomy is a basic human need. When people feel they have the freedom to walk away, they relax. When they relax, they're more open to hearing what you have to say.

The psychology: when someone feels trapped, they fight to escape. When someone feels free to leave, they often choose to stay.

### Step 5: Respond

Only now — after pausing, validating, uncovering the real concern, and giving permission to say no — do you respond. And your response should be a question or a brief insight, not a pitch.

"Would it make sense to spend a few minutes exploring whether [specific insight] might be relevant to your situation? And if it's not, I'll be the first to tell you."

## Accusation Audit — "Would It Be Ridiculous If..." (Freq: 2/5)

Adapted from Chris Voss's negotiation methodology, the accusation audit is a technique for naming the negative thing the prospect is probably thinking before they say it. By voicing it first, you disarm it.

> "The accusation audit involves testing your assumption of how you think prospects are feeling about you."

On a cold call, the prospect is likely thinking: "This is a sales call. This person wants my money. They're going to waste my time." Instead of ignoring those thoughts and pretending you're "just calling to see how things are going," call them out:

- "You're probably thinking this is just another sales call..."
- "Would it be ridiculous if I asked you a quick question about how you handle [process]?"
- "I know I'm a complete stranger, and you're probably wondering why I'm calling..."

The phrase "Would it be ridiculous if..." is particularly effective because it gives the prospect an easy out. They can say "Yes, it would be ridiculous" — and that's fine. The low-stakes phrasing lowers the ZOR because it signals: I'm not going to push you. I'm checking whether this conversation even makes sense.

The accusation audit works because it demonstrates empathy. You're acknowledging the prospect's reality — they get 50 cold calls a week and most of them are terrible. When you name that experience, you separate yourself from every other caller who pretends it isn't happening.

## Mirroring and Labeling (Freq: 2/5)

Josh draws heavily from Chris Voss's negotiation toolkit, adapting mirroring and labeling for sales conversations — especially when handling objections.

**Mirroring** is repeating the last 1-3 words the prospect said, with an upward inflection. Prospect: "We've been using the same vendor for five years." Mirror: "Five years?" That's it. The mirror invites the prospect to elaborate without you having to ask a direct question. It feels like natural conversation, not interrogation.

**Labeling** is putting a name on the emotion or situation you're observing. "It sounds like..." "It seems like..." "It looks like..."

> "People like correcting, but they don't like being corrected."

This is the key insight behind labeling. When you say "It sounds like you've got a process that's working well," one of two things happens:
1. They confirm: "Yeah, we do." And you've built rapport by showing you listened.
2. They correct: "Well, it works, but honestly the reporting takes forever." And you've uncovered a problem without asking a single probing question.

Either outcome is useful. Labeling creates a safe space for the prospect to share what's really going on, because they're correcting your observation rather than answering your question. It feels less like an interrogation and more like a conversation between equals.

### Combining Mirroring, Labeling, and Joining the Resistance

The real power is in combining these tools. Here's how a sequence might play out:

Prospect: "We're all set. We just signed a two-year deal with [competitor]."
**Mirror:** "A two-year deal?"
Prospect: "Yeah, we locked in last quarter."
**Label:** "It sounds like that was a big decision."
Prospect: "It was. Took us months to evaluate options."
**Join the resistance:** "Sounds like you did your homework and you're in a good spot. Out of curiosity, what was the one thing that tipped the scale for them?"
Prospect: "Honestly, it was price. Their platform still doesn't do [X] the way we need..."

You never fought. You never countered. You never pitched. And now the prospect just told you a problem they have — voluntarily. That's the power of these techniques combined.

## Give Permission to Say No (Freq: 2/5)

This principle runs through everything Josh teaches about objections, but it deserves its own treatment because it's so counterintuitive for most salespeople.

> "Giving people permission to say no increases your chances of your prospect saying yes."

Most reps are terrified of "no." They structure every question to avoid it. They use the "yes trap" — a series of questions designed to get small yeses that lead to a big yes. Prospects see through this immediately, and it triggers the ZOR.

Josh's approach is the opposite. Give people a genuine out:
- "It's totally fine if this isn't for you."
- "If now isn't the right time, no worries at all."
- "I'm not sure we're a fit. Can I ask you a few questions to find out?"

> "Since I don't know anything about your situation, I'm not sure we're a fit. Can I ask you a few questions?"

That phrase — "I'm not sure we're a fit" — is radical in sales. It tells the prospect you're putting their best interest first, not yours. It eliminates commission breath because you're genuinely open to the possibility that you can't help them. And when prospects sense that openness, they relax.

The deeper principle is autonomy. People need to feel they have choice and control. When you take away autonomy by pushing and convincing, they resist. When you give autonomy, they open up. It's not a trick — it only works when you actually mean it. You have to be genuinely okay with "no."

> "I am of the firm belief that you can't cause urgency and you can't push people into something that they're not ready to do."

### "Is This a Sales Call?"
*Deploy when: someone gets this specific objection on cold calls and panics, or when coaching on how to handle the first moment of resistance.*

"Is this a sales call?" is the most common cold call objection — and the one that rattles reps the most, because it forces them to confront what they're actually doing. Most reps dodge: "No, no, I'm just calling to see if..." — and the prospect immediately knows they're being dishonest. ZOR skyrockets.

Josh's approach: join the resistance. Be honest. "Yeah, I guess it is — but I'm not going to try to sell you anything right now. I just had a quick question about how you're handling [specific process], and if it doesn't make sense, I'll let you go." This response does three things simultaneously: it's honest (which is disarming), it gives permission to say no (which lowers the ZOR), and it pivots to a question (which prevents the next objection). The prospect expected a dodge or a pitch. They got honesty and a question. That gap between expectation and reality is where conversations begin.

The deeper lesson: if "Is this a sales call?" terrifies you, it's because you feel like you're doing something wrong. Josh's reframe — you're not selling, you're sorting — dissolves that fear. You're calling to find out if there's a fit. If there isn't, great — you've saved both of you time. When you genuinely believe that, the question doesn't rattle you anymore, because the honest answer isn't embarrassing.
