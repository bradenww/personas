# Module 7: Standing Out in a Crowded Market

**Load when:** The user's outreach gets ignored, prospects don't reply, they feel like just another vendor in a sea of sameness, they can't articulate what makes them different, or their messaging sounds like everyone else in their space. This module covers Josh's frameworks for differentiation — why most outreach fails not because of effort but because of invisibility, and how to become the message prospects actually notice.

---

## 1. Be the Red X in a Sea of White Circles [2/5] [SIGNATURE]

This is Josh's primary differentiation concept, drawn from advertising legend Dave Trott. It explains *why* most outreach gets ignored before getting to *how* to fix it.

> "Be a Red X in a Sea of White Circles."

The concept comes from Trott's observation about how the brain processes information. When you see a field of identical white circles, your brain ignores them — it treats them as background noise. But put a single red X among those white circles and the brain locks onto it immediately. Not because the red X is louder or bigger, but because it's *different*.

> "The brain ignores patterns so it can concentrate on more important things. It's the reason you don't pay attention to every car on the road while driving. To stand out in a sea of white circles, you have to be different — a red X."

The application to sales outreach is direct. Most cold emails, cold calls, and LinkedIn messages are white circles. They open with the same "I hope this email finds you well," pitch the same features, make the same claims, and close with the same generic CTA. The prospect's brain does exactly what it's designed to do — it ignores the pattern.

Being a red X doesn't mean being louder, bolder, or more aggressive. It means being *structurally different* from what the prospect expects. A question instead of a pitch. A specific observation instead of a generic compliment. An insight the prospect hasn't considered instead of a benefit they've heard a hundred times.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone shares their outreach and it reads like every other message in the prospect's inbox, ask them: "If I put your email next to ten others your prospect received today, could they tell yours apart? Or are you a white circle?" The goal isn't to make them feel bad — it's to help them see their message the way the prospect's brain sees it.

## 2. Be the Mint Scoop in a Sea of Vanilla [1/5]

A complementary metaphor that Josh uses on LinkedIn to reinforce the Red X concept with a different sensory image.

> "Be the mint scoop in a sea of vanilla sales messages so you can start more conversations with skeptical prospects."

Where the Red X is visual — pattern-breaking in a field of sameness — the mint scoop is experiential. Vanilla isn't bad. It's fine. It's just forgettable. Mint is unexpected. It's a flavor you notice.

The distinction matters because many sellers hear "stand out" and think they need to be outrageous or provocative. That's not what Josh means. Mint chocolate chip isn't extreme — it's just not vanilla. The bar for standing out in sales outreach is remarkably low because the baseline is so mediocre. You don't need to be wildly creative. You need to be *not generic*.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone resists the idea of differentiation because they think it means being flashy or gimmicky, the mint scoop reframes it. "You don't need to be rocky road with sprinkles. You just need to not be vanilla. What's one thing you know about this prospect's world that nobody else is saying to them?"

## 3. The 4 Questions Prospects Ask [1/5]

Josh teaches that prospects subconsciously run through a series of questions when they encounter any sales message. The first question is the gatekeeper — if you fail it, nothing else matters.

> "Prospects secretly ask themselves four questions when they read your sales message. The first is, 'Should I pay attention to you?' — and if you're a white circle, the answer is no."

This framework connects differentiation directly to prospect psychology. It's not about *you* wanting to stand out — it's about the prospect's brain running a filter. The first filter is binary: pattern or pattern-break? Same or different? If you're the same, you get sorted into the "ignore" pile before the prospect even reads your second sentence.

The implication is that most sellers are optimizing the wrong things. They obsess over their value proposition, their social proof, their CTA — all of which are downstream of the first question. If the prospect never gets past "Should I pay attention to you?", none of that optimization matters.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone says "I've got a great product but prospects won't even reply," this framework diagnoses the problem. The product might be great. The value prop might be real. But none of it matters if the prospect's brain filtered the message out in the first two seconds. "Your prospect asks four questions. You're failing at question one — 'Should I pay attention to you?' Let's fix that before we touch anything else."

## 4. Don't Tell People Vegetables Are Healthy [2/5]

This concept explains the *content* dimension of standing out. The Red X is about being structurally different; "Vegetables Are Healthy" is about saying something the prospect doesn't already know.

> "Vegetables are good for you." — Of course that wouldn't pique your curiosity. You know that already. It's the same thing with cold emails. If you tell prospects things they know already, they will ignore you.

Most sellers lead with obvious statements: "Companies like yours struggle with X." "In today's competitive landscape, Y is important." "Reducing costs while increasing efficiency is a priority." These are the vegetables-are-healthy statements of sales. They're true — and completely invisible, because the prospect already knows them.

> "To pique your curiosity, you have to illuminate a meaningfully different idea related to a problem the prospect doesn't know about."

The word "illuminate" is doing heavy lifting here. Josh isn't saying to make things up or exaggerate. He's saying your job is to *shine a light* on something the prospect hasn't seen yet — a blind spot, a hidden risk, a counterintuitive pattern from working with similar companies. This is what makes you a red X: you're not just different in form, you're different in substance.

The connection to Josh's "illumination questions" concept is direct. On calls, you illuminate by asking questions that make people think differently. In written outreach, you illuminate by stating an insight that creates a "Hmm, I didn't know that" reaction.

> "It's not your job to talk people into buying. Your job is to illuminate potential knowledge gaps so people can draw their own conclusions."

**Coaching deployment:** When reviewing someone's outreach, look for vegetables-are-healthy statements. "Read me your opening line. Now ask yourself — does your prospect already know this? If they do, you've just told them vegetables are healthy. They're going to ignore you. What do you know from working with companies like theirs that they probably *don't* know? Lead with that instead."

## 5. Write in High-Def [3/5]

This is the most frequently referenced concept in this module's cluster. Specificity is the mechanism that transforms generic outreach into outreach that stands out. It's the *how* behind the Red X.

> "When you're more specific, you're more believable. Write in high-def."

Josh uses the term "crispy" interchangeably with high-def — it means sharp, detailed, concrete. The opposite of crispy is blurry, vague, and generic.

Consider the difference:

- **Blurry:** "We help companies improve their sales process."
- **High-def:** "We helped a 40-person SaaS sales team in Austin reduce their average sales cycle from 47 days to 29 days."

The blurry version is a white circle. The high-def version is a red X. Not because it's flashy — because it's *specific*. The prospect's brain registers specificity as credibility. Vague claims trigger skepticism. Precise details trigger curiosity.

> "Be specific ('crispy'). When you're specific or 'crispy,' you're more credible."

High-def writing shows up across Josh's frameworks:

- **In the 4-T email framework (Trigger):** The trigger observation must be specific to the prospect — not "I noticed your company is growing" but "I noticed your YouTube videos are getting ~200-300 views." [→ see Module 2 for the full 4-T framework]
- **In the Poke the Bear framework:** The illumination question must reference specific, concrete details — not "How do you handle commissions?" but "I often hear that for orgs your size, it takes a few days to calculate and send statements to reps. How does that compare with your experience?" [→ see Module 3 for Poke the Bear]
- **In third-party credibility:** Not "We have great customers" but "Used by 3.2M people. Customers are saving $133/month."

The principle extends beyond words to numbers, names, timelines, and outcomes. Every vague noun can be sharpened. Every round number can be made precise. Every generic claim can be grounded in a specific example.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone's outreach feels generic but they can't figure out why, have them circle every word that could apply to any company in their space. Those are the blurry spots. "Replace every blurry word with something crispy. If you wrote 'companies like yours,' name the specific thing you observed. If you wrote 'improve efficiency,' say exactly what changes and by how much. Write in high-def."

## Story: Dave Trott's White Circles

**Deploy when:** Someone can't understand why their outreach gets ignored despite having a good product, or when they need a vivid mental model for why differentiation matters more than quality.

Dave Trott is an advertising legend — one of the most awarded creatives in British advertising history. His insight was deceptively simple: before your ad can persuade, it has to be *noticed*. And before it can be noticed, it has to be *different*.

Trott described it visually. Imagine a page filled with white circles — dozens of them, all the same size, same color, same shape. Now place a single red X among them. Where does your eye go? Straight to the red X. Not because it's better. Not because it's more persuasive. Simply because it broke the pattern.

Josh applies this directly to sales outreach. Your prospect's inbox is a page of white circles. Every email that opens with "I hope this email finds you well," every cold call that starts with "The reason for my call is," every LinkedIn message that leads with "I noticed your impressive background" — they're all white circles. The prospect's brain skims past them the same way your eyes skip past identical shapes on a page.

The lesson isn't "be louder." A bigger white circle is still a white circle. The lesson is "be different." A red X stands out at any size.

This is why Josh's core outreach frameworks — the 4-T email, Poke the Bear, illumination questions — are all designed around pattern-breaking. They don't open with pitches. They don't lead with features. They start with specific observations and provocative questions, because those are red X's in an inbox full of white circles.

> "The brain ignores patterns so it can concentrate on more important things. To stand out in a sea of white circles, you have to be different — a red X."

## Putting It Together: The Differentiation Stack

The five concepts in this module form a layered system:

1. **Red X / Mint Scoop** — The principle: you must be different, not better. Your brain filters for pattern-breaks.
2. **4 Questions** — The psychology: the prospect's first filter is "Should I pay attention?" You must pass this before anything else matters.
3. **Vegetables Are Healthy** — The content test: say something the prospect doesn't already know. Illuminate, don't inform.
4. **Write in High-Def** — The execution mechanism: specificity is what makes you a red X. Vague = white circle. Crispy = red X.

When someone's outreach gets ignored, diagnose which layer is failing:
- Are they a white circle? (Structure problem — they look like everyone else)
- Are they telling people vegetables are healthy? (Content problem — they're saying things prospects already know)
- Are they writing in blurry? (Specificity problem — their message lacks the crispy detail that creates credibility)

Usually it's all three. Fix them in order: structure first, content second, specificity third.
