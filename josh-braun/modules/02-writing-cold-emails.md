## Core Concept 1: The 4-T Framework (Trigger, Think, Third-Party, Talk) [3/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

The 4-T Framework is Josh's primary cold email structure — four sentences, each with a specific psychological purpose. It "taps into the psychology of how to make a great sales pitch without sounding sales-oriented."

### The Four Elements

**1. Trigger — Make an observation about the prospect.**
What is something you can deduce from an observation you made about the prospect? If A, then B is probably true. The bridging phrase "which suggests" transitions into the deduction. This shows you did your homework and earns the right to the prospect's attention.

**2. Think — Ask a question that creates tension.**
"Ask a question that makes prospects think differently about their current solution. You're creating tension and poking the bear. You want to evoke a 'Hmm, I'm not sure' response." This is NOT a leading question — it is a neutral question that illuminates a potential gap. The prospect should feel curiosity, not pressure.

**3. Third-Party Credibility — Provide social proof.**
Stats, customer results, recognizable names. Example: "Used by 3.2m people. Customers are saving $133/month." This builds trust without you having to make claims about yourself.

**4. Talk — A simple, low-pressure CTA.**
"Think this might help?" or "Have you looked into [product]?" The prospect must feel free to say no. No pressure. No "let's book 15 minutes Tuesday at 2pm."

### Why It Works

"Every line has a purpose: catch interest, provoke thought, build trust, and invite a reply. 4-T taps into curiosity, relevance, social proof, and consent."

### Full 4-T Example

> **Trigger:** "Hey Josh — It looks like your YouTube videos are getting ~200-300 views."
>
> **Think:** "How are you ensuring they get promoted to a larger audience of salespeople?"
>
> **Third-Party Credibility:** "I've been optimizing YouTube videos for three years for influencers like Alex Hormozi that boost views and subscribers. Money back if you're not happy after 30 days. Here's a taste."
>
> **Talk:** "Think this might help? Either way; keep up the great work on your channel."

### Coaching Application

When reviewing a user's cold email, diagnose which of the four elements is missing or weak. Most bad emails fail at Trigger (generic, no observation) or Think (leading question instead of illumination question). Walk the user through rewriting each element one at a time.

## Core Concept 2: Write in High-Def / Be Specific ("Crispy") [3/5] [EMPHATIC]

"When you're more specific, you're more believable. Write in high-def."

Being "crispy" means replacing vague, generic language with concrete, specific details. Specificity is what separates an email that gets read from one that gets deleted.

### The Specificity Principle

Generic language triggers the brain's pattern-matching filter — "I've seen this before, ignore." Specific language breaks through because it signals real knowledge and real relevance.

**Generic (low-def):** "We help companies grow revenue."
**Specific (high-def):** "I noticed your YouTube videos are getting ~200-300 views." (Exact numbers. Specific platform. Observable fact.)

**Generic (low-def):** "Our customers see great results."
**Specific (high-def):** "Customers are saving $133/month." (Exact dollar amount. Not "significant savings.")

### Where to Apply Crispy Language

- **The Trigger sentence:** Use specific, observable data about the prospect (numbers, dates, job title changes, company news)
- **The Third-Party line:** Use exact stats, not vague claims ("3.2m people" not "millions of users")
- **The Think question:** Reference a specific process or challenge, not a category ("How are you ensuring they get promoted to a larger audience of salespeople?" not "How are you handling marketing?")

### Coaching Application

When a user's email feels flat or generic, ask: "Where's the high-def? Show me one sentence with a real number, a real observation, or a real name." Push them to replace every vague phrase with something concrete and verifiable.

## Core Concept 3: Be the Red X / Mint Scoop [2/5] [SIGNATURE]

"Be a Red X in a Sea of White Circles." Josh references advertising legend Dave Trott's concept: "The brain ignores patterns so it can concentrate on more important things. It's the reason you don't pay attention to every car on the road while driving. To stand out in a sea of white circles, you have to be different — a red X."

### The Four Questions Prospects Ask

"Prospects secretly ask themselves four questions when they read your sales message. The first is, 'Should I pay attention to you?'" — and if you're a white circle (same as every other email in their inbox), the answer is no.

### The Mint Scoop Variation

"Be the mint scoop in a sea of vanilla sales messages so you can start more conversations with skeptical prospects."

### What Makes an Email a "White Circle"

- Opens with "I hope this email finds you well"
- Leads with your company name and what you do
- Lists features
- Asks for 15 minutes
- Sounds like every other sales email the prospect got that day

### What Makes an Email a "Red X"

- Opens with a specific observation about the prospect (Trigger)
- Asks a question that makes them think differently (Think)
- Uses the prospect's language, not your marketing language
- Feels like it was written by a human who actually looked at their situation

### Coaching Application

Ask the user to read their email aloud and answer honestly: "If you got this email, would you stop scrolling? Or does it sound like every other sales email in your inbox?" If the answer is "it sounds like everyone else," it is a white circle. Help them find the one thing that makes it a red X.

## Core Concept 4: Don't Tell People Vegetables Are Healthy [2/5]

"'Vegetables are good for you.' — Of course that wouldn't pique your curiosity. You know that already. It's the same thing with cold emails. If you tell prospects things they know already, they will ignore you."

### The Principle

Most cold emails fail because they state the obvious. "Data security is important." "Growing revenue matters." "Efficiency saves time." The prospect already knows this. There is zero reason to keep reading.

"To pique your curiosity, you have to illuminate a meaningfully different idea related to a problem the prospect doesn't know about."

### Application to Cold Emails

The Think element of the 4-T framework is where this principle lives. The question you ask must surface something the prospect has NOT already considered. It should make them pause and think "Hmm, I'm not sure about that."

**Vegetables-Are-Healthy version:** "Are you concerned about data breaches?"
**Illumination version:** "How are you ensuring your team's Slack DMs aren't being indexed by third-party apps you approved last quarter?"

The first tells them something they know. The second illuminates a specific problem they might not have considered.

### Coaching Application

When reviewing a user's email, find the "vegetables are healthy" sentence — the line that states something obvious. Ask: "Would this make the prospect stop and think? Or would they say 'Yeah, I know'?" If they'd say "I know," it needs to be rewritten as an illumination question.

## Core Concept 5: Speak to Struggles, Not Features [3/5]

"Speaking to the struggles or jobs your prospects want to get done is a surefire way to increase cold email response rates because your prospects will get the feeling you have the answer to what they are looking for."

### Features vs. Struggles

Most cold emails lead with what the product does. Josh's approach flips it: lead with what the prospect is struggling with.

This comes directly from the JTBD framework (see Module 8). When you understand the "jobs" your buyer is trying to get done and the struggles they face, you can write emails that feel like you are reading their mind.

### The Secret Language

"If you're Basecamp, you've uncovered your buyer's secret language." The JTBD interview process reveals the exact words, phrases, and emotions your buyers use to describe their problems. When you use those words in your cold email, the prospect feels understood — like you already know their situation.

### Feature Language vs. Struggle Language

**Feature language:** "Our platform automates commission calculations with 99.9% accuracy."
**Struggle language:** "I often hear that for orgs your size, it takes a few days to calculate and send statements to reps." [PARAPHRASE — VERIFY: adapted from CaptivateIQ Poke the Bear example for email context]

The feature language is about you. The struggle language is about them. The prospect sees themselves in the struggle language.

### Coaching Application

Ask the user: "Who are the last 5 customers who bought from you? What were they struggling with before they found you?" Use those exact struggles — in the customer's language, not marketing language — as the foundation of the Trigger and Think sentences.

Josh learned how to increase cold email response rates by applying the Jobs-To-Be-Done framework he learned at Basecamp. He mapped the timeline of struggles that caused a CEO of a digital agency to buy Basecamp:

An email to a similar prospect wouldn't say "Basecamp is a project management tool with X features." It would say something like: "I noticed your agency recently grew from 5 to 15 people. ## Quick-Reference: Cold Email Diagnostic Checklist

When coaching someone on a specific email, run through these checks:

1. **Is there a real Trigger?** Does the first sentence reference something specific and observable about the prospect? (Not "I came across your profile.")
2. **Does the Think question illuminate?** Would the prospect say "Hmm, I'm not sure" — or "Yeah, I already know that"? (Vegetables test.)
3. **Is the Third-Party line crispy?** Specific numbers, names, or results — not vague claims?
4. **Is the CTA low-pressure?** Can the prospect easily say no without feeling awkward?
5. **Is it a Red X or a White Circle?** Read it aloud. Does it sound like every other sales email, or does it stand out?
6. **Struggle language or feature language?** Is the email about the prospect's world, or about your product?
7. **Is it written in high-def?** Find every vague word and ask: "Can I make this more specific?"
