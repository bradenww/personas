# Module 9: Networking and In-Person Selling

**Load when:** The user has a conference, trade show, or networking event coming up. They don't know what to say when they meet prospects in person. They hate elevator pitches, feel awkward working a room, or want to make in-person connections that actually lead to business. This module covers Josh's approach to ditching the pitch in live settings, a 3-step conference framework, and the discipline of qualifying fast so you spend your time on the right conversations.

---

## 1. Ditch the Pitch [2/5] [SIGNATURE]

Most people walk into a networking event armed with an elevator pitch — a rehearsed 30-to-60-second monologue about who they are and what their company does. The problem: monologues trigger the Zone of Resistance just as fast in person as they do on the phone. The moment someone at a conference feels like they're being pitched, they start scanning the room for an exit. Same instinct as "just looking" in a department store — self-preservation.

> "Ditch the pitch. Poke the bear."

The principle that drives Josh's entire cold calling framework applies with even more force in person: **have a dialogue, not a monologue.** Instead of preparing a pitch about yourself, prepare a question about them. Your opening move at a networking event should be a qualifying question — not a value proposition.

Here's why this works: when you ask someone a question, their brain feels obligated to answer. It's involuntary. A question opens a conversation. A pitch closes one. The person who asks the questions controls the direction of the conversation without creating pressure — because questions give the other person autonomy to respond however they want.

> "People don't believe what you say. They believe what they say."

This is even more true face-to-face. When a prospect tells you about their problem in their own words — standing three feet away from you, looking you in the eye — they've persuaded themselves that the problem is real and worth solving. You didn't have to convince anyone of anything. You just asked the right question and listened.

The elevator pitch fails for the same reason leading questions fail on cold calls: it assumes everyone has the problem you solve. It broadcasts a solution before confirming a problem exists. Ditching the pitch means accepting that you're for some people but not for everyone — and that finding out who's who is the actual job.

### What "Ditch the Pitch" Looks Like in Practice

**The old way (monologue):** "Hi, I'm Chris. I'm the CEO of [company]. We help mid-market companies reduce their commission calculation time by 80% using our automated platform. We've worked with companies like Salesforce and HubSpot, and our customers typically see ROI within the first quarter."

The prospect nods politely, says "That's interesting," and finds someone else to talk to. You delivered information. You didn't start a conversation.

**The new way (dialogue):** "Hi, I'm Chris. Quick question — are you involved in managing commissions at your company?"

If they say yes, you've found someone who might have the problem you solve, and you've opened a door to learn more. If they say no, you just saved yourself a 60-second pitch that would have gone nowhere — and you can move on to the next person without either of you feeling awkward.

> "If the answer is no, why bother pitching at all?"

That single line captures the entire philosophy. The traditional elevator pitch wastes time because it treats every person at the event as a potential buyer. Ditching the pitch means sorting first — finding out if there's a reason to keep talking before you say anything about yourself.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone tells you they're preparing an elevator pitch for a conference, stop them. "Write down your pitch. Now cross out every sentence that's about you or your company. What's left? Nothing? Good. Now write one question that would tell you whether this person has the problem you solve. That's your opener. Everything else comes after they answer."

## 2. The 3-Step Conference Framework [1/5]

This is the tactical structure that turns "Ditch the Pitch" from a philosophy into a repeatable process. Three steps, designed to be executed in a short networking conversation — the kind where you have two to five minutes with someone before one of you moves on.

### Step 1 — Ask a Qualifying Question

Lead with a question that identifies whether the person you're talking to has a problem you solve. The question should be specific enough that the answer tells you something useful, and neutral enough that it doesn't feel like a pitch.

The qualifying question does two things at once: it starts a conversation (because the other person's brain is wired to answer questions), and it sorts (because the answer tells you whether to keep going or move on). You're not asking for their time. You're not asking for a meeting. You're asking whether a topic is relevant to them. That's low-stakes enough that people answer honestly.

The question must be tailored to what you sell — it can't be generic. "What challenges are you facing?" is too broad. It doesn't sort anyone. A strong qualifying question is specific to the problem your product solves and makes the prospect think about their current situation.

[PARAPHRASE — VERIFY] Josh has emphasized that the qualifying question at a conference should work like the "Identify if There's a Problem" step in Poke the Bear — a neutral question about their current process, not a leading question that assumes a pain point.

### Step 2 — Provide an Insight

If the qualifying question reveals a problem — if the person says something that tells you they're dealing with the issue you solve — your next move is to provide an insight. Not a pitch. Not a feature list. An insight: a brief, specific observation about how their situation could be better.

This is the in-person equivalent of the illumination question on a cold call. You're shining a light on something they might not have considered. The difference is that in person, you can be slightly more direct because the conversation is already warm — they've just told you about their problem, so you have permission to offer a perspective.

The insight should be quick — one or two sentences. It should identify a problem and position you as someone who might have a solution, without claiming to have the answer. The goal is to create enough curiosity that they want to continue the conversation, not to close a deal on the spot.

> "It's not my job to talk people into buying. My job is to illuminate potential problems. Some people will be interested in talking further. Others won't."

### Step 3 — Take Charge of Setting the Meeting

This is where most people fumble. They have a great conversation, the prospect is interested, and then they say something like "We should grab coffee sometime" or "Let me send you my card." Both of these hand control to the prospect and virtually guarantee no follow-up happens. The prospect goes home with 40 business cards and follows up with none of them.

Instead: set the meeting right there. Pull out your phone, open your calendar, and propose a specific time. "I'd love to continue this conversation. How's Thursday at 2?" Don't ask *if* they want to meet — that gives time to oscillate and hesitate. Propose *when*.

Taking charge of the logistics isn't pushy — it's a service. You're making it easy for the other person. They don't have to think about scheduling. They don't have to remember to email you next week. The meeting is set, and both of you can move on to the next conversation knowing there's a concrete next step.

**Coaching deployment:** Before the event, have the user prepare three things and write them down: (1) Their qualifying question. (2) Their insight — one or two sentences about how the prospect's situation could improve. (3) A default meeting time they can propose on the spot. "You're not winging this. You walk in with a qualifying question, an insight, and your calendar open. That's it. No pitch deck, no brochure, no 'let me tell you about our platform.' A question, an insight, and a meeting."

## 3. Qualify Fast, Move On [1/5]

At a networking event, time is your scarcest resource. A room of 100 people and a 90-minute cocktail hour means you have roughly one minute per person if you talk to everyone. You won't talk to everyone — but the math makes the point: every minute you spend with someone who doesn't have the problem you solve is a minute you're not spending with someone who does.

> "If the answer is no, why bother pitching at all?"

This is the in-person version of "Be a Sorter, Not a Seller." Your qualifying question isn't just a conversation starter — it's a filter. If someone tells you they don't manage commissions, they don't have a content team, they don't deal with the problem your product addresses — thank them, wish them well, and move to the next person. No pitch. No "Well, just in case you ever need..." No awkward lingering.

This feels counterintuitive to people who've been taught that networking is about "building relationships with everyone." Josh's philosophy is different: you're not there to collect business cards. You're there to find the people who have the problem you solve and start a real conversation with them. Quality of conversations matters more than quantity of handshakes.

### The Math That Makes This Click

The $45,000 Conference Sale story (see below) illustrates this perfectly: Chris talked to roughly 100 people at a networking event. Out of those 100, 11 had the problem he solved — and he set up meetings with all 11. That's an 11% hit rate, which means 89% of the people in the room were not prospects. If Chris had spent five minutes giving each of those 89 people an elevator pitch, he would have burned his entire evening and booked zero meetings. By qualifying fast and moving on, he spent his time on the 11 people who mattered.

The discipline here is emotional, not tactical. It feels rude to end a conversation after 30 seconds. It feels like you're being dismissive. But the opposite is true: you're respecting their time by not subjecting them to a pitch they don't need, and you're respecting your own time by spending it where it can actually create value.

> "You're for some people, but you're not for everyone."

**Coaching deployment:** When someone is anxious about "working the room," reframe the goal. "You're not trying to make 50 friends tonight. You're trying to find the 5 to 10 people who have the problem you solve. Ask your qualifying question. If they're not the right person, say 'Great meeting you' and move on. No guilt. You're sorting, not selling."

## Stories

### The $45,000 Conference Sale
*Deploy when: the user is preparing for a networking event, skeptical that "ditching the pitch" works in practice, or needs a concrete example of the 3-Step Conference Framework producing real revenue.*

A CEO named Chris went to a networking event with roughly 100 people in the room. Instead of preparing an elevator pitch, he used a conversational approach built on the three steps.

**Step 1 — He asked a qualifying question** that identified a problem he solves. He led with the question, not with who he was or what his company does. When someone asks you a question, your brain feels obligated to answer — it's an involuntary response. The question immediately sorted the room: some people had the problem, most didn't.

**Step 2 — When someone had the problem, he provided an insight.** He quickly communicated how the prospect's life could be better — identified their problem and positioned himself as someone who could potentially help. Not a pitch. Not a demo. An insight that created enough curiosity to continue the conversation.

**Step 3 — He took charge of setting the meeting.** He didn't say "Let's connect sometime." He didn't hand over a business card and hope for a follow-up. He proposed a specific meeting time on the spot, denying the prospect time to oscillate or hesitate. He made it easy — set the meeting and asked if the time would work.

**The results:** Chris set up 11 meetings in one hour from a room of 100 people. Instead of doing a monologue, they had a dialogue. Those 11 meetings turned into $45,000 in revenue.

The math is instructive. One hour. 100 people. 11 meetings. $45,000. That's roughly $4,100 per meeting — and each meeting started with a single question, not a pitch. The people who didn't have the problem got a polite 30-second conversation and moved on. The people who did have the problem got a real dialogue and a concrete next step.

This story is the definitive proof point for Ditch the Pitch. The traditional approach — preparing a polished elevator pitch and delivering it to anyone who will listen — would have produced a fraction of these results. Chris didn't sell anything at the event. He sorted. He found the 11 people who had the problem, gave each of them an insight, and set a meeting. The selling happened later, in a proper conversation with context and focus.

> "Instead of doing a monologue, they had a dialogue."

## Putting It Together: The Networking Architecture

The three concepts in this module form a simple sequence:

1. **Ditch the Pitch** — The philosophy. Replace your monologue with a dialogue. Lead with a question, not a value proposition.
2. **The 3-Step Conference Framework** — The structure. Qualifying question, insight, set the meeting. Three steps, repeatable with every person you talk to.
3. **Qualify Fast, Move On** — The discipline. If the person doesn't have the problem you solve, move on immediately. No guilt, no lingering, no "just in case" pitches.

When someone is nervous about a networking event, diagnose which layer needs work:
- Are they dreading the event entirely? (Mindset problem — they're thinking about it as "selling." Load Module 1 for detachment and the surfer's mind.)
- Do they have a pitch but no qualifying question? (Ditch the Pitch problem — they need to replace their monologue with a question.)
- Are they having good conversations but nothing comes of them? (Step 3 problem — they're not setting the meeting on the spot.)
- Are they spending 20 minutes with each person? (Qualify Fast problem — they're not sorting, they're socializing.)

The key insight across all three: networking events are cold calls without the phone. The same principles apply — lower the Zone of Resistance, ask neutral questions, sort instead of sell, and detach from the outcome. The only difference is that you can read body language and the person can't hang up on you. Both of those work in your favor.
