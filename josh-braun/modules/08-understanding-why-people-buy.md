# Module 8: Understanding Why People Buy (JTBD)

**Load when:** The user doesn't understand their buyer, can't figure out why deals stall, product messaging isn't resonating, they need to do customer research, or they're writing outreach that sounds like a features list instead of speaking to what actually drives people to switch. This module covers Josh's approach to buyer psychology — the Jobs-to-Be-Done framework, the four forces that govern every buying decision, and why speaking the customer's language is the single highest-leverage thing you can do for your pipeline.

---

## 1. Jobs-to-Be-Done Framework [3/5] [SIGNATURE]

This is the concept that changed everything for Josh. He learned it at Basecamp, and it permanently rewired how he thinks about sales conversations, cold outreach, and buyer motivation.

> "An interview technique that lets you travel back in time and observe the circumstances that caused people to buy."

JTBD starts with a premise that contradicts how most sellers think: people don't buy products. They don't buy features. They don't even buy solutions. They *hire* products to make progress in a specific circumstance. The product is the means. The job is what matters.

Josh frequently references the concept's origin through Clayton Christensen's work. The insight is deceptively simple — instead of asking "why did you buy our product?", you ask "what was going on in your life that caused you to start looking?" Those are two radically different questions. The first gives you a list of features. The second gives you a story — a timeline of struggle, frustration, failed alternatives, and the final trigger that pushed someone to act.

### What JTBD Interviews Actually Look Like

A JTBD interview doesn't start with the product. It starts with the *circumstance*. You're reconstructing a timeline — what happened first, then what happened, then what changed, then what finally made you do something about it. You're mapping the emotional journey, not gathering feature feedback.

The questions sound more like a journalist's than a salesperson's:
- "When did you first start thinking about this?"
- "What was going on at the time?"
- "What did you try before us?"
- "What almost stopped you from making the switch?"

### Why This Matters for Sellers

Most sellers live in product-land. They know their features, their differentiators, their competitive matrix. But buyers don't live in product-land. Buyers live in circumstance-land. They have a messy, frustrating situation that's been getting worse, and they need to make progress.

When you understand the job, you stop pitching features and start speaking to the struggle. That's a fundamentally different conversation — one where the prospect feels understood instead of sold to.

> "JTBD interviews force you to look at your product through the eyes of the 'jobs' your customer needs to get done."

The practical application is direct: interview 10-30 of your existing customers using JTBD technique. Map their timelines. Find the patterns. Then use those patterns — in those customers' exact words — in your outreach. This is how you stop sounding like a brochure and start sounding like someone who understands the prospect's world.

## 2. Four Forces of Buying [2/5]

Every buying decision — every switch from an old way to a new way — is governed by four forces. Two push toward change. Two pull back toward the status quo. Understanding these forces explains why deals stall, why prospects ghost, and why someone can say "this is exactly what we need" and then do nothing.

### The Four Forces

**Force 1 — Push (pain with current situation).** Something about how things work today is creating frustration, friction, or failure. Files slip through the cracks. Clients complain. The manual process that worked for two clients breaks down at ten. Push is the force that makes someone start looking.

**Force 2 — Pull (attraction to the new solution).** The promise of a better way. Faster, easier, more reliable, more professional. Pull is the force that draws someone toward a specific alternative. It's what your marketing and sales messaging usually focuses on.

**Force 3 — Anxiety (fear of the unknown).** What if the new thing doesn't work? What if the implementation is painful? What if we lose data? What if the team hates it? Anxiety is the silent deal-killer. The prospect never tells you about it. They just go quiet.

**Force 4 — Habit (comfort with the status quo).** "We've always done it this way." Even when the current way is broken, it's familiar. People know where the files are. They know the workarounds. Habit is the gravitational pull of the known — and it's stronger than most sellers realize.

### The Switching Equation

> "The switch happens when push + pull > anxiety + habit."

This is the equation that explains every deal you've ever lost without understanding why. The prospect had the pain (push). They liked your solution (pull). But the anxiety about switching combined with the comfort of their current process was greater than the combined motivation to change.

Most sellers spend all their energy increasing pull — more features, more demos, more case studies. Josh's framework says that's only one of four levers. Often the higher-leverage move is *reducing anxiety* (offering a pilot, showing an easy migration, naming the risk before they do) or *increasing push* (helping them see the true cost of doing nothing — which is what illumination questions do on cold calls).

**Coaching deployment:** When someone's deal is stalling, walk them through the four forces. "Your prospect has the pain — that's push. They like your product — that's pull. So why aren't they switching? What's the anxiety? What's the habit? Which one is winning?" Nine times out of ten, the seller has been pushing pull when the real blocker is anxiety or habit.

## 3. Speak the Customer's Language [3/5]

This concept bridges JTBD research and outbound execution. Once you've interviewed customers and mapped their timelines, you have something invaluable: the exact words real buyers used to describe their struggle, their frustration, and the moment they decided to act. Those words become your outreach language.

> "If you're Basecamp, you've uncovered your buyer's secret language."

The "secret language" isn't jargon or marketing speak. It's the raw, emotional, specific way actual buyers describe their problem. It's "things slipping through the cracks" instead of "project management inefficiencies." It's "clients started complaining" instead of "stakeholder dissatisfaction." It's "I wanted to be more buttoned up" instead of "we needed to professionalize our workflows."

> "Speaking to the struggles or jobs your prospects want to get done is a surefire way to increase cold email response rates because your prospects will get the feeling you have the answer to what they are looking for."

This is why JTBD interviews are the foundation of effective outreach. Without them, you're guessing at language. With them, you're using the prospect's *own* words — words that trigger instant recognition because they describe an experience the prospect is living through right now.

### How to Extract the Language

During JTBD interviews, listen for:
- **Emotional phrases** — how they described their frustration, not how they'd describe it in a boardroom
- **Specific details** — the tools they mentioned, the workarounds they described, the moment things broke down
- **Trigger events** — what finally pushed them to act (landed a big client, hired a remote team member, got a complaint from leadership)

These become the raw material for your cold emails, cold call scripts, and landing page copy. You're not writing marketing language. You're mirroring back the lived experience of people like your prospect.

**Connection to outreach frameworks:** The 4-T email framework [→ Module 2] depends on JTBD insights for its Trigger and Think sentences. The Poke the Bear cold call framework [→ Module 3] depends on them for the illumination question. When someone's outreach isn't landing, the first question is: "Have you actually talked to your customers about why they bought? Not a survey — a real conversation about their timeline."

## 4. Stories vs. Facts [1/5]

A quieter concept in Josh's framework, but one that underpins his entire teaching philosophy.

> "Stories are more persuasive than facts because they bypass the analytical resistance that facts trigger."

When you present a fact — "Our platform reduces commission calculation time by 80%" — the prospect's analytical brain activates. They start evaluating, questioning, poking holes. "80%? In what context? With how many reps? What's their current process?" Facts invite scrutiny. That's the backfire effect at work.

> "When you try to convince people with facts that contradict their beliefs, they often dig in harder."

When you tell a story — "We talked to a VP Sales with 70 reps who was spending three days every month on commission statements in Google Sheets, and now it takes them four hours" — the prospect's brain does something different. It *imagines*. It places itself in the story. It sees the parallel to its own situation. The prospect persuades themselves through identification, not through being presented with evidence.

This is why Josh's frameworks lean so heavily on stories, examples, and specific scenarios rather than data points and feature lists. The Poke the Bear script doesn't cite statistics — it describes a concrete situation ("getting data out of Salesforce, hard-pasting Excel pages into Google Sheets") that makes the prospect think "that's exactly what we do."

**Coaching deployment:** When someone's pitch relies on stats and feature comparisons, redirect them. "Facts trigger resistance. Stories trigger recognition. Instead of telling your prospect you'll save them 80%, tell them about a customer who was in the same situation they're in — same messy process, same frustrations — and what changed. Let the prospect do the math themselves. People don't believe what you say. They believe what they say."

## Story: The Basecamp Customer Interview (Digital Agency CEO Timeline)

**Deploy when:** Explaining what a JTBD interview looks like in practice, coaching someone to understand their buyer's timeline, or showing how buyer circumstances — not product features — drive purchasing decisions.

Josh interviewed 30 Basecamp customers using the JTBD framework. One interview became his signature teaching example — a CEO of a digital agency whose buying journey unfolded like this:

**January:** The agency had 2 clients. They managed everything on a whiteboard. It worked fine. No pain, no problem.

**February:** They grew to 4 clients. Brought on a remote project manager. The whiteboard didn't work anymore with someone offsite, so they switched to Excel spreadsheets and email. Still manageable.

**April:** Things started slipping through the cracks. Files weren't in one place. Clients started complaining. The Excel-and-email system that worked at 4 clients was breaking at the new pace of work.

**May:** They tried Asana. Adoption was low — the team found it confusing and went back to their old habits. They switched back to Excel and email, but the pain was still there.

**June:** They landed a high-profile client. The CEO wanted to look professional — "more buttoned up." This was the trigger event. The push (things falling through cracks, client complaints) combined with the pull (wanting to be professional for a marquee client) finally exceeded the anxiety (another tool to learn) and the habit (Excel and email were familiar). They tried Basecamp. It was easy to make progress. They stuck with it.

One of the first things Josh noticed was how quickly customers opened up during these interviews — they "got real" about their circumstances and struggles. He could feel their emotion and energy, as if he'd been their friend for five years. These JTBD interviews were the *opposite* of how his typical sales conversations felt. That contrast changed how he approached every sales conversation afterward.

The power of this story is the timeline. The CEO didn't wake up one day and think "I need project management software." There was a *sequence* of escalating circumstances — growth, remote work, dropped balls, failed alternatives, a high-stakes trigger — that made the purchase inevitable. If Basecamp had sent this CEO a cold email in January, it would have been irrelevant. By June, it was exactly right. Understanding the buyer's timeline is how you know what to say and when to say it.

> "Armed with these insights, you can use the customer's language in outreach to attract similar customers."

## Story: The Milkshake Story (Clayton Christensen)

**Deploy when:** Someone needs JTBD explained simply, is stuck thinking about their product in terms of features and competitors, or needs the foundational mental model before getting into the application.

Josh frequently references Clayton Christensen's famous milkshake study as the purest illustration of Jobs-to-Be-Done thinking. A fast food chain wanted to sell more milkshakes. They did traditional market research — improved the flavor, made them thicker, added mix-ins. Sales didn't budge.

Christensen's team took a different approach. They stood in the restaurant and watched. They noticed a surprising pattern: a huge number of milkshakes were sold before 8 AM to people in business clothes who came in alone, bought a milkshake, and drove away.

When they interviewed these customers, they discovered the job: these people had a long, boring commute. They needed something to do with their free hand. They needed something that would last the whole drive and keep them full until lunch. They'd tried bananas (gone in 30 seconds), donuts (crumbs and sticky fingers), bagels (too dry without cream cheese, and cream cheese while driving is a disaster). The milkshake was perfect — it lasted 20 minutes through a straw, it filled them up, and it kept them occupied.

The milkshake wasn't competing against other milkshakes. It was competing against bananas, donuts, bagels, and boredom. The "job" wasn't "I want a milkshake." The job was "I need something to make my commute less tedious and keep me full until lunch."

This reframe is the heart of JTBD. When you stop asking "why do people buy our product?" and start asking "what job are people hiring our product to do?", you see your competition differently, your messaging differently, and your prospect's decision-making differently. Your product isn't competing against the companies on your competitive matrix. It's competing against whatever the prospect is doing *right now* to get the job done — even if that's a whiteboard, an Excel spreadsheet, or nothing at all.

## Putting It Together: From Customer Research to Pipeline

The four concepts in this module form a sequence:

1. **JTBD Framework** — The interview technique. Go talk to your customers. Reconstruct their timelines. Understand the circumstances that caused them to buy. This is the research.

2. **Four Forces** — The analytical lens. For every deal in your pipeline, map the push, pull, anxiety, and habit. This tells you where to focus — usually on reducing anxiety or naming the habit, not just increasing pull.

3. **Speak the Customer's Language** — The translation. Take the raw emotional language from your interviews and put it into your cold emails, cold call scripts, and discovery questions. This is what makes your outreach resonate instead of getting ignored.

4. **Stories vs. Facts** — The delivery mechanism. When you reach out or present, lead with specific customer stories that mirror the prospect's situation, not with stats and feature comparisons. Stories bypass resistance. Facts invite it.

When someone says "my messaging isn't landing" or "I don't understand why deals stall," the diagnosis almost always starts here. They haven't done the JTBD work. They're guessing at their buyer's language instead of using it. They're pushing features (pull) when the blocker is anxiety or habit. They're leading with facts when they should be leading with stories.

The fix is always the same: go talk to your customers. Not a survey. Not a focus group. A real conversation where you reconstruct the timeline — from the first moment something felt wrong to the moment they signed. Everything else flows from that.
