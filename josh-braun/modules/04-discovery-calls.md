# Module 4: Running Better Discovery Calls

## When to Load This Module
User has a discovery call coming up, wants better questions to ask, keeps losing deals after the first call, or turns discovery into a product demo.

---

## Buyers Have the Answers; Sellers Have the Questions (Freq: 2/5) [EMPHATIC]

Most reps walk into a discovery call with a pitch loaded and ready. They treat it like a presentation — here's what we do, here's why it's great, here are the features. That's backwards. Discovery is not a stage for your product. It's an investigation into the prospect's world.

> "Solutions have no value without problems. Sales isn't about pitching. It's about uncovering."

The entire premise of a good discovery call is that you don't have the answers yet. The buyer does. They know their pain. They know what keeps them up at night. They know what they've tried and why it failed. Your job is to ask the right questions so they tell you all of it — in their own words.

> "Buyers have the answers; sellers have the questions."

This is a fundamental identity shift. You're not the expert presenting solutions. You're the investigator uncovering problems. If the prospect finishes the call feeling like they talked more than you did, you probably ran a great discovery call. If you finish the call and realize you did most of the talking, you ran a demo — not a discovery.

The moment you start pitching during discovery, you've lost. You've shifted from understanding to convincing. And convincing triggers the Zone of Resistance. The prospect stops sharing, starts evaluating, and begins building objections in their head while you're still mid-sentence.

> "Do you understand me?" is the first question prospects ask themselves. If the answer is no, they won't trust your recommendation.

Before you can recommend anything, the prospect needs to feel understood. That only happens when you listen more than you talk and ask questions that prove you care about their situation — not your quota.

## The 4 Key Discovery Questions (Freq: 2/5)

Josh teaches four specific questions that form the backbone of any discovery call. They're sequenced intentionally — each one builds on the last, moving from aspiration to measurement to blockers to urgency.

### Question 1: The Future Happiness Question

> "If you're looking back 6 months from now, what has to have happened for you to feel really happy with your progress in {area}?"

This is the opening move. It gets the prospect out of their current frustration and into an aspirational state. They stop defending their current setup and start imagining what "great" looks like. That shift matters because thinking about happiness is intrinsically motivating — it creates energy in the conversation that complaint-focused questions don't.

Notice the phrasing: "looking back 6 months from now." You're asking them to stand in the future and look backwards. That's different from asking "Where do you want to be?" because it assumes progress has already happened. It makes the desired outcome feel real and achievable rather than abstract.

### Question 2: The Success Measurement Question

> "How do you measure success?"

Once the prospect has described their ideal future, this question forces them to get specific. Vague aspirations ("we want to be more efficient") become concrete metrics ("we want to reduce onboarding time from 6 weeks to 2 weeks").

This question also helps you quantify the cost of inaction. If they measure success by a metric they're currently failing at, the gap between where they are and where they want to be becomes impossible to ignore. And they've articulated it themselves — you didn't have to point it out.

### Question 3: The Blocker Question

> "What has stopped you from resolving this?"

This is the question most reps skip, and it's arguably the most important one. If the prospect has had this problem for a while and hasn't fixed it, there's a reason. Maybe they tried a competitor and it didn't work. Maybe internal politics blocked the initiative. Maybe they don't have budget. Maybe they don't actually care enough to fix it.

Whatever the blocker is, you need to know. Because if the blockers are still in place, what's different this time? If nothing has changed, buying your product won't change the outcome either. This question separates real opportunities from tire-kickers. It also surfaces objections early — before you've invested hours building a proposal for a deal that was never going to close.

### Question 4: The 1-10 Scale Question

> "On a scale of one to ten — with ten being mission critical and one being irritating but livable — where are you?"

This is your urgency thermometer. Josh has a simple framework for reading the answer:

- **8-10:** Green light. This is a priority. Keep the conversation moving.
- **4-7:** Yellow light. Proceed with caution. They recognize the problem but may not be motivated enough to act. Dig deeper into what would move it up the scale.
- **1-3:** Red light. They're telling you this is livable. Pushing harder won't help. You might need to park this one and revisit later.

The beauty of this question is that it gives you information without you having to guess. You don't have to interpret tone or read between the lines. They're telling you directly how much this matters to them. And because it's a scale — not a yes/no — there's no defensiveness. It's just a number.

## Future Visualization Technique (Freq: 1/5)

This is a specific technique Josh uses to help prospects articulate value in their own language — before you've said a word about your product.

> "Let's say your team is using our product for 2 months and they're able to build more connections. What would that allow them to do that they're not able to do today?"

The key phrase is "what would that allow them to do." You're not asking them to evaluate your product. You're asking them to imagine the downstream impact. And when they describe that impact — in their own words, with their own examples — they're selling themselves.

This technique works because people trust their own conclusions more than anyone else's claims. When the prospect says "If we could do X, that would free up my team to focus on Y, which is where we really need to be" — that's infinitely more compelling to them than you saying the same thing in a slide deck.

Use future visualization after the prospect has described a problem. They've told you what's broken. Now help them see what "fixed" looks like. The contrast between the two creates urgency that you don't have to manufacture.

One important note: keep the prompt grounded. Don't say "Imagine a world where everything is perfect." Say "Let's say your team is using this for 2 months." Two months is specific. It's believable. It's close enough to feel real. Grand, abstract visions trigger skepticism. Near-term, concrete visualization triggers motivation.

## Avoid Gotcha Questions (Freq: 2/5)

A gotcha question is one designed to trap a prospect into admitting a weakness so you can sell a solution. Reps use them all the time without realizing it.

> "Questions designed to trap a prospect into admitting a weakness. The root cause: intent is to sell instead of to understand."

Examples of gotcha questions: "Wouldn't it be great if you could do X?" "Have you calculated how much that's costing you?" "Don't you think your team deserves better tools?" These all have a built-in answer. The prospect knows exactly where you're steering them, and they resent it.

The damage isn't just that gotcha questions feel manipulative — it's that they destroy trust. The prospect was opening up about their real challenges, and then you weaponized what they told you. Now they'll give you less information, not more. You've turned a discovery conversation into a chess match where the prospect is trying to avoid giving you ammunition.

The fix is simple: check your intent before asking any question. Are you asking because you genuinely want to understand their situation? Or are you asking because you already know what answer you want and you're trying to lead them there?

If your discovery call feels like a cross-examination, you're doing it wrong. If it feels like a conversation with a friend who happens to know a lot about their industry, you're doing it right.

> "It's not my job to talk people into buying. My job is to illuminate potential problems. Some people will be interested in talking further. Others won't."

The prospect who answers your question honestly and says "actually, this isn't a big problem for us" is giving you a gift. They're saving you both time. A gotcha question prevents that honesty by making the prospect feel like any admission of a problem will be used against them.

## Adapt to Knowledge Level (Freq: 1/5)

Not all prospects show up to discovery calls with the same level of understanding. Some have researched your category extensively, read the G2 reviews, and talked to three competitors already. Others barely know your product category exists. You can't run the same discovery script for both.

> "How much do you know about X?" — rather than following a rigid script.

Josh's approach: ask early in the call where the prospect is in their understanding. A simple "How much do you know about [your product category]?" tells you everything you need about how to calibrate the rest of the conversation.

If they're highly informed, skip the category education. They don't need you to explain what a CRM does. They need you to explain why your CRM is different from the three others they're evaluating. Jump to differentiation and their specific use case. Spending time on basics with an informed buyer signals that you're not listening — and triggers the same ZOR as a generic pitch.

If they're early in their research, slow down. Educate without pitching. Help them understand the landscape and the problems your category solves. This is where the "teaching = selling" philosophy shines. The rep who educates an uninformed buyer earns trust. The rep who pitches an uninformed buyer creates confusion and resistance.

The worst thing you can do is treat every discovery call like the prospect knows nothing. Informed buyers feel condescended to. Uninformed buyers feel overwhelmed. Neither converts.

## Move Off the Product (Freq: 1/5)

Most reps can't resist talking about their product during discovery. The prospect mentions a problem, and the rep's instinct is to immediately say "Oh, we solve that." Every time you do this, you're ending the discovery too early.

Moving "off the product" means keeping the conversation focused on the prospect's situation, not your features. When they mention a pain point, resist the urge to jump to your solution. Instead, go deeper: "Tell me more about that." "How long has that been going on?" "What have you tried to fix it?"

The reason this matters is that every feature you mention during discovery is a feature the prospect can object to. If you say "We have automated reporting," the prospect might think "We tried automated reporting with our last vendor and it was terrible." Now you're defending a feature instead of understanding a problem.

Keep discovery about them. Save your product for the demo — which you'll run far better because you'll know exactly which problems to solve and which stories to tell.

Josh includes "Move Off the Product" as a key belief in his discovery call curriculum alongside "Detach From the Outcome" and "Solutions Have No Value Without Problems." These aren't just techniques — they're the philosophical foundation. If you believe solutions have no value without problems, you'll naturally spend your discovery call uncovering problems instead of presenting solutions.

## Stories

### How Liz Sold Me a Problem I Didn't Know I Had
*Deploy when: the user keeps pitching too early on discovery calls, or when they need to understand the difference between selling a solution and illuminating a problem.*

Josh tells the story of being on the receiving end of great prospecting. A seller named Liz didn't lead with her product. She didn't pitch features. She didn't try to book a demo. Instead, she illuminated a problem Josh didn't know he had.

This is the discovery equivalent of an illumination question — but applied to the entire call arc. Liz didn't assume Josh had a problem. She didn't tell him he had a problem. She asked questions and shared observations that caused him to realize, on his own, that something he thought was working fine actually wasn't.

That's the gold standard for discovery: the prospect walks away from the call thinking "I didn't realize I had that problem, but now I can't stop thinking about it." When that happens, you don't need to push for next steps. The prospect pulls you forward because they want to solve what you helped them see.

The contrast Josh draws is between Liz's approach and what most sellers do — which is show up with a solution and then try to convince the prospect they have the matching problem. That's backwards. Liz showed up curious, asked the right questions, and let the problem surface naturally. The sale followed because the problem was real, not manufactured.

This story embodies the core discovery philosophy: your job is not to pitch. Your job is to help prospects see something they couldn't see before. If the problem is real, the deal moves forward. If it's not, you've saved both parties time. Either outcome is a win.

### The Basecamp JTBD Interviews (Cross-Reference)
*Deploy when: the user wants to understand how great discovery questions come from understanding buyer psychology.*

Josh's experience interviewing 30 Basecamp customers using the Jobs-to-Be-Done framework fundamentally changed how he approaches discovery. The key observation: JTBD interviews felt completely different from typical sales conversations. Customers "opened up and got real" — sharing emotions, frustrations, and timelines as if they'd been friends for 5 years.

The takeaway for discovery calls: if your discovery conversation doesn't feel like a JTBD interview — if the prospect isn't opening up, sharing real struggles, getting emotional about their problems — you're probably asking the wrong questions. You're interrogating instead of eliciting. The best discovery calls feel like research conversations, not sales qualification.
