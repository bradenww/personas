# Module 10: Using Specific Language That Lowers Defenses

## When to Load This Module
User wants exact phrases to use, needs help with wording, keeps triggering defensive reactions, or wants to sound more natural on calls and in emails.

---

## 8 Language Patterns That Lower Resistance (Freq: 2/5)

Josh teaches eight specific language patterns that lower the Zone of Resistance. Each one works because it gives the prospect autonomy, signals low pressure, or taps into a hardwired human response. These aren't tricks — they only work when your intent is genuinely to discover, not to manipulate. The patterns share a single principle: surrender control so the prospect feels safe enough to tell you the truth.

### Pattern 1: "Help me out for a brief moment"

> "Humans are hard-wired to help — a trait that could have developed during human evolution when people were forced to cooperate in hunting."

This phrase works on cold calls because it activates a deep cooperative instinct. When you say "Can you help me out for a brief moment?" you're not asking for a meeting. You're not pitching. You're asking for help — and the human brain responds to that differently than it responds to a sales request. The word "brief" matters too. It communicates: I respect your time, and this won't take long.

Compare: "Do you have a few minutes to discuss how we help companies like yours..." versus "Can you help me out for a brief moment?" The first is a thinly disguised pitch. The second is a genuine, low-stakes request. Prospects hear the difference instantly.

### Pattern 2: "Might" Instead of "Can"

> "'Can' suggests certainty; 'Might' describes what's possible and gives freedom to choose. Autonomy lowers resistance."

This is a one-word swap that changes the entire energy of a sentence. "Can" sounds definitive, almost pushy — "We can save you 30% on your commissions process." "Might" sounds exploratory — "This might save you time on your commissions process."

"Can" implies: I already know this will work for you. "Might" implies: I'm not sure yet, and neither are you — let's find out. That uncertainty is disarming because it's honest. You genuinely don't know if your solution is a fit until you've had a conversation. "Might" reflects that reality. "Can" pretends you've already diagnosed the problem.

Use "might," "potentially," and "nearly" — words that don't promise certainty. Certainty from a stranger triggers suspicion. Possibility from a stranger invites curiosity.

### Pattern 3: "Would it make sense..."

> "Gives people freedom to choose."

"Would it make sense to spend a few minutes exploring whether this is relevant to your situation?" This is a question, not a statement — which means it prevents objections rather than inviting them. It also hands the decision to the prospect. You're not saying "Let me show you" or "I'd love to walk you through." You're asking whether the conversation itself makes sense.

The prospect can say "No, it doesn't make sense" — and that's fine. That easy out is exactly what makes the phrase work. When someone can say no without awkwardness, they're more willing to say yes.

### Pattern 4: "I'm not sure we're a fit"

See the dedicated section below — this pattern is important enough to stand on its own.

### Pattern 5: Labeling ("It sounds like..." / "It seems like...")

> "People like correcting, but they don't like being corrected."

Labeling is putting a name on what you observe. "It sounds like you've got a solid process for handling that." "It seems like the reporting side is where things get tricky." You're not asking a question — you're making an observation and letting the prospect respond.

The magic of labeling is that it creates a safe space for correction. When you say "It sounds like everything is working well," the prospect often responds by telling you what isn't working well. They're not answering a probing question — they're correcting your observation. That distinction matters psychologically. Answering questions feels like an interrogation. Correcting observations feels like a conversation between equals.

### Pattern 6: Defusing Statements ("Oh, that's okay" / "No problem")

> "Whenever you hear an objection say one of these defusing statements — 'Oh, that's okay.' 'No problem.' That's going to calm you down and eliminate commission breath."

These two-word phrases do double duty. First, they calm you down. When a prospect says "Not interested," your fight-or-flight kicks in and you want to counter. Saying "Oh, that's okay" interrupts that reflex. Second, they calm the prospect down. They expected you to push back. When you don't, the tension dissolves. The prospect often keeps talking — because you just proved you're not going to pressure them.

### Pattern 7: "Use words that don't promise certainty"

> "Use words that don't promise certainty: 'Nearly,' 'Might,' 'Potentially.'"

This extends beyond the "might" pattern. Across all your language — emails, calls, LinkedIn messages — strip out words that sound like guarantees and replace them with words that sound like possibilities. "We help companies reduce errors" becomes "Some teams we work with have been able to nearly eliminate errors." The second version is less bold but more believable. Certainty from a stranger smells like commission breath. Hedged language smells like honesty.

### Pattern 8: "I'm not sure" / Leading with Uncertainty

> "'I'm not sure' tells prospects you're putting their best interest first."

Saying "I'm not sure" at the start of a conversation — "I'm not sure this is relevant to you," "I'm not sure if we can help" — is the opposite of what most sales training teaches. Most reps are told to project confidence. Josh's insight is that projecting uncertainty about the fit (not about yourself) communicates something powerful: you're not desperate. You're not assuming. You're open to the possibility that there's no deal here — and that's okay. That energy is the opposite of commission breath.

## Elicitation over Interrogation (Freq: 2/5)

> "Elicitation is the art of getting someone to share information without feeling questioned — gentle prompts that invite people to volunteer the truth on their own."

This concept is the bridge between the individual language patterns above and how you use them in real conversations. Most salespeople interrogate: they fire questions at prospects one after another. Even good questions feel invasive when they come in rapid succession. Elicitation is different — it uses gentle prompts, observations, and open space to invite the prospect to share on their own terms.

> "It works because people have a natural urge to correct or confirm what they believe isn't quite accurate. Intelligence agencies use it, and therapists use it."

The intelligence agency comparison is deliberate. Trained interrogators know that direct questioning makes people clam up. The best information comes when the subject doesn't feel like they're being questioned at all — when they're correcting a misunderstanding, explaining something to someone who "doesn't quite get it," or filling an awkward silence.

### Elicitation vs. Interrogation in Practice

**Interrogation:** "What's your biggest challenge with your current commission process?"
**Elicitation:** "I imagine for most teams your size, calculating commissions is pretty straightforward..." (pause)

The interrogation version demands an answer. The elicitation version makes a slightly incorrect assumption and lets the prospect's natural urge to correct do the work. If commissions really are straightforward, they'll confirm it and you've learned something. If they're not, the prospect will jump in with "Actually, it's a nightmare because..." — and now you're in a real conversation they chose to have.

### How to Practice Elicitation

The shift is from questions to statements-that-invite-correction:

- Instead of "How do you currently handle X?" try "It sounds like your team handles X with [common approach]..."
- Instead of "Is that a problem for you?" try "I imagine that works pretty well for most teams..."
- Instead of "Would you be open to a conversation?" try "I'm guessing the timing probably isn't great for this..."

Each elicitation invites the prospect to correct you. And when they correct you, they're sharing information voluntarily — which feels completely different from answering questions under pressure.

## "Would It Be Ridiculous If..." (Freq: 2/5)

> "The accusation audit involves testing your assumption of how you think prospects are feeling about you."

"Would it be ridiculous if..." is Josh's go-to accusation audit phrase, adapted from Chris Voss's negotiation methodology. It works by naming the negative thought the prospect is probably already having — and framing it as something you'd understand if they confirmed it.

**Why it works:** On a cold call, the prospect is thinking some version of: "This is a waste of my time." "This person wants to sell me something." "I need to get off this phone." Those thoughts are the Zone of Resistance. Most reps ignore those thoughts and barrel ahead with their pitch. The accusation audit does the opposite — it calls them out.

- "Would it be ridiculous if I asked you a quick question about how you handle [process]?"
- "You're probably thinking this is just another sales call..."
- "I know I'm a complete stranger, and you're probably wondering why I'm calling..."

The phrase "would it be ridiculous" is calibrated to lower the stakes as far as possible. "Ridiculous" is almost humorous — it's not heavy or serious. It gives the prospect an easy out: "Yes, it would be ridiculous." And that's fine. You can respond with "Fair enough — appreciate your time" and move on. No damage done.

But most prospects don't say yes. They say some version of "No, go ahead" — because you just demonstrated that you understand their perspective. You acknowledged the awkwardness of the situation instead of pretending it doesn't exist. That gap between what they expected (a pushy pitch) and what they got (self-awareness and empathy) is what creates the opening.

The accusation audit is especially powerful at the start of cold calls and when re-engaging prospects who've gone quiet. In both situations, the prospect likely has negative associations — and naming those associations before they voice them takes away their power.

## "I'm Not Sure We're a Fit" (Freq: 2/5)

> "Since I don't know anything about your situation, I'm not sure we're a fit. Can I ask you a few questions?"

This single phrase is one of the most powerful things Josh teaches — and one of the most counterintuitive. Most sales training says to project confidence: "We've helped hundreds of companies just like yours." Josh says the opposite: lead with genuine uncertainty about whether you can help at all.

**What this phrase communicates:**
1. **I'm not assuming anything about your situation.** Most cold callers assume the prospect has a problem. That assumption triggers the ZOR because the prospect feels boxed in.
2. **I'm putting your interest first.** If you're genuinely not sure there's a fit, you're signaling that you'd rather walk away than waste their time. That eliminates commission breath.
3. **I'm asking for permission, not forcing a conversation.** "Can I ask you a few questions?" gives the prospect a choice. They can say no. And knowing they can say no makes them more likely to say yes.

The phrase only works when you actually mean it. If you say "I'm not sure we're a fit" but your intent is to book a meeting with everyone, the prospect will hear the dishonesty. Your tone will betray you. Your follow-up questions will feel leading instead of curious.

> "When you assume that everyone's a fit for what you have, people go into the zone of resistance, because you're going to try to be talking people into things."

When you genuinely believe you're a sorter — that you're for some people but not for everyone — "I'm not sure we're a fit" stops being a technique and starts being the truth. And the truth is disarming in ways that no technique can replicate.

### Variations

- "I have no idea if this is even relevant to you..."
- "I'm not sure this would make sense for your team..."
- "This may not be the right time, but..."
- "I don't know enough about your situation to know if we can help..."

Each variation communicates the same thing: I'm open to "no." And openness to "no" is the fastest path to an honest "yes."

## Surrender Control / Give an Easy Out (Freq: 2/5)

> "People need to feel they have choice and control. When you take away autonomy — by pushing, convincing, pressuring — they resist. When you give autonomy, they open up."

This is the principle underneath every language pattern in this module. Every phrase Josh teaches — "might" instead of "can," "would it make sense," "I'm not sure we're a fit," "it's totally fine if this isn't for you" — is a different way of surrendering control to the prospect.

> "It's totally fine if this isn't for you."

That sentence is the ultimate easy out. And the psychology is clean: when someone feels trapped, they fight to escape. When someone feels free to leave, they often choose to stay.

> "Giving people permission to say no increases your chances of your prospect saying yes."

This isn't reverse psychology. It's not a trick. It works because it's rooted in a genuine respect for the other person's autonomy. When you give an easy out, you're telling the prospect: "I see you as a person who can make their own decisions, not as a target I need to close." That shift in how you see the prospect changes how you sound. Your tone softens. Your pace slows. Your questions become genuinely curious instead of strategically leading.

### Where to Place Easy Outs

- **At the start of a cold call:** "If this doesn't make sense, I'll let you go."
- **After asking a question:** "And if the answer is that everything's working great, that's totally fine."
- **When proposing a next step:** "Would it make sense to continue this conversation, or is this not the right time?"
- **In cold emails:** "Either way, keep up the great work" or "No worries if the timing isn't right."

The key: every easy out must be genuine. If you say "It's totally fine if this isn't for you" but then follow up five more times when they say no — you've proven the easy out was a lie. The prospect will never trust you again, and they shouldn't.

> "I am of the firm belief that you can't cause urgency and you can't push people into something that they're not ready to do."

Surrendering control means accepting that the prospect's timeline is their timeline, their decision is their decision, and your job is to illuminate — not to convince.

### The Chris Voss Negotiation Transcript
*Deploy when: someone needs to see language patterns working in a real conversation, or when they think these techniques sound too theoretical to use.*

Josh published a real negotiation transcript demonstrating Chris Voss's method in action — mirroring, labeling, and accusation audits chained together in a live conversation. The transcript is the best proof that these language patterns aren't academic. The other person in the conversation doesn't know they're being "mirrored" or "labeled." They just feel heard. They open up. They share information they wouldn't have shared if they'd been asked directly.

The key lesson from the transcript: these techniques don't work as isolated moves you deploy mechanically. They work when they flow from genuine listening. When you actually care about what the other person is saying, mirroring happens naturally — you repeat their words because you're processing what they said. Labeling happens naturally — you name what you're observing because you're paying attention. The language patterns in this module are the same way. "Would it be ridiculous if..." works because it reflects real empathy. "I'm not sure we're a fit" works because it reflects real humility. The words are the surface. The intent is the engine.

When Josh teaches from this transcript, the lesson isn't "memorize these moves." It's "listen so carefully that mirroring and labeling happen naturally, because you actually care about what the other person is saying." The language follows the intent. Get the intent right and the language takes care of itself.
