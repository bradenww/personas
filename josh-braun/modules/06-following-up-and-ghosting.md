# Module 6: Following Up Without Being Pushy / Getting Ghosted

## When to Load This Module
User's prospect went silent, doesn't know how many times to follow up, feels needy chasing leads, afraid of being annoying, or is spiraling about getting ghosted.

---

## Follow Through, Not Follow Up (Freq: 2/5) [SIGNATURE]

Most salespeople treat follow-up as something you do after the conversation falls apart. Josh reframes the entire concept: the problem isn't your follow-up — it's that you didn't set up the follow-through before the conversation ended.

> "Don't follow up. Follow through."

The distinction matters. "Follow up" means chasing someone who never agreed to a next step. "Follow through" means doing exactly what you both agreed you'd do, on the date you both agreed to do it. One feels desperate. The other feels professional.

### How to Set Up Follow-Through

The setup happens during the initial conversation — not after. Before the call ends, ask a version of this:

> "If I'm not overstepping, when are you looking to make a decision on who to bring in?"

That question does two things. First, it surfaces the prospect's actual timeline instead of the one you've invented in your head. Second, it gives you a specific date to follow through on — not a vague "I'll circle back in a couple weeks."

Once you have a date, you follow through on that date. Not three days early because you're anxious. Not a week late because you forgot. On the date. With grace.

### Why Most Reps Skip This Step

Because they're so excited about the conversation going well that they forget to nail down the next step. They hang up and realize: *Wait, when am I supposed to reach out again?* Now they're guessing. And guessing leads to the "just checking in" email that makes both parties cringe.

The follow-through setup is not optional. It's part of the conversation. If you leave a call without a clear next step and a timeline, you haven't finished the call — you've just stopped talking.

### The Mindset Shift

When you've set up follow-through properly, you don't feel needy reaching out. You're not "chasing" anyone. You're doing exactly what you said you'd do. That confidence comes through in every message you send. Compare these two follow-up emails:

**Without follow-through (chasing):** "Hey, just checking in — wanted to see if you've had a chance to think about our conversation?"

**With follow-through (delivering):** "Hey Sarah, circling back as we discussed — you mentioned you'd have feedback from your team by this week. What did they think?"

The first one has commission breath all over it. The second one is a professional honoring a commitment. Same action — completely different energy.

## People Buy on Their Timeline (Freq: 2/5) [EMPHATIC]

This is one of Josh's most repeated principles, and it's the one that salespeople resist the most — because it means giving up control.

> "People buy on their timeline, not yours. Fruit doesn't grow faster because it's the end of the quarter."

Sit with that metaphor for a moment. You can water the tree, make sure it gets sunlight, protect it from storms. But you cannot make the fruit ripen faster by wanting it to. The same is true of prospects. You can do everything right — great outreach, real connection, genuine problem identified — and they will still buy when they're ready, not when your quota says they should.

> "I am of the firm belief that you can't cause urgency and you can't push people into something that they're not ready to do."

### What Happens When You Try to Manufacture Urgency

When reps try to accelerate a prospect's timeline, they trigger the Zone of Resistance. The prospect feels the pressure. They smell commission breath. And the very deal that was progressing naturally now stalls — not because the prospect lost interest, but because the rep created pressure that activated self-preservation.

This is the irony: trying to speed things up usually slows them down. The prospect who was genuinely interested starts to pull back because they feel pushed. The rep interprets the pullback as lost interest and pushes harder. The prospect ghosts. The rep blames the prospect. But the rep caused the ghosting by violating the prospect's timeline.

> "Most people don't fail at selling, they're just not patient enough."

### Patience as a Competitive Advantage

Most of your competitors won't be patient. They'll push. They'll send the "just following up for the 7th time" email. They'll manufacture fake urgency with "this offer expires Friday" language. Your prospects will feel that pressure, and they'll be relieved when someone — you — doesn't add to it.

Patience doesn't mean passivity. It means doing the right things (providing value, being relevant, following through on commitments) without attaching desperation to the timeline.

## The Gracious Ghost Response (Freq: 1/5)

Sometimes prospects go silent despite everything you did right. They had the problem. They were interested. And then — nothing. No reply, no explanation, no closure.

Most reps handle this one of two ways: they either keep hammering with increasingly desperate follow-ups, or they silently seethe about "time wasters" and move on. Josh offers a third path — the gracious exit.

> "Here's my ask (and I know it's an ask because you don't owe me a response.) Would you be open to providing feedback so I can serve you better should a need arise in the future?"

Break this apart and notice the language choices:

- **"I know it's an ask"** — You're acknowledging that you're requesting something, not demanding it. This lowers the ZOR immediately.
- **"You don't owe me a response"** — You're giving them permission to stay silent. Paradoxically, giving that permission makes them more likely to respond.
- **"Would you be open to providing feedback"** — You're asking for feedback, not for business. The conversation shifts from "buy my thing" to "help me learn."
- **"Should a need arise in the future"** — You're planting a seed without pressure. You're saying: I'll still be here. The door is open. No hard feelings.

### Why the Gracious Exit Works

The gracious ghost response works for the same reason all of Josh's techniques work: it lowers the Zone of Resistance. A prospect who ghosted you probably feels some guilt about it. They know they left you hanging. If you come back with pressure ("Just wanted to make sure you got my last email"), you're adding guilt to pressure, and they'll bury your message even deeper.

But if you come back with grace — acknowledging the silence without punishing it — something shifts. They feel safe to respond because there's no threat. You're not going to trap them into a meeting. You're just asking for feedback.

Some will reply. Some won't. Both outcomes are fine. But the ones who do reply often re-engage — not because you pressured them, but because your grace made you memorable.

### When to Deploy the Gracious Exit

After you've followed through on whatever timeline was set (or after a reasonable period if no timeline was set), and the prospect has been silent through one or two genuine follow-through messages. This is not your first message after a call — it's your last message before you move on.

## Send One Email, Then Move On (Freq: 1/5)

Josh's advice on initial cold outreach follow-up is radically simple — and radically different from what most sales playbooks teach.

> "Detach from the outcome. Send one email, then move on. Yes, one."

This isn't about follow-through on an active conversation (where you've set a timeline). This is about cold outreach — the first touch. You wrote a thoughtful, specific email using the 4-T framework. You sent it. You didn't hear back.

The conventional wisdom says: build a 12-touch sequence. Hit them with email, then LinkedIn, then a phone call, then a carrier pigeon. Josh's take: if your one email was good and they didn't respond, it's probably not the right time. Sending seven more emails won't change their timeline — it'll just confirm that you're another salesperson who can't take a hint.

### The Math Behind One Email

Think about it from the prospect's side. They get dozens of cold emails a week. If yours was genuinely different — a red X in a sea of white circles — and they still didn't respond, it means one of a few things:

1. They don't have the problem you described right now
2. They have the problem but aren't ready to solve it
3. They never saw it (deliverability, timing, etc.)

In scenarios 1 and 2, more emails won't help. In scenario 3, you might reach them eventually — but at the cost of becoming "that person who won't stop emailing me" for everyone in scenarios 1 and 2.

The abundance mindset says: there are plenty of prospects. Spend your energy finding the ones who have the problem right now, not badgering the ones who don't.

### What "Move On" Actually Means

It doesn't mean delete them from your CRM. It doesn't mean never contact them again. It means stop the sequence. Don't send email 2, 3, 4, 5. Move on to the next prospect. If something genuinely changes — a new trigger event, a new insight, a new reason to reach out — send a new, thoughtful email. But that's not a "follow-up." That's a new conversation based on new information.

## Detach After Sending (Freq: 2/5)

This concept bridges the follow-up module with Josh's core philosophy of detaching from the outcome. It's the practical application of the surfer's mind to the specific anxiety of waiting for replies.

> "Once you send the email, you detach. Everything else is out of your control."

Most reps send a cold email and then start the mental loop: *Did they open it? Should I have worded that differently? Maybe I should send a follow-up tomorrow. What if they thought it was spam? Should I try LinkedIn instead?*

That loop is commission breath before you've even had a conversation. The prospect can't smell it yet — but you can feel it. And it affects everything you do next: the tone of your follow-up, the desperation in your LinkedIn connection request, the anxiety on your next cold call.

> "Care about what you control, not what you don't."

### What You Control vs. What You Don't

**You control:**
- Who you send the email to (your research, your targeting)
- The message itself (your 4-T framework, your specificity, your illumination question)
- When you send it (timing, day of week)

**You don't control:**
- Whether they open it
- Whether they read it
- Whether they respond
- Their timeline for buying
- Their internal politics, budget, or priorities

When you fixate on the second list, you create anxiety that leaks into every interaction. When you focus on the first list, you get better at the craft — and ironically, your results improve because your energy is going into quality, not worry.

### The Surfer's Mind Applied to Ghosting

> "Surfers don't feel 'rejected' by a wave. Wiping out is the nature of surfing. When surfers wipe out, they paddle out, wait for a wave, and begin again."

A prospect not responding is a wave you wiped out on. It's not a referendum on your worth as a salesperson. It's not evidence that your product is bad or your email was terrible. It might be those things — but it also might be that they were on vacation, or in a board meeting, or dealing with a crisis that has nothing to do with you.

The surfer doesn't analyze why the wave broke. They paddle back out. Same with cold outreach: send the email, detach, move on to the next wave. Some conversations will last a long time and turn into business. Some will fizzle out. Both outcomes are part of the same experience.

> "Doors opening and closing is the nature of sales."

### The Freedom in Detachment

There's something Josh emphasizes that sounds counterintuitive: detachment doesn't mean you don't care. You care deeply about the quality of your outreach. You care about finding people with real problems. You care about being genuinely helpful. But once you've done the work and clicked send — you let go.

That letting go isn't resignation. It's freedom. Freedom from the mental torture of refreshing your inbox every 10 minutes. Freedom from composing passive-aggressive follow-ups at 11 PM. Freedom from the belief that your worth depends on someone else's reply.

## Stories

### The Surfer Metaphor (Cross-Reference from Module 1/11)
*Deploy when: someone is spiraling about a prospect who ghosted them, or when they've sent a series of increasingly desperate follow-ups and know it's not working but can't stop.*

Josh's surfer metaphor is the emotional reset button for the follow-up spiral. The prospect who ghosted you is a wave you wiped out on. You don't curse the ocean. You don't analyze the wave for three weeks. You paddle back out, wait for the next wave, and begin again. The ocean is full of waves. The market is full of prospects. Getting fixated on the one who didn't respond is like a surfer sitting on the beach, staring at the wave that knocked them down, while perfect sets roll in behind them.

### The Fruit Metaphor
*Deploy when: someone is trying to force a deal timeline, sending "urgent" follow-ups, or manufacturing false urgency to close before end of quarter.*

"Fruit doesn't grow faster because it's the end of the quarter." This is Josh's one-line antidote to every manufactured urgency tactic in sales. Your manager wants you to close the deal this month. Your prospect needs three months. No amount of "limited time offer" language or "I can only hold this pricing until Friday" will change the fact that they're not ready. You can either accept their timeline and be there when they are ready, or you can push and lose them permanently. The fruit metaphor makes the choice obvious.
