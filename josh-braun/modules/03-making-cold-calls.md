# Module 3: Making Cold Calls Without Sounding Salesy

**Load when:** The user needs to make cold calls, wants a script or framework, sounds awkward or pushy on the phone, or dreads picking up the phone. This module covers Josh's complete cold call architecture — the Poke the Bear framework, illumination questions, permission-based openers, and the critical distinction between neutral and leading questions that separates calls that open conversations from calls that trigger hang-ups.

---

## 1. The Poke the Bear Framework [4/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

This is Josh's flagship cold call structure and the concept he teaches most emphatically across sources. Seven steps that replace pitching with curiosity. The entire design principle: get the prospect to think differently about their current solution without you having an agenda.

> "Pique curiosity by asking a question that gets prospects to think differently about their current solution without having an agenda. In other words, poke the bear."

### The 7 Steps

**Step 1 — Permission.** Introduce yourself honestly, acknowledge you're a stranger. "Hi Pete, Beth Jones with CaptivateIQ. We've never spoken before." Then ask if they can help you out for a moment. No tricks, no false familiarity, no begging for 27 seconds. Honesty is disarming because it's the opposite of what prospects expect from a cold caller.

**Step 2 — Verify.** Confirm you're talking to the right person. "To make sure I'm in the right place, are you involved in calculating commissions at ACME?" This shows respect for their time. It also prevents you from pitching someone who can't make the decision — a waste of your energy and theirs.

**Step 3 — Context.** Give a brief, relevant reason for the call. "We're working with sales teams with over 70 reps, and I came across your name." This is one sentence — not a pitch, not a value prop, just enough context so they know why you called *them* specifically. The specificity matters. "Sales teams with over 70 reps" signals you understand their world. Generic context ("companies like yours") signals you don't.

**Step 4 — Identify if There's a Problem.** Ask a neutral question about their current process. "Is calculating and running commissions a manual process involving spreadsheets, or is it automated?" You're not assuming they have a problem. You're discovering whether one exists. This is the sorting mindset — you're for some people, but you're not for everyone.

**Step 5 — Labeling.** Reflect back what you heard using Chris Voss's labeling technique. "Sounds like you're getting data out of Salesforce, hard-pasting Excel pages into Google Sheets and then manually making adjustments, one sheet at a time." Labeling does two things: it makes the prospect feel understood, and it slows the conversation down. Both of these lower the Zone of Resistance. When someone feels heard, they stop defending and start talking.

**Step 6 — Poke the Bear.** This is the illumination question — the step that gives the framework its name. "I often hear that for orgs your size, it takes a few days to calculate and send statements to reps. How does that compare with your experience?" Notice: this is not a leading question. It's neutral. You're not telling them they have a problem. You're letting them discover it. If they say "Actually, we've got it down pretty well," that's fine — you've learned something. If they say "Yeah, it takes us about a week," they just articulated a problem you didn't have to sell them on.

**Step 7 — Tease What's Possible.** "I'm calling to see if you'd be open to learning about a different approach sales orgs with over 60 reps are using to calculate error-free commission statements in a few hours." The word "open" gives them autonomy. You're not pushing a meeting — you're offering one. The specificity ("error-free commission statements in a few hours") paints a concrete picture of what's possible without making a hard claim.

> "Ditch the pitch. Poke the bear."

### Why Poke the Bear Works When Normal Scripts Don't

Most cold call scripts are built around making statements. Poke the Bear is built around asking questions. The structural difference matters because of a principle Josh returns to constantly:

> "It's easier to raise objections when you make statements. It's harder to raise objections when you're asking a question."

When you make a statement — "We help companies reduce commission calculation time by 80%" — the prospect's brain immediately searches for reasons to disagree. That's the Zone of Resistance. But when you ask a question — "How long does it take your team to calculate commissions?" — the prospect's brain searches for an answer. You've redirected their mental energy from defending to reflecting.

The second reason is even more powerful:

> "People don't believe what you say. They believe what they say."

When you ask a question and the prospect articulates their own problem, they've persuaded themselves. You didn't have to convince anyone of anything. The problem is real to them because *they* said it, not you. This is the core of Josh's entire philosophy applied to a phone call: selling without convincing.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone shows you their cold call script and it's built around statements ("We help companies..." "Our platform enables..." "The reason for my call is..."), have them rewrite every statement as a question. "Your script has seven statements and one question. Flip it. Poke the Bear is seven questions and maybe one statement. The prospect should be doing most of the talking."

## 2. Illumination Questions [3/5] [SIGNATURE]

Step 6 of Poke the Bear — the "poke" itself — depends entirely on illumination questions. This is the concept that makes the whole framework work. Without a strong illumination question, the other six steps are just polite small talk.

> "The superpower is you need to shine a light on a problem that people don't know about because every one of your prospects is running in their own sneakers today. I call these illumination questions."

An illumination question does one thing: it gets the prospect to realize they might have a problem they weren't thinking about. It does NOT prompt a desired response. It does NOT assume the answer. It shines a light and lets the prospect draw their own conclusion.

> "It's not your job to talk people into buying. Your job is to illuminate potential knowledge gaps so people can draw their own conclusions."

### How to Build an Illumination Question

Start with this: **What do you know that your prospect might not know that can hurt them?**

You know your industry. You've talked to hundreds of companies like theirs. You've seen patterns they can't see from inside their own organization. You know the hidden costs, the blind spots, the things that break at scale, the risks they've normalized because they've never seen an alternative. Package that knowledge into a neutral question.

The construction follows a pattern: reference what you've heard from similar companies, state a specific consequence, then ask how it compares with their experience. "I often hear that for orgs your size, [specific problem]. How does that compare with your experience?"

Use language that doesn't promise certainty: "Nearly," "Might," "Potentially." These words give the prospect freedom to agree or disagree without feeling boxed in. "Nearly" is different from "definitely." "Might" is different from "will." Certainty creates pressure. Possibility creates curiosity.

### The Test for a Good Illumination Question

If your question makes the prospect think "Hmm, I'm not sure" — that's an illumination question. It opened a gap in their knowledge that they now want to close. If it makes them think "This person is trying to sell me something" — it's a leading question dressed up as curiosity, and the prospect's ZOR just went up.

The difference between an illumination question and a leading question isn't subtle — it's structural. An illumination question has no built-in answer. A leading question has a trap door. Prospects can feel the difference in their gut, even if they can't articulate why.

**Coaching deployment:** When someone can't figure out their illumination question, ask them: "Tell me three things you've learned from working with companies like your prospect's that they probably don't know. Things that would make them say 'Wait, really?' Now turn one of those into a question that doesn't assume they have the problem — just asks if they do."

## 3. The First 30 Seconds [2/5]

The first 30 seconds of a cold call determine everything. Most reps blow it by launching into a pitch — "The reason for my call is..." — which immediately triggers the Zone of Resistance. The prospect's brain goes into fight-or-flight before you've said anything useful.

Josh's approach is the opposite: be honest, be human, and ask for permission.

### Three Rules for the Opening

**1. Be honest about who you are.** Say "We've never spoken before" right away. Don't pretend you know them. Don't use false familiarity ("Hey Pete, how's your Tuesday going?"). Prospects can smell it. Honesty is disarming precisely because it's rare — most cold callers try to create false rapport, and prospects are conditioned to detect it.

**2. Ask for help, not time.** "Can you help me out for a brief moment?" works because humans are hard-wired to help — a trait that likely developed during human evolution when people were forced to cooperate in hunting. This triggers a fundamentally different response than "Do you have 27 seconds?" The help request activates cooperation instinct. The time request activates the defense instinct.

**3. Verify before you go further.** Confirm you're talking to the right person in the right role before you say anything else. "To make sure I'm in the right place, are you involved in [their function]?" This shows respect for their time and signals something important: you're not carpet-bombing everyone in the company with the same script. You called *them* for a reason.

> "Most cold call objections can be avoided by nailing the first 30 seconds."

The entire purpose of the opening is to lower the Zone of Resistance — not to sell, not to pitch, not to create urgency. Just to earn the next 60 seconds of conversation. If you can get past the first 30 seconds without triggering the ZOR, the rest of the Poke the Bear framework has room to work.

> "Whenever people feel like they are being persuaded, they enter the Zone of Resistance. The ZOR is a reflex reaction to sales pressure."

**Coaching deployment:** Have the user record or role-play their current opening. Count the seconds before they make a statement about their product or company. If it's under 15 seconds, their opening is a pitch, not a permission request. "Your prospect decided to hang up before you finished your second sentence. Let's rebuild: who are you, why should they not hang up, and are they even the right person?"

## 4. Neutral Questions vs. Leading Questions [3/5]

This distinction is the difference between a cold call that opens a conversation and one that triggers the Zone of Resistance. It's the single most common mistake Josh sees in cold call scripts — questions that *feel* like questions but function as pitches.

> "Leading questions encourage a desired answer so they feel manipulative. Illumination questions are neutral."

**Leading question:** "Wouldn't it be great if you could calculate commissions in hours instead of days?" — This has a built-in answer. The prospect knows you want them to say yes. They can feel the trap door under the question. ZOR goes up.

**Neutral question:** "I often hear that for orgs your size, it takes a few days to calculate and send statements to reps. How does that compare with your experience?" — This has no built-in answer. The prospect could say "Actually, we've got it down to a few hours" and that would be a perfectly valid response. The neutrality is what makes it safe to answer honestly.

The shift is fundamental: instead of leading questions that assume a problem, ask neutral questions that *discover* whether a problem exists. You're a sorter, not a seller. Some prospects will have the problem. Others won't. Both outcomes are fine — and meaning that is what makes the neutrality authentic.

> "Ask an illumination question about a potential problem and pass control to the prospect so they can make up their own mind."

### Why Neutral Questions Prevent Objections

When you use neutral questions, you also prevent objections before they happen.

> "Stop handling objections. Prevent them."

The reason most reps get hit with "not interested" or "we're all set" within the first 15 seconds is because their opening was a series of leading questions or statements that triggered defensiveness. The prospect isn't really saying they're not interested — they're saying "I can feel you trying to sell me something and I want it to stop." A neutral question doesn't trigger that response because there's nothing to defend against.

This connects to one of Josh's core principles: shifting from statements to questions. "It's easier to raise objections when you make statements. It's harder to raise objections when you're asking a question." Leading questions are statements disguised as questions — the prospect's brain treats them as statements. Neutral questions are genuine questions — the prospect's brain treats them as invitations to think.

**Coaching deployment:** Have the user read their script questions aloud. After each one, ask: "Does this question have a right answer? If a prospect gave you the 'wrong' answer, would you be disappointed?" If yes, it's a leading question. Rewrite it so that any answer — including one that disqualifies the prospect — is useful information.

## Putting It Together: The Cold Call Architecture

The four concepts in this module form a layered system:

1. **The First 30 Seconds** — The door. Get through it without triggering the ZOR, or nothing else matters.
2. **Neutral Questions** — The principle. Every question in your call should be genuinely neutral — no trap doors, no leading, no built-in right answers.
3. **Poke the Bear** — The structure. Seven steps that turn neutral questions into a complete conversation arc.
4. **Illumination Questions** — The engine. The single question in Step 6 that makes the prospect think differently about their current situation.

When someone's cold calls aren't working, diagnose which layer is failing:
- Are they getting hung up on immediately? (First 30 seconds problem — their opening triggers the ZOR)
- Are they getting "not interested" after their first question? (Leading question problem — their questions feel like pitches)
- Are they having polite conversations that go nowhere? (Illumination problem — they're not poking the bear, just making small talk)
- Are they booking meetings but losing deals in discovery? (Wrong module — load Module 4)
