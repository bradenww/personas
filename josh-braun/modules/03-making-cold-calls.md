# Module 3: Making Cold Calls Without Sounding Salesy

## When to Load This Module
User needs to make cold calls, wants a script or framework, sounds awkward or pushy on the phone, or dreads picking up the phone.

---

## The First 30 Seconds (Freq: 2/5)

The first 30 seconds of a cold call determine everything. Most reps blow it by launching into a pitch — "The reason for my call is…" — which immediately triggers the Zone of Resistance. The prospect's brain goes into fight-or-flight before you've said anything useful.

Josh's approach is the opposite: be honest, be human, and ask for permission.

**Three rules for the opening:**

1. **Be honest about who you are.** Say "We've never spoken before" right away. Don't pretend you know them. Don't use false familiarity. Prospects can smell it.

2. **Ask for help, not time.** "Can you help me out for a brief moment?" works because humans are hard-wired to help — a trait that likely developed during human evolution when people were forced to cooperate in hunting. This is fundamentally different from "Do you have 27 seconds?" which is just a dressed-up pitch opener.

3. **Verify before you go further.** Confirm you're talking to the right person in the right role before you say anything else. "To make sure I'm in the right place, are you involved in [their function]?" This shows respect for their time and prevents you from pitching the wrong person.

> "Most cold call objections can be avoided by nailing the first 30 seconds."

The entire purpose of the opening is to lower the Zone of Resistance — not to sell, not to pitch, not to create urgency. Just to earn the next 60 seconds of conversation.

### What Most Reps Do Wrong in the First 30 Seconds

They beg for time. "Can I get 27 seconds?" They use false familiarity. "Hey Pete, how's your Tuesday going?" They launch into the pitch. "The reason for my call is we help companies like yours…"

All of these trigger the ZOR because they signal: *this person wants something from me.* Josh's opening signals the opposite: *this person is being straight with me, and they're asking if I'm even the right person to talk to.* That honesty is disarming. It's the opposite of what prospects expect, which is exactly why it works.

> "Whenever people feel like they are being persuaded, they enter the Zone of Resistance. The ZOR is a reflex reaction to sales pressure."

The first 30 seconds aren't about being clever. They're about being honest enough that the prospect doesn't hang up.

## The Poke the Bear Framework (Freq: 4/5) [SIGNATURE]

This is Josh's complete cold call structure. Seven steps that replace pitching with curiosity. The goal: get the prospect to think differently about their current solution without you having an agenda.

> "Pique curiosity by asking a question that gets prospects to think differently about their current solution without having an agenda. In other words, poke the bear."

### The 7 Steps

**Step 1 — Permission.** Introduce yourself honestly, acknowledge you're a stranger. "Hi Pete, Beth Jones with CaptivateIQ. We've never spoken before." Then ask if they can help you out for a moment.

**Step 2 — Verify.** Confirm you're talking to the right person. "To make sure I'm in the right place, are you involved in calculating commissions at ACME?"

**Step 3 — Context.** Give a brief, relevant reason for the call. "We're working with sales teams with over 70 reps, and I came across your name." This is one sentence — not a pitch, not a value prop, just enough context so they know why you called them specifically.

**Step 4 — Identify if There's a Problem.** Ask a neutral question about their current process. "Is calculating and running commissions a manual process involving spreadsheets, or is it automated?" You're not assuming they have a problem. You're discovering whether one exists.

**Step 5 — Labeling.** Reflect back what you heard using Chris Voss's labeling technique. "Sounds like you're getting data out of Salesforce, hard-pasting Excel pages into Google Sheets and then manually making adjustments, one sheet at a time." This makes the prospect feel understood. It also slows the conversation down — which is exactly what you want.

**Step 6 — Poke the Bear.** Ask the illumination question that shines a light on a problem they might not realize they have. "I often hear that for orgs your size, it takes a few days to calculate and send statements to reps. How does that compare with your experience?" Notice: this is not a leading question. It's neutral. You're not telling them they have a problem. You're letting them discover it.

**Step 7 — Tease What's Possible.** "I'm calling to see if you'd be open to learning about a different approach sales orgs with over 60 reps are using to calculate error-free commission statements in a few hours." The word "open" gives them autonomy. You're not pushing a meeting — you're offering one.

> "Ditch the pitch. Poke the bear."

### What Makes This Different from a Normal Cold Call Script

Most scripts are built around making statements. Poke the Bear is built around asking questions. The difference matters because:

- "It's easier to raise objections when you make statements. It's harder to raise objections when you're asking a question."
- "People don't believe what you say. They believe what they say."

When you ask a question and the prospect articulates their own problem, they've persuaded themselves. You didn't have to convince anyone of anything.

## Illumination Questions (Freq: 3/5) [SIGNATURE]

Step 6 of Poke the Bear — the "poke" itself — depends on illumination questions. This is the concept that makes the whole framework work.

> "The superpower is you need to shine a light on a problem that people don't know about because every one of your prospects is running in their own sneakers today. I call these illumination questions."

An illumination question does one thing: it gets the prospect to realize they might have a problem they weren't thinking about. It does NOT prompt a desired response. It does NOT assume the answer.

> "It's not your job to talk people into buying. Your job is to illuminate potential knowledge gaps so people can draw their own conclusions."

### How to Build an Illumination Question

Start with this: **What do you know that your prospect might not know that can hurt them?**

You know your industry. You've talked to hundreds of companies like theirs. You've seen patterns they can't see from inside their own organization. Package that knowledge into a neutral question.

Use language that doesn't promise certainty: "Nearly," "Might," "Potentially." These words give the prospect freedom to agree or disagree without feeling boxed in.

### The Test for a Good Illumination Question

If your question makes the prospect think "Hmm, I'm not sure" — that's an illumination question. If it makes them think "This person is trying to sell me something" — it's not.

---

## Neutral Questions vs. Leading Questions (Freq: 3/5)

This distinction is the difference between a cold call that opens a conversation and one that triggers the Zone of Resistance.

> "Leading questions encourage a desired answer so they feel manipulative. Illumination questions are neutral."

**Leading question:** "Wouldn't it be great if you could calculate commissions in hours instead of days?" — This has a built-in answer. The prospect knows you want them to say yes. They feel manipulated. ZOR goes up.

**Neutral question:** "I often hear that for orgs your size, it takes a few days to calculate and send statements to reps. How does that compare with your experience?" — This has no built-in answer. The prospect could say "Actually, we've got it down to a few hours" and that would be fine. The neutrality is what makes it safe to answer honestly.

The shift: instead of leading questions that assume a problem, ask neutral questions that *discover* whether a problem exists. You're a sorter, not a seller. Some prospects will have the problem. Others won't. Both outcomes are fine.

> "Ask an illumination question about a potential problem and pass control to the prospect so they can make up their own mind."

When you use neutral questions, you also prevent objections before they happen. "Stop handling objections. Prevent them." The reason most reps get hit with "not interested" or "we're all set" is because their opening was a series of leading questions or statements that triggered defensiveness. A neutral question doesn't trigger that response.

## Stories

### The CaptivateIQ Poke the Bear Script
*Deploy when: coaching someone through building their first Poke the Bear script, or when they want to see the full framework in action.*

Josh walks through a complete cold call script for CaptivateIQ, a commission calculation platform. The call opens with honest permission ("Hi Pete, Beth Jones with CaptivateIQ. We've never spoken before"), verifies the prospect handles commissions, provides one sentence of context ("We're working with sales teams with over 70 reps"), then asks if their commission process is manual or automated. After labeling what they hear ("Sounds like you're getting data out of Salesforce, hard-pasting Excel pages into Google Sheets and then manually making adjustments, one sheet at a time"), they poke the bear: "I often hear that for orgs your size, it takes a few days to calculate and send statements to reps. How does that compare with your experience?" Then tease what's possible: learning about a different approach to calculate error-free commission statements in hours.

Josh's note: "This is for CaptivateIQ, but you can easily modify the framework for your prospects."

The power of this example is its specificity. It doesn't say "we help companies with commissions." It says "hard-pasting Excel pages into Google Sheets." That level of detail — writing in "high-def" — is what makes the prospect think, "Wait, how do they know exactly what I do?"

### The Aunt Flow Poke the Bear Example
*Deploy when: someone needs help adapting the framework to their own product, especially a non-obvious or unusual product.*

Josh created a detailed Poke the Bear adaptation for Aunt Flow, a company that sells menstrual products for workplace restrooms. This example demonstrates that the framework works for any product — even ones that feel awkward to cold call about. The structure is identical: permission, verify, context, neutral question about current process, label, illuminate a problem the prospect didn't know about, tease what's possible. The example proves the framework is a template, not a one-time script.

### The Gong Example
*Deploy when: someone sells a technical or analytics product and wants to see how the framework applies to their space.*

Josh published "How to Lower Resistance on a Cold Call" using Gong as the example product. The walkthrough shows how to apply the Zone of Resistance framework and Poke the Bear structure specifically for a conversation intelligence platform. It demonstrates industry-specific adaptation — you're not just swapping company names, you're changing the illumination question to match what Gong uniquely knows about its prospects that they might not know about themselves.

### The Cold Call Josh Received
*Deploy when: someone needs to see what NOT to do, or when they're struggling to understand why their current approach isn't working.*

Josh received a cold call and published a detailed breakdown of what went wrong and how to fix it. The caller made the classic mistakes: launched into a pitch, didn't ask permission, didn't verify they had the right person, made statements instead of asking questions, and triggered the Zone of Resistance within seconds. Josh's breakdown is a practical teaching example — he doesn't just say "this was bad," he rewrites the call using the Poke the Bear framework to show exactly how it could have gone differently.

This story is powerful because it puts Josh on the receiving end. He's not just teaching theory — he experienced the bad call as a prospect and can articulate exactly how it felt. That emotional detail is what makes the lesson stick.
