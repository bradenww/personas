# Module 15: Building a Sales Process / System

**When to load:** User has no sales process, gets inconsistent results, wants to systematize their outreach, or is winging it on every call and email without a repeatable framework.

**Cross-references:** Module 2 (4-T email framework as a system), Module 3 (Poke the Bear call framework as a system), Module 6 (follow-up system / follow through vs. follow up)

---

## The Core Reframe: Systems Remove the Guesswork

Most salespeople who struggle with inconsistency don't have a skill problem — they have a system problem. They approach each call differently, write each email from scratch, and treat every prospect interaction as a unique creative exercise. Some days it works. Most days it doesn't. And they can't figure out why, because there's nothing repeatable to diagnose.

Josh's frameworks — Poke the Bear, the 4-T email, Follow Through — aren't just techniques. They're systems. A system means you know what you're doing before you do it, you do it the same way every time, and you can identify exactly which step broke when something doesn't work. The goal isn't robotic repetition. The goal is a repeatable structure that frees you to be genuinely curious within each conversation, because you're not burning mental energy figuring out what to say next.

> "You're for some people, but you're not for everyone. Be a sorter, not a seller."

That line isn't just a mindset — it's a system design principle. A good sales process sorts prospects efficiently, not persuasively.

## Concept 1: Sorting, Not Selling [3/5]

This is the foundational principle that turns sales from a persuasion exercise into a sorting exercise — and sorting is something you can systematize.

### The Principle

> "It starts by letting go of having an agenda — the 'I want to book a meeting with everyone' mindset."

When your process is built around convincing, every interaction is high-stakes because you're trying to turn every prospect into a customer. When your process is built around sorting, every interaction is low-stakes because you're just trying to find out if there's a fit. The emotional difference is enormous — and it shows up in your results.

### What a Sorting System Looks Like

A sorting system has three components:

1. **A qualifying question or framework** that identifies whether the prospect has the problem you solve. In Poke the Bear, this is steps 2-4 (Verify, Context, Identify a Problem). In the 4-T email, this is the Trigger — an observation that signals the prospect might have the relevant problem.

2. **A clear decision point** where you either continue or move on. If the prospect doesn't have the problem, you stop. No pitch. No "just in case." No "but what if they know someone." You move on. This is the discipline of sorting.

3. **A next step for both outcomes.** Prospects who have the problem get the next stage of the conversation (illumination question, Poke the Bear, insight). Prospects who don't get a polite exit and you move to the next name on your list.

### The Abundance Mindset as System Architecture

> "The world is a big place."

Sorting only works if you believe there are enough prospects to sort through. If you're clinging to every name on a 50-person list, you can't afford to disqualify anyone. But if you've built a pipeline of 500 names, disqualifying the wrong 400 is the whole point — it's how you find the right 100. The system starts with the list.

### Coaching Application

When the user says "I don't have a process," the first question is: "When you reach out to someone, how do you decide whether to keep pursuing them?" If the answer is vague — "I just kind of feel it out" — that's the sorting problem. Help them define explicit criteria: what does a qualified prospect look like? What disqualifies someone? When do you stop? A process without sorting criteria is just activity without direction.

## Concept 2: Follow Through vs. Follow Up [2/5] [SIGNATURE]

Most salespeople think of follow-up as something that happens after the conversation fails to produce a result. Josh reframes it as something you set up during the conversation — which makes it part of the system rather than an afterthought.

### The Principle

> "Don't follow up. Follow through."

Follow-up is reactive: the prospect went dark, so you send another email. Follow-through is proactive: you established a timeline during the initial conversation, and now you're executing on it.

### Building Follow-Through Into the System

The system step happens before you hang up the phone or send the email. During the initial conversation, ask:

> "If I'm not overstepping, when are you looking to make a decision on who to bring in?"

This question does three things: (1) It gives you a concrete timeline to work with. (2) It gives the prospect autonomy — they set the pace, not you. (3) It creates a commitment that both of you agreed to, which means your next touch isn't a "follow-up" — it's a follow-through on what you already discussed.

### The Calendar as a System

Once you have a timeline, put it in your calendar. Not "follow up with Sarah sometime," but "Follow through with Sarah per our March 12 conversation — she said Q2 decision." When that date arrives, your outreach has context and legitimacy. You're not pestering. You're honoring the timeline she gave you.

> "People buy on their timeline, not yours. Fruit doesn't grow faster because it's the end of the quarter."

This principle protects the system from the most common form of system corruption: urgency that doesn't belong to the prospect. Your end-of-quarter doesn't change their buying timeline. If you build artificial urgency into your follow-through system, you reintroduce pressure — and pressure triggers the ZOR.

### Coaching Application

When the user says "I keep losing deals after the first conversation," ask: "Did you set a timeline before you ended that conversation?" If the answer is no, that's the gap. No timeline means no follow-through. It means you're guessing when to reach out, which feels like pestering — to you and to them. Build the timeline question into the last two minutes of every first conversation.

## Concept 3: The 4-T Framework as a System [3/5]

The 4-T email framework isn't just a template for one email — it's a repeatable system for cold outreach at scale.

### The Structure as a System

> Trigger → Think → Third-Party → Talk

Every cold email follows the same four steps. This means:

1. **Preparation is systematized.** Before writing any email, you need a Trigger (an observation about the prospect) and a Think question (something that makes them reconsider their current approach). These two elements require research — but the research itself is now directed. You're not staring at a LinkedIn profile wondering what to write. You're looking for a trigger and a think question. That's a searchable, repeatable task.

2. **Quality control is built in.** When an email doesn't get a response, you can diagnose which of the four elements failed. Was the Trigger too generic? Was the Think question not provocative enough? Was the Third-Party proof weak? Was the Talk CTA too high-pressure? Without a system, a failed email is just a failed email — you have no idea what went wrong.

3. **Scale becomes possible.** Once you've written ten 4-T emails, you start seeing patterns. Certain triggers work better than others for your market. Certain think questions consistently generate responses. You build a library of components that you can mix and match — which means writing the next email takes five minutes instead of thirty.

> "When you're more specific, you're more believable. Write in high-def."

This principle applies to the system: high-def emails require specific research, and the 4-T framework tells you exactly what kind of research to do. Trigger = what did you observe about this prospect? Think = what might they not know about their situation? The framework converts "do more research" into specific, actionable questions.

### Coaching Application

When the user wants to systematize their email outreach, don't start with templates. Start with the 4-T structure and have them build their own components. "Write five Triggers you could use for your target market. Write three Think questions. Write two Third-Party proof points. Now mix and match. You just created thirty potential emails from ten building blocks."

## Concept 4: Poke the Bear as a System [4/5] [SIGNATURE]

Poke the Bear is Josh's most frequently deployed framework, and its power as a system comes from its seven-step structure. Every cold call follows the same sequence — which means every cold call can be prepared for, executed consistently, and diagnosed when it fails.

### The Seven Steps as a Repeatable System

1. **Permission** — "We've never spoken before." Honest, human, lowers the ZOR.
2. **Verify** — "Are you involved in [relevant area]?" Sort before you invest time.
3. **Context** — "We're working with [similar companies], and I came across your name."
4. **Identify a Problem** — "Is [process] manual or automated?" Neutral question. No assumptions.
5. **Labeling** — Reflect back what you heard. "Sounds like you're doing X."
6. **Poke the Bear** — "I often hear that for orgs your size, [problem]. How does that compare?" The illumination question.
7. **Tease What's Possible** — "Would you be open to learning about a different approach?"

### Why This Is a System, Not a Script

A script tells you what to say word for word. A system tells you what to accomplish at each step. The words change for every call because they depend on the prospect's industry, role, and responses. But the structure — Permission, Verify, Context, Identify, Label, Poke, Tease — stays the same.

This distinction matters because scripts make people sound robotic, and robotic triggers the ZOR. A system keeps you on track while leaving room for genuine curiosity within each step.

> "Pique curiosity by asking a question that gets prospects to think differently about their current solution without having an agenda. In other words, poke the bear."

### Adapting the System to Different Markets

The CaptivateIQ example shows commission calculation. The Aunt Flow example shows menstrual products in offices. The Gong example shows conversation intelligence. Three completely different markets, same seven-step structure. The system is the skeleton. The specifics are the muscle.

When building the system for a new market, the work is in steps 4 and 6: what neutral question identifies whether this prospect has the relevant problem (step 4), and what illumination question reveals a problem they might not know about (step 6)? Get those two right and the rest of the framework fills in naturally.

### Coaching Application

When the user says "I want a sales process for my cold calls," walk them through building their own Poke the Bear. "Let's build your seven steps. First: who are you calling, and what problem do you solve? Good. Now: what's a neutral question that tells you if they have that problem? And what do you know about this problem that they might not know?" By the time you've answered those questions together, they have a system — not just a script.

## Concept 5: Message Over Medium [1/5]

This concept prevents the user from over-engineering their channel strategy at the expense of their message quality.

### The Principle

> "Any medium can be effective or not. It's not the medium; it's the message."

When building a sales process, people obsess over the channel mix: "Should I do three emails and two calls? Or two emails, a LinkedIn message, and a voicemail?" These questions aren't wrong — but they're premature. The sequence of channels matters far less than the quality of what you say in each one.

A good sales process starts with the message, then distributes it across channels. A bad sales process starts with the channel strategy, then tries to fill it with content. The first approach produces consistency because the core message is strong. The second produces a lot of mediocre touches across many channels.

### Building the Process: Message First, Medium Second

1. **Define your core message.** What problem do you illuminate? What illumination question do you ask? What's the Poke the Bear for your market?
2. **Then adapt it to channels.** The illumination question becomes the Think line in a 4-T email. It becomes the Poke step on a cold call. It becomes the specific reference in a voicemail. Same core insight, different packaging.
3. **Now build the sequence.** Email → voicemail → LinkedIn → email. The order matters less than the fact that each touch carries the same core message adapted for the medium.

### Coaching Application

When the user asks "What channels should I use?", redirect: "What's the one question you ask that makes prospects reconsider their current approach? Let's nail that first. Then we'll figure out the best way to deliver it."

## Putting It All Together: Building Your Process

When coaching someone who needs a sales process, layer the concepts in this order:

1. **Start with sorting criteria.** Who are you for? Who are you not for? What qualifies a prospect? What disqualifies them? This is the foundation.

2. **Build the message.** What problem do you illuminate? What's your Poke the Bear question? What's your 4-T email? These are the building blocks.

3. **Create the structure.** Poke the Bear for calls. 4-T for emails. The voicemail framework for voicemails. Same message, three systematic formats.

4. **Add follow-through.** Set timelines during conversations. Calendar every follow-through date. Never "just check in."

5. **Choose the channels last.** Once the message is strong, distribute it across the channels where your prospects are most reachable.

The process is: Sort → Message → Structure → Follow-Through → Channels. Most people build it backwards — starting with channels and ending with (or never getting to) sorting criteria. That's why they get inconsistent results.
