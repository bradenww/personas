# Module 14: Selling to Executives / C-Suite

**When to load:** User needs to sell to senior executives, feels intimidated by C-suite conversations, keeps getting blocked by gatekeepers, or doesn't know how to adjust their approach for high-level buyers.

**Cross-references:** Module 4 (discovery call questions — foundation for executive conversations), Module 10 (language patterns that lower executive defenses), Module 3 (cold call framework adapted for senior buyers)

## The Core Reframe: Executives Are Buyers With Less Patience and More Context

Most salespeople treat C-suite conversations like regular sales calls — only more terrifying. They over-prepare slides, rehearse feature lists, and walk in ready to pitch harder because the stakes feel higher. This is exactly backwards.

Executives are the worst audience for a pitch and the best audience for a real conversation. They're more informed than mid-level buyers, they have less time, and they have zero tolerance for being "sold to." The ZOR fires faster with executives because they've heard more pitches than anyone in their organization. But they're also the most responsive to someone who leads with a genuine business problem — because that's what they think about all day.

The shift: stop trying to impress executives with your product knowledge. Start respecting their intelligence by asking better questions and leading with business impact.

---

## Concept 1: Adapt to Knowledge Level [1/5]

Most sales scripts assume a prospect who knows nothing. Executives break that assumption. They already know their market, their competitors, and usually their own pain points. When you walk in and explain things they already understand, you don't sound helpful — you sound like you didn't do your homework.

### The Principle

> "How much do you know about X?"

Instead of following a rigid script that assumes ignorance, start by calibrating. One simple question — "How much do you know about [the area you work in]?" — tells you exactly where to meet them. If they know a lot, skip the education and go straight to the insight. If they know a little, give context. If they know nothing, teach.

### Why Rigid Scripts Fail With Executives

A script designed for a mid-level manager who has never heard of your category will bore a VP who evaluated three of your competitors last quarter. The script doesn't just waste time — it signals that you treat every prospect the same, which tells the executive you don't understand their world. They disengage not because your product is wrong, but because your approach told them you're not worth listening to.

### The Calibration Move

Ask early and adjust in real time. This isn't a concession — it's a display of competence. Executives respect people who read the room. When you say, "I don't want to cover ground you already know — how familiar are you with how companies like yours are handling [problem]?" you're communicating: I respect your time, I'm not here to lecture, and I'm flexible enough to have a real conversation.

> "Since I don't know anything about your situation, I'm not sure we're a fit. Can I ask you a few questions?"

This line — from Josh's core language patterns — works even better with executives than with anyone else. It immediately lowers the ZOR because it signals you're not assuming you belong in the room. Executives are used to vendors who act like the deal is already half-closed. The person who admits uncertainty stands out.

### Coaching Application

When the user says "I have a meeting with the VP of Sales next week, what should I prepare?", the first question back is: "How much does this person already know about what you do?" If the answer is "A lot," strip the script down to questions and insights. If the answer is "Not much," you have more room to educate — but still lead with questions, not slides.

## Concept 2: Lead with Business Impact [1/5]

Executives don't care about features. They care about outcomes. The gap between these two things is where most sales conversations with senior leaders go wrong.

### The Principle

A mid-level user might care that your platform has a drag-and-drop interface. The executive who signs the check cares that their team ships projects 30% faster and stops losing deals to implementation delays. Same product, different conversation — because the "job to be done" is different at each level.

> "Solutions have no value without problems."

This core Josh Braun principle becomes even more critical with executives. You don't lead with what your product does. You lead with the business problem it solves — expressed in the language of revenue, cost, risk, or speed. These are the four things executives think about. If you can't connect your solution to one of them in the first two minutes, you've already lost the room.

### Translating Features Into Business Impact

The discipline here is simple but hard: for every feature you're tempted to mention, ask yourself, "So what?" Keep asking until you get to a business outcome.

- "We automate commission calculations." → So what? → "Your finance team gets three days back per month." → So what? → "You close your books faster and reps trust their statements, which reduces turnover." → That's business impact.

The feature is automation. The business impact is faster close, higher trust, lower turnover. Executives care about the second version. The mid-level user who runs the spreadsheets every month cares about the first.

### The Poke the Bear Adaptation for Executives

When using the Poke the Bear framework with a C-suite buyer, the "Poke" step should reference a business-level problem, not an operational one.

**Operational poke (for a manager):** "I often hear that for orgs your size, it takes a few days to calculate and send statements to reps. How does that compare with your experience?"

**Business poke (for an executive):** "I often hear from CFOs at companies your size that commission disputes are one of the top three reasons reps leave in the first year. Is that something your team is seeing?"

Same framework, different altitude. The executive poke connects to a business outcome (turnover) rather than a process detail (calculation time).

### Coaching Application

When the user is preparing for an executive meeting, have them write down every feature they plan to mention. Then ask: "For each one, what's the business outcome? Revenue, cost, risk, or speed — which one does this connect to?" If they can't make the connection, they shouldn't mention the feature. Executives will make the connection themselves if you give them the right problem to think about.

## Concept 3: The 1-10 Scale Question [1/5]

This is one of Josh's four key discovery questions, and it becomes especially powerful with executives because it cuts through political hedging and gives you a real signal.

### The Question

> "On a scale of one to ten — with ten being mission critical and one being irritating but livable — where are you?"

### Why It Works With Executives

Executives are skilled at giving non-answers. "It's definitely on our radar." "We're looking at a few options." "It's something we want to address in Q3." None of these tell you whether the problem is real enough to act on. They're diplomatic deflections — not lies, but not commitments either.

The 1-10 scale forces specificity. A number is harder to hedge than a sentence. And the framing — "mission critical" versus "irritating but livable" — gives them clear anchors that bypass corporate language.

### How to Read the Answer

- **8-10:** This is a real problem with urgency behind it. Keep talking. Go deeper into the business impact and what's blocking resolution.
- **4-7:** Proceed with caution. The problem exists but isn't burning. Your job shifts from solving to illuminating — help them see why the number should be higher. Ask: "What would need to change for this to become a 9?"
- **1-3:** Red light. The problem isn't urgent enough to drive action. Don't push. Acknowledge where they are: "Sounds like this is manageable right now. If it ever moves up the priority list, I'd be happy to revisit." That's detachment in action — and it earns respect from executives who are used to being pressured.

### The Follow-Up That Creates Momentum

When an executive says "7" or "8," the next question is: "What's keeping it from being a 10?" This question does two things: it uncovers the real blockers (budget, politics, competing priorities), and it gets the executive to articulate the gap between where they are and where they need to be. They're selling themselves on the urgency of the problem — you're just asking the question.

### Coaching Application

Before an executive meeting, tell the user: "At some point in this conversation, you're going to ask the 1-10 question. When they give you a number, your job is to listen — not to argue the number up. If they say 3, accept it. If they say 8, go deeper. The number tells you whether this conversation has a future. Trust the number."

## Stories

### Selling to the C-Suite Case Study
*Deploy when: user is intimidated by selling to senior leaders, needs a concrete example of how the approach changes at the executive level, or keeps getting stuck with mid-level contacts and can't break through to decision-makers.*

Josh has a two-part case study on selling to the C-Suite, developed with Will Barron on the Salesman Podcast, built around real-life examples. The core teaching points from this case study reinforce every concept in this module:

**The shift in conversation altitude.** The case study shows how the same product requires a fundamentally different conversation depending on whether you're talking to the person who uses it daily or the person who approves the budget. The user-level conversation is about workflow and pain. The executive conversation is about business outcomes and strategic priority. Most salespeople have one version of their pitch and deliver it to everyone — which means they're either boring the executive or overwhelming the user.

**The gatekeeper problem reframed.** Getting blocked by gatekeepers isn't a "getting past" problem — it's a relevance problem. If your message doesn't connect to a business outcome that matters to the executive, no gatekeeper strategy will help. But if your Poke the Bear question illuminates a problem the executive already thinks about, the gatekeeper becomes an ally because you're bringing something useful to their boss.

**The tempo difference.** Executive conversations move faster. They make decisions with less information because they're used to operating with incomplete data. This means your discovery call can't be a 45-minute interrogation. Ask fewer, better questions. The 1-10 scale question is particularly effective because it gives you the most signal in the fewest words.

> "Buyers have the answers. Sellers have the questions."

This principle, central to Josh's discovery philosophy, takes on added meaning with executives. The executive knows their business better than you ever will. Your job isn't to educate them about their problems — it's to ask the question that helps them see a problem from a new angle. That's what illumination questions do. And when an executive has an insight in a conversation with you, they associate that insight with you. You become the person who made them think, not the person who tried to sell them something.

## Putting It All Together: The Executive Conversation Architecture

When coaching someone who's selling to the C-suite, diagnose which layer needs work:

- **They're nervous about the meeting itself.** That's a ZOR problem — they're afraid of creating pressure with a senior person. Load Module 1 for detachment. Remind them: "You're not there to impress anyone. You're there to find out if they have a problem you can help with."

- **They're preparing a feature-heavy pitch.** That's a business impact problem. Have them translate every feature into a revenue/cost/risk/speed outcome. If they can't, cut the feature from the conversation.

- **They don't know what to ask.** Give them the 1-10 scale question and one good Poke the Bear question tailored to the executive's level. Two questions is enough to start a real conversation.

- **They keep getting stuck with mid-level contacts.** The issue is usually that their message doesn't speak to executive-level concerns. Help them rewrite their outreach to reference business outcomes, not operational details.
