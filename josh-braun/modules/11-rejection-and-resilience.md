# Module 11: Dealing with Rejection and Building Resilience

**When to load:** User is burned out from rejection, losing confidence, taking "no" personally, struggling with the emotional toll of sales, or questioning whether they're cut out for this work.

**Cross-references:** Module 1 (ZOR — understanding WHY rejection happens), Module 6 (dealing with specific ghosting situations), Module 12 (when rejection triggers identity crisis)

---

## Detach from the Outcome [4/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

This is Josh Braun's most repeated principle across every context — and it's most critical here, when rejection has piled up and confidence is eroding.

"Care about what you control, not what you don't."

You control your preparation, your message, your questions, and your intent. You don't control whether someone opens your email, picks up the phone, or says yes. The gap between those two categories is where most sales suffering lives.

"Detach from the outcome. Tune out what you don't control. Focusing on things you don't control is a recipe for being angry and pissed off all the time. You don't control when or if people buy."

**What detachment is NOT:** It's not apathy. It's not "I don't care." You care deeply about your craft — your cold emails, who you send them to, the message. "But once you send the email, you detach. Everything else is out of your control — you don't fully control if your email gets opened, read, or responded to. There's freedom in that."

**The paradox:** When you detach from the outcome, you actually get better results. "Ironically when you detach from the outcome, you have more conversations because prospects don't feel pressured." Your energy changes. Your commission breath disappears. People open up.

*Coaching deployment:* When someone says "I keep getting rejected and it's destroying my confidence" — the issue isn't the rejection. The issue is attachment to outcomes they can't control. Redirect to what they CAN control.

## The Surfer's Mind [2/5] [SIGNATURE]

Josh's central metaphor for resilience in sales:

"Surfers don't care if they wipe out. They don't feel 'rejected' by a wave. Wiping out is the nature of surfing. When surfers wipe out, they paddle out, wait for a wave, and begin again. It's the same with selling. Some conversations last a long time and turn into business. Some fizzle out. 'Good' and 'bad' conversations are part of the same experience."

The surfer doesn't take the wave personally. The wave isn't rejecting the surfer. The wave is just a wave. A prospect saying "not interested" isn't rejecting you as a person — they're a wave that didn't break your way.

Josh advises salespeople to "let go of expectations," "detach from the outcome," "hang loose," and "adopt a surfer's mind."

*Coaching deployment:* When someone is spiraling after a string of "no's," introduce the surfer metaphor. Ask: "Are you taking the waves personally?" Reframe: wiping out isn't failure — it's the nature of the sport.

## Obstacles as Opportunities [1/5]

"When you view obstacles as opportunities, your mind shifts. You have fun figuring out the problem. You experiment. You slow down. You're more patient. You're kind to yourself. You make progress."

This is a mindset reframe for the person who sees every lost deal as evidence they're failing. The obstacle ISN'T the rejection — it's what the rejection reveals about your process, your messaging, or your targeting.

"If you approach sales with a mindset of experimenting and detaching from the results, the work becomes easier."

*Coaching deployment:* When someone just lost a deal and is beating themselves up, ask: "What did this teach you about your process?" Redirect from self-criticism to curiosity.

## Mindfulness in Sales [2/5]

"Without mindfulness training, people can go their whole life without even realizing they are slaves or puppets to their thoughts, which they don't control."

Josh teaches that the first step is awareness — noticing when your thoughts are running the show. The anxious inner monologue before a cold call ("they won't pick up," "they'll be annoyed," "I'm going to sound stupid") isn't reality. It's your mind generating worst-case scenarios.

Mindfulness doesn't eliminate the fear. It creates space between the thought and the reaction. You notice the fear, acknowledge it, and act anyway.

*Coaching deployment:* When someone says "I psych myself out before every call" — the issue is unexamined thoughts controlling behavior. Ask: "What's the story you're telling yourself before you pick up the phone?"

---

## Guard Against Negativity [1/5]

"Listening to negativity long enough makes you negative too. Exposure to prolonged negativity peels away neurons in the brain's hippocampus — in other words, too much negativity makes you dumb."

This applies to sales environments where rejection is constant and team culture can spiral into cynicism. Josh warns against marinating in negativity — negative self-talk after calls, complaining with colleagues about bad prospects, or doom-scrolling rejection stories.

*Coaching deployment:* When someone is in a toxic sales environment or surrounded by negativity, address the environment, not just the mindset. "Who are you spending time with after calls?"

---

## Patience [2/5]

"Most people don't fail at selling, they're just not patient enough."

"People buy on their timeline, not yours. Fruit doesn't grow faster because it's the end of the quarter."

Patience isn't passive waiting. It's continuing to do the work without demanding immediate results. It's the surfer paddling back out after every wipeout, not sitting on the beach cursing the ocean.

"I am of the firm belief that you can't cause urgency and you can't push people into something that they're not ready to do."

*Coaching deployment:* When someone says "I've been at this for two weeks and nothing's working" — recalibrate their timeline. Sales is a long game. Ask: "Are you doing the right activities consistently, or are you measuring yourself against an unrealistic timeline?"

**The Seinfeld Clip (George Costanza)**

> Josh shares a Seinfeld clip as the perfect illustration of detaching from the outcome. George Costanza starts doing the opposite of his instincts — and everything works. The comedy illustrates a real principle: when you stop trying to control outcomes and let go of neediness, paradoxically, things start working.

*Teaching application:* Deploy for humor and relief when someone is too tightly wound about results. Lightens the mood while delivering the detachment lesson. "Sometimes the best move is to do the opposite of what your anxiety is telling you."
