# Module 13: Leaving Voicemails That Get Callbacks

**When to load:** User hates leaving voicemails, voicemails never get returned, wants a framework, or is skipping voicemail entirely because it feels pointless.

**Cross-references:** Module 3 (cold call framework — voicemail complements calls), Module 7 (standing out / differentiation), Module 1 (detachment mindset for when voicemails don't get returned)

---

## The Core Reframe: Voicemails Aren't About Callbacks

Most people evaluate voicemails by a single metric: did the prospect call back? When the answer is almost always no, they conclude voicemails are a waste of time and stop leaving them entirely. This is the wrong frame.

Josh treats voicemails as a familiarity tool, not a conversion tool. A voicemail that never gets returned still does work — it plants your name, your company, and a seed of curiosity in the prospect's mind. The next time they see your email or pick up your call, you're not a complete stranger. The voicemail already introduced you.

The mistake is expecting voicemail to do the job of a conversation. It can't. But it can do the job of creating recognition — and recognition lowers the Zone of Resistance on every subsequent touch.

---

## Concept 1: The Voicemail Framework [1/5]

Josh's voicemail framework is built on three principles: short, specific, and curiosity-driven. No pitch. No feature dump. No "I'd love to schedule 15 minutes." Just enough to make the prospect wonder what this is about.

### The Structure

A voicemail should be one or two sentences that reference something specific to the prospect, then a callback number. That's it.

**Example:** "Hi Josh, this is Kim with Descript. I'm calling about the Inside Selling podcast. My number is 555-555-5555. Please call me back when you can."

### Why This Works

Notice what's missing: no pitch, no value proposition, no "we help companies like yours do X." The voicemail creates an open loop. The prospect hears their own podcast name and thinks, "What about my podcast?" That curiosity is the only thing a voicemail needs to generate.

### The "Crispy" Principle

"When you're specific or 'crispy,' you're more credible." A voicemail that says "I'm calling about your business" is a white circle — generic, ignorable, instantly deleted. A voicemail that says "I'm calling about the Inside Selling podcast" is a red X — specific enough to demand attention.

Specificity signals research. Research signals respect. Respect lowers defenses. The prospect thinks, "This person actually looked into what I do," which is a fundamentally different reaction than, "Great, another vendor who bought a list."

### Coaching Application

When the user asks "What should I say in a voicemail?", resist the urge to help them craft a pitch. Instead, ask: "What's something specific you noticed about this prospect that you could reference in one sentence?" The voicemail writes itself from there. If they can't answer that question, the problem isn't their voicemail — it's their research.

## Concept 2: Voicemail for Familiarity, Not Callbacks [1/5]

This concept changes how the user measures voicemail success — which changes whether they bother leaving them at all.

### The Principle

"Think of your voicemail as another way to expose your prospect to your message. Use voicemail to pique curiosity and familiarity."

A voicemail is a touchpoint, not a closing mechanism. Its job is to make the prospect slightly more familiar with your name and slightly more curious about what you do. That's a success even if they never call back.

### Detach from the Callback

"Don't expect prospects to call you back. Some will. Some won't."

This is the surfer's mind applied to voicemail. You don't control whether someone returns your call. You control the quality and specificity of your message. Leave a good one, then let it go. The voicemail is doing invisible work — building familiarity — whether or not the phone rings.

### The Multi-Touch Effect

Voicemail works best as part of a sequence, not in isolation. A voicemail followed by an email followed by a LinkedIn touch creates a cumulative effect. Each touch lowers the ZOR a little more. By the time you connect live, the prospect has encountered your name three or four times. You're no longer a cold caller — you're a familiar name.

### Coaching Application

When the user says "I stopped leaving voicemails because nobody calls back," reframe the metric: "You're measuring the wrong thing. A voicemail that doesn't get returned but makes your next email feel familiar is a voicemail that worked. The question isn't 'Did they call back?' It's 'Will they recognize my name the next time I reach out?'"

## Concept 3: Message Over Medium [1/5]

This concept prevents the user from falling into the trap of chasing channels instead of improving their message.

### The Principle

"Any medium can be effective or not. It's not the medium; it's the message."

Voicemail, video, email, DMs, direct mail — none of these channels are inherently good or bad. A terrible email and a terrible voicemail fail for the same reason: the message triggers the ZOR. A great email and a great voicemail succeed for the same reason: the message is specific, curious, and pressure-free.

### The Channel-Hopping Trap

Salespeople who struggle often blame the medium. "Email doesn't work anymore." "Nobody listens to voicemails." "LinkedIn is dead." Then they jump to the next trendy channel and get the same bad results — because they brought the same bad message with them.

The problem is never which channel you're using. The problem is what you're saying when you get there. A generic pitch delivered via video is still a generic pitch. An illumination question delivered via voicemail is still an illumination question.

### Coaching Application

When the user asks "Should I even bother with voicemail?" or "Is [channel] dead?", redirect to the message: "The channel doesn't matter. The message does. If your message would be boring in an email, it'll be boring in a voicemail too. Let's fix the message first, then figure out which channels to put it in."

## Putting It All Together: The Voicemail Workflow

When coaching someone on voicemails, layer the concepts in this order:

1. **Reframe the goal** — You're not trying to get a callback. You're trying to build familiarity and curiosity. If they call back, great. If not, the voicemail still did its job.

2. **Build the message** — Reference something specific about the prospect. One sentence of context, callback number, done. No pitch. No features. No "I'd love to chat about."

3. **Make it crispy** — Specificity is credibility. The more specific the reference, the more the prospect feels seen rather than targeted.

4. **Detach** — Leave the voicemail and move on. Don't check if they called back fourteen times. The voicemail is one touch in a multi-touch sequence.

5. **Remember: message over medium** — If voicemails aren't working, the issue is probably the message, not the medium. Apply the same fix you'd apply to a cold email that isn't getting responses: find the pressure and remove it.
