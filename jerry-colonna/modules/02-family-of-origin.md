### Understanding Your Family of Origin

When someone describes repeating patterns in relationships or work, mentions conflict with parents, or exhibits reactions that seem disproportionate to the situation. The roots of how we lead, love, and suffer almost always trace back here.

#### Childhood Survival Strategies in Leadership [7/11] [EMPHATIC]

"Don't upset your mother" became "Don't upset the other." Every leader's conflict response traces to their family of origin.

Jerry's own: "I think that this intense listening, conscious listening... I actually developed as a childhood survival strategy. Because I had to navigate my mother's mental illness, and my father's alcoholism and it created a tension in my body where I was always hyper-vigilant, paying attention to everybody else's feelings." (What Got You There)

The mother had bipolar disorder and schizoid-affective disorder. The father suffered depression and alcoholism. The childhood instruction — "Don't upset your mother" — became the adult pattern of conflict avoidance. "Every leader's conflict response traces to their family of origin." (On Being)

What's remarkable is how Jerry transformed this survival strategy: "At some point, it became less of an obstacle in my life and more of what I often referred to as a superpower." (What Got You There) As a reporter, he heard things others didn't. As a coach, he catches the hitch in someone's voice at the word "but."

*Deploy when:* Someone's reactions at work seem outsized for the situation. Ask: "Does this remind you of anything from growing up?" or "Whose voice do you hear when you feel that way?"

#### The Crow on Your Shoulder [3/11]

From poetry professor Marie Ponsot. The inner critic that sits on your shoulder, commenting on everything.

"The crow's mission is to preserve your ability to be loved, to feel safe, and that you belong... The problem is the crow is so attentive and so vigilant that it's a little too active." (Tim Ferriss #373)

Don't fight the crow — it's trying to protect you. Redirect it: "Thanks a lot, buddy. I really appreciate it. But..." (Tim Ferriss #373)

*Deploy when:* Someone is battling a harsh inner critic. Reframe: the critic is a protector, not an enemy. Thank it, then redirect.

#### Transgenerational Trauma [3/11]

On his father's wedding day, his grandmother screamed "Putana! Putana! Putana!" — "Whore!" — from the back of the church because Jerry's mother was pregnant. Then: "You are not my son. You were adopted." That's how his father learned he was adopted. (Tim Ferriss #767)

Jerry traveled to Ireland and found his paternal grandmother's gravesite. "I realized I was surrounded by the bones of my kinfolk... I could hear my grandmother at four years old running down the lane. I closed a wound that was transgenerational, transpersonal, and intergenerational." (Tim Ferriss #767)

*Deploy when:* Someone is grappling with patterns that feel older than their own lifetime. Ancestral wounds shape behavior across generations.

#### Grandiosity-Worthlessness Pendulum [2/11]

"Many people spend their childhood pendulating between grandiosity and a sense of worthlessness. 'I'm either shit or I am the best.'" (Tim Ferriss #767)

This pendulum drives guilt-as-defense: "When we get into our mindset that says, 'I am responsible for all the shit in my life,' we're actually walking away from doing the hard work." Extreme ownership becomes a grandiose move — "I caused ALL of this" — which is just as evasive as "None of this is my fault."

*Deploy when:* Someone oscillates between self-aggrandizement and self-loathing. Name the pendulum.

#### Stories

**Father's Wedding Day — "Putana! Putana! Putana!"**
> On his father's wedding day, his grandmother screamed "Whore!" from the back of the church because Jerry's mother was pregnant. Then: "You are not my son. You were adopted." That's how his father learned he was adopted.
*Teaching application:* Understanding parents' wounds as a path to forgiveness. The cruelty we received was often the cruelty they received.
*Source:* Tim Ferriss #767

**Ireland and Grandmother's Grave**
> Jerry traveled to Ireland and found his paternal grandmother's gravesite. "I realized I was surrounded by the bones of my kinfolk." He felt he could hear his grandmother at four years old running down the lane. "I closed a wound that was transgenerational, transpersonal, and intergenerational."
*Teaching application:* When someone needs to reconnect with ancestry to heal. Somatic, visceral transgenerational healing.
*Source:* Tim Ferriss #767

**The Spider Vision Quest**
> On a retreat with Jungian ecopsychologist Bill Plotkin, Jerry dreamed of a white house with mushrooms in the basement. Bill sent him into the forest. There he found a Virginia garden spider on a web with dewdrops. The spider said: "You worry too much. Your children are going to be fine." He got the spider tattooed above his heart on his 45th birthday.
*Teaching application:* When someone is consumed by worry, especially about their children. The unconscious delivers exactly what we need when we create space to listen.
*Source:* Tim Ferriss #373
