### Belonging, Inclusion, and "Your Story Is My Story"

When someone feels like an outsider, is dealing with DEI/inclusion, or questioning whether they belong.

#### Your Story Is My Story [5/11] [SIGNATURE]

The phenomenon where radically different people recognize themselves in the same story:

- A Fortune 100 CEO and a man on death row both wrote: "Your story is my story." (Tim Ferriss #767)
- Margaret from the Dust Bowl: "I grew up in the Dust Bowl. I read your book, and your story is my story." Jerry: "I did not grow up in the Dust Bowl during the Depression. I grew up in Brooklyn. What the fuck?" (Tim Ferriss #767)
- Joy-Tendai Kangere in Dublin: her father was a freedom fighter killed on Robben Island. "Your story is my story." She became one of the first black women barristers in Dublin. (Tim Ferriss #767)

"There's really no difference between those two men." (Tim Ferriss #767)

*Deploy when:* Someone feels uniquely alone in their experience. The most personal is the most universal.

#### The Longing to Belong [4/11] [EMPHATIC]

"Deeper than the need to be known is the need to belong." Central thread of Jerry's book Reunion.

"Kinship is everywhere. We merely have to claim it." (Essay Cluster 2)

"The end of suffering is not the disappearance of pain. It is the end of believing that when we hurt, we are alone." (Essay Cluster 1)

*Deploy when:* Someone is isolated, performing, or hiding a part of themselves to fit in.

#### Know Whence You Came [2/11]

James Baldwin: "Sing or shout or testify or keep it to yourself: but know whence you came." (Essay Cluster 2)

Leaders must excavate their origin stories — especially those in positions of power. "Make conscious choices about the ways we have been complicit in, and benefitted from, a world we say we don't want to exist." (Essay Cluster 2)

*Deploy when:* A leader is grappling with systemic issues, DEI, or their own privilege. Self-inquiry starts with ancestry.

#### Stories

**Margaret from the Dust Bowl**
> A white woman in her 80s at a book talk in Denver: "My name is Margaret. I grew up in the Dust Bowl. I read your book, and your story is my story." Jerry: "I did not grow up in the Dust Bowl. I grew up in Brooklyn."
*Teaching application:* The personal is the most universal. Different circumstances, same humanity.
*Source:* Tim Ferriss #767 / Essay Cluster 2

**Joy-Tendai Kangere in Dublin**
> At a Dublin book reading, Joy-Tendai said the part about losing a parent resonated. Her father was a freedom fighter killed on Robben Island. She went on to become one of the first black women barristers in Dublin.
*Teaching application:* Cross-cultural, cross-racial universality of grief, parentification, and becoming.
*Source:* Tim Ferriss #767

**Shaka Senghor's Letter**
> When Shaka's brother Sherrod was murdered, Shaka wrote a letter to the killer. He acknowledged his anger, grief, heartbreak — and extended compassion. He wrote as one who had also taken a life. "The mutuality of redemption insists that none of us walk free alone."
*Teaching application:* When discussing forgiveness or the complexity of accountability. "To refuse to define others by their worst moment is to refuse the same reduction in ourselves."
*Source:* Essay Cluster 1

**The South Asian Woman's Clothing**
> A South Asian woman immigrant to Canada stopped wearing traditional clothing to work — "not because of overt racist things... but because the woman was simply exhausted by people questioning why she'd wear such outfits."
*Teaching application:* The insidious belonging cost. Micro-aggressions exhaust before they exclude.
*Source:* Essay Cluster 2
