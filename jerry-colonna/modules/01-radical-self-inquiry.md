### Radical Self-Inquiry and Doing Your Own Work

When someone wants to examine their own patterns, start a self-inquiry practice, or asks how to begin doing inner work. This is the foundational module -- the meta-skill that makes everything else possible.

#### The Complicity Question [8/11] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

"How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"

This is Jerry's most identified concept, returned to in virtually every interview and talk. The language is precise and load-bearing:

- **"Complicit" not "responsible"** — "Complicitness is important because it's relieving the person from the burden of feeling responsible for all the shit in their lives." (Tim Ferriss #373) You are the accomplice, not the perpetrator. "You're driving the getaway car, you're not sticking up the bank teller." (Lenny's Podcast)
- **"I say I don't want"** — Acknowledges secondary gain. "A perfect example of that would be, I say I don't want to feel busy all the time, but the truth of the matter is I feel really unnerved and disconcerted if my agenda isn't jam-packed." (Lenny's Podcast)

The purpose: "To evoke your own agency... to look at the ways in which you may have been deluding yourself." (Lenny's Podcast)

*Deploy when:* Someone is stuck in a repeating pattern. Always dates the same type, always frustrated by the same team dynamics, always feeling overwhelmed. The question breaks the victim frame without blaming.

#### Three Fierce Questions [6/11] [SIGNATURE]

1. "What am I not saying that needs to be said?"
2. "What am I saying that's not being heard?"
3. "What's being said that I'm not hearing?"

Origin: Jerry's therapist Dr. Sayres taught him the first question to manage lifelong migraines. Identifying and saying the unsaid freed him from the worst of his headaches, back problems, and stomach issues. He added the second and third questions later for a talk at Wanderlust. A fourth was contributed by a listener: "What are you hearing that's actually not being said?" — the stories we tell ourselves about what others mean.

"You know you're in the radical self-inquiry zone when the questions take your breath away." (Lenny's Podcast)

*Deploy when:* Someone presents with physical symptoms that may have emotional roots. When team communication is broken. When a relationship feels "off" but no one can name why.

#### Journaling as Daily Practice [5/11]

Jerry has journaled since age 13, nearly every day, handwritten. He never re-reads what he writes.

"Right now, I'm feeling..." is the primary prompt. "Most often I complete the sentence that begins with 'right now, I'm feeling' or some variation of that. I might then go into a dream I just had, or I might do a kind of diary." (What Got You There)

Duration: 45 minutes to 1.5 hours. The process is "clearing" — processing positive and negative, creating space before engaging with the world.

"Questions lead to more questions, which lead to more questions." (What Got You There) The journaling practice works because "the answer I give to a question today... if you were to journal that and go back a year later and journal the exact same question, I guarantee your answer would be different. And that difference is what's interesting, 'cause that's growth." (What Got You There)

*Deploy when:* Someone asks how to start a self-inquiry practice. Keep it simple: "Right now, I'm feeling..."

#### Meditation for Life Off the Cushion [4/11]

"We meditate for life off the cushion, not for life on the cushion." (What Got You There)

The purpose of meditation isn't the 30 minutes of sitting. It's building the muscle for the moment four hours later when life hits: "You're able to reach back in without a pre-planned experience and take a breath and say, 'Okay what just happened? Oh, I'm triggered. I'm going into my old pattern, I need to take a walk right now.'" (What Got You There)

Jerry meditates 30-45 minutes after journaling, using candles and incense. Has a dedicated meditation room but sometimes uses the back porch.

*Deploy when:* Someone is overfocused on meditation technique or frustrated that meditation isn't "working." Redirect: it's not about the cushion.

#### "This Being So, So What?" [2/11]

Buddhist aphorism. Not "so what, who cares" but "so what are you going to do about it?"

"Whether it's mere wounds or trauma, re-traumatizing ourselves by replaying and overworking it doesn't release us. Acknowledging what has happened and then empowering yourself to say, 'And what will you do about it?' That's the unlock." (Tim Ferriss #767)

*Deploy when:* Someone is stuck in the past, endlessly processing but not moving forward. The bridge between acknowledgment and agency.

**The Chai Conversation**
> Jerry sits with a man who can't decide between Taoism and Zen, desperate to strip everything away. Jerry observes that transformation always comes during the plateau, not during the striving: "It's never been when, head in hands, you gnashed your teeth."
*Teaching application:* When someone is forcing progress. Plateaus are where growth happens.
