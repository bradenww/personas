# Module 1: Starting a Company / Taking the Leap

This module covers the moment someone is considering leaving their job to start a business, paralyzed by fear, trapped in someone else's expectations, or asking "should I quit?" Deploy it whenever the user's core blocker is courage -- not tactics, not information, but the willingness to act.

---

#### "Guarantee Bad, Chance at Good" [5/14] [SIGNATURE]

The origin story framework Hormozi returns to in every long-form interview. The core logic: if staying on your current path guarantees an outcome you don't want (just delayed), then any path with a non-zero chance of what you want is rationally superior.

> "Guarantee bad, chance at good. And I was like, well, I might as well take a chance."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 89*

The mechanism is moving from amygdala (vague, emotional fear) to prefrontal cortex (specific, logical analysis) by spelling out the worst case in granular detail:

> "Let's play it out. Let's see what actually would happen... Is it going to change what I eat? Is it going to change where I sleep?... My worst case scenario is I have shelter and food and I'm still breathing air. So that's the downside. And my upside is everything I've wanted."
> *Source: mw952*

Hormozi's own worst-case assessment when leaving consulting: "I'll have a story, some self-inflicted shame, and I can always get my job back" (diary-ceo).

Counterintuitively, he advocates knowing your Plan B in excruciating detail -- contradicting Arnold Schwarzenegger's "don't have a plan B":

> "I think you should look at your plan B in excruciating detail."
> *Source: impact, ~01:18:27*

Knowing the Plan B was survivable is what enabled him to pursue Plan A with full commitment.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Any quit-or-stay decision where the status quo leads to a known bad outcome on a delayed timeline. Force the user to spell out both paths to their logical conclusions in specific, concrete terms. The vague fear dissolves when it's made specific.

#### Fear Is a Mile Wide and an Inch Deep [6/14]

The most frequently occurring concept in this module. Fear looks like an ocean from the outside. Take the first step and you discover there's ground underneath.

> "Fear is a mile wide and an inch deep."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 75 (attributed to Leila Hormozi)*

The shame people fear is self-inflicted and temporary:

> "How long after you do something embarrassing is it funny? If it is going to become funny eventually, it might as well become funny now."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

The people whose judgment you fear are not thinking about you:

> "I would have these shame fantasies of what they were saying about me while I was sleeping on the gym floor... They weren't thinking about me to begin with, and nor do they really care, and they're going to all die eventually."
> *Source: impact, ~01:21:14*

At scale, judgment is statistically irrelevant:

> "You have a 100% chance of bothering somebody on Earth by existing, and so if every action you take has somebody who will hate it, then it kind of cancels everything out and you might as well just do whatever you're going to do anyways."
> *Source: impact, ~00:34:38*

From the books -- fear of judgment is named as the single most expensive emotion:

> "Don't be afraid of what other people think. If someone won't speak at your funeral, you shouldn't care about their opinion while you're alive. Honor the few who believe in you by having courage."
> *Source: leads*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is catastrophizing about a decision they haven't played out. When someone is paralyzed by what acquaintances, coworkers, or extended family will think. When someone hasn't actually articulated what the worst case is -- they've just felt it.

#### Courage as the Foundational Trait [5/14]

Not a binary ("are you courageous or not?") but a continuous spectrum that can be measured and improved. Courage is the prerequisite that makes all tactical knowledge usable.

> "Courage is the quintessential trait that leads to everything else. You have to first have courage."
> *Source: impact, ~00:39:39*

> "Courage is not acting without fear, but despite fear."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 79*

> "It's not are you courageous or not. It's how courageous are you."
> *Source: impact, ~00:39:39*

Hormozi places himself on the courage spectrum: above average but explicitly below Elon Musk, who he uses as the apex model for concentrated bets with maximum personal exposure (impact). The spectrum framing matters because it removes the all-or-nothing pressure -- you don't need to be fearless, you need to be slightly more courageous than you were yesterday.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone frames courage as a personality trait they either have or don't. Reframe it as a behavior on a spectrum, then ask: "What would one notch more courageous look like for you right now?"

---

#### "I'd Rather Be a Failure Than a Coward" [4/14]

A reframe of the identity threat. Most people avoid action because they fear being seen as a failure. Hormozi fears a different label more.

> "I'd rather be a failure than a coward."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 79*

> "It only means you're a coward if you allow that fear to change your behavior in an aversive way, as in not towards what you want."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

The technique: use the more threatening label to override the lesser one. If "coward" stings more than "failure," then inaction becomes more painful than action. This is not motivational -- it's an identity hack. Hormozi doesn't resolve the tension between fear and action; he uses the worse label to override the lesser one (diary-ceo, impact, mw670, mw830).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone knows what to do but won't act. When someone is rationalizing inaction with "prudence" or "waiting for the right time." Ask: "Which label would bother you more -- that you tried and failed, or that you knew what to do and didn't do it?"

#### "His Dream Must Die in Order for Mine to Live" [4/14] [EMPHATIC]

Hormozi calls this the single hardest thing he ever did. When someone else's expectations for your life conflict with your own direction, one vision must be surrendered. There is no compromise.

> "His dream has to die in order for mine to live."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 63*

His father -- an Iranian-born tenured mechanical engineering professor -- wanted the consulting/MBA/private equity path. Having no siblings and essentially no mother in his emotional landscape, his father's approval was "literally everything" (mw670, ~00:07:50). The apex of living out his father's dream coincided with Hormozi's deepest depression:

> "Every day, I was like, I hope I don't wake up."
> *Source: mw670, ~00:12:55*

The realization that broke the pattern:

> "I was winning at a game that I didn't design, and so I'm actually by default the loser."
> *Source: impact*

The relationship with his father "struggled for years" after he left (diary-ceo). This is not a tidy reconciliation story -- the cost was real and prolonged.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is living someone else's dream -- parent's, spouse's, society's -- and can't articulate why they're miserable on their current path. The key insight: the relationship may suffer, possibly permanently. But the alternative is already killing them.

#### The Three Ps for Business Ideas [3/14]

The framework for choosing what business to start, applied at the zero-to-one stage.

> "Business ideas typically come from one of three Ps: Pain, Past Profession, or Passion."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

- **Pain:** A problem you personally experience deeply. Hormozi's example: nasal breathing problems for 20+ years, trying every product from the US, Europe, Amazon, and foreign countries. Two failed surgeries (diary-ceo).
- **Past Profession:** The skill or job you just left -- already economically validated. You know the industry, the customers, the pain points.
- **Passion:** Genuine obsession with a domain (weakest of the three unless combined with one of the others).

The ideal case is where two or all three overlap. But Pain and Past Profession are stronger starting points than Passion alone because they come pre-loaded with market knowledge and personal urgency (diary-ceo, impact, mfm732).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone at zero is paralyzed by "too many ideas" or "I don't know what to start." Run them through the Three Ps to narrow from infinite possibilities to a shortlist grounded in their actual experience and pain.

#### The J-Curve / Trade Good for Great [3/14]

Big decisions have a period of negative returns immediately after, which makes you think you chose wrong. This is the natural shape of transition, not signal.

> "Sometimes you have to trade good for great and there's always going to be a J curve. There's going to be a dip where instead of being immediately rewarded for the decision you're immediately punished."
> *Source: impact, ~01:11:03*

> "Humans learn from immediate feedback, which is why the J-curve is so psychologically devastating and why most people reverse course prematurely."
> *Source: impact*

When you leave a stable job for a startup, the first weeks or months will feel worse than what you left. Your income drops, your status drops, your comfort drops. The amygdala screams "you chose wrong." But the J-curve is the expected shape -- not a signal of a bad decision. Most people reverse course during the dip, which means they pay the cost of the transition without ever reaching the payoff.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone just made the leap and is in the dip -- questioning everything, feeling punished. Normalize the J-curve. The pain is the toll, not the verdict.

### Stories

**Quitting Consulting / The Midnight Escape** [5/14] [SIGNATURE]
> Hormozi graduated from Vanderbilt in three years, scored above Harvard's median on the GMAT, and landed at a prestigious management consulting firm working on a Space and Cyber project with four-star generals. He owned a paid-for condo overlooking the city. His father was at peak pride. And Hormozi was at his lowest: "Every day, I was like, I hope I don't wake up" (mw670, ~00:12:55).
>
> He didn't confront his father or make a grand announcement. He fled: "I basically left in the dark of night. I was basically a stowaway to my own fear" (impact). He drove across the country, called his father from somewhere in Texas, took a minimum wage personal trainer job cleaning gym floors. The relationship with his father "struggled for years" (diary-ceo).
>
> His internal calculus: "Guarantee bad, chance at good. And I was like, well, I might as well take a chance" (diary-ceo). And the fear that outweighed failure: "If I failed I would have to go back and tell my dad he was right and that felt like worse than death for me" (impact, ~01:21:14).

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is paralyzed by family or social expectations. The key insight: "I was winning at a game that I didn't design, and so I'm actually by default the loser." Also illustrates that courage often looks messy -- fleeing in the dark, not confronting head-on -- and that the cost (years of strained relationship) is real, not a fairy tale.
*Sources:* diary-ceo, impact, mw670, mw830

**Sleeping on the Gym Floor** [5/14] [SIGNATURE]
> After leaving consulting, Hormozi opened a gym beneath a parking garage in Huntington Beach, CA. He had $5,000 left -- exactly one month's rent. Equipment was sandbags wrapped in duct tape. He sold 29 members into a gym with no real equipment in two weeks, "all from pity, not charisma" (mw670). He slept on the gym floor for approximately six months, sleeping about four hours a night. First session at 4:30 AM, last around 7:30 PM. No shower -- he used the YMCA. Cars overhead sounded like gunshots. Kids partied above the garage at night while he tried to sleep. He didn't know he could hire employees until a member (an entrepreneur) confronted him about being short with the class (impact, mw670). First month revenue: $4,972, exactly matching rent (impact).
>
> During that time: "I would have these shame fantasies of what they were saying about me while I was sleeping on the gym floor" (impact, ~01:21:14). His Vanderbilt peers, his consulting colleagues. "They weren't thinking about me to begin with."

*Teaching application:* The ur-origin story. Deploy when someone romanticizes entrepreneurship or when they're in a hard season and need to know the actual texture of early-stage grinding. Credentials don't build businesses -- grinding through failure does. The gap between failure and success was volume, not strategy.
*Sources:* offers, leads, impact, money-models, mw670

**Leila's "I'd Sleep with You Under a Bridge"** [2/14] [SIGNATURE]
> Nine months into dating Leila, Alex had lost everything. He'd sold his five gyms and put the money into a new company; his partner took the money. A payment processor held $100K in funds, then refused to release it. He had $23,000 left and owed a salesman $22,000 in commissions. After paying, he had $1,000 to his name. This was Christmas Eve. He was meeting Leila's family for the first time.
>
> Lying in Leila's parents' spare room, he told her:
> "I'm a sinking ship. If you want to leave me, I 100% respect that." (mw490, 00:55:29)
>
> "She grabbed my chin to tilt my head up to meet her eyes and said, 'I'd sleep with you under a bridge if it came to that.'" (mw490)
>
> Leila's internal calculus: "Worst case scenario is I end up at my dad's house... I'm 23, who cares? I've gained a ton of experience and a ton of knowledge." (mw490, 00:59:49) And her reframe during the hardest moments: "Think about how epic this story is going to be. Like when we make it." (mw490, 01:02:36)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when discussing loyalty under adversity as the most reliable signal of a true partner -- in business or in life. Also demonstrates the power of reframing current struggles as future narrative. Leila's worst-case analysis is itself a masterclass in the "Guarantee Bad, Chance at Good" framework applied to a relationship.
*Sources:* mw490, mw670
