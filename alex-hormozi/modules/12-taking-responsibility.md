# Module 12: Taking Responsibility / Overcoming a Victim Mindset

This module covers the moment someone is externalizing their situation -- blaming the market, their partner, their employees, the economy, bad luck, or any other force outside themselves. It also covers the subtler version: feeling powerless and unable to see what is actually in their control. Deploy when the user is stuck in a narrative where things are happening TO them rather than BECAUSE of them.

---

#### "Power Follows Blame" [9/14] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

Hormozi's most universal principle, appearing in virtually every source. The logic is simple and non-negotiable: blame and power are coupled. Whoever you blame is the person you give control over your life. The only allocation that gives you 100% power to change your situation is 100% responsibility.

> "Wherever you point the finger of blame, power follows. And so whoever I blame for the life I have is the person who I give all the power over my existence, over my circumstances."
> *Source: mw830, 00:44:34*

> "Power follows blame. And so if you blame the government who you think is supposed to save you, or you blame politicians who you think is supposed to save you, or blame your company that you work for who you think is supposed to save you, or you blame the market, it doesn't really matter. Or your parents, whoever you blame is the person you ultimately give power to."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

> "Where we place the finger of blame is also where power flows. If you blame your parents for your life, your parents have power over your life."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

The critical extension -- not just turning blame inward, but treating the entire external world as fixed:

> "The best way to move through the world is to see your entire surroundings as immutable and you as mutable. You're the only mutable thing in the entire world."
> *Source: mw830, 00:44:34*

This is not about fairness. It is not about whether external forces actually contributed to your situation. They probably did. The question is purely strategic: does blaming them give you any power to change? If not, the blame is a luxury you cannot afford.

Hormozi applies this recursively -- to prospects externalizing in sales conversations ("my husband won't let me"), to entrepreneurs blaming the market, to employees citing interest rates, to his own team during COVID. The principle is the same at every level: the finger of blame is a compass pointing at where you've surrendered control.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone says "the market is bad," "my employees won't do what I say," "my partner doesn't support me," or any variant of "this external thing is preventing my success." Ask: "Does blaming that thing give you any power to fix it?" The answer is always no. Then redirect: "What CAN you control?"

#### "If We Cannot Control It, We Do Not Talk About It" [4/14]

The operational implementation of "power follows blame" inside Hormozi's companies. Not a suggestion -- a strict conversational rule.

> "If we cannot control it we do not talk about it. Period. Only talk about the things that we can control."
> *Source: impact, ~01:52:59*

The context makes this visceral. This rule was forged during COVID-19, when 100% of Hormozi's gym-owner customers were legally banned from operating overnight. Revenue dropped from $37M to $31M. Every team member had legitimate reasons to panic. Hormozi's response was to ban the discussion of anything uncontrollable:

> "Hey interest rates are higher, it's harder for us to do sales -- can we control that? No. Why are you talking about it?"
> *Source: impact*

The rule is not about denying reality. Interest rates ARE higher. The market IS harder. COVID DID shut down gyms. Acknowledging those facts changes nothing. What changes things is the list of controllable variables -- and every minute spent discussing the uncontrollable is a minute not spent on those.

In diary-ceo, the principle connects to his broader philosophy of absolute responsibility: "Claim 100% responsibility because it's the only allocation that gives you 100% of the power to change." In mw952, it manifests as cosmic irrelevance -- the deliberate lowering of stakes that keeps you focused on what you can do rather than paralyzed by what you cannot.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is spiraling about economic conditions, competition, government policy, algorithm changes, or any external force. Interrupt the spiral with: "Can you control that?" If no: "Then why are we talking about it? What CAN you control right now?" This is especially powerful for teams -- install it as a conversational rule and watch how much faster meetings become.

#### The Iron Is the Iron [4/14]

The weightlifting metaphor that strips identity and narrative from the equation entirely. Results respond to inputs, not to your story about yourself.

> "It was kind of like in the weightlifting world, that the iron is the iron. Whether you're black, you're white, you're a woman. 500 pounds is 500 pounds."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

This is Hormozi at his most egalitarian and most ruthless simultaneously. The barbell does not ask about your childhood. The market does not check your self-esteem. Revenue does not adjust for how hard your year has been. The inputs produce the outputs regardless of your narrative.

The flip side -- and this is where the principle becomes genuinely liberating rather than harsh:

> "You'll get business, and you can also get business and make money even if you don't deserve it, which, for me, was actually a really comforting point."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

> "I can actually be a shitty person and horrible and suck at everything, but if I still work out and I still eat this way, I can still look this way. Even though I don't deserve it."
> *Source: mw670, ~01:40:43*

Hormozi calls this "the cheat code for people who hate themselves" (mw670). The universe does not check your self-esteem before delivering results -- it checks your behaviors. You do not need to believe you are worthy before taking action. You do not need to resolve your trauma, fix your self-image, or achieve inner peace. You just need to do the things that produce results, and the results will come regardless of how you feel about yourself.

This cuts both ways against the victim mindset: you cannot use your identity or circumstances as an excuse for poor results, AND you cannot use your self-doubt as an excuse for inaction. The iron is the iron.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone uses their background, identity, or self-image as a reason they cannot succeed. When someone says "I don't deserve success" or "people like me don't make it." Also when someone with low self-worth is using imposter syndrome as permission not to act. The reframe: "The market doesn't care who you are. It cares what you do. That's the best news you'll hear today."

#### "Should" Is the Root of All Pain [4/14]

The gap between what you think should happen and what is actually happening is the precise measurement of your pain. Every "should" is an unmet demand on the universe.

> "Should is the root of all pain... all the things that we think should happen but aren't is basically the measurement of our pain."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

The prescription is to eradicate "should" from internal dialogue entirely -- for yourself and projected onto others. Replace with two alternatives: "is" (what is actually happening right now) and conditional if-then statements ("if you want X, then Y increases the likelihood"). Unconditional shoulds are demands on the universe that create permanent gaps between expectation and reality.

In mw952, the "should" problem manifests as the When-Then Fallacy:

> "I should go to the gym once I have energy. I should start saving money once I'm rich. That's when I'll do it. It just conflates sequence."
> *Source: mw952*

The gym gives you energy. Saving money makes you rich. The prerequisite IS the outcome, not something that precedes it. "Should" inverts the actual causal sequence.

Tony Robbins reinforces this from a different angle:

> "Pain is part of life. Suffering is an option. You've heard that phrase, but it's a fact. It's not just a phrase."
> *Source: tony-robbins, [29:06]*

Tony distinguishes between the unavoidable friction of doing hard things (pain) and the self-inflicted amplification of that friction through language and identity (suffering). "Should" is the mechanism that converts pain into suffering -- it takes an already difficult reality and adds a layer of indignation that it shouldn't be this way.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is tortured by how their life "should" look versus how it is. When someone compares themselves to peers and says "I should be further along by now." When someone is angry at circumstances for being unfair. The intervention: replace every "should" with "is" and see if the pain survives the translation. "My business should be making more money" becomes "My business is making X. If I want it to make Y, what inputs would increase the likelihood?"

#### "You Can Make Money Even If You Don't Deserve It" [3/14]

The market responds to inputs, not worthiness. This is the direct antidote to the form of victimhood that sounds like humility -- "I don't deserve it" -- but functions identically to "I can't do it."

> "You'll get business, and you can also get business and make money even if you don't deserve it, which, for me, was actually a really comforting point."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

> "I can actually be a shitty person and horrible and suck at everything, but if I still work out and I still eat this way, I can still look this way. Even though I don't deserve it."
> *Source: mw670, ~01:40:43*

The market is a machine. You put inputs in. Outputs come out. The machine does not run a background check on your character, your childhood, or your moral standing. This is meant to be liberating: you do not need to earn the right to succeed before you start succeeding. You just need to do the work.

In mw670, Hormozi frames this explicitly as "the cheat code for people who hate themselves." Decouple self-worth from outcomes. Take the actions that create success regardless of how you feel about yourself. The feelings can catch up later -- or not. The results don't care either way.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone with low self-worth uses their self-image as an excuse not to act. When imposter syndrome is preventing someone from charging what they're worth, making the ask, or putting themselves out there. When someone says "who am I to do this?" The answer: "The market doesn't check your credentials for worthiness. It checks your inputs."

### Stories

**The COVID Hybrid Gym Pivot** [SIGNATURE]
> In March 2020, 100% of Hormozi's customers -- gym owners -- were legally banned from operating overnight. Revenue dropped from $37 million to $31 million. His team operated under a single rule: "If we cannot control it, we do not talk about it." In seven days, they built "the hybrid gym" -- streaming classes from different gym owners across the community, 12 hours a day, so members maintained memberships and had more classes than before. Meanwhile, 8-10% of all gyms in the country went out of business within 30 days. Hormozi's diagnosis of why: "They were waiting for a good enough reason to give up. They were looking for a socially acceptable reason." Two waves of closures followed -- the first wave gave up, the second ran out of resources. Hormozi's team did $31 million in sales in an industry that was not legally allowed to do business. (impact, ~01:52:21)

*Teaching application:* The definitive responsibility-under-crisis story. Deploy when someone faces a catastrophic external disruption and feels helpless. The lesson is twofold: (1) in a crisis, focus exclusively on what you can control, and (2) crises create asymmetric advantage -- "all of our competitors are screwed and so there'll be more for us because we'll work harder while they get disheartened." Most people use a crisis as a socially acceptable permission slip to quit. The ones who refuse to discuss the uncontrollable are the ones left standing.
*Sources:* impact

**Mr. Gibbons and "Success Is the Only Revenge"** [2/14]
> When Alex was 14 or 15 years old, Mr. Gibbons -- a high school admin teacher who became his daily workout partner -- gave him the principle that redirected his entire relationship with blame and resentment: "The only thing that you can do is win so big that all of them constantly compare themselves to you. And then you'll forget they exist... It's not the best revenge. It's the only one. There's no other revenge. Because everything else is petty." (mw670, ~01:03:31)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone's motivation is oriented around proving a specific person wrong -- an ex, a parent, a former boss, a competitor. The insight: if your goal is shaped by someone else's actions, they're still controlling you. Revenge fantasies are just blame wearing a motivational costume. The solution is not to suppress the anger but to redirect it: win so overwhelmingly at your own game that the comparisons happen naturally. Then you forget they exist -- which is the real victory.
*Sources:* mw670

**"People Want You to Do Well But Not Better Than Them"** [2/14]
> After Alex started succeeding, the gym members who had supported him as an underdog turned against him. Friends dropped off. The transition from underdog to threat was swift and confusing. His observation: "People want you to do well but not better than them." (mw670, ~01:30:48) The only people who never turned were those already doing better than Alex. Hate came exclusively from people below or at his level.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is hurt or confused by backlash from people who used to support them. The reframe: hate is a leading indicator of progress, not a signal to stop. People turning against you after you succeed is not a reason to blame them or to shrink back -- it is evidence that you have moved to a new level. The victim response is to feel betrayed. The ownership response is to recognize the pattern as inevitable and keep building.
*Sources:* mw670

**The Three Layers of Objection (The Onion of Blame)** [2/14]
> In the Ed Mylett interview, Hormozi maps Albert Ellis's cognitive distortion framework onto the way people externalize responsibility. Layer 1 is circumstances -- "Everything's unfair. Nothing goes my way." This is the outermost, easiest layer to voice. Layer 2 is other people -- "My husband won't let me. My coworkers won't support me." The finger points outward. Layer 3 is self-doubt -- "Will this work for me? What if I fail?" The deepest and most honest layer. (ed-mylett)
>
> "First, people are upset and distorted about the universe. Everything's unfair. Nothing goes my way. The next level is because of insert person blame. Finger goes out. My kids, my husband, my coworkers, my mom, whatever it is, won't let me do this thing."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

*Teaching application:* Most people live on Layer 1 or 2 their entire lives. They blame circumstances or other people and never reach the real issue: their own fear. Deploy this framework when someone presents a cascade of external reasons for their situation. Don't accept the first layer. Don't accept the second. Keep peeling until you get to Layer 3 -- which is almost always some version of "I'm afraid I'll try and it won't work." That is the conversation worth having.
*Sources:* ed-mylett
