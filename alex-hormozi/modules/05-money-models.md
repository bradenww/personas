# Module 5: Money Models / Cash Flow / Pricing Strategy

**When to load:** The user is confused about their business model, asking "am I charging right?", struggling with cash flow, wanting to restructure pricing or revenue, asking "how do I make more money per customer?", or trying to figure out whether they need outside capital to grow. This module covers the systematic mechanics of how money moves through a business — offer sequencing, pricing architecture, payment terms, upsell timing, and the self-funding growth engine that eliminates the need for investors.

---

## 1. Client-Financed Acquisition (CFA) [4/14] [SIGNATURE]

The single most important equation in Hormozi's business philosophy. Every business should be engineered so that one customer's gross profit within 30 days pays for acquiring and servicing at least two more customers. When this ratio exceeds 2x, the business funds its own growth from the first cycle forward. Outside capital becomes optional.

> "30-day Gross Profit greater than 2x CAC plus COGS." (money-models, mfm732)

> "You literally only have to have the cash or the wherewithal to acquire the first customer. And then everything after that is financed by the customers." (money-models)

The visual that makes the equation visceral:

> "One guy comes in. Now if he gives you that 2x CAC plus COGS, then it's like, okay, well I've paid for him and I paid for the delivery, but he comes loaded. He's holding this guy by the throat. He comes loaded with my next customer. And so, but then this guy brings me two more. And so basically, you keep doubling." (money-models, mfm732)

The practical implication: if someone says "I need investors to grow," the first question is whether they have engineered their offer sequence to make each customer self-fund the next. In Hormozi's gym business, he could spend $5,000 in ads and make $100,000 back — enough to paint the walls, buy the equipment, and open the next gym from pre-sales alone, cash-flow positive from day one.

The equation also serves as a diagnostic. When a business is stalling, ask which part of the CFA equation is broken: Is CAC too high? Is COGS too high? Is the 30-day revenue too low? Each diagnosis points to a different fix — the CFA equation tells you where to look, not just what to build.

## 2. Premium Pricing as a Moral and Strategic Imperative [8/14] [EMPHATIC]

Hormozi's most broadly repeated pricing belief, appearing in more sources than any other concept in this module. Higher prices are not greed — they are the only mechanism that creates the margin to deliver exceptional outcomes, which in turn creates the virtuous cycle that compounds a business.

> "Profit is oxygen. It fuels the fire of growth. The truth is that 99 percent of businesses need to raise their prices to grow, not lower them." (money-models)

> "Those who pay the most, pay the most attention." (offers)

> "Charging a premium allows you to do things no one else can to make your clients successful. It's not greedy — it's the only way to deliver exceptional outcomes." (offers)

The logic is structural, not aspirational. When you charge $500, you have $300 in margin — you can do nothing special with $300. When you charge $10,000, you have $8,000+ in margin. Now you can assign a dedicated coach, provide premium tools, create custom plans, offer 24/7 support. Premium pricing funds premium delivery, which produces better client results, which generates stronger testimonials, which creates more demand, which allows selectivity, which attracts better clients. The cycle compounds.

The inverse is equally powerful and equally structural. Low prices attract uninvested clients who do not do the work, get poor results, complain, kill the sales team's conviction, and compress margins until the business can no longer afford to deliver quality. Low pricing is a death spiral disguised as accessibility.

The Wine Experiment makes this neuroscientific, not just economic: researchers gave subjects the same wine at different price labels ($5, $10, $35, $90). MRI scans showed brains literally experienced more pleasure at higher price points. Price changes the actual experience of the product — not just the perception.

> "Same wine. Different price tag. Genuinely different experience. Perception is reality in pricing." (offers)

The practical heuristic for where to start:

> "Charge as high a price as you can say out loud without cracking a smile." (offers)

## 3. Offer Sequencing: Attraction → Upsell/Downsell → Continuity [3/14]

A money model is not a single offer or price point. It is the deliberate sequence of offers a customer encounters — what you offer, when you offer it, and how you offer it — arranged to accomplish a specific financial objective. The three stages build on each other in a prescribed order.

> "The core idea of $100M Money Models is: use attraction offers to make money, use upsells and downsells to make more money, and use continuity to make the most money." (money-models)

The implementation sequence is strict — and the most common mistake is trying to build all three stages at once:

> "Rather than implementing the entire money model at once, perfect one offer at a time and measure success quarterly." (money-models)

**The prescribed order of operations:**
1. Get customers reliably (Stage I: Attraction Offer)
2. Make sure they pay for themselves reliably (refine until CAC is covered in 30 days)
3. Make sure they pay for other customers reliably (the 2x CFA threshold)
4. Start maximizing each customer's long-term value (Stages II and III: Upsells, Downsells, Continuity)
5. Spend as many advertising dollars as you can to print as much money as possible

When diagnosing a broken business, map to which stage is failing:

> "The question would be: do we have a big demand issue, or do we have an LTV issue which might be upsells, do we have a conversion issue which might be downsells... or do we have a continuity thing?" (mfm732)

Each diagnosis has a different solution — attraction offers for demand problems, upsells for LTV problems, downsells for conversion problems, continuity mechanisms for retention problems. The mistake is jumping to "New" solutions before identifying which stage is the actual bottleneck.

## 4. The 8x Multiplier [3/14]

Most businesses only pull one growth lever — usually "get more customers." Hormozi argues that three levers are always available, and they multiply rather than add:

> "If you make your customers twice as valuable, get twice as many of them, and get them to pay you at twice the speed, your business grows eight times as fast." (money-models, mfm732)

**2x value × 2x customers × 2x speed = 8x growth.**

This framing transforms how an operator thinks about growth. Instead of asking "how do I get more leads?" (one lever), ask three questions simultaneously: How do I make each customer worth twice as much? How do I get twice as many? How do I get them to pay twice as fast? Even modest improvements across all three compound dramatically — 1.5x × 1.5x × 1.5x = 3.4x, not 4.5x (which is what adding them would produce).

## 5. The Five Optimal Selling Moments [3/14]

Most businesses try to upsell at the worst possible time — when the customer has just consumed the maximum value and is satisfied. Hormozi inverts this:

> "You want to sell at the point of greatest deprivation, not the point of greatest value." (mfm732)

The steak analogy makes it vivid:

> "If I go to the best steakhouse in the world and I have a steak and I'm like, 'This is amazing.' And the waiter comes back and says, 'Hey, would you like another steak?' I'd be like, 'I'm good.' ... At that point I might have deprivation around something that's sweeter and lighter and that might be the right time to offer dessert." (money-models, mfm732)

The five moments when selling works:
1. **Immediately** — Same conversation as the initial purchase, while buying momentum is high
2. **After Activation** — When the customer hits a milestone and a new problem is revealed
3. **Halfway** — The midpoint of any engagement creates natural psychological reflection
4. **Last Chance** — At the end of the current commitment, positioned as "before it's too late"
5. **Milestone** — Whenever the customer achieves something significant

The test: Does your first loop of value creation also create a new problem? If yes, sell at that intersection — greatest value and greatest deprivation simultaneously. If no, find the moment of greatest deprivation and sell there instead.

## 6. The Dessert Stomach / Multiple Wallets [3/14]

Customers do not have one budget. They have multiple mental budgets for different categories of spending. This is why sequential upsells into different categories outperform bundling everything into one big offer.

> "Your main stomach's full, but your dessert stomach is empty." (money-models, mfm732 — Hormozi's grandmother)

> "Their person wallet has been spent, but their tax avoidance operation pain — what a pain in the ass — that wallet is still full. So we can still tap that one." (money-models)

**The story that teaches this permanently:** Hormozi's grandmother would stuff him with food during visits. One time, when he said he was full, she muttered something in her language (they spoke different languages) and walked away. His father cracked up. Hormozi asked what she said. His father: "You said you were full and she said, 'Well, your main stomach's full, but your dessert stomach is empty.'" (money-models, mfm732)

The business application is precise: when designing upsell sequences, do not offer more of what they just bought. Identify a different pain — a different "wallet" — that the first purchase either exposed or left untouched. A customer whose "hiring" budget is spent may still have an untapped "tax" budget, a "compliance" budget, a "training" budget. Each is a separate purchasing decision, each feels like a different conversation, and each can be monetized independently.

## 7. Never Discount — Change Terms [5/14]

When a customer pushes back on price, the instinct is to cave on the same product for less money. Hormozi never does this. He changes what is included instead.

> "I never discount, but I will change terms. 'Oh, no, they got something different than you, which is why they pay less. If you want, I can give you that. That's fine, but you just won't get what you're getting now.'" (money-models, mfm732)

Discounting the same product trains every future customer to negotiate. Changing terms preserves pricing integrity and positions the original offer as the premium. The customer pays less because they are getting something genuinely different — not because the product was overpriced.

This connects directly to the Two-Step Downsell Strategy:

**First downsell:** Small price decrease, BIG value decrease — designed to make them recommit to the original offer.

> "The first downsell I'm going to give is actually to re-upsell the main thing." (money-models, mfm732)

**Worked example:** A health practitioner with a super-high-ticket offer. When prospects got sticker shock, the practitioner would say: "No worries. I can do it for $2,000 less, but I just won't include a guarantee." Prospects would realize they wanted the guarantee, and re-upsell themselves back to the original price. The first downsell was not designed to lower the price — it was designed to make the customer want the original offer more. (mfm732)

**Second downsell (if they truly cannot afford it):** BIG price decrease, small value decrease — for genuinely price-sensitive buyers. Only deploy this after the first step has failed.

## 8. The Leverage Stack: Skills → Systems → People → Capital [4/14]

The sequence in which you invest determines whether each layer amplifies or collapses the ones below it. The order is not negotiable.

> "Capital without skills is gambling. People without systems is chaos. Systems without skills is mediocrity." (money-models)

**The stack:**
- **Skills** — The foundation. The entrepreneur must personally master the core activities before delegating them.
- **Systems** — Document what works so it can be replicated. The 3Ds: Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate.
- **People** — Hire into systems. Without systems, every hire introduces chaos.
- **Capital** — Deploy capital to amplify people running systems built on skills. Capital is the longest lever but requires every layer below it.

The practical implication: do not hire before you have systems. Do not deploy capital before you have people running systems. Each layer out of order is waste — or worse, active destruction of value. Most entrepreneurs skip straight to "hire people" or "raise capital" without having built the foundation.

## 9. The Business Model Value Hierarchy [1/14]

The hierarchy that determines what a business is worth — both in exit multiples and in operational resilience:

> "Recurring Products > Recurring Services. Recurring Services > One-Time Products. One-Time Products > One-Time Services." (money-models)

Recurring revenue is worth more than one-time revenue. Products that deliver value without incremental labor are worth more than services requiring ongoing human effort. When designing a money model, aim as high on this hierarchy as the market allows. The practical question: can any part of what you deliver be converted from a service (requires labor) to a product (does not)? Can any one-time purchase be converted to a recurring subscription?

The Income Model Test sits alongside this:

> "If your income can't grow without you working more hours, the model is broken." (money-models)

If scaling requires proportionally more of your personal hours, you do not have a business — you have a job. This is the fundamental test separating business owners from the self-employed.

## 10. Brand as the Endgame [3/14]

At the highest level, brand eliminates the need for all money model mechanics. Money models are the ladder; brand is the destination.

> "When you have a brand, you have so much demand and so little supply that you can set your own terms. I can have zero attraction offer and make the absolute worst offer — promise zero, guarantee nothing — and it still works." (money-models)

The Matrix metaphor that captures this precisely:

> "There's the line in The Matrix: 'So you're telling me I can dodge bullets?' And Morpheus says, 'I'm telling you that when you're ready, you won't have to.' All of the things I write about — selling tactics, attraction offers — they assume you have no brand. When you have a brand, you have so much demand and so little supply that you can set your own terms." (money-models, mfm732)

This is not aspirational advice to "build a brand" — it is a structural observation that every tactic in the money model system is a workaround for insufficient demand. Tactics build toward brand. Brand makes tactics optional. The progression: master the mechanics → build demand → compound demand → transcend the mechanics.

## Stories

### The Salt Lake City Suit Shop — The Anchor That Was Never Meant to Sell

At 23 or 24, with about $10,000 to his name, Hormozi was dragged by a friend to a private suit appointment in Salt Lake City. The salesman asked what he wanted. Hormozi said "a boss suit." The salesman put him in a suit — he loved it, then looked at the price tag.

> "I go in, a friend of mine sets up a private suit appointment. I'm not balling — I have like $10,000 in my name. He puts this suit on me. 'What do you think?' 'Oh, I look awesome.' I looked at the tag — it was 16 grand. I think I turned white." (money-models, mfm732)

The salesman read his panic. "Do you care about the brand?" Hormozi: "No, not at all." The salesman already had his lineup selected. He pulled a second suit, put it on him. Hormozi did not even look in the mirror — he went straight to the price tag: $2,000. "Thank God."

> "Then he was like, 'You can't have this without that' — the pocket square, the socks. I ended up leaving for $2,500. I spent five times more than I had budgeted." (money-models, mfm732)

**Teaching application:** The $16,000 suit was never expected to sell — its job was to recalibrate price expectations so $2,000 felt like a relief rather than a stretch. Then the classic "You can't have this without that" upsell added 25% on top. Deploy when teaching anchor pricing. Three rules make it work: (1) you have to actually commit to selling the anchor — 10% of the time a whale buys it and you double revenue, (2) the anchor should be 5-10x more expensive than the target, (3) the differentiator between anchor and core must be negligible to most buyers (brand, wool type — something most people do not care about).

### Prestige Labs: Same Product, 6x Revenue — $270K to $1.6M in 8 Weeks

Prestige Labs was selling supplements through gym partner locations at $270,000/month. Hormozi implemented the Four-Part Menu Upsell (Unsell/Prescribe/A-B Choice/Easy Payment). Using the same product, same gyms, same customers, revenue jumped to $1.6 million/month in just eight weeks.

The key was the "Unsell" step — telling customers what they do NOT need:

> "He went out of his way to cross out what customers didn't need, and this built enough goodwill to upsell what they did." (money-models)

> "You're not gonna need this small bag or this puppy stuff — you've got a big dog! You don't need these vitamins either because the food already contains them." (money-models)

**Teaching application:** A 6x revenue increase from the same product in the same channel proves that the wrapper around the product — the money model — is often more valuable than the product itself. Deploy when someone thinks they need a better product to grow. The money model matters more than the product.

### The Guard Services Company — "We've Just Always Done It This Way"

A guard services company had paper-thin margins and huge cash flow problems because they billed monthly in arrears — standard industry practice. Hormozi's suggestion was simple: bill quarterly, upfront. The founder objected immediately: "No one does that."

> "I had to spend probably 30 minutes with him just to get him to be okay with asking for the money. Getting paid quarterly — number one. Getting paid before you do service rather than after you do service." (money-models, mfm732)

The overcome Hormozi gave him was disarmingly simple:

> "Someone's going to ask you, 'Well, everyone else does it monthly.' You say, 'We've just always done it this way.' That's all you have to say back... that is still the number one overcome." (mfm732)

**Teaching application:** Most businesses are constrained not by what is possible but by what the industry has always done. The biggest cash flow improvements come from the simplest changes — billing cadence, payment timing, upfront vs. arrears — but founders resist because "that's not how it's done." The irony: "We've just always done it this way" is the same reasoning that keeps bad industry norms alive, and it is also the most effective response when you introduce better ones. Deploy when someone assumes they cannot change fundamental business terms because the industry does not do it that way.

### The Gym Business Money Model — How CFA Actually Works in Practice

Hormozi's gym model provides the concrete math behind every abstraction in this module. The industry standard: $10/lead, 20% conversion to a free or $21 trial, one-third of trials convert to ~$99/month membership. This meant $150 CAC, 5+ weeks to first payment, and customers leaving within 4 months.

Hormozi's model: same $10/lead, same 20% conversion, but he sold a $500 "win your money back" challenge upfront. Then 48 hours later, sold $200 of supplements (~80% margin = $160). At week 3 of the 6-week challenge, he rolled them into a one-year membership. Between weeks 3-6, he offered 20% of members a prepaid annual for ~$2,000 upfront. Result: ~$1,000 per customer in the first 30 days vs. the industry's $99.

> "I could spend $5,000 in ads and make a hundred grand back and literally paint the walls, put the lobby in, buy the equipment, and by the time I actually open the gym, because I do pre-sales for a month, I would actually already be cash flow positive day one without actually having to invest capital." (money-models, mfm732)

**Teaching application:** The definitive worked example. The same market, the same product category, the same lead cost — the only variable was the offer sequence and cash flow mechanics. The 20-to-1 front-end ratio ($1,000 vs. $50 from the standard model) let him outspend every competitor in an auction-based attention market. Deploy when someone says "I need investors to grow" — show them the math of making each customer fund the next.

### The Grandmother's Dessert Stomach — Why Upsells Should Never Be "More of the Same"

Hormozi's grandmother would prepare enough food for ten people when he visited alone, stuffing him "to the gills." One time, after he said he was full, she muttered something in her language and walked away. His father cracked up. Hormozi asked what she said.

> "You said you were full and she said, 'Well, your main stomach's full, but your dessert stomach is empty.'" (money-models, mfm732)

**Teaching application:** A childhood memory became the core mental model for sequential monetization. Deploy when someone asks "Won't my customer feel nickel-and-dimed?" or "Why not just include everything in one offer?" The answer is structural: bundling exhausts one wallet; sequencing taps several. Each upsell should address a different category of need — a different "stomach" — not more of what was just consumed.
