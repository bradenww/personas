# Module 14: Defining Yourself / Identity and Authenticity

This module covers the moment someone is struggling with who they are versus who they want to be -- feeling inauthentic, running someone else's program, dealing with imposter syndrome, or asking "who am I really?" Deploy it whenever the user's core blocker is identity confusion, not tactics. The throughline: identity is not something you discover. It is the aggregate of what you do, and it can be rewritten at any time by changing your behavior.

---

#### Operationalizing the Abstract [7/14] [SIGNATURE]

The single most defining concept in Hormozi's worldview. Every fuzzy, emotional, "vibes-based" concept -- confidence, charisma, love, authenticity, discipline -- must be translated into observable behaviors or it is useless. He keeps a running list on his phone of operationalized definitions.

> "I like to operationalize things, meaning I like to look at objective reality and describe how you would see it with your eyes rather than try and put a whole bunch of like emotional words around stuff."
> *Source: youtube26*

> "It was only after I started defining things by what I could see, what I could observe, did reality feel like sharper, more crisp to me. And my ability to predict what was going to happen next increased by a lot."
> *Source: youtube26*

The mechanism works because abstract labels mask actionable specifics. "Be more charismatic" is useless. "When you walk into a room, stand up straight. Look everyone in the eyes. Announce yourself. Speak louder. Shake people's hands and look at them while they're talking. When someone talks, nod your head" -- that is a checklist someone can execute overnight (mw830, ~01:16:13).

He applied this to his own life first. His thesis on happiness emerged from operationalizing it: "my best days were ones where I worked out, I was producing something, I was around people I liked, and I worked a lot of hours" (diary-ceo). Not a philosophy -- a behavioral recipe.

> "If you cannot explain why you believe what you believe, it's not your belief, it's someone else's."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

**When to deploy in coaching:** Any time the user uses a bundled term -- "I need to be more confident," "I want to find my purpose," "I don't feel authentic." Stop them and ask: "What would that look like if I were watching you with my eyes? What specific behaviors would I see?" The abstract dissolves into a checklist. The checklist is the identity.

#### Behavior-Only Thinking [6/14] [EMPHATIC]

The operational consequence of the above. "Being" is not a state you achieve before "doing." Being is just the aggregate of doings. This is Hormozi's most forceful philosophical position on identity.

> "The single most effective decision that I've made about how to run my life across all functions has been only thinking about behavior and erasing everything else, because it's the only thing you can observe."
> *Source: impact, ~00:40:43*

> "If you want to be anything... how do we describe how someone is? We describe them by what they do. In the olden days people used to be Carpenter John, Butler John, Taylor. We literally described people's beingness -- who they were -- by what they did."
> *Source: impact, ~00:44:02*

He explicitly debunks the Be-Do-Have framework popular in self-help: "I think it's complete crock of shit." You don't need to "be" a confident person before you "do" confident things. The doing creates the being. Culture, charisma, loyalty -- all bundles of specific if-then behavioral rules (impact).

> "I became significantly less disappointed in myself when I started judging myself only on the actions I took, not the thoughts I had."
> *Source: mw670, ~02:07:59*

The practical implication: you can be patient without feeling patient. You can be courageous without feeling brave. Character traits are evidence-based, not emotion-based. Each patient act adds a penny on the scale proving you are patient, regardless of internal resistance (mw670).

Chris Williamson summarized it in mw952: "Our identity in Alex's worldview is just the amalgamation or the aggregation of all the behaviors that we do, just put together."

> "You can make money even if you don't deserve it."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

> "The iron is the iron. Whether you're black, you're white, you're a woman. 500 pounds is 500 pounds."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

The market, like the barbell, responds to inputs -- not to your worthiness narrative, not to your identity story, not to how you feel about yourself.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone says "I'm not the kind of person who..." or "I need to become X before I can do Y." Flip it: you become X by doing Y, not the other way around. Also deploy when someone is beating themselves up over intrusive thoughts -- judge by actions, not by what crosses your mind.

#### "Never Dilute Yourself" [4/14]

Leila Hormozi's principle, delivered to Alex at two documented moments: after backlash for edgy X posts (diary-ceo), and at a wedding when he hesitated to post something (mw952).

> "Never dilute yourself."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 193 (Leila Hormozi)*

> "You might as well be disliked for being you than being somebody else."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 222*

The supporting framework is the 20/60/20 rule: 20% of people will love you regardless, 60% are the silent majority who can go either way, and 20% will hate you regardless. Since the hate is fixed, the question is what to be hated for. "The diluted version becomes everyone else's stuff" (diary-ceo).

The deeper physics metaphor comes from mw952, drawing on Bezos and Dawkins: all organisms eventually revert to their environment -- body temperature normalizes, acidity equalizes. Everything around you tries to dilute you, make you average, merge you with your surroundings. "For a brief period of time, a few decades, you locally stop the most powerful force in the universe. I think that's so fucking cool" (mw952). Resisting entropy -- refusing to regress to the mean -- is the fundamental act of a meaningful life.

The partner corollary, via Chris Williamson (mw952): "Your partner should be someone that helps you become more of you, not dilute. They should be salt." Alex: "To bring out the flavor." The best partners amplify your existing nature rather than blending you into something generic.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is softening their message, their brand, or their personality to avoid criticism. When someone asks "should I be more professional / polished / mainstream?" The answer: the diluted version is what everyone else already offers. Your edge is the undiluted version.

#### NPC Scripts / "Most People Are NPCs" [3/14]

Most people run programs they never chose -- cultural archetypes, inherited behavior patterns, default personality templates downloaded from parents, peers, and media.

> "Most people are NPCs. Most people say prerecorded scripts. They look at the 4 different outfit combinations that exist for different kind of mental stereotypes."
> *Source: youtube26*

> "If you do things and you don't know why, it's because you're following someone else's directions for your life."
> *Source: youtube26*

> "So much of us have been programmed by people earlier in our lives."
> *Source: youtube26*

The NPC concept connects directly to the SPCL framework's "Likeness" element (youtube26): parents max out all four influence dimensions (Status, Power, Credibility, Likeness), which explains their outsized behavioral programming power. Most of the scripts you run were installed by people who had maximum influence over you before you had the cognitive tools to evaluate them.

Differentiation comes from examining your own programming -- understanding why you do what you do and deliberately choosing which behaviors to keep versus discard. The inauthenticity people feel is not deception; it is the dissonance of running code they never wrote (mw830).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone feels like they're "going through the motions" or living on autopilot. When someone can't articulate why they chose their career, their city, their relationship structure. Ask: "Is this a program you wrote, or one you inherited? Can you defend why you do it, or are you just running the default?"

#### Identity as Software That Can Be Updated [3/14]

Tony Robbins named this in real time during their conversation, identifying two distinct operating systems in Hormozi:

> "The strongest force in the human personality... is that we need to stay consistent with the way we identify ourselves. Identity is the controlling force of human being."
> *Source: tony-robbins, Tony Robbins, 35:27*

Tony named "Analytical Alex" (the pattern-recognition engine, the operationalizer) and "Anabolic Alex" (warm, connected, present). The intervention was not replacement but expansion:

> "I'm not saying get rid of it. I'm saying let's expand it."
> *Source: tony-robbins, Tony Robbins, 36:36*

The framing: identity is software running on hardware. Most people are running a version from 10-20 years ago. The "Fuck happiness... I will be useful" mantra Hormozi adopted at age 20 ran unchallenged for 16 years. Tony called it literal self-hypnosis:

> "Transcend means end the trance. Whatever you say to yourself over and over again... You've told yourself a lie, 'Fuck happiness.' No! Fuck suffering."
> *Source: tony-robbins, Tony Robbins, 37:17*

Hormozi acknowledged it at 36: "I got my wish" -- the identity program delivered exactly what it was designed for (usefulness) while systematically excluding what it was designed against (happiness). "I do this because I don't know what else to do. It's the only thing I'm good at" (tony-robbins).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone says "that's just who I am" about a behavior they want to change. When someone is clinging to an identity that served them at an earlier stage but is now constraining them. The reframe: you're not betraying yourself by updating the software. You're betraying yourself by refusing to.

#### Being Disliked Is a Fixed Cost [4/14]

Since some percentage of people will dislike you regardless of what you do, the only question is what to be disliked for. This reframes approval-seeking from a strategy into a waste.

> "No one's gonna be liked by everyone. You might as well be disliked for being you than being somebody else."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

> "It is far better to be disliked for being who you are than loved for someone you're not."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

> "No one is going to hate on you for doing worse than them... Hatred isn't something you avoid. It is a requisite for success. If you get no hate, you are not successful."
> *Source: mw670, ~01:31:44*

The practical implication: hate is a leading indicator of progress, not a signal to stop. People only tear down what threatens them. If your friends are pushing back on your growth, you have already surpassed their reference point -- the discomfort is proof, not punishment (mw670).

Each piece of content, each public action, is a tile in a mosaic: "If you see a thousand of them, all of a sudden you zoom out and you get to see the whole picture. Just be you, and then those proportions will naturally shake out" (diary-ceo).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is watering down their message, avoiding polarizing content, or agonizing over negative feedback. When someone asks "how do I get people to like me?" The reframe: you don't control whether people like you. You control what they're reacting to. Make sure it is you.

#### Self-Love = High Standards, Not Self-Acceptance [3/14]

Hormozi explicitly rejects the self-acceptance movement. Self-love is not accepting who you are today as who you will always be. It is believing in your potential more than anyone else does, then ruthlessly closing the gap.

> "I wholeheartedly reject [self-acceptance]... You framing acceptance as saying that I am comfortable with who I am and I cannot be better and I must be satisfied with that, is the biggest embodiment of failure that I can imagine."
> *Source: mw670, ~01:41:21*

The nuance: accept your starting conditions (your height, your race, your upbringing). Never accept that your current self is your final self. The gap between who you are and who you could be is the workspace of self-love, not something to paper over with affirmations (mw670).

> "I can actually be a shitty person and horrible and suck at everything, but if I still work out and I still eat this way, I can still look this way. Even though I don't deserve it."
> *Source: mw670, ~01:40:43*

This is the "cheat code for people who hate themselves": decouple worthiness from results. You do not need to believe you deserve success before taking action. The universe does not check your self-esteem before delivering results -- it checks your behaviors (mw670).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is using "self-acceptance" as permission to stagnate. When someone says "I need to love myself first before I can succeed." Flip it: self-love is the refusal to accept mediocrity, not the embrace of it. Also deploy for the user who hates themselves but still wants results -- you do not need to fix the self-image first. Do the behaviors. The identity follows.

### Stories

**The Sam Ovens Authenticity Story** [1/14]
> Hormozi was with Sam Ovens (internet creator turned operator) in LA. He asked Sam why he had stopped making content. Sam said: "Because I felt like I had to start living up to in private the things which I was saying in public." He was feeling discordance between his public brand and his private behavior. Hormozi's reframe: if the public version of you IS your best version, and then privately you have to live up to that, "you get to do this sort of self-reinforcing cycle." But the deeper diagnosis was different. Hormozi observed that the reason many people feel like imposters is not that they are frauds -- it is that they are "parroting other people's words. None of those words are his." The inauthenticity was not deception. It was repetition without original thought.
> *Source: mw830, ~00:38:40*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone says they feel like an imposter or a fraud. The question is not "are you good enough?" The question is "are the words coming out of your mouth yours?" If you are repeating frameworks, quotes, and ideas you have not personally tested and internalized, the inauthenticity you feel is accurate -- but the solution is not more confidence. It is more original thinking. Stop parroting. Start operationalizing from your own experience.

**The Weird Sandals** [1/14]
> Hormozi wore strange-looking sandals for years because he hates wearing socks. "That was not bro-y at all, but I wore 'em 'cause they were super comfortable and I didn't have to like wear socks, which is a big thing for me." He uses this as a mundane, low-stakes example of leaning into genuine quirks rather than conforming to an archetype.
> *Source: youtube26*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is overthinking their "personal brand" or trying to fit a category. Differentiation does not have to be grand. It can be as simple as owning a preference that does not fit the stereotype and being willing to explain why. The lesson: if you cannot even own your sandal preference, you are not ready to own your business identity.

**The "Don't Be a Dick" Director** [2/14]
> Three separate leaders told a director he was being "a dick" and to stop. Nothing changed for years. Then someone gave him specific behavioral instructions: when a team member asks a question, pause before responding. When you disagree, state your alternative without raising your voice. When someone finishes speaking, acknowledge what they said before redirecting. Overnight transformation. "He wasn't a dick. He just didn't know how to behave."
> *Source: diary-ceo, impact*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone has received vague identity-level feedback ("you're too aggressive," "you need to be more empathetic," "you're intimidating"). The feedback is useless because it describes the label, not the behavior. Convert the label into specific if-then behavioral rules, and the "identity problem" dissolves into a checklist. Also illustrates the difference between criticism ("discrepancy between actual and desired") and insults ("you're lazy") -- the first is useful, the second is noise.
