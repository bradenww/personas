# Module 19: Using Pain and Fear Productively

This module covers the moment someone is either paralyzed by fear and pain -- frozen, unable to act, consumed by what people will think or what they might lose -- or is using pain as fuel but burning out, asking "how do I use this pain?" or "I'm afraid and I can't move." Deploy it when the user's core state is emotional paralysis or unsustainable rage-fueled performance. The question is not whether to feel pain or fear -- those are constants. The question is whether pain and fear are using you, or you are using them.

---

#### Fear of Judgment Is the Most Expensive Emotion [7/14] [EMPHATIC]

The single most common reason people do not start, do not sell, do not reach out, do not publish, do not ask. Hormozi names it explicitly as the number one bottleneck preventing entrepreneurship -- not lack of tactics, not lack of capital, not market conditions. Fear of what other people will think.

> "Don't be afraid of what other people think. If someone won't speak at your funeral, you shouldn't care about their opinion while you're alive. Honor the few who believe in you by having courage."
> *Source: leads, Section 2; also offers, Quote #18*

> "Fear -- particularly fear of judgment, shame, and parental disapproval -- is the actual bottleneck preventing most people from starting, not lack of tactics or information."
> *Source: diary-ceo, Theme 1.1*

The funeral test is the operational filter: picture your funeral. Who is actually there? Who is speaking? The person whose opinion is stopping you -- are they in the room? If someone will not be at your funeral, their opinion of your life is irrelevant. You are paying rent on a judgment that was never charged.

Hormozi takes this further with a naming exercise. The amorphous "what people will think" has infinite power. A specific person named "Tom" has almost none:

> "It's usually not even a thing that I'm afraid of, it's one person's judgment I'm afraid of. And then when I name the person, then it becomes real. Instead of being this amorphous, like, people, society, judgment, it's like, Tom. Do I really care what Tom thinks?"
> *Source: mw670, ~00:19:29*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone says "I can't because people will think..." or "what if I fail publicly?" Force two things: (1) name the specific person whose judgment they fear, and (2) apply the funeral test. The vague "people" dissolves into a specific person who almost certainly is not thinking about them at all.

#### "In Three Generations, Everyone Will Be Dead" / Cosmic Irrelevance [7/14] [SIGNATURE]

The mortality filter. Hormozi uses death not as existential despair but as a liberation device -- a persistent psychological operating system that prevents catastrophizing from taking hold in the first place. This framework deepens across his appearances over four years: from specific individuals not caring (2023) to the scale of the universe itself (2025).

> "It doesn't matter whether I achieve all of my goals or I don't achieve all of the goals -- in three generations, I'll be forgotten. And the only people who were naysaying against me will also be dead. And so then it's like, just do it for me."
> *Source: mw670, ~00:03:28*

> "I'm on a planet spinning around the sun inside of a galaxy, inside of a universe that will never reach the end of because it's expanding faster than the speed of light. The fact that there was a mistake in my book printing -- no one's going to care."
> *Source: mw952, ~resilience tools section*

> "There's gonna be a caterer at the funeral. Some people are gonna like the food. Some people are gonna comment that it was too cheap."
> *Source: mw952*

> "They weren't thinking about me to begin with, and nor do they really care, and they're going to all die eventually."
> *Source: impact, ~01:21:14*

That last quote is Hormozi's internal monologue while sleeping on the gym floor, imagining his Vanderbilt peers and former consulting colleagues discussing his failure. The shame fantasies dissolved when he realized they were imaginary -- the people he was terrified of were not thinking about him at all. The Queen of England died and nobody thinks about her. Someone will argue over the appetizers at your funeral. You could be an animal's lunch.

This is not nihilism. It is tactical stakes reduction. When you are catastrophizing about a business loss, reminding yourself that the universe is expanding faster than the speed of light makes the crisis feel like what it is: a local, temporary disruption.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is catastrophizing about reputation, public failure, or social humiliation. When someone believes "everyone is watching." When the stakes feel existential but are actually local. The intervention: zoom out until the problem is the correct size.

#### Death as Motivator and Freedom Generator [4/14]

A distinct concept from cosmic irrelevance, though they share DNA. Where cosmic irrelevance reduces stakes, death-as-motivator generates activation energy. The ancient Greek observation that gods envied mortals -- not despite the uncertainty of their lives, but because of it. Mortality is what makes action meaningful.

> "Every day, I was like, I hope I don't wake up. And that was the thing where I was like, this is a big enough problem... if you don't want to wake up then what have you got to lose?"
> *Source: mw670, ~00:12:55*

This was Hormozi's emotional state before leaving Baltimore. The suicidal ideation was not clinical depression -- it was the body's signal that the current path was incompatible with living. And that signal became the activation energy: if the worst case is already where you are, every alternative has positive expected value.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is in a situation so bad they have nothing to lose -- but paradoxically cannot move. Reframe: you are already at the bottom. The only direction available is up. The fear of falling is irrational when you are already on the ground.

#### The Region Beta Paradox [2/14]

Sometimes better things feel worse than worse things. A situation that is tolerable but unfulfilling prevents the activation energy required to change. If your job were terrible, you would quit. Because it is merely mediocre, you stay for decades.

> "Paradoxically, you would go two miles quicker than you would go one mile... sometimes better things can feel worse than worse things."
> *Source: mw670 (Chris Williamson), ~00:13:51*

The "okay" job. The "fine" relationship. The "decent" business that is not growing. These tolerable situations are more dangerous than crises because they never trigger the escape response. Ask: would you be better off if things were worse -- because worse would give you the activation energy to change? If yes, artificially lower your threshold for action rather than waiting for a crisis to force your hand.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone describes their situation as "fine" or "okay" but clearly wants more. The intervention is not to manufacture crisis but to name the paradox: your comfort is the cage. A crisis would free you. So act as if the crisis has already arrived.

#### "I'd Rather Be a Failure Than a Coward" / Coward vs. Failure [5/14] [EMPHATIC]

The identity reframe that resolves paralysis. Most people avoid action because they fear being seen as a failure. Hormozi fears being seen as a coward more -- someone who knew what they should do and did not do it.

> "I'd rather be a failure than a coward."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 79*

> "It only means you're a coward if you allow that fear to change your behavior in an aversive way, as in not towards what you want."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 79*

> "Courage is the quintessential trait that leads to everything else. You have to first have courage."
> *Source: impact, ~00:39:39*

Courage is not the absence of fear. It is acting despite fear. And Hormozi treats it not as a binary but as a spectrum -- measurable, improvable, like any other skill. He places himself above average but explicitly below Elon Musk on this spectrum: "It's not are you courageous or not. It's how courageous are you" (impact, ~00:39:39).

The practical mechanism: replace the feared identity. If "failure" is the label you are afraid of, swap it for the worse one. Would you rather try and fail, or not try and know you were a coward? For most people, cowardice is the more unbearable label. Use the more threatening identity to override the lesser one.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone knows what to do but will not act. When someone is rationalizing inaction as "prudence" or "waiting for the right time." The intervention: "You are not being patient. You are being a coward. Which label can you live with?"

#### "Fear Is a Mile Wide and an Inch Deep" [3/14]

Leila Hormozi's formulation. Fear looks like an ocean from the outside. Take the first step and you discover there is ground underneath.

> "Fear is a mile wide and an inch deep."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 75 (attributed to Leila Hormozi)*

The friends you lose get replaced. The shame is self-inflicted and temporary. Hormozi adds a time-compression test:

> "How long after you do something embarrassing is it funny? If it is going to become funny eventually, it might as well become funny now."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

Fear exists in the vague, not in the specific. The amygdala generates terror; the prefrontal cortex deflates it. The mechanism: go from vague to specific. Spell out the actual worst case in granular detail. "I'll have a story, some self-inflicted shame, and I can always get my job back." Once the worst case is specified, it almost always shrinks.

> "Let's play it out. Let's see what actually would happen... Is it going to change what I eat? Is it going to change where I sleep?... My worst case scenario is I have shelter and food and I'm still breathing air. So that's the downside. And my upside is everything I've wanted."
> *Source: mw952, resilience tools section*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is frozen. Force the worst case through their prefrontal cortex by making them describe it in concrete, material detail. Not "I'll be ruined" -- that is amygdala talk. "I'll lose X dollars, I'll have to move back to Y, and I'll be embarrassed for Z months." Once specified, the fear contracts to its actual size.

#### Pain as Fuel with Guardrails [5/14] [SIGNATURE]

The most operationally important concept in this module. Hormozi is explicit that his primary activation energy was negative emotion: insufficiency, resentment, fear of being a coward, desire to prove people wrong. This is not an accident -- it is a feature of most high performers.

> "I got here from pushing anger for sure."
> *Source: tony-robbins, 9:45*

> "Heroes use pain. Villains are used by it."
> *Source: mw670, ~00:56:59*

The distinction is the guardrail. If your goal is oriented around showing a specific person up -- your ex, your father, your old boss -- they have already won. They changed the game you are playing. The revenge fantasy must be transcended:

> "The only thing that you can do is win so big that all of them constantly compare themselves to you. And then you'll forget they exist... It's not the best revenge. It's the only one. There's no other revenge. Because everything else is petty."
> *Source: mw670, ~01:03:31 (from Mr. Gibbons, Hormozi's high school teacher)*

The Cat and the Cheese (from a rat study via Jordan Peterson): a starving rat pulls toward cheese with X force. Add the smell of a cat behind it, and it pulls harder. Motivation equals desire for reward plus fear of consequence. One engine alone is weaker than both together. When you cannot get motivated by the upside alone, deliberately engage the downside. What are you running from? The "cat" (insufficiency, wasted potential, someone else's control over your life) combined with the "cheese" (your vision) creates maximum motivation (mw670, ~00:38:48).

But push motivation has a shelf life. Tony Robbins draws the distinction in direct conversation with Hormozi: push motivation -- duty, anger, willpower -- is what gets you started. Pull motivation -- serving something bigger, contribution, joy -- is what sustains. Push burns out; pull energizes:

> "There's push motivation... and pull motivation. Pull motivation is where there's something out there that you want to serve more than yourself that'll get you up early, keep you up late. And it isn't hard. It isn't duty. It is like what I'm made for."
> *Source: tony-robbins, 6:10*

Hormozi acknowledges this tension without fully resolving it. In the 2026 Tony Robbins episode, he is actively grappling with the transition from push to pull as his primary motivational architecture.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Two scenarios. First: when someone is using their pain productively but risks becoming consumed by it, when their entire motivation is proving a specific person wrong. Validate the fuel -- pain works. Then redirect: "Are you using the pain, or is it using you? Is your goal oriented toward your own game, or toward an audience of one person who hurt you?" Second: when someone has been running on anger for years and is burning out. Name the transition: push got you here, but pull is what sustains. The question is what you are building toward, not what you are running from.

#### All Conditions of Reality Are Painful [4/14]

The reframe that eliminates pain as a diagnostic signal. Growing is painful. Stagnating is painful. Declining is painful. All three states hurt. If pain is present in every condition of reality, it cannot tell you whether you are on the right path.

> "When you're growing in a business, it's very painful. When you're stagnating... it's very painful. When you're declining... it's very painful. And so that means that all conditions of reality are painful. And so if pain is a prerequisite for reality, then it means it's just a signal that we are alive."
> *Source: mw830, 00:15:32*

Pain is oxygen, not a compass needle. It "becomes irrelevant" as diagnostic information. The question is never "am I in pain?" -- you are. The question is: "Am I in the right kind of pain?" Growth pain is the only kind that compounds.

Hormozi pairs this with a harder claim: feelings have zero correlation with performance. He cites Olympic weight team data where athletes' subjective mood had no measurable relationship to their output:

> "Feeling bad has zero correlation to your performance in the gym... if it has no correlation, then it's almost irrelevant and I can just keep living my life."
> *Source: mw830, 00:16:32*

The highest level is not "feel bad but do it anyway." It is "don't even consider how you feel. It's just not a thought. You just do it."

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone interprets pain as a signal they are on the wrong path. When someone says "if this were right, it wouldn't hurt this much." The reframe: it hurts because you are alive. The only question is whether you are hurting while growing or hurting while stagnating.

#### "8.4 Billion Chances to Make a First Impression" [2/14]

The fear of embarrassment is vastly overweighted. Most people will forget you exist. Those who remember will forgive everything if you eventually solve their problem.

> "I think you have 8.4 billion chances to make a first impression, probably more than that because most people are going to forget that they ever encountered you in the first place."
> *Source: impact, ~00:34:13*

> "You have a 100% chance of bothering somebody on Earth by existing, and so if every action you take has somebody who will hate it, then it kind of cancels everything out and you might as well just do whatever you're going to do anyways."
> *Source: impact, ~00:34:38*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is paralyzed by perfectionism or fear of looking foolish. The math: 8.4 billion people. Most will never encounter you. Of those who do, most will forget. Of those who remember, most will forgive. The universe has no memory. Act accordingly.

#### "Should" Is the Root of All Pain [3/14]

A quieter concept but one Hormozi returns to across appearances. All pain is the gap between what is and what you believe should be. The "should" is the problem, not the reality.

> "Should is the root of all pain... all the things that we think should happen but aren't is basically the measurement of our pain."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is tortured by how their life "should" look versus how it actually is. When someone is comparing their trajectory to others'. The intervention: eradicate "should" from internal dialogue. Replace with "is" (what is actually happening) and conditional if-then statements ("if you want X, then Y increases the likelihood"). Unconditional shoulds are demands on the universe that create permanent gaps between expectation and reality.

#### Words Program Emotions (Transformational Vocabulary) [2/14]

From the Tony Robbins episode. The words you habitually attach to experiences literally become your experience. Not a metaphor -- a neurological mechanism.

> "The words you attach to an experience become your experience."
> *Source: tony-robbins, 9:10*

Tony demonstrates this with Hormozi's mother: she used the word "humiliated" for every discomfort -- a stubbed toe, a minor inconvenience, a misunderstanding. That word funneled every experience through the same disproportionate emotional state. Three business partners experience the same event: one says "enraged," one says "pissed off," one says "annoyed." Same event, radically different emotional responses based on habitual vocabulary.

Tony's own practice: he eliminated the word "depressed" from his vocabulary entirely. Wrote "Only a loser is depressed" on his mirror. Never used the word again. The emotion followed the language, not the other way around.

> "Pain is part of life. Suffering is an option. You've heard that phrase, but it's a fact. It's not just a phrase."
> *Source: tony-robbins, 29:06*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone consistently describes their experience with maximally intense language ("devastated," "destroyed," "humiliated"). The intervention: "What word are you using? Is that word accurate, or is it programming you to feel worse than the situation warrants?" Changing the word changes the biochemistry. This is not positive thinking -- it is precision in self-programming.

### Stories

**Leaving Baltimore / "I Was a Stowaway to My Own Fear"** [3/14]

The foundational fear story. Hormozi was working as a management consultant, doing everything "right" by his father's plan: Vanderbilt (graduated in three years), consulting job, GMAT, on track for Harvard or Stanford MBA. But the moment his father was most proud of him was when Alex was the saddest. He did not want to be alive.

He was so afraid of his father's disapproval that he did not tell his father he was leaving until he was physically five states away, driving to California. He called from the road: "Hey, by the way, I'm going to California." His father said, "Well, let's come over. We'll talk about it." Alex responded, "I'm already here."

> "I basically left in the dark of night. Like no one knew. I was basically a stowaway to my own fear."
> *Source: impact, ~01:05:34*

> "If I failed I would have to go back and tell my dad he was right and that felt like worse than death for me."
> *Source: impact, ~gym floor story*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is paralyzed by family or social expectations. Even the person telling them to be bold was terrified -- so terrified he fled the state before making the phone call. Courage does not look like confidence. It often looks like running away from one thing slightly faster than you are running from the other. The first big wall becomes evidence for every subsequent wall.
*Sources:* mw670, impact, diary-ceo

**The DUI as Conversation Catalyst** [1/14]

After leaving Baltimore, Hormozi accumulated multiple business partnerships: one in a gym, another in four gyms, one in a chiropractic/dental marketing agency. The stress led to drinking, which led to a DUI -- a head-on collision at six miles per hour. No injuries. He saw a performance coach who told him: "Your stress and these conversations could literally kill you. They almost did."

The next day, Alex called the first partner and ended the relationship. "And it was horrible. But the moment I was done, I was like, I got to call the other guy." He called the next one immediately. Momentum.

The DUI was not a wake-up call to stop drinking. It was a wake-up call that he was drinking to avoid conversations that were slowly killing him.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is using coping mechanisms -- drinking, overwork, distraction, scrolling -- to avoid hard conversations. The real problem is never the coping mechanism. It is the thing they are coping with. Name the conversation. Then have it. Also demonstrates Region Beta Paradox in action: the tolerable stress never triggered the escape response. The DUI did.
*Sources:* mw670

**The Shame Fantasies on the Gym Floor** [2/14]

Sleeping on the gym floor after losing everything, Hormozi imagined his Vanderbilt peers and former consulting colleagues discussing his failure. Kids partied above his garage gym while he lay there in the dark, running shame fantasies about what "they" were saying.

> "They weren't thinking about me to begin with, and nor do they really care, and they're going to all die eventually."
> *Source: impact, ~01:21:14*

The shame was entirely self-generated. The audience in his head was imaginary. This is the lived origin of both the "funeral test" and the cosmic irrelevance framework -- they were not theorized. They were forged by a man lying on a gym floor, dissolving fantasies that existed only in his own mind.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is convinced "everyone is talking about me" or "everyone knows I failed." They are not. They are thinking about their own problems. The shame fantasy is a solitary performance with no audience.
*Sources:* impact, mw670

**Mother's Button / Transformational Vocabulary** [1/14]

Hormozi's mother used the word "humiliated" for everything -- a template word that funneled every negative experience through the same disproportionate emotional state. After understanding her through his grandfather's stories ("to understand is to forgive"), Hormozi came home from college. She pressed the usual button to start their standard fight. This time, he felt nothing. She pressed harder. He looked at her and said, "I get it. I'm sorry. You had a tough, tough life." She broke down crying -- because she felt understood for the first time.

Tony Robbins' reframe in conversation with Hormozi: the word programs the emotion. Change the word, change the biochemistry.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone has a template emotional word that flattens all negative experience into one disproportionate feeling. Identify the word. Replace it. Also demonstrates that understanding another person's pain -- not winning the argument -- is what dissolves decades of conflict.
*Sources:* tony-robbins, mw670
