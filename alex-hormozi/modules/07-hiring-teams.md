# Module 7: Hiring / Building a Team

This module covers the moment someone needs to make their first hire, is struggling to find good people, asking "when should I hire?" or "who should I hire first?" Deploy it whenever the user's core challenge is transitioning from solo operator to team builder -- when the business works but depends entirely on them.

---

#### Intelligence = Rate of Learning [4/14] [EMPHATIC]

Hormozi's #1 hiring criterion. He defines intelligence operationally -- not as IQ, credentials, or experience, but as the speed at which someone acquires new behaviors after exposure.

> "I define intelligence as rate of learning. And so if I can get someone who has a high rate of learning versus somebody else, maybe that person starts a little bit behind, but in six months they're going to pass the other person who might have experience."
> *Source: mfm764, [6:04]*

Applied to team evaluation:

> "How many times do you need to be exposed to the same condition in order to change your behavior? If I teach someone something, that script, and then on the first try, they say DEF, and someone else, it takes five tries for them to say DEF, they are not as intelligent as the first person."
> *Source: mw830, [01:29:46]*

This is why Sam's 600 developer interviews for Skool (see Stories) proved definitive: the signal was not who started strongest but whose trajectory was steepest. Hormozi treats every other hiring attribute -- attitude, culture fit, domain knowledge -- as secondary to this one variable because it determines the ceiling, not just the starting point.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Any hiring decision where the user is torn between "experienced but plateaued" vs. "green but sharp." Reframe the question from "who can do this today?" to "who will be better in six months?" Rate of learning is observable within a single interview: give feedback and watch how fast they incorporate it.

#### Hire for the Smallest Skill Deficiency [3/14]

A framework that dissolves the false "hire for attitude vs. hire for aptitude" debate. The answer depends on which gap is smaller relative to the role.

> "We always just should hire for the person with the smallest skill deficiency. And then we have to ask the question as the business owner: given that skill deficiency, is it worth us or worth our resources to train them?"
> *Source: mfm764, [4:04]*

The key insight: attitude and aptitude are not opposites -- they are both bundles of skills with different training timelines:

> "I take the position that I think every skill is trainable. It's just is it worth training?"
> *Source: mfm764, [6:04]*

The heuristic in practice:
- **Low-skill roles (cashier, assistant):** Hire for attitude, train aptitude. Teaching a cashier to run a register takes hours; teaching someone to not be unpleasant takes years.
- **High-skill roles (AI researcher, VP):** Hire for aptitude, tolerate personality gaps. Teaching deep technical skills takes years; teaching professional communication is relatively fast.
- **Always:** Calculate the training cost of the gap. The toddler analogy -- "If I take a toddler, I can get them to be an adult in 18 years and be perfect, but I don't know if I have 18 years to wait" (mfm764).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is agonizing over a "great culture fit but no experience" vs. "experienced but rough around the edges" choice. Don't debate philosophy -- measure the gap. Whichever skill deficiency is smallest wins.

#### The Management Diamond [3/14]

The diagnostic framework for when someone on your team isn't performing. Most founders default to "they're not motivated" or "they're lazy." Hormozi starts from the opposite assumption: the problem is almost always the leader's, not the employee's.

> "Fundamentally people don't do stuff for a reason. I have to figure out what that reason is."
> *Source: mfm764, [2:03]*

Walk through five checks in strict order:

1. **What** -- Did I communicate what I wanted done? (Your failure.)
2. **How** -- Do they know how to do it? (Training issue.)
3. **When** -- Did I set a deadline? (Process issue.)
4. **Blocking** -- Is something preventing them? (Resource/priority issue.)
5. **Motivation** -- Are they not motivated? (The last resort explanation.)

> "They don't know what you want done, they don't know how to do it, they weren't motivated, or they don't know when you want it done. Or there's something blocking them."
> *Source: mfm764, [2:03]*

The critical design: you exhaust four explanations that are your responsibility before arriving at the one that might be theirs. This keeps the conversation non-attacking and productive, and it catches the actual problem most of the time -- which is usually communication, not character.

The cold outbound intervention at Gym Launch (see Stories) is the Management Diamond in reverse: the executive team diagnosed a motivation problem ("shiny object!") when the actual issue was a knowledge problem -- they couldn't see the milestones because they weren't close enough to the work.

**When to deploy in coaching:** Any time the user says "my employee isn't doing their job." Walk them through the diamond before they fire someone. Most performance problems resolve at steps 1-4.

#### Talent Snowball [3/14]

Growth attracts talent; talent accelerates growth. This virtuous cycle is also vicious in reverse -- stagnation repels talent, which causes more stagnation. The strategic implication: the CEO's primary job changes fundamentally around $5M in revenue.

> "Jesus telling Peter: 'You're no longer a fisherman. You're going to be a fisher of men.' Once you get to like 5 million in revenue, that's what you have to become -- a collector of people."
> *Source: mfm764, [15:08]*

The snowball compounds across ventures:

> "The snowball of talent... some people start following you from thing to thing. You start to have this core team that just like they're good. And when you do the next thing, they're all with you."
> *Source: mfm764, [16:09]*

The highest return on capital is not in ads, not in product, not in real estate:

> "The highest returns on capital we get as entrepreneurs is talent. Like full stop. Where else do you get 10x, 20x, 100x return so reliably?"
> *Source: mfm764, [26:18]*

At scale, Hormozi's framing shifts from "find a good candidate" to naming specific individuals: "Let's find John from [specific company]." These hires take 6-18 months of courting because A-level talent doesn't need your company -- they are giving you a company (impact, ~01:41:02).

The arbitrage is enormous:

> "The difference between good and the top 0.1% is probably still 25x. The arbitrage is that you don't typically have to pay the best guy 25 times more than the good guy. You can just pay him three times more and then all the increase in throughput goes to the company."
> *Source: impact, ~01:41:02*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is past product-market fit and asking "what's my job now?" The answer: become a collector of people. Also when someone is debating whether to pay 2x market rate for an A-player -- the math always works because the output delta is 25x, not 2x.

#### Grand Slam Offer for Employees [2/14]

Apply the same offer-design thinking from $100M Offers to recruiting. Most companies compete on compensation alone -- that is competing on price, which means their employer brand is commoditized.

> "What's the grand slam offer from an employee perspective rather than a grand slam offer from a product perspective? Because it's still the exact same process of acquiring talent as it is to acquire customers. It's just a reverse funnel."
> *Source: mfm764, [24:17]*

Hormozi's employer value proposition at Acquisition.com has five pillars:
1. **Growth** -- Fast revenue growth creates constant career advancement. "The faster you grow, the more talent you get."
2. **Meritocracy** -- Advancement based on performance, not tenure. A team member got promoted 90 days in.
3. **Guaranteed Demand** -- Unlike startups, demand is built-in. Joining ACQ is lower-risk.
4. **Mission** -- Many hires are long-time content consumers. The mission itself is the draw.
5. **Above-Market Compensation** -- Matching or exceeding market rates, but never leading with this alone.

His go-to recruiting question is not a pitch but a discovery:

> "My favorite question for these types of scenarios is asking, what would it take?"
> *Source: diary-ceo*

It assumes yes. Instead of crafting a pitch and hoping, you let the candidate tell you exactly what would close them.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is struggling to attract talent and thinks the problem is budget. It probably isn't. Ask them to describe their employer offer as if it were a product -- does it pass the Value Equation? Dream outcome, perceived likelihood, time delay, effort required?

#### "Clouds to Dirt" (Full-Stack Leaders) [2/14]

Hormozi's filter for truly exceptional leaders: they operate at every altitude of their function, from 30,000-foot strategy to ground-level execution.

> "Clouds to dirt. So vertically integrated full stack skill sets... I have yet to find truly exceptional people that aren't full stack."
> *Source: mfm764, [19:11]*

A sales leader who can build attribution models but can't cold-call is incomplete. A marketing leader who can write copy but can't architect a funnel is incomplete. The best people can go from clouds (strategy, architecture, vision) to dirt (making calls, writing scripts, running reports) without losing effectiveness at either level.

This is paired with the litmus test for true A-players:

> "The true AAA talent -- one, they're not employees. They are partners. And they see themselves as partners."
> *Source: mfm764, [18:11]*

The diagnostic: do you seek this person's advice on complex problems? If you're only directing them ("do this, then this"), they're an employee. If you're going to them because you're genuinely unsure what to do, they're a partner.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When evaluating senior hires. If the candidate can only speak in strategy or only in tactics, they are half-built. Also when someone says "I need a visionary" -- ask whether that visionary can also do the work.

#### 3Ds: Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate [3/14]

The systematic process for transferring any job from founder to hire. Most founders skip straight to delegation and wonder why the new hire fails. The 3Ds force you to build the system before you build the team.

> "First you have to document everything that you do... Then you demonstrate... Then they duplicate."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

1. **Document:** Write a step-by-step checklist of exactly what you do. Every step, every script, every tool. "If it is not documented, it cannot be transferred. Most founders skip this and wonder why hires fail" (leads). The act of documenting forces you to discover your own process -- you will realize you don't follow your own checklist when you try to demonstrate it.

2. **Demonstrate:** Do the documented process in front of the person being trained. Their questions and confusion reveal gaps in the documentation. Refine the checklist until a competent person can follow it without asking.

3. **Duplicate:** Have the employee execute while you watch. Only grant autonomy after they have proven they can replicate your results.

The 30-Stop Script Training method is the operational implementation:

> "In the first 30 minutes, we might get through a third of it. And I'm going to probably stop you like 30 times."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

Go sentence by sentence. Stop, correct, have them repeat correctly multiple times. Stack sentences. The person should be able to "sing it" by the end. This is the opposite of how 95% of organizations train -- letting them read through and giving notes at the end, which does not work.

The prerequisite is critical: the founder must master each method personally before hiring someone to do it. You cannot document what you have not done (leads).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user is about to make their first hire. Before they post a job listing, ask: "Have you documented what this person will do? Can you demonstrate it? Because if you hand someone a vague role description, they will fail, and you'll conclude that hiring doesn't work."

#### Firing Severity Matrix (Yellow/Orange/Red) [2/14]

A graduated framework that prevents both premature firing (wasting good people) and pathological patience (letting underperformers metastasize).

> "Yellow: they can still do their job but they're not doing it as well as they should and they're definitely not going to grow. Orange: they can't actually do their role right now. Red: they can't do the role right now and that thing is required for us to grow."
> *Source: mfm764, [28:19]*

Cross-reference severity with business criticality: Is this role the current constraint on growth? Red + constraint = immediate action. Yellow + non-constraint = coach, monitor, deprioritize. About half the time, people turn it around with clear coaching and specific feedback. The other half, they don't.

The emotional armor for making the hard call:

> "I just put the greater good as the frame -- my little spiritual armor -- of going into it knowing that this person might get upset... I owe it to the other zillion people who have put their careers, their lives, in my hands."
> *Source: mfm764, [29:19]*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When the user knows someone needs to go but is paralyzed by guilt or conflict avoidance. Categorize the severity, cross-reference with the constraint, and reframe the decision: keeping an underperformer is choosing one person's comfort over the entire organization's potential.

### Supporting Concepts

**The Leverage Stack (When to Hire):** Skills -> Systems -> People -> Capital. Build in strict order. "Capital without skills is gambling. People without systems is chaos. Systems without skills is mediocrity" (money-models). This answers "when should I hire?" -- after you have systems, before you deploy capital. If you hire before documenting your systems, you are paying someone to improvise.

**Outsource What You Suck At, Not What Makes Money:** "You want to outsource the things that you're bad at and probably aren't making money... Do you suck at it? Do you hate it, and then does it not generate revenue? Then it probably makes sense to outsource that first" (mw490). New entrepreneurs instinctively hire for sales or marketing because those tasks consume their time. Wrong move -- those are the revenue-generating activities you are good at. Hire for bookkeeping, support, fulfillment.

**The Barbell Hiring Strategy:** "I love lots of young, hungry people who are here for growth... and people at the back half of their career that are here that they've made enough money" (diary-ceo). People in the middle "tend to be the pain in the ass for everybody" -- the careerists obsessed with titles, promotion plans, tit-for-tat value exchange.

**The Unicorn Decomposition:** "You probably won't find a unicorn, but you can find a white horse. And you can find a rhinoceros to get the horn. And you can find some fireflies to give you the twinkles" (diary-ceo). Don't try to find one person who can replace you entirely. Break the role into component skills and hire three specialists.

**Barrels vs. Ammunition (Keith Rabois):** "Most people are ammunition... you need to find more barrels" (diary-ceo). A barrel can independently scope, execute, and ship a project end-to-end. "The square root of the number of people in a company generate 50% of the work." Adding ammunition without barrels doesn't increase output.

**C-Level Hires Must Bring Their Network:** "If somebody comes in as a C-level exec and has no network of people that they've worked with in the past, that's weird" (mfm764, [16:09]). Either they've never identified talent (bad leader) or their past talent doesn't want to work with them again (worse leader). Expect senior hires to build out their function by recruiting people they've worked with before.

**Identifying Expertise in Hiring (Three Signals):** Evaluate (1) quality and quantity of metrics they track, (2) behaviors they'll take to influence those metrics, (3) how those metrics and behaviors influence revenue. "If someone can't dial it into actions, then it's very unlikely that they're going to take them" (diary-ceo). The "explain it like a golden retriever" test: if a candidate uses jargon that confuses you, they either don't understand it or are obfuscating.

**The Swamp ($1-3M Revenue):** "At $1M with 20% margins, your entire $200K profit might be needed to hire one A player" (diary-ceo). This creates an impossible choice -- sacrifice all profit for talent, or work six extra hours a day yourself. Both paths work. Both are painful. This is why entrepreneurship commands outsized returns.

### Stories

**Sam's 600 Developer Interviews** [2/14]
> Sam, co-founder of Skool, was non-technical and needed a world-class CTO. He didn't post a job listing. He interviewed 600 developers. He asked each one who the best coders were. Then he talked to those people and asked them who the best coder they knew was. Eventually a name -- Daniel -- kept coming up as "God tier." Sam found a way in, recruited Daniel, who became co-founder and CTO of Skool (diary-ceo, mfm764).
>
> The 600-interview process also proved Hormozi's intelligence-as-rate-of-learning thesis: across hundreds of practice interviews, the signal was not who started strongest but whose trajectory was steepest. Rate of improvement between interviews predicted performance better than initial skill level.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone says they "can't find good people." Recruiting exceptional talent is a referral chain, not a job posting. Each conversation gets you closer to the person everyone else considers the best. Also illustrates that volume applies to hiring just as it applies to sales -- 600 conversations is not normal, and that's the point.
*Sources:* diary-ceo, mfm764

---

**The Director Everyone Called a Dick** [2/14]
> A director-level employee at Acquisition.com was highly skilled but universally disliked. Four executives had one-on-ones telling him to "stop being a dick." Nothing changed. He came to Hormozi's office worried about being fired. Hormozi's approach: "I don't care if you are a dick. I just care that everyone else thinks you are." He identified the specific behaviors: cutting people off in meetings, telling people he knew their department better than them. Then he gave specific replacement behaviors:
>
> "When you cut people off when they talk -- stop. Start: when someone else is talking, wait until they finish. If you're not sure, ask if they're done."
> *Source: diary-ceo*
>
> "The next week, people were like, what happened to him? He's like a new man. And the thing is, is that he wasn't a dick. He just didn't know how to behave" (diary-ceo, impact).

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is struggling to give feedback that actually changes behavior. "Don't be a dick" is an insult, not feedback. "When someone else is talking, wait until they finish" is feedback. The principle: observable behaviors, not vague labels. Four failed interventions with judgments; one successful intervention with specifics.
*Sources:* diary-ceo, impact

**The $60,000 Aunt** [1/14]
> A restoration company owner converted 55% of leads -- not calls, leads. His secret: his aunt, paid $60,000 per year, with one job. "The moment a lead comes in, you stop having sex with your husband, you stop cooking, you stop loving your child, and you immediately call the lead." She made 2-3 calls per day. That single hire brought in millions in revenue.
>
> "You're not going to pay for it out of the dollars you have now. You're going to pay for it out of the dollars that you're going to make."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is overthinking their first hire or can't "justify" the expense. The highest-leverage hire is often the simplest -- one person doing one thing with total commitment. The ROI is measured in future revenue, not current budget.
*Sources:* diary-ceo

**The Leverage Cascade** [1/14]
> Hormozi hired one director of people. That person then hired a director of brand, who organized an entire media team. The same director of people hired an internal director of marketing, who reached out to six agencies and selected the best one to run ads. All of this cascaded from a single hire.
>
> "It still starts with the first action, which is, if I hadn't hired that person, I would have had to move one level down on that level of leverage."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is overwhelmed trying to do everything themselves, or when they think they need to hire ten people. The first domino is not "hire a salesperson" or "hire a marketer" -- it is "hire the person who will build the team." The leverage is in the chain, not the individual hire.
*Sources:* ed-mylett

**The Cold Outbound Intervention** [1/14]
> When Hormozi started cold outbound for Gym Launch, his executive team -- after three to four months of watching him pour time into it with essentially zero sales -- staged an intervention. "They said you're putting all of your time and attention to this. We think this is a shiny object, using my words against me." But the team was "too far away to know the progress we were making." They just saw no sales. Hormozi knew the granular milestones: data lists, enrichment, pickup rates, hook scripts, offer scripts, show-up rates, follow-up sequences.
>
> "We have a hundred steps that we have to take and we're 33% of the way there. But if you're a beginner you don't know you're 33% of the way there."
> *Source: impact, ~00:10:50*

*Teaching application:* The Management Diamond in reverse. The executive team diagnosed a motivation problem ("shiny object!") when the actual issue was a knowledge problem -- they couldn't see the milestones because they weren't close enough to the work. Deploy when a leader wants to kill an initiative or fire someone: "Are we genuinely not making progress, or are we just measuring progress with too few milestones?"
*Sources:* impact
