# Module 18: Selecting a Market / Evaluating a Business Idea

This module covers the moment someone is choosing which market to enter, evaluating whether their niche is viable, or asking "is this a good market?" or "should I niche down?" Deploy it whenever the user's core question is about market selection -- not offer design (Module 3), not lead generation (Module 4), not focus (Module 2), but the upstream decision of which market to serve in the first place.

---

#### Four Indicators of a Great Market [5/14] [SIGNATURE]

The single most important framework in Hormozi's system for market selection. He is explicit about the hierarchy: the market you choose determines more than your offer, your sales skills, or your work ethic combined.

> "Starving Crowd (market) > Offer Strength > Persuasion Skills."
> *Source: offers, Section 2.9*

The four indicators, in order:

1. **Massive Pain** -- Not casual desire. Desperate, urgent need. The intensity of the pain directly determines the price ceiling:

> "The degree of the pain will be proportional to the price you will be able to charge."
> *Source: offers, Section 3.2*

2. **Purchasing Power** -- They must have money or access to it. A starving crowd that cannot pay is a charity case, not a market.

3. **Easy to Target** -- You need to be able to find and reach them efficiently:

> "I make my life easier by looking for easy-to-target markets. Examples of this are avatars that have associations they belong to, mailing lists, social media groups, channels they all watch."
> *Source: offers, Section 3.2*

4. **Growing Market** -- A rising tide forgives mistakes. Growth means you can be mediocre at execution and still succeed while you learn.

The framework is not theoretical. It is the diagnostic Hormozi applies before entering any market, and the reason he returns to it across every major source:

> "At the end of the day, if there is a ton of demand for a solution, you can be mediocre at business, have a terrible offer, and have no ability to persuade people, and you can still make money."
> *Source: money-models, Section 7, Quote 9*

**When to deploy in coaching:** Before any market entry decision. Before a pivot. When someone says "I don't know what market to serve." Run all four indicators as a checklist. If any of the four is missing -- particularly pain and purchasing power -- the market is suspect regardless of how exciting the idea feels.

#### The Hot Dog Stand Test [4/14] [SIGNATURE]

Hormozi's most visceral metaphor for why market hunger matters more than offer sophistication. A mediocre offer to a starving crowd beats a brilliant offer to an indifferent one.

> "If you're on the corner hocking hot dogs when the bars close up at 2am, with mobs of starving drunk folks, you're gonna sell out your hotdogs."
> *Source: offers, Section 4.1; money-models, Section 1d*

The hot dog vendor doesn't need a Grand Slam Offer. Doesn't need NLP sales techniques. Doesn't need a funnel. The starving crowd does all the work. This is Hormozi's litmus test for whether someone has a market problem or an offer problem -- and the answer is almost always market.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone has an elaborate offer but no customers. When someone is optimizing their funnel, their copy, their sales script -- but hasn't validated that the market is hungry. Ask: "Are you selling hot dogs at 2AM to drunk people, or are you selling gourmet meals at 11AM to people who already ate?" If the crowd isn't starving, no amount of offer engineering fixes it.

#### "When Clueless Operators All Make Money, That's the Signal" [3/14]

The market heat signal. When unskilled, unsophisticated operators are all making money in an industry despite poor marketing, terrible pricing, and weak operations, that is the strongest possible indicator of massive supply-demand imbalance.

> "When I see a lot of people who don't know what they're doing, all making money, I'm like, 'Okay, there's something there that's good.'"
> *Source: mfm732, [42:30]; money-models, Section 3*

This is Hormozi's primary market selection heuristic applied to his own decisions. When asked what industry he'd choose if starting from zero, he pointed to "pseudo-medical" services -- med spas, teeth whitening, laser skin, laser hair removal -- specifically because the incompetent success rate was high (mfm732, [39:30-41:30]).

The inverse is equally diagnostic: if skilled operators are struggling, the market is saturated or dying. Don't optimize tactics in a dead market.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When evaluating a new market opportunity. Instead of asking "is this a good idea?", ask: "How are the worst operators in this space doing?" If idiots are printing money, there's alpha. If smart people are grinding for scraps, run.

#### Niche Slapping [4/14] [EMPHATIC]

Hormozi's term for entrepreneurs who hop from market to market, never committing long enough to find a winning offer. This is delivered with genuine frustration -- one of the few topics where his emotional register shifts upward.

> "If you keep hopping from niche to niche, hoping that the market will solve your problems, you deserve to be niche slapped."
> *Source: offers, Section 4.8*

The grass is never greener:

> "All businesses and all markets have unpleasant characteristics. The grass is never greener once you get to the other side."
> *Source: offers, Section 2.10*

In Diary of a CEO, he makes it even more direct:

> "Don't make me niche slap you."
> *Source: diary-ceo, Section 4.11*

The underlying logic: running multiple businesses simultaneously is "an exercise in arrogance" -- assuming a third of your time can beat a competitor giving 100% (diary-ceo). In Impact Theory, the Shot Glass Analogy makes this visual:

> "Imagine you've got one cup of water and you need to overflow another cup. Let's line up seven shot glasses because millionaires have seven streams of income apparently. Now we're going to pour that water into all of those glasses and then hope that it somehow overflows. But it's not going to."
> *Source: impact, ~01:32:45*

The conclusion is stated as near-universal:

> "You can make any of them work, but none of them will work unless you focus on one of them."
> *Source: impact, ~01:32:45*

And for emphasis, the Zuckerberg Test:

> "Zuckerberg didn't have a side hustle."
> *Source: impact, ~00:53:20*

The problem is almost never the market -- it's the offer, the execution, or the patience. Most people quit a market too early, not too late (offers, Section 2.10).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone wants to pivot after three months. When someone blames the market for poor results. When someone is running 2-3 businesses simultaneously and wondering why none of them are working. When someone says "I tried that niche and it didn't work" -- probe how long they tried and how many iterations they ran before concluding it was the market's fault.

#### Channel Demand, Don't Create It [3/14]

A principle that directly constrains market selection. You are not inventing desires. People already want to lose weight, make money, fix their health, find love. Your job is to present your offer as the obvious vehicle for a desire that already exists.

> "We are not trying to create demand. We are trying to channel it."
> *Source: offers, Section 2.3*

This means: stop trying to convince people they have a problem. Find people who already know they have one. If you have to "educate the market" about why they need your product, you've chosen the wrong market -- or at minimum, you're playing on hard mode.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is trying to "create a new category" or "educate the market." When someone's core challenge is making prospects understand why they need the solution. The Hormozi reframe: if they don't already feel the pain, move on. Find the people who wake up at 3AM thinking about this problem.

#### Technology Democratizes Consumption, Consolidates Production [2/14]

A structural observation about market dynamics in the internet age. Technology allows the best in the world to serve everybody, replacing thousands of mediocre operators.

> "Technology democratizes consumption and consolidates production... which means if you're the best in the world, you get to do it for everybody."
> *Source: mfm732, [1:06:44]*

Miss Rachel has 34 million preschool students on YouTube. Khan Academy replaced thousands of math tutors. The best piano teacher in the world is now available to everyone with a phone. This is both an opportunity and an existential threat: if you're average, you will be replaced by the best. If you're the best, you capture the entire market.

Applied to content strategy specifically, this means niche content with low view counts can be extraordinarily valuable if it reaches the right market. A piano repair guy with 1,000 views may be reaching nearly 100% of his addressable market (youtube26).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When evaluating whether a market can support a business long-term. When someone is worried about competition -- are they competing against local mediocrity (opportunity) or against the internet's best (existential risk)? When someone is choosing between a niche they can dominate and a broad market where they'll be average.

#### Passion for Excellence, Not Passion for the Product [2/14]

A direct rebuttal of the "follow your passion" narrative in market selection.

> "Are you passionate about cookies?... He said no. But he looked at the market and what he was passionate about was being excellent, and he did amazingly well."
> *Source: impact, ~01:35:11*

Hormozi's friend was in fitness, saw the cookie store market opportunity, and applied his passion for excellence to a domain he had zero emotional attachment to. The lesson: passion for a specific product is overrated. Passion for excellence is portable across industries. When choosing a market, evaluate the market fundamentals (Four Indicators), not whether the product excites you.

> "You can be passionate about ideals and translate that to anything."
> *Source: impact, ~01:35:11*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone says "but I'm not passionate about [the business opportunity]." When someone is choosing between a market they love with bad fundamentals and a market they're neutral about with great fundamentals. Redirect from product passion to excellence passion.

### Stories

**The Starting-Over Playbook** [1/14]
> Asked "how long to get to a million dollars if you started from zero?", Hormozi answers: "A year." He's not speculating -- "having lost everything twice," this is his tested playbook:
>
> (1) Find a local service business with excess delivery capacity. Look for businesses that undercharge. (2) Ask the owner: "If I send you 100 customers, how cheaply would you deliver?" (3) Get agreement on their minimum delivery cost. (4) Sell that service at a massive markup based on your sales ability. (5) Keep the spread.
>
> His preferred industry: "Pseudo-medical" -- med spas, teeth whitening, laser skin, laser hair removal. Why? Because clueless operators are already making money (massive demand-supply imbalance), the services command premium pricing (pain + purchasing power), and the customer base is easy to target.
>
> In the gym business specifically, the terms were even better: "I get to keep the $500. After six weeks, you convert them. The memberships are all yours. Zero CAC for you. I take all the risk." He did about $100K every 21 days with this model.
> *Source: mfm732, [39:30-41:30]*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone at zero asks "what business should I start?" The playbook is deliberately market-first -- choose the market by fundamentals (Four Indicators + Clueless Operators signal), then leverage existing delivery capacity so you don't need capital or a product. The user only needs one thing: the ability to sell.

**The Cookie Store Friend** [1/14]
> A friend of Hormozi's was in the fitness industry. He started a cookie store in California. Hormozi asked him: "Are you passionate about cookies?" He said no. "But he looked at the market and what he was passionate about was being excellent, and he did amazingly well."
> *Source: impact, ~01:35:11*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is stuck choosing between passion and fundamentals. The friend evaluated the market (cookies in California: massive demand, easy-to-target customers, growing). He brought his portable skill -- excellence and operational discipline -- to a domain he had no emotional attachment to. The result validated the market-first approach. Passion follows results, not the other way around.

---

**The Shot Glass Analogy** [2/14]
> "Imagine you've got one cup of water and you need to overflow another cup. Let's line up seven shot glasses because millionaires have seven streams of income apparently. Now we're going to pour that water into all of those glasses and then hope that it somehow overflows. But it's not going to."
>
> The visual is deliberate: limited resources (one cup of water) spread across multiple opportunities (seven shot glasses) guarantee failure in all of them. "You can make any of them work, but none of them will work unless you focus on one of them."
> *Source: impact, ~01:32:45*

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is evaluating multiple markets simultaneously, hedging their bets across niches, or running parallel experiments to "see which one sticks." The shot glass analogy makes the math visceral: dividing your resources by seven doesn't give you seven chances at success. It gives you seven guaranteed failures. Pick one, overflow it, then add.

**The Gym Business as Market Selection Case Study** [3/14]
> Hormozi's entire career arc is a market selection case study. He chose the gym industry not because he loved fitness (though he did) but because it had the Four Indicators: massive pain (gym owners are drowning), purchasing power (gym owners are business owners with revenue), easy to target (gym owner associations, Facebook groups, industry events), and growing (the fitness industry was expanding). He then engineered the money model: a $500 "win your money back" challenge upfront, $200 supplements at 80% margin 48 hours later, year-long membership conversion at week 3, and prepaid annual upsell at weeks 3-6. Result: ~$1,000 per customer in 30 days vs. the industry's $99. This 20:1 front-end ratio let him spend $5,000 on ads and make $100,000 back -- enough to "literally paint the walls, put the lobby in, buy the equipment" for a new gym location, cash-flow positive from day one.
> *Source: mfm732, [2:03-6:04]; offers; money-models*

*Teaching application:* Deploy as the comprehensive worked example of market-first thinking. Hormozi chose the market, then engineered the offer to exploit the market's characteristics. The offer was brilliant -- but only because it sat on top of a market with all four indicators firing. In a dead market, the same offer sequence would have produced nothing.
