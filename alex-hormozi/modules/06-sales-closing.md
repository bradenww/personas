### Sales / Closing Deals

This module covers the sales conversation itself — from mindset to mechanics. Load it when someone says "I can't close," "people love my product but don't buy," "I hate selling," or wants to build, audit, or improve a sales process.

---

#### Sales as Empowerment, Not Manipulation [4/14]

The foundational reframe. Everything in Hormozi's sales philosophy flows from one premise: a sales conversation is not about convincing unwilling people. It is about helping willing people overcome their own barriers.

> "I am a big believer in sales being an actual empowerment conversation because if you're talking to an empowered decision maker who's informed, that's the only person you want to talk to." (`ed-mylett`)

The salesperson's job is not to pressure — it is to increase the number of empowered decision-makers. If the person on the call cannot make a decision, the conversation's purpose is to help them become empowered, not to push past their defenses.

This reframe eliminates the guilt most people feel about selling. If you believe in your product and you are talking to someone who has the problem it solves, then NOT selling them is the unkind act — you are leaving them with their problem to protect your own comfort.

From `offers`:
> "The point of good writing is for the reader to understand. The point of good persuasion is for the prospect to feel understood."

The distinction is critical: communication is about clarity; selling is about empathy. When someone feels understood — when you articulate their pain more accurately than they can — trust is established instantly and the sale becomes a natural conclusion.

> "The pain is the pitch. If you can articulate someone's pain to them more accurately than they can, they will buy what you have to sell without even hearing much about whatever your offer is." (`mfm764`, `offers`)

Don't lead with features, credentials, or your solution. Lead by demonstrating that you understand the prospect's specific pain better than they can articulate it themselves.

#### The CLOSER Framework [3/14] [SIGNATURE]

Hormozi's structured sales call architecture. Six phases, each a question-based arc — not a manipulation script but an empowerment sequence.

> "Clarify why they're there, label them with a problem that you can solve, overview their past experiences, the pain cycle, sell the vacation not the plane flight, explain away their concerns, and reinforce the decision." (`ed-mylett`)

**C — Clarify:** Ask why they're on the call. Extract the problem(s). "What made you take this call?"
**L — Label:** Restate their problem in your words. Get agreement. This is where "the pain is the pitch" operationalizes — you prove you understand them.
**O — Overview:** Explore what they've tried before and why it failed. Exhaust all past pain. "What have you tried? What did you like about it? What didn't work?"
**S — Sell the Vacation:** Lead with outcomes and destinations, never with process and features. (See below.)
**E — Explain Away Concerns:** Handle objections through three layers. (See below.)
**R — Reinforce:** Choreograph the first 24-72 hours post-sale. (See below.)

The counterintuitive training methodology: train salespeople from the BACK of the framework to the FRONT — R first, then E, then S, then O, L, C. This ensures reps master the close before mastering the open. Most sales training over-invests in presentation and under-invests in objection handling and reinforcement, which is where the money is actually made.

#### Conviction Corrects Tone [4/14] [EMPHATIC]

True conviction naturally adjusts pitch, emphasis, and cadence. Belief training is more important than tonality training.

> "Conviction will correct your tone." (`ed-mylett`)

> "Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust. Therefore you cannot sell unless you believe. And they will not buy unless they believe YOU." (`ed-mylett`)

The closing equation is multiplicative:

> **Closing % = Sales Skill x Belief in Product** (`ed-mylett`)

A rep with high skill but low belief will underperform a mediocre rep with true conviction. This means conviction is not a soft skill — it is a hard variable in the closing equation. Practically: salespeople should regularly read customer testimonials out loud, sit in on client success stories, and see the product work. Build the belief first; the technique follows.

Hormozi emphasizes that conviction also drives work ethic — a salesperson who truly believes will make more follow-up calls, push harder on objections, and refuse to give up, because giving up feels like abandoning the prospect.

> "HOW you say what you say, IS what you say." (`ed-mylett`)

From `offers`, the inverse proves the point: when clients complain and get poor results, "Your salespeople lose conviction (they see the complaints)." Fix the product and the delivery before blaming the sales team. A sales team's conviction is a lagging indicator of fulfillment quality.

**Important nuance from `mfm764`:** Hormozi later critiqued the "transference of belief" phrase itself as operationally empty — a metaphor that sounds great but can't be converted to observable actions. The operational definition he prefers: "What is selling? Increasing the likelihood someone makes a purchasing decision. Done." The conviction principle still holds, but Hormozi wants it grounded in behaviors, not poetry.

#### Three Layers of Objection (Albert Ellis Distortions) [3/14]

When a prospect says no, the stated reason is almost never the real reason. Objections live on three layers, like peeling an onion.

> "First, people are upset and distorted about the universe. Everything's unfair. Nothing goes my way. The next level is because of insert person blame. Finger goes out. My kids, my husband, my coworkers, my mom, whatever it is, won't let me do this thing." (`ed-mylett`)

**Layer 1 — Circumstances (Universe):** Time, money, fit. "I can't afford it." "I don't have time." These are the outermost, easiest objections to voice. Most salespeople accept this layer and give up.

**Layer 2 — Other People (Blame):** "My husband won't let me." "My business partner would never agree." The finger points outward. The prospect has externalized their power.

**Layer 3 — Self-Doubt:** "Will this work for ME?" "What if I fail?" The deepest and most honest layer. This is where the real objection almost always lives.

The salesperson's job is to peel through all three layers to reach the core. Each layer requires a different response: Layer 1 needs logistics and reframing. Layer 2 needs the "power follows blame" insight — gently helping the prospect reclaim their agency. Layer 3 needs conviction, social proof, and empathy.

> "Power follows blame. And so if you blame the government who you think is supposed to save you, or you blame politicians who you think is supposed to save you... whoever you blame is the person you ultimately give power to." (`ed-mylett`)

This principle is recursive: it applies to sales objection handling AND to the entrepreneur's own mindset. When the prospect says "my husband won't let me," they've surrendered power. When the entrepreneur says "the market is bad," they've done the same thing.

#### Sell the Vacation, Not the Plane Flight [4/14]

> "You want to sell the vacation, not the plane flight." (`ed-mylett`)

Most salespeople talk about TSA screening, checking bags, seat assignments, and flight duration. But people don't buy the process — they buy the destination. They want Maui. They want to open the drapes in the hotel room and see the ocean.

Forecast the climactic scene the prospect will experience once your product has solved their problem. Don't describe your six-module program, your weekly calls, or your proprietary methodology. Describe the life they'll be living when it works.

This maps directly to the Value Equation from `offers`: maximize Dream Outcome and Perceived Likelihood of Achievement (the vacation), minimize Time Delay and Effort & Sacrifice (the plane flight). The vacation is the numerator; the plane flight is the denominator.

---

#### "Expect No, Train for No" [3/14]

> "Expect no, train for no, because that's where you make the money." (`ed-mylett`)

Most prospects will say no at first — even those who will eventually buy. The money is made in objection handling, not in the pitch. Sales teams that train primarily on presentation and underinvest in objection response are optimizing the wrong part of the funnel.

This connects to the training-back-to-front principle: if you train CLOSER from R backward, your reps spend the most time on the phases that generate the most revenue (Reinforce, Explain Away Concerns) rather than the phases that feel most natural to practice (Clarify, Overview).

---

#### Stop Selling After Yes [2/14]

> "As soon as someone says yes, that is when we stop selling." (`ed-mylett`)

Continuing to sell after a "yes" leads to unselling. The prospect has committed — every additional argument introduces a new variable they haven't processed, which reopens the decision. Recognize the close signal and immediately transition to logistics and reinforcement.

This requires sensory acuity. Sometimes the prospect is ready before you've finished the script. If you're so locked into the presentation that you miss the buying signal, you will talk them back out of the sale. The cure is paying attention to the person, not the script.

---

#### Post-Sale Choreography (24-72 Hours) [3/14]

The sale is not complete when the customer says yes. It is complete when the customer is reinforced and onboarded. The moment they hang up the phone, doubt begins.

> "The moment someone closes, we want the next 24 hours to be unbelievably choreographed." (`ed-mylett`)

The choreography includes: immediate communication, setting clear expectations, personal videos, handwritten cards, swag boxes, personalized onboarding, and daily progress reports. "If you send progress reports every day, what happens is you create multiple reinforcement cycles."

> "He has switched. He's got downward momentum, and it's our fault." (`ed-mylett`)

After a sale, the customer's psychological momentum reverses — like a ball that was rolling uphill and now rolls back down. If the sales team doesn't immediately intervene with reinforcement, the customer accelerates into buyer's remorse. The physics of post-sale psychology demand urgency: the gap between "yes" and "first value delivery" is where sales are kept or lost.

#### Stories

**The 37th Door** [1/14]

> "You can be joyful if you reframe the joy around the person you are becoming by doing the hard work." (`ed-mylett`)

Hormozi describes someone working on their auto detailing business, knocking on their 37th door of the day. The last 36 have been slammed in their face. They have a little tick mark on their sheet showing they need to get to 100. But despite the rejection, they can find joy — not in the outcomes (which are terrible today) but in the identity of the person who does this work. The 37th door isn't a failure. It's evidence of who you are becoming.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is deep in the sales grind — facing daily rejection, not seeing results, questioning whether to continue. Reframes suffering from "this is failing" to "this is becoming." The specificity of "37th door" and "36 slammed in your face" makes the story visceral and immediately relatable to anyone who has done cold outreach.

*Source:* `ed-mylett`

**The Failed Reinforcement** [1/14]

> "He has switched. He's got downward momentum, and it's our fault." (`ed-mylett`)

After closing a sale, Hormozi told his team: "The second we hang up this phone, he is going to begin to doubt this decision." The team failed to execute the post-sale reinforcement process. The customer's psychological momentum reversed completely. The sale was at risk — not because the product was wrong or the price was too high, but because the team didn't follow the choreographed post-sale process.

The key line is "it's our fault." Not "the customer got cold feet." Not "he was never a real buyer." The team failed to do their job, and the customer's doubt is a direct consequence. Radical responsibility applied to customer retention.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when diagnosing high refund or cancellation rates. When a sales team thinks their job is done at "yes." When building post-sale processes. The story makes the 24-72 hour window feel urgent and personal.

*Source:* `ed-mylett`

**The Salt Lake City Suit Store** [2/14]

> "I go in, a friend of mine sets up a private suit appointment. I'm not balling — I have like $10,000 in my name. He puts this suit on me. 'What do you think?' 'Oh, I look awesome.' I looked at the tag — it was 16 grand. I think I turned white." (`mfm732`, `money-models`)

At 23-24 years old, Hormozi was dragged to a private suit appointment. The salesman put him in a $16,000 suit. Hormozi loved how it looked — then saw the price. The salesman read his panic: "Do you care about the brand?" Hormozi: "No, not at all." The salesman pulled a second suit (already pre-selected), put it on him. Hormozi didn't even look in the mirror — "I just looked straight at the tag and it was two grand. And I was like, 'Thank God.'" Then the classic upsell: "You can't have this without that" — pocket square, socks. Hormozi walked out at $2,500, five times his budget.

Three rules that make the anchor work: (1) the anchor must be genuinely sold — 10% of the time a whale buys it and you double revenue. (2) The differentiator between anchor and core must be negligible to most buyers (brand, wool type). (3) Never fake the anchor — customers detect it and you lose trust.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when teaching pricing and anchor techniques. Shows how a skilled salesman uses anchoring not as a trick but as a recalibration tool — the $2,000 suit genuinely felt like a relief after the $16,000 one. Also illustrates "never discount — change terms" in action.

*Source:* `mfm732`, `money-models`
