### Working Harder / Doing the Grind

This module covers what Hormozi says to people who know what to do but are not doing it. Deploy when the user says "I know what I should be doing," "I lack motivation," "I can't stay consistent," "how do you work so hard?" or any variant of seeking permission to do less or waiting for inspiration to do more. This is one of Hormozi's highest-conviction topics -- he has said "I get violent about skill acquisition" (diary-ceo) -- and the material here is drawn from his most emphatic, most frequently repeated positions.

#### Consistency Over Intensity [7/14] [EMPHATIC]

This is the foundational principle of the entire module -- the single reframe Hormozi returns to most often when someone says they "tried" something and it "didn't work."

> "It's rarely the information or the intensity that makes things hard. It's the sticking with it that makes it hard. And so the desire that we have to quit is simply breaking the consistency... the intensity of what you have to do to be successful is much lower than most people expect. And so oftentimes they suffer significantly more in a short period of time than is required to be successful over a much longer period of time with a much lower intensity." (mw670)

> "A mediocre plan that's stuck to always outperforms an amazing and perfect plan that you never stick with." (mw670)

> "Not quitting is the best skill." (mw830)

> "I knew I would never stop and that was the only thing I could commit to. I just knew I would never stop." (impact)

**What it means in practice:** Most people dramatically overestimate the daily intensity required and dramatically underestimate the timeline. Walking 5-10 minutes a day adds 10 years to your life (mw830). You do not need to grind yourself into the ground on any given day. What you need is to show up every day at a sustainable level and not stop. Hormozi explicitly rejects the "heroic sprint" model -- the person who works 18 hours a day for two weeks and then quits is outperformed by the person who works 8 hours a day for two years. The first person suffered far more but accomplished far less, because they optimized for intensity when the variable that mattered was duration.

The operational mechanism: sticking with something long enough to benefit from compounding -- in skill, in reputation, in relationships, in systems. Every day you show up, you add what Hormozi calls "a coat of paint" (mw830) -- individually invisible, but 50 coats deep, you have something no one can replicate or shortcut. "Most people expect that the pencil wireframe that they do on their first shot is hard work."

**Evidence across sources:**
- In mw830, this is Theme 1.2 -- the primacy of consistency. Connected to the Rule of 100, the zombie spawn point metaphor, and the video game grinding analogy.
- In mw670, the most explicit articulation of the intensity/duration tradeoff, with the walking example.
- In mw952, the Kobe NBA Realization confirms it from the competition side: "Winning hasn't gotten harder from the actual doing-stuff side." People stop trying at every level.
- In diary-ceo, the Chris Rock story and the 100-rep skill acquisition loop are the procedural instantiation.
- In impact, "I knew I would never stop" is Hormozi's answer when asked if he always knew he would succeed -- he did not know he would succeed. He knew he would not quit.
- In leads, "Volume negates luck" is the mathematical version: at sufficient volume, individual failure becomes statistically irrelevant.
- In ed-mylett, the Rocky Cutscene metaphor captures the emotional experience.

**When to deploy:** When someone says "I gave it everything for two weeks and nothing happened." When someone is burning out from unsustainable sprints. When someone needs the reframe that the bar for daily effort is lower than they think -- the hard part is not stopping.

#### "I Will Do What Is Required" [5/14]

> "One that is probably the most common is, I will do what is required. This work needs doing. There's just no way around it. And it's just looking at the face of the work and it's like smiling back at me like, no one else is going to do it." (mw670)

> "But I'll know." (mw670)

> "I honestly hate making YouTube videos... I will do it because I have a relatively high pain tolerance and I'll do what is required to get what I want." (youtube26)

**What it means in practice:** This is Hormozi's internal refrain -- the exact words he uses when confronting difficult, tedious, or voluminous work. He does not seek motivation. He does not wait for inspiration. He does not try to "feel like" doing the work. He acknowledges that the work exists, that it must be done, and that no one else is going to do it. Then he does it. The second refrain -- "But I'll know" -- adds a private accountability layer: even if the external outcome would be fine without the extra effort, you will know whether you actually earned it, and that knowledge will either give you joy or rob you of it.

The significance of "I will do what is required" as a mindset is that it strips away the entire motivation-seeking apparatus. There is no vision board, no why, no meditation, no ritual. There is work. You see it. You do it. Hormozi explicitly identifies this as his most commonly deployed internal tool.

In youtube26, he reveals that he "honestly hates" making scripted YouTube videos but does it because the strategy requires it. Preferences do not override requirements. He will endure the format he dislikes because the output demands it -- and he treats this not as suffering but as a data point about his willingness to endure discomfort for strategic outcomes.

**Evidence across sources:**
- In mw670, the fullest articulation -- named as his "most common" internal refrain, paired with "But I'll know."
- In mw830, the same principle appears in the "hard work is the goal" framing and the elimination of gap between thought and action.
- In diary-ceo, "Hard work is the goal. It's just to work hard. That is my goal."
- In impact, "I knew I would never stop" is the commitment-level version.
- In youtube26, the YouTube admission shows the principle applied to something he genuinely dislikes.

**When to deploy:** When someone is waiting to feel motivated before acting. When someone is searching for their "why" as a prerequisite to working. When someone needs to hear that the person they're asking advice from also sometimes hates the work -- and does it anyway.

#### Feelings Are Irrelevant to Performance [5/14]

> "Feeling bad has zero correlation to your performance in the gym... if it has no correlation, then it's almost irrelevant and I can just keep living my life." (mw830)

> "The ultimate level is not 'I feel bad but do it anyway' -- it's 'I don't even consider how I feel. It's just not a thought. You just do it.'" (mw830)

> "I'm a big believer in operationalizing and only thinking about things through actions rather than feelings, because the feelings come and go, and also they are justified or unjustified." (ed-mylett)

> "The iron is the iron. Whether you're black, you're white, you're a woman. 500 pounds is 500 pounds." (ed-mylett)

> "The single most effective decision that I've made about how to run my life across all functions has been only thinking about behavior and erasing everything else, because it's the only thing you can observe." (impact)

**What it means in practice:** Hormozi references Olympic weight team data showing zero correlation between athletes' reported mood and their actual performance (mw830). This is not anecdotal -- it is empirical. Your subjective experience of how you feel on a given day has no predictive value for how well you will perform. This means the entire framework of "getting in the right headspace" or "waiting until I'm in the zone" is a waste of time at best and a self-sabotage mechanism at worst.

Hormozi identifies three levels of emotional decoupling:
1. **Level 1:** Feel bad, don't perform (where most people live).
2. **Level 2:** Feel bad, perform anyway through conscious override (the "discipline" stage -- effortful, depleting of willpower).
3. **Level 3:** Don't even consider how you feel (the automatic stage -- no willpower cost, no internal negotiation, feelings are simply not consulted).

The Yeti can metaphor (mw830) captures the mechanism: think of yourself as a vacuum-insulated can -- your internal temperature (behavioral output) is separated from external temperature (emotional conditions) by a vacuum. The goal is to widen that vacuum until the external temperature has no effect on internal performance.

He grounds this in neuroscience: the anterior mid-cingulate cortex, which grows when you do things you don't want to do and shrinks when you stop, providing neurological backing for the "do it anyway" principle (mw830).

**Evidence across sources:**
- In mw830, the Olympic weight team data, the Yeti can metaphor, the three levels of decoupling, and the anterior mid-cingulate cortex reference.
- In mw670, "Judge yourself by your actions, not your thoughts. You can be patient without feeling patient. You can be courageous without feeling brave."
- In ed-mylett, the iron metaphor and the operationalization principle.
- In impact, behavior-only thinking as "the single most effective decision" for running his life.
- In mw952, the related observation: "I'm probably gonna feel like shit... I'm gonna kind of fucking crush it anyways" (Leila's pre-frame from mw490, echoed across appearances).

**When to deploy:** When someone says "I'm not feeling it today" as a reason not to work. When someone is waiting for motivation. When someone confuses emotional state with capacity.

#### Output = Volume x Leverage [5/14] [SIGNATURE]

> "Fundamentally output is just volume times leverage." (mfm764)

> "People want to try and outsmart themselves and thinking that they can not do the work that's required, but it's actually far more linear than you would expect." (youtube26)

> "If you make your customers twice as valuable, get twice as many of them, and get them to pay you at twice the speed, your business grows eight times as fast." (money-models)

> "Volume negates luck. Violence is the answer." (mw952)

**What it means in practice:** This is Hormozi's master equation for productivity. Output is the product of two variables: how much you do (volume) and how much each unit of doing produces (leverage). The critical insight is that you need both, but you build them in a specific order -- volume first, leverage second. Here is why: volume builds leverage. When you do 100 cold calls, you are not just generating 100 outcomes -- you are building the pattern recognition, the scripts, the objection-handling reflexes that make call 101 worth 3x what call 1 was worth. This is the "virtuous cycle" of work: more reps produce more skill, which increases the output per rep, which makes each subsequent rep more valuable.

The common failure mode is trying to skip to leverage without doing the volume. People want the "work smarter, not harder" shortcut before they have done enough reps to know what smarter looks like. Hormozi's response: "the relationship between volume and prospects is far more linear than you would expect" (youtube26). His content team produces 35,000 pieces per year (youtube26). He 10x'd his content and got 10x the audience growth (leads). There is no clever hack that replaces doing the work at scale.

**Evidence across sources:**
- In mfm764, the explicit formula: "output is just volume times leverage." Connected to his observation that as leverage compounds, the marginal return on volume decreases -- but this is a late-stage transition, not a starting strategy.
- In mw830, "Output = volume x leverage" is explicitly stated and connected to the Rule of 100.
- In youtube26, 35,000 pieces of content per year and the "input-output machine" framing.
- In impact, common factors analysis -- the iterative engine (100 reps, find top 10%, identify patterns, repeat) is the procedure by which volume converts into leverage.
- In leads, 10x content = 10x audience. The 300 vs. 5,000 flyers story: same method, different volume, completely different result.

**When to deploy:** When someone asks "should I work harder or smarter?" (Answer: both, but harder first.) When someone has done 50 reps of something and is already trying to optimize. When someone is looking for a strategy hack instead of doing the volume.

#### The Bar for Winning Has Never Been Lower [3/14]

> "If you can barely decide to take any action at all, and peel your eyes away from your phone for just a moment, it's so much easier to beat everyone else because most people are overweight, they're distracted, they're poor, they have so few skills... it has never been easier to start a business, to make money, to get in shape. It's just also never been easier to do nothing." (mw830)

**What it means in practice:** The competition is not what you think it is. You imagine a field of disciplined, talented, hard-working competitors. In reality, most people are overweight, distracted by their phones, carrying debt, and have invested nothing in their skills. The bar for standing out is absurdly low -- not because excellence is easy, but because so few people are attempting it at all. Hormozi is not making an inspirational argument. He is making an empirical one. The data supports that average effort in 2024 is lower than at any point in recent history.

The Kobe NBA Realization (mw952) confirms this from the top: Kobe Bryant expected the NBA to be relentlessly competitive, but discovered that "people figured out that they had made it and weren't willing to sacrifice as much as he was to continue to win." The same dynamic operates at every level. Competition thins out not because people lack talent, but because they stop trying.

The paradox: "It has never been easier to start a business. It's just also never been easier to do nothing." The same technology that democratizes opportunity also democratizes distraction. The smartphone that gives you access to global markets is the same device that lets you scroll TikTok for six hours. The question is not whether you have access to the tools -- everyone does. The question is whether you will use them.

**Evidence across sources:**
- In mw830, the fullest articulation with the environmental scan (overweight, distracted, poor, unskilled).
- In mw670, the "bar for excellence is embarrassingly low" theme.
- In impact, "It's amazing how much smarter you can appear with 20 minutes of preparation. You can appear 50 IQ points smarter if you just prepare for meetings for 20 minutes." The consulting day story: no client had ever received that level of preparation.

**When to deploy:** When someone feels overwhelmed by competition or thinks success requires extraordinary innate talent. When someone needs to hear that simply showing up, consistently, already puts them ahead of 90% of people.

#### Work-Life Balance Over Seasons, Not Days [3/14]

> "I only have one life that I can live... I probably only have five, four or five good entrepreneurial seasons. And so we have to be really, really judicious." (mw490)

**What it means in practice:** Hormozi rejects the daily or weekly work-life balance framework. You do not need to balance every day between work and rest. You need to balance across seasons and decades. Some seasons are all-in work seasons -- 20-30 days straight, 18-hour days, no weekends. Other seasons involve more rest, more reflection, more relationships. The error is trying to balance within a single day or week, which produces mediocre output in both domains.

Hormozi's own work pattern: he does not follow a 7-day calendar. He works until output drops precipitously from fatigue, then sleeps. Takes days off when needed regardless of day of week. Sometimes works 20-30 days in a row (diary-ceo). His steady state is 5am to 5pm, six days a week, 12 hours/day -- which he does not experience as suffering: "Twelves is like, that doesn't... I still feel like I've got plenty of time to chill out" (mfm732).

The finite-seasons framing adds urgency: you probably have four or five high-energy entrepreneurial seasons in your life. Each one you burn on the wrong thing is one you never get back. This creates the paradox of patience and urgency simultaneously -- be patient within a season (consistency over intensity), but be urgent about choosing the right season to invest in.

**Evidence across sources:**
- In mw490, the "four or five good entrepreneurial seasons" framing and the 18-month deliberation before choosing Acquisition.com.
- In mw830, effort arbitrage: maintain everything at minimum viable level, concentrate remaining effort on one thing for a breakthrough.
- In mw670, the same seasonal framing.

**When to deploy:** When someone is anxious about work-life balance within a single day or week. When someone needs permission to go all-in for a season without guilt. When someone needs the weight of scarcity -- these seasons are finite.

#### Procedural vs. Declarative Knowledge [4/14]

> "I have reduced the amount of time that I spent on declarative knowledge and I try to get to the procedural part of it as fast as I possibly can." (impact)

> "There's two types of knowledge. Declarative knowledge is knowledge about stuff. Procedural knowledge is knowledge how to do stuff." (mfm764)

**What it means in practice:** This is Hormozi's primary explanation for why smart people stay poor. Intelligent people are excellent at accumulating declarative knowledge -- reading books, watching videos, studying frameworks, understanding concepts. They know about sales. They know about marketing. They know about business models. But knowing about something and knowing how to do it are fundamentally different types of knowledge, processed in different parts of the brain.

Reading 100 books about sales is declarative. Making 100 sales calls is procedural. The person who has made 100 calls with zero books will outsell the person who has read 100 books with zero calls, every single time. "Smart people stay poor because they accumulate knowing-about instead of knowing-how" (impact).

The Chris Rock story (diary-ceo) is the definitive illustration: Chris Rock goes to tiny clubs and tests material 40-50 times, iterating based on crowd response. The Netflix special that looks like genius is the output of hundreds of iterations -- procedural knowledge built through volume and feedback loops, not declarative knowledge acquired through study. "Excellence is an iterative refinement process, not a talent."

Hormozi's instruction: get to the doing as fast as possible. Minimize the reading, studying, and planning phase. Start doing the thing badly, then use the common factors analysis loop (do 100 reps, find the top 10%, identify what differentiated them, repeat) to improve through volume.

**Evidence across sources:**
- In impact, the fullest articulation -- the reason smart people underperform financially.
- In diary-ceo, the Chris Rock story and the "100 reps" skill acquisition loop. "I get violent about skill acquisition."
- In mw830, the zombie spawn point metaphor -- find the one input that works and press it relentlessly.
- In mfm764, the formal definition and the midwit meme: beginners say "just do the work," intermediates add complexity, experts return to "just do the work."

**When to deploy:** When someone has read 10 books about starting a business and has not started one. When someone is consuming content about their goal instead of pursuing it. When someone is using education as a form of procrastination.

#### Stories

**The Rocky Cutscene**
> "The rocky cutscene lasts two minutes in the movie, but it can last five years in real life, and there is no background music, you know what I mean?" (mw670, ed-mylett)

Hormozi uses this constantly. The training montage in Rocky -- running up the steps, punching the meat, doing the sit-ups -- lasts 30 seconds to two minutes on screen. In reality, it represents months or years of daily grinding with no soundtrack, no camera crew, and no guarantee of a triumphant payoff at the end. You are living through what will someday be compressed into a highlight reel. The suffering is real in duration even though it will be compressed in the retelling. And critically: "We want a guarantee from a world that doesn't give any. And the fact that it doesn't give a guarantee is what makes it worth it, bro." (ed-mylett)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is in the grind and it feels endless. When someone is comparing their current struggle to other people's compressed success stories. When someone needs to hear that the montage is real-time years, not movie minutes -- and that is exactly what makes it meaningful.
*Sources:* mw670, ed-mylett, mw830

**The 37th Door**
Hormozi describes someone working on their auto detailing business, knocking on their 37th door of the day, having had the last 36 slammed in their face, with a tick mark showing they need to get to 100. Despite the rejection: "You can be joyful if you reframe the joy around the person you are becoming by doing the hard work." (ed-mylett)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is deep in daily rejection -- cold calls, door knocks, outreach -- and questioning whether to continue. The reframe is not "this will pay off" (it might not). The reframe is that the person doing the knocking is becoming someone different than the person who stayed home. The 37th door is not about the door. It is about you.
*Sources:* ed-mylett

**Chris Rock's Creative Process**
Chris Rock goes to tiny clubs and tests material 40-50 times, iterating based on crowd response. "The Netflix special is the output of hundreds of iterations." (diary-ceo) What the audience sees is a polished, seemingly effortless hour of comedy. What produced it was systematic volume plus feedback loops -- the same "common factors analysis" process Hormozi applies to everything. Do 100 reps, identify the top 10%, figure out what made them work, do 100 more.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is paralyzed by the gap between their current output and what they aspire to. When someone expects their first attempt to be their masterpiece. When someone needs to understand that mastery comes from repetition, not inspiration.
*Sources:* diary-ceo

**The Google Doc Guarantee**
A friend of Hormozi offered a guarantee on a product: "All you have to do is open up a Google Doc, say what you're going to do every day, and at the end of the day say what you did. Just do that for six weeks. If you do that and don't get results, I'll give you your money back." The completion rate for writing twice a day for six weeks: less than 1%. (mw952)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone insists they want results but has not demonstrated basic consistency. The test is astonishingly simple -- write two sentences a day for six weeks. Almost nobody does it. This is the gap between aspiration and action made concrete. "Simple" and "easy" are not the same thing. The bar for trying is embarrassingly low, and most people still do not clear it.
*Sources:* mw952

**10,000 Asks Framework**
Make 10,000 asks. At a 1% conversion rate, that is 100 customers. The math works at sufficient volume regardless of your individual rejection rate. "It would be statistically absurd for this not to work at sufficient volume." (impact)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is stuck at zero and overwhelmed by uncertainty. Converts "I don't know if this will work" into "at sufficient volume, it is mathematically inevitable." The problem is never the strategy -- it is the volume.
*Sources:* impact, leads

**Working Side-by-Side**
A new director of marketing joined Hormozi. On his first day, Hormozi said: "Great, pull up a document, let's go." The director responded: "Oh, like now." Hormozi: "You got something else to do?" Over eight hours of shared screen work, the director's perception of work intensity shifted in real time. Hormozi's thesis: "Consistency cannot be seen in a snapshot, a sound bite, or a reel. The only way to transfer work ethic is proximity -- working next to someone who doesn't stop." (mw952)

*Teaching application:* Deploy when discussing the power of proximity to higher standards, or when someone's environment is calibrated to mediocre effort. You cannot Instagram your way to someone's work ethic. You have to sit next to them and watch them not stop.
*Sources:* mw952
