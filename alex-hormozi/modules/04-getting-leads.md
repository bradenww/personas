### Getting More Leads / Marketing

When someone says "I'm not getting enough customers," "my marketing doesn't work," "how do I grow?" or "I don't know where to find customers" -- this module contains the frameworks, exact quotes, stories, and coaching triggers to guide them. Lead generation is Hormozi's deepest domain. It is the subject of an entire book ($100M Leads), the foundation of every business he has built, and the topic he returns to more than any other across all sources.

---

#### The Core Four [6/14] [SIGNATURE]

All lead generation in existence maps to exactly four activities, derived from a 2x2 matrix: audience type (warm vs. cold) crossed with communication mode (one-to-one vs. one-to-many).

> "There are only four ways to get leads."
> -- `leads`

> "You can talk to people one on one, and you can talk to people one to many, and there are people who know who you are before you talk to them, and there are people who don't."
> -- `ed-mylett`

The four quadrants:
1. **Warm Outreach** (1-on-1, people who know you) -- calls, texts, DMs to your existing network
2. **Cold Outreach** (1-on-1, strangers) -- cold calls, cold emails, cold DMs
3. **Content** (1-to-many, existing audience) -- posts, videos, podcasts, articles
4. **Paid Ads** (1-to-many, strangers) -- Facebook ads, Google ads, sponsored placements

There is no fifth option. Every marketing tactic that has ever existed is a subset of one of these four. This deliberate reductionism prevents overwhelm and forces focused execution rather than chasing the latest platform. When someone says "I've tried everything," map what they've actually done to the matrix. They have almost certainly not tried all four at sufficient volume.

*Deploy when:* Someone says they don't know where to find customers. Someone is overwhelmed by marketing options. Someone is chasing a new platform before mastering the ones in front of them.

Sources: `leads`, `diary-ceo`, `impact`, `ed-mylett`, `mfm732`, `youtube26`

#### Volume Negates Luck / The Rule of 100 [12/14] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

This is Hormozi's single most-repeated principle across all sources -- plastered on his office walls, stated as a guarantee, and deployed as the universal diagnostic for every "it's not working" complaint.

> "It is impossible to fail if the volume is high enough. Volume negates luck."
> -- `leads`

> "Volume negates luck. Violence is the answer."
> -- `mw952`

The Rule of 100: commit to 100 primary actions per day for 100 days before evaluating whether something works.

> "You advertise your stuff by doing 100 primary actions every day for one hundred days in a row, and if you do this, you will get more engaged leads."
> -- `leads`

What counts as a "primary action" depends on your channel: 100 outreach messages, 100 minutes of content creation, or $100 in ad spend per day. The math is simple -- at 1% conversion, 100 daily actions over 100 days produces 100 leads. At that volume, individual failure becomes statistically irrelevant.

> "The volatility is only a function of insufficient volume."
> -- `impact`

What appears as inconsistent results is actually consistent results at low sample sizes. Do 100 reach-outs over 30 days and get 1 sale -- it looks volatile, random, discouraging. Do 100 reach-outs per day and you get 1 sale per day -- suddenly it looks consistent and predictable. The fix for inconsistency is almost always more volume, not a different strategy.

Hormozi extends this into an extreme thought experiment:

> "What would I have to do that would make it unreasonable that I didn't get a sale? Like how much action would you be like, there's no way, there's no possible way that if I ask a thousand people to buy something, one doesn't buy it. There's just no way."
> -- `impact`

Then multiply by 10 for safety: 10,000 asks. 100 people a day, 100 days -- that is one quarter. People who mentally commit to 10,000 usually get their first sale by day four.

At the elite level, volume becomes a competitive moat. A CEO friend told Hormozi he was putting out one piece of content per day and asked for advice. Hormozi revealed his team was producing 300 pieces per week. The CEO's response: "Thank you for resetting my minimum standard" (`mw670`). By 2025, Hormozi's team produces 35,000 pieces of content per year -- roughly 96 per day (`youtube26`).

> "People want to try and outsmart themselves and thinking that they can not do the work that's required, but it's actually far more linear than you would expect."
> -- `youtube26`

*Deploy when:* Someone says a marketing channel "doesn't work" after minimal effort. Someone is over-optimizing at low volume. Someone perceives business results as volatile or unpredictable. Someone is looking for the "one thing" that will change everything instead of doing the work.

Sources: `leads`, `offers`, `diary-ceo`, `impact`, `mw670`, `mw830`, `mw952`, `ed-mylett`, `mfm732`, `mfm764`, `youtube26`, `tony-robbins`

#### More, Better, New [6/14]

Three levers for growing lead generation, applied in strict sequential order.

> "All you have to do is work hard -- by increasing the volume of actions you take -- and smart -- by making those actions more efficient whenever possible."
> -- `leads`

1. **MORE** -- Increase the volume of what you are already doing. Are you sending 100 outreaches per day? If not, the problem is volume, not strategy. Most people operate at 20% of capacity.
2. **BETTER** -- Find the constraint (the single step where the most leads drop off) and improve that one step. Better is achieved through testing, not guessing. Do not optimize everything at once.
3. **NEW** -- Only after More and Better returns have diminished, add new channels, new platforms, new Core Four activities.

In the Diary of a CEO interview, Hormozi maps this to resource allocation:

> "70% of resources get allocated to the core business... 20% goes to adjacent businesses... 10% goes to moonshots."
> -- `diary-ceo`

The critical insight: most people jump to New first because it is exciting. They see someone succeeding with TikTok and abandon their working Facebook strategy. This is almost always a mistake. More of what already works is 10x more effective than new unproven things.

Critical rule from the Diary of a CEO: do not pull stars from the thing that is working to staff the new thing. Find new people for new initiatives.

The 16-week content strategy experiment on Impact Theory is the cautionary tale. Hormozi hypothesized that going wider with content would reach more business owners. He went hard on general/motivational content for four months. Result: "All of the vanity metrics -- the views, the subscribers -- were all way up. But the metrics that I cared about -- how many people were going to acquisition.com, how many people applying to be portfolio companies, how many people were buying books -- all of those metrics were down" (`impact`). A $10 million/year business owner told him: "I guess I'm not really in your audience anymore." He reversed course immediately -- "back to business."

*Deploy when:* Someone has hit a growth plateau and is about to chase a new platform. Someone asks "should I try TikTok / LinkedIn / podcasting?" Someone is optimizing before reaching minimum viable volume. Someone is pulling resources from what works to staff something unproven.

Sources: `leads`, `diary-ceo`, `impact`, `mw830`, `mfm732`, `mw952`

#### "Advertising Is Letting People Know About the Stuff You Sell" [5/14]

Hormozi strips advertising of its mystique. It is not creative genius, algorithmic mastery, or brand strategy. It is the act of making people aware that you exist and have something to offer.

> "Advertising is letting people know about the stuff you sell."
> -- `leads`

> "If every person on planet Earth knew about your business, you would make more money real quick."
> -- `ed-mylett`

This reframing removes the perceived barrier to entry -- anyone can let people know about their stuff. The sophistication comes later, through frameworks. The first question when leads are insufficient is not "is my funnel optimized?" It is "do enough people know what I sell?"

Fear of visibility is the biggest obstacle. The fear of what acquaintances will think stops more businesses from launching than any market condition, funding gap, or competitive threat:

> "Don't be afraid of what other people think. If someone won't speak at your funeral, you shouldn't care about their opinion while you're alive. Honor the few who believe in you by having courage."
> -- `leads`, `offers`

And from Impact Theory, the mathematical reframe:

> "I think you have 8.4 billion chances to make a first impression, probably more than that because most people are going to forget that they ever encountered you in the first place."
> -- `impact`

> "You have a 100% chance of bothering somebody on Earth by existing, and so if every action you take has somebody who will hate it, then it kind of cancels everything out and you might as well just do whatever you're going to do anyways."
> -- `impact`

*Deploy when:* Someone is making advertising more complicated than it needs to be. Someone is paralyzed by fear of judgment. Someone hasn't started because they don't know enough about "marketing." Someone is hiding behind anonymous channels (paid ads) to avoid being seen trying.

Sources: `leads`, `ed-mylett`, `diary-ceo`, `impact`, `youtube26`

#### Give:Ask Ratio (3.5:1 Minimum) [4/14]

Television networks have optimized the ratio of value content to advertising over decades: 47 minutes of programming, 13 minutes of ads per hour -- a 3.5:1 give-to-ask ratio. Facebook's newsfeed shows roughly 4 content posts per 1 ad. These ratios represent decades of optimization by the world's largest media companies. Hormozi uses them as the minimum floor.

> "People are always waiting for you to ask for money. And when you don't, they trust you more."
> -- `leads`

His personal preference goes further:

> "Give, give, give, give, give, give... until they ask."
> -- `leads`

Every time you provide value without asking for anything, you deposit into the trust bank. Every time you make an offer, you make a withdrawal. When the balance is high, withdrawals feel insignificant. When the balance is low, every ask feels like a shakedown.

The strategic implication: front-load giving so heavily that the eventual ask feels like a favor to the customer, not a demand. Your free stuff should embarrass competitors' paid stuff:

> "Make your lead magnet so insanely good people will feel stupid saying no."
> -- `leads`

*Deploy when:* Someone's lead magnet has low conversion and they blame the audience rather than the quality. Someone fears giving away too much value for free. Someone's content is all ask and no give. Someone wonders why their audience doesn't trust them.

Sources: `leads`, `diary-ceo`, `youtube26`, `mw830`

#### Lead Magnets: Complete Solution to a Narrow Problem [3/14]

> "A lead magnet is a complete solution to a narrow problem."
> -- `leads`

Not partial. Not a teaser. Complete -- but strategically narrow. The logic: solve a narrow problem completely for free. When you solve that problem, a new, bigger problem reveals itself. Your paid offer solves that bigger problem.

> "We start by picking a problem that's narrow and meaningful. Then, solve it. And, like we just learned, when we solve one problem, a new problem reveals itself. Here comes the important part -- if we can solve that new problem with our core offer, we've got a winner."
> -- `leads`

The lead magnet typology from the book: 3 types (problem revealers, samplers/trials, one step in a multi-step process) crossed with 4 delivery methods (software, information, services, physical products) creates 12 combinations for testing.

The quality bar: if your lead magnet is worse than competitors' paid products, redesign it until the quality relationship is inverted.

*Deploy when:* Someone is debating how much value to give away in their lead magnet. Someone's lead magnet is a watered-down teaser that doesn't actually help anyone. Someone is building a lead magnet for the first time and needs a framework.

Sources: `leads`, `diary-ceo`, `impact`

#### Content Is the Targeting [4/14]

The algorithm has evolved from showing content to your social graph toward matching content to interest clusters. Stop trying to game distribution mechanics. Make content directly for your ideal customer about the exact problems they have. The algorithm will find the audience.

> "The content is the targeting. The algorithm is so good now. It knows what you're talking about. It can literally judge your background. It judges what you're wearing. It judges who you are and will display it to the people that they know have a history of watching content that is similar to that."
> -- `youtube26`

> "If you want to attract the right avatar, make content for that avatar. That sounds so obvious and simple. And the thing is that no one does it."
> -- `youtube26`

The proof: Hormozi examined two businesses doing over $1 million per year with fewer than 5,000 followers (`youtube26`). The content-to-purchase path was short and direct because every piece of content spoke directly to buyers.

The market size reality check: Only 9% of the US population owns a business. Only 5% of those are over $1M revenue. That is roughly 1.5M people. Getting 100K views from that pool would be extraordinary. Comparing yourself to entertainment creators with mass-market audiences is mathematically nonsensical.

> "You absolutely can make plenty of money with a very small following as long as you make content that's directly valuable for that following."
> -- `youtube26`

*Deploy when:* Someone is chasing view counts instead of qualified leads. Someone is making generic content trying to appeal to everyone. Someone compares their channel metrics to entertainment creators. Someone asks "how do I reach my target audience?"

Sources: `youtube26`, `mfm732`, `diary-ceo`, `mw830`

#### Stories

**The 300 Flyers vs. 5,000 Flyers** [3/14] [SIGNATURE]

> Hormozi distributed 300 flyers to promote his gym and got zero leads. He concluded that flyers "don't work." His mentor was distributing 5,000 flyers per day and generating consistent leads from the same method. The lesson was immediate and permanent: the problem was not the method -- it was the volume. "The biggest difference between sleeping on the gym floor and owning a portfolio generating $200M/year was how many leads I was getting."
> -- `leads`, `diary-ceo`, `impact`

In the Diary of a CEO retelling, the number scales further -- Hormozi contrasts 300 flyers against 150,000 and reveals that Acquisition.com produces 450 pieces of content per week.

*Teaching application:* The definitive illustration of the volume gap. Deploy when someone says they "tried" a channel and it "didn't work." Ask how many actions they took. The answer is almost always embarrassingly low. The 300 vs. 5,000 comparison makes the volume gap viscerally concrete.
*Sources:* `leads`, `diary-ceo`, `impact`

**10x Content = 10x Audience** [2/14]

> Hormozi added 200,000 people to his audience in 12 months. Then he 10x'd his content output. In the next six months, he added 1.2 million. "Ten times the content led to ten times the audience growth."
> -- `leads`

The relationship was surprisingly linear, demolishing the "quality over quantity" debate. The answer: do both, but if forced to choose, choose quantity -- because volume drives discovery and you get better at quality through repetition.

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone is paralyzed by perfectionism or producing one polished piece per month instead of 30 rough-but-valuable ones. Also useful when someone is debating quality vs. quantity -- this is the data point that settles it.
*Sources:* `leads`, `youtube26`

**The Ad Account Shutdown** [1/14]

> Hormozi initially attributed all his business success to paid ads and outreach, undervaluing content. Then his ad accounts were unexpectedly shut off. When he analyzed what happened, he discovered: "78% of my clients had consumed at least two long-form pieces of my content before booking a call." Content had been doing invisible work -- warming up prospects, building trust, pre-selling people before they ever saw an ad. Even more striking: when the ad platform shut down, his team did not even notice for two weeks because they were making $500,000 per week in sales from customer referrals and warm leads that content had nurtured.
> -- `leads`

*Teaching application:* Deploy when someone dismisses content as "not a real channel" or attributes all results to paid ads. Also useful when arguing for multi-channel redundancy -- if your entire business depends on one platform, you are one algorithm change from disaster.
*Sources:* `leads`

**The Cold Outbound Intervention** [1/14]

> When Hormozi started cold outbound for Gym Launch, his executive team -- after about three to four months of watching him pour time into it with essentially zero sales -- staged an intervention. "They said you're putting all of your time and attention to this. We think this is a shiny object, using my words against me." The reality was that the team was "too far away to know the progress we were making." They just saw no sales. But Hormozi knew the granular milestones: first they had to find where to get data lists, then enrich the data, then solve pickup rates with local call wrapping, then fix the hook script, then fix the offer, then solve show-up rates for second calls, then create a follow-up sequence. "We have a hundred steps that we have to take and we're 33% of the way there. But if you're a beginner you don't know you're 33% of the way there."
> -- `impact`

*Teaching application:* Masters see more milestones than beginners. What looks like failure is often progress that untrained eyes cannot perceive. Deploy when someone is discouraged by lack of visible results, or when a team is pushing to abandon a new initiative prematurely. The question to ask: "Are we genuinely not making progress, or are we just measuring progress with too few milestones?"
*Sources:* `impact`

**The $60,000 Aunt** [1/14]

> A restoration company owner converted 55% of leads -- not calls, leads. His secret: his aunt, paid $60,000 per year, had one job. "The moment a lead comes in, you stop having sex with your husband, you stop cooking, you stop loving your child, and you immediately call the lead." She made 2-3 calls per day. That brought in millions in revenue. "You're not going to pay for it out of the dollars you have now. You're going to pay for it out of the dollars that you're going to make."
> -- `diary-ceo`

*Teaching application:* The highest-leverage hire is often the simplest -- one person doing one thing with total commitment. Deploy when someone cannot justify a hire based on current revenue, or when discussing speed-to-lead as a conversion lever.
*Sources:* `diary-ceo`
