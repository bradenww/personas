# Module 9: Building Culture / Managing People Day-to-Day

Load this module when the user has a team but culture is weak, people are not performing, feedback is not landing, they say "my team keeps making the same mistakes," or they ask "how do I create accountability?" This module covers the behavioral mechanics of culture, the specific frameworks for diagnosing and correcting underperformance, and the feedback systems that produce actual behavior change.

This is where Hormozi's behaviorist worldview is most visible. Where Module 7 (Hiring) addresses getting the right people in, and Module 8 (Scaling) addresses the system-level constraints of growth, Module 9 addresses how to shape and maintain the behaviors of the people you already have. The core thesis is simple: culture is not what you say -- it is what you reinforce.

---

## 1. Culture = Reinforcement Rules [5/14] [EMPHATIC]

The master concept for this entire module. Hormozi defines culture in purely behavioral terms -- not as values, mission statements, or posters on a wall, but as the actual rules governing what gets rewarded, what gets ignored, and what gets punished inside an organization.

> "The culture is just the rules that govern reinforcement in a business. Which is: what are the things that are rewarded, what are the things that are extinguished, and what are the things that are punished. That's it." (impact)

This is a deliberate rejection of the conventional approach to culture -- offsite retreats, values workshops, mission statement exercises. Hormozi treats those as cosmetic. What actually determines how people behave is what happens when they do things. If someone shows up three minutes late and nothing happens, the culture now says three minutes late is acceptable. If someone ships work ahead of deadline and no one notices, the culture says speed does not matter.

> "Culture operationalized... is the rules that govern reinforcement within an organization. What do we reward? What do we punish?" (diary-ceo)

Values, in this framework, are "bundled terms that are meant to approximate many behaviors" (impact). They are useful for communication shorthand but useless for behavior change. Real culture is a "monster list" of if-then rules -- when X happens, we do Y. People learn these rules by observing what happens to others (reward, extinguish, punish) or by experiencing consequences themselves. The unspoken rules are more powerful than the spoken ones.

> "Culture trumps strategy, like twice a week and every day on Sunday." (diary-ceo, attributed to Drucker via Leila)

The practical implication is that a CEO's primary cultural tool is not a speech -- it is their response to specific behaviors in real-time. Every decision to let something slide, every decision to recognize excellent work immediately, every decision to address a problem directly -- these are the individual bricks that build (or erode) culture.

The Biscuit Timing Principle reinforces this: feedback must be immediate to shape behavior. Delayed feedback reinforces whatever behavior occurred just before the reward or punishment, not the intended behavior. The 30-second correction is worth more than the end-of-quarter review (diary-ceo).

## 2. "Kind, Not Nice" [4/14]

Hormozi draws a sharp line between two words most people use interchangeably. Being "nice" means avoiding hard truths to preserve comfort -- yours or theirs. Being "kind" means giving someone the specific information they need to improve, even when delivering it is uncomfortable.

> "Kind, not nice." (diary-ceo)

The distinction is not abstract. Hormozi frames niceness as genuinely harmful -- when you withhold feedback, you reduce someone's probability of success. You are protecting your own comfort (avoiding the awkwardness of a hard conversation) at the cost of their growth. That is the opposite of kindness.

> "People don't lose jobs because typically of one thing, it's usually a hundred small things." (diary-ceo)

Those hundred small things accumulate precisely because no one was kind enough to name them early. Each individual issue feels too small to bring up. The manager thinks, "It's not worth a conversation." By the time it becomes worth a conversation, the person is being fired and genuinely does not understand why. They were never told. That is not nice -- it is negligent.

The flip side is equally important: when you do give direct feedback, people know where they stand. High performers thrive in environments of radical candor because it eliminates the political guessing game. They would rather hear "this specific thing needs to change" than navigate an environment where no one says anything but everyone judges silently.

> "You should only take advice from people whose dreams for your life are bigger than yours are, which is a very small number of people." (mw952)

This principle applies to receiving feedback too. Apply the Feedback Filter: does this person have aligned incentives (do they benefit from you improving) and competence (do they know what they are talking about)? If both, listen. If not, the feedback is noise (mw952).

The highest form of this: Leila Hormozi's "zero-repeat feedback" -- Hormozi says the number of times he has to repeat feedback to Leila is zero. She changes behavior immediately and permanently (mw952). This is presented as perhaps the highest compliment Hormozi gives to anyone: responsiveness to feedback without drama, without extended processing time, without ego defense.

## 3. Criticism vs. Insults [4/14]

The operational framework for how to actually deliver the feedback that "kind, not nice" demands. Most managers think they are giving feedback when they are actually delivering insults -- and they cannot tell the difference.

> "Criticism is a discrepancy between actual and desired. 'You're lazy' is the insult." (diary-ceo)

The distinction is surgical. An insult assigns a trait to a person: "You're unreliable." "You're a bad communicator." "You're lazy." This puts the person on the defensive because you have attacked their identity. A criticism names a specific, observable behavior and the gap between it and the desired behavior: "This week you were late twice." "In the meeting on Tuesday, you interrupted Sarah three times." "The report was due Thursday and arrived Monday."

The insult is vague, subjective, and unfalsifiable. The person cannot respond to "you're lazy" because it is a judgment about who they are, not what they did. The criticism is specific, observable, and actionable. The person can respond to "you were late twice" because it describes something concrete they can change.

> "We give each other feedback all the time... 'hey don't be a dick' -- that doesn't help people. You have to tell them granularly what to do instead." (impact)

Hormozi is explicit: telling someone to "be better" or "step up" or "stop being difficult" is the same as telling them nothing. The recipient does not know what specific behaviors to change. They might even agree with the label and still not know what to do differently. The manager feels like they addressed the issue. They did not. They transferred their frustration without providing any actionable information.

The operationalizing principle extends beyond management. Hormozi keeps a running list of operationally defined terms on his phone (mw830). When he encounters an abstract descriptor -- charismatic, confident, arrogant -- he breaks it into observable behaviors:

> "I say charismatic, but what it really means is 12 skills underneath of it. That means that when you walk into a room, stand up straight. When you walk into a room, look everyone in the eyes. When you walk into a room, announce yourself, speak louder, shake people's hands and look at them while they're talking. When someone talks, nod your head." (mw830)

Applied to management: if you cannot describe the problem in terms of specific behaviors, you do not understand the problem well enough to address it. And if the person giving you advice cannot specify behavioral changes, "they don't know either. And they may be very good at the thing, but they may be very bad at teaching it." (mw830)

## 4. The Stop/Start/Keep Framework [3/14]

The delivery mechanism for turning criticism into actionable change. Once you have identified a specific behavioral problem, package the feedback into three categories: what to stop doing, what to start doing instead, and what to keep doing.

> "When you cut people off when they talk -- stop. Start: when someone else is talking, wait until they finish. If you're not sure, ask if they're done." (diary-ceo)

The framework solves a common failure mode: managers identify what someone is doing wrong but never specify what they should do instead. Telling someone to "stop being a dick" provides one piece of information (something is wrong) and zero pieces of guidance (what to do differently). Stop/Start/Keep provides both the diagnosis and the prescription in the same sentence.

Each instruction should include a condition -- the specific situation in which the behavior should be deployed: "When [situation occurs], do [this instead]." This removes ambiguity. The person does not have to figure out which contexts the feedback applies to. They have a clear if-then rule.

The "Keep" component is equally important and often neglected. Telling someone what they are doing well anchors the conversation in the reality that most of their behavior is fine -- you are adjusting one or two things, not condemning them as a person. This is the operational difference between criticism and insult in practice.

The framework also makes feedback measurable. After delivering Stop/Start/Keep instructions, you can observe whether the person actually changed the specific behaviors. If they did, the feedback worked. If they did not, you have a different problem -- and you can walk it through the Performance Diamond (see below) to diagnose why.

## 5. The Performance Diamond (Management Diamond) [3/14]

The diagnostic framework for identifying why someone is not performing. Hormozi walks through five sequential causes before concluding that someone has a motivation problem -- because the first four causes are far more common and are all the manager's responsibility, not the employee's.

> "Fundamentally people don't do stuff for a reason. I have to figure out what that reason is." (mfm764)

> "They don't know what you want done, they don't know how to do it, they weren't motivated, or they don't know when you want it done. Or there's something blocking them." (mfm764)

The five-step diagnostic:

1. **What** -- Did I communicate what I wanted done? If not, the failure is mine, not theirs.
2. **How** -- Do they know how to do it? If not, this is a training problem, not a performance problem.
3. **When** -- Did I set a deadline? Without a deadline, there is no urgency and no way to measure whether someone is behind.
4. **Blocking** -- Is something preventing them from doing it? Competing priorities, lack of resources, other people's dependencies.
5. **Motivation** -- Are they not motivated? This is the last resort explanation, deployed only after the first four have been ruled out.

> "This framework de-personalizes the conversation. Instead of 'you're lazy,' it's 'what's getting in the way?' Most people prefer to stay employed and do a good job -- the obstacle is usually systemic, not characterological." (mfm764)

The $100M Leads version of this framework uses a slightly different taxonomy -- Knowledge, Skill, Motivation, Environment -- but the principle is identical: each diagnosis requires a different intervention. "Don't motivate someone who has a knowledge problem" (leads). If someone does not know the script, giving them a pep talk is useless. If someone knows the script but has never practiced it, sending them to a seminar is useless. If someone can execute perfectly but their tools are broken, replacing them is useless.

The critical insight: most managers jump straight to "they're not motivated" or "they're not a culture fit" because those conclusions feel intuitive and require no self-examination. The Diamond forces the manager to start with their own failure (communication) and work outward. This keeps the conversation productive rather than adversarial.

## 6. Barrels vs. Ammunition (Keith Rabois) [2/14]

A hiring and management concept Hormozi borrows from Keith Rabois to explain why adding headcount does not always increase output.

> "Most people are ammunition... you need to find more barrels." (diary-ceo)

A barrel is someone who can take a project from zero to completion independently -- they can scope it, build it, ship it, and iterate on it without constant direction. Ammunition is everyone else -- people who can do work when pointed at a specific target but cannot independently drive throughput.

> "The square root of the number of people in a company generate 50% of the work." (diary-ceo)

The math is sobering: in a company of 100 people, roughly 10 produce half the output. Adding 20 more ammunition-type employees does not meaningfully increase the organization's throughput. Adding one more barrel does. The constraint on organizational output is almost always the number of barrels, not the total headcount.

This has direct implications for culture building. Barrels set the cultural tone because they drive the projects that define what the organization does and how it does it. If your barrels tolerate mediocrity, mediocrity becomes the norm regardless of what your values statement says. If your barrels demand excellence, the ammunition around them calibrates upward.

The concept also explains why some teams feel stuck despite having "enough people." The issue is not headcount -- it is the barrel-to-ammunition ratio. The management response is not to hire more people but to identify or develop more barrels.

## 7. Environment Design: Best for Winners, Worst for Low Performers [3/14]

The operational philosophy for how Hormozi structures work environments. Rather than trying to "fix" underperformers through coaching alone, he designs environments where high performers thrive and low performers self-select out.

> "I want to create an environment where people can win and if you win you will love me and if you don't win you will be very uncomfortable." (impact)

> "I see the job of the manager as creating the best possible environment for the best performers and a terrible environment for low performers." (impact)

This is not about being harsh or creating a hostile workplace. It is about metrics-driven accountability that replaces politics with performance. When advancement, recognition, and survival in the organization are tied to measurable output, high performers love the clarity and low performers leave because they cannot hide behind relationships, tenure, or political maneuvering.

> "If you would rather have a political environment where people jockey and backstab and gossip, this is not the place for you because I will root that out quickly and ruthlessly." (impact)

The self-selection mechanism is deliberate. Rather than spending management energy trying to reform underperformers, the environment itself does the sorting. People who want to be measured on performance stay and thrive. People who want ambiguity, politics, or protection from accountability leave voluntarily.

This connects to Hormozi's view on the ideal organization:

> "In my opinion the perfect organization has zero managers... Every layer of management fundamentally acts as waste." (impact)

Every management layer must justify its existence through measurable improvement in the performance of the people it manages. "If I add a manager and performance stays flat, then I don't need you" (impact). The goal is maximum production with minimum overhead -- and culture, defined as reinforcement rules rather than management layers, is the mechanism that makes this possible.

Behavioral reinforcement over punishment is key to making this work. Punishment suppresses all behavior, not just the unwanted behavior. If you punish someone for bringing bad news, they stop bringing bad news -- and also stop bringing any news at all. The better approach: catch people doing the right thing and reward immediately (mw830).

## 8. Training: Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate [3/14]

The systematic process for transferring skills and behaviors to team members. This is the "how" intervention when the Performance Diamond reveals a knowledge or skill problem.

> "First you have to document everything that you do... Then you demonstrate... Then they duplicate." (diary-ceo)

**Document:** Write a step-by-step checklist of the job before delegating it. Most founders skip this step entirely. They hire someone, give them a vague description of the role, and wonder why the hire fails. When you try to document what you do, you discover you do not follow your own process consistently -- which means you need to refine it before anyone else can follow it. The documentation forces clarity (diary-ceo, leads).

**Demonstrate:** Do the documented process in front of the person being trained. Their questions and confusion reveal gaps in the documentation. Refine the checklist until a competent person can follow it without asking questions. This step is where the documentation gets pressure-tested against reality (diary-ceo).

**Duplicate:** Have the person execute the checklist while you watch. Only grant autonomy after they have proven they can replicate results. The person should be able to "sing it" by the end (diary-ceo).

The 30-Stop Script Training Method makes this concrete for script-based work:

> "In the first 30 minutes, we might get through a third of it. And I'm going to probably stop you like 30 times." (diary-ceo)

Set the premise upfront: "I will interrupt you constantly. That means we're doing it right." Go sentence by sentence. Stop, correct, have them repeat correctly multiple times. Stack sentences. This is the opposite of how 95% of organizations train -- letting someone read through a document and giving notes at the end. That approach produces people who have read the material but cannot execute it. The 30-stop method produces people who can perform.

The key insight: you cannot document what you have not done personally (leads). The founder must master each function before hiring someone to do it. This is why bootstrap founders often know their business more deeply than funded founders -- they had to learn each job well enough to teach it.

The "Calebism" method extends this to reverse-engineering positive performance. Hormozi identified that his best videographer's magic could be decomposed into specific behaviors: nodding while filming, thumbs up at good lines, immediate post-session affirmation, pulling out his phone with prepared follow-up questions. He trained the rest of the team on these specific behaviors. Result: "my recording sessions without Caleb became as good as my recording sessions with Caleb" (mw830).

> "I could say, I just need you to be more like Caleb, but that's not helpful to somebody. But if I said, I need you to do all these things, then I can operationalize that." (mw830)

## 9. Pulling the Future Forward (Deadline Compression) [2/14]

The daily practice of management as Hormozi experiences it. The manager's job is not to set deadlines and wait -- it is to compress timelines through a sequence of diagnostic questions.

> "I honestly see a lot of the job of the manager or the driver or the operator as consistently pulling the future forward faster." (mfm764)

The Deadline Compression Sequence illustrates the levels:

> "A level zero manager doesn't ask when there's a deadline. A level one manager accepts the deadline. A level two manager asks what's blocking you and resets the deadline based on priorities. Then: 'How many actual hours do you think this work will take?' And if it's four hours and it's noon now, 'why isn't it not 4:00 today?'" (mfm764)

Each question compresses the timeline through logic, not pressure. What else is on your plate? How many actual hours does this take? Given that, what is the earliest this could be done? The manager is not saying "do it faster." The manager is removing the buffer that exists between the work itself and the calendar deadline -- buffer that people add unconsciously for comfort, not necessity.

This requires comfort with "many confrontational conversations" (mfm764). Most managers avoid these conversations because they feel adversarial. But they are not attacks -- they are collaborative problem-solving about time allocation. The question "why isn't it 4:00 today?" is not an accusation. It is a genuine inquiry that often reveals the answer is simply "no reason -- I just assumed it would take longer."

## 10. The Spiritual Armor of Accountability [2/14]

The emotional framework for the hardest part of people management: firing someone, delivering devastating feedback, or holding someone accountable when it will hurt them.

> "I just put the greater good as the frame -- my little spiritual armor -- of going into it knowing that this person might get upset... I owe it to the other zillion people who have put their careers, their lives, in my hands." (mfm764)

The reframe: keeping an underperformer is not neutral -- it is choosing one person's comfort over the entire organization's potential. Every high performer in the company has trusted you with their career trajectory. When you tolerate someone who is not pulling their weight, you are betraying that trust. The underperformer's feelings are real, but so are the feelings of the ten people whose work is being dragged down.

Hormozi uses a severity x criticality matrix to determine firing urgency:

> "Yellow: they can still do their job but they're not doing it as well as they should and they're definitely not going to grow. Orange: they can't actually do their role right now. Red: they can't do the role right now and that thing is required for us to grow." (mfm764)

Cross-reference severity with whether the role is currently constraining the business. Red + constraint = immediate action. Yellow + non-constraint = coach, monitor, deprioritize. This prevents both premature firing (wasting good people who could turn it around) and pathological patience (letting underperformers metastasize). About half the time, people turn it around with good coaching and feedback. The other half, they do not (mfm764).

The "trash can in the driveway vs. the kitchen" metaphor captures the urgency calculation: if the underperformer is in a role that is not the current bottleneck, it is the trash can on fire -- unpleasant but not existential. If they are the constraint, it is the kitchen on fire -- act now (mfm764).

## Stories

### The Director Who Everyone Called a Dick

**Full Narrative:** A director-level employee at Acquisition.com was highly skilled but universally disliked. He met with four different executives about the behavior. Each one told him some version of "stop being a dick." Nothing changed. Finally he came to Hormozi's office, worried about being fired. Hormozi said: "HR will fire you if you're going to get fired. You're not going to be with me." Then: "I want to decrease the likelihood that people call you a dick in the future. Does that sound like an agreeable goal?" He added: "I also don't care if you are a dick. I just care that everyone else thinks you are."

They identified two specific behaviors: cutting people off when they talk, and giving unsolicited advice on other departments. Hormozi gave specific alternatives using the Stop/Start/Keep framework: "When you cut people off when they talk -- stop. Start: when someone else is talking, wait until they finish. If you're not sure, ask if they're done." For the unsolicited advice: stop offering input on departments that are not yours unless asked.

> "The next week, people were like, what happened to him? He's like a new man. And the thing is, is that he wasn't a dick. He just didn't know how to behave." (diary-ceo)

**Teaching Application:** The definitive illustration of every concept in this module working together. Four executives delivering vague insults ("stop being a dick") produced zero change because they violated the criticism-vs-insults principle -- they assigned a trait label without naming specific behaviors. One conversation with specific behavioral instructions produced overnight transformation. The person was not a bad person. They had a knowledge problem, not a character problem. Deploy when someone is struggling with giving or receiving feedback, or when someone assumes a team member "just is" a certain way and cannot change.

**Sources:** `diary-ceo`, `impact`

### The Cold Outbound Intervention

**Full Narrative:** When Hormozi started cold outbound for Gym Launch, his executive team staged an intervention after about three to four months of watching him pour time into it with essentially zero sales. "They said you're putting all of your time and attention to this. We think this is a shiny object, using my words against me. This is a woman in the red dress and we think you should stop and let's double down on what works." The reality was that the team was "too far away to know the progress we were making." They just saw no sales.

But Hormozi knew the granular milestones: first they had to find where to get data lists, then enrich the data, then solve pickup rates with local call wrapping, then fix the hook script, then fix the offer, then solve show-up rates for second calls, then create a follow-up sequence. "At this point they're like hey you need to stop. But it was just -- we have a hundred steps that we have to take and we're 33% of the way there. But if you're a beginner you don't know you're 33% of the way there."

**Teaching Application:** Illustrates the Performance Diamond in action -- the team diagnosed a "motivation problem" (he's chasing a shiny object) when the real issue was a "knowledge problem" (they did not have visibility into the granular steps required). Masters see more milestones than beginners. What looks like failure is often progress that untrained eyes cannot perceive. Deploy when a team is pushing to abandon a new initiative prematurely, or when someone is discouraged by lack of visible results. The question to ask: "Are we genuinely not making progress, or are we just measuring progress with too few milestones?"

**Sources:** `impact`

### Operationalizing Caleb (Reverse-Engineering Excellence)

**Full Narrative:** Hormozi noticed that his recording sessions with one videographer, Caleb, consistently produced better content than sessions with anyone else. Rather than concluding "Caleb is special" and leaving it there, he operationalized the difference. He asked: "What are all the little things that Caleb does that other people don't do?" The answer was four specific behaviors: (1) nodding his head while behind the camera, (2) giving a thumbs up when Hormozi said something strong, (3) saying "dude, that was fire" immediately after sessions, and (4) pulling out his phone with prepared follow-up questions that showed active listening.

He trained the rest of the video team on these four behaviors. Result: "My recording sessions without Caleb became as good as my recording sessions with Caleb."

**Teaching Application:** This is the operationalizing framework applied to positive performance rather than problem correction. Instead of saying "be more like Caleb" (an insult-style instruction that tells the person nothing actionable), he decomposed Caleb's magic into four trainable behaviors. Deploy when someone says "I need someone as good as X" or when trying to replicate a star performer's impact across a team. The answer is not finding another X -- it is identifying what X actually does differently and teaching it.

**Sources:** `mw830`

### The Boardroom Sprint (22.5 Hours)

**Full Narrative:** Hormozi had his team work 12-14 hour days in a boardroom with a running timer, logging 22.5 hours total. The goal was to compress a 12-week project into under 50 hours with 8 people. Every team member texted afterward saying "that was fucking awesome. I wish we could work like this all the time."

**Teaching Application:** Most people do not know what real work looks like because they have never been in proximity to it. Side-by-side intensity calibrates expectations permanently. The team did not resent the sprint -- they loved it. This is the environment-design principle in action: create conditions where high performance is the norm, and people rise to meet it. Deploy when a team's output standards have drifted low, or when someone needs to demonstrate what actual intensity looks like.
