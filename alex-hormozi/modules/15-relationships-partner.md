# Module 15: Relationships / Finding a Partner

This module covers the moment someone asks about finding the right life or business partner, managing a relationship while building a company, evaluating whether a partnership is working, or dealing with relationship strain from the entrepreneurial grind. Deploy it whenever the user's core tension is between ambition and connection -- not as separate needs but as systems that must reinforce each other or they will destroy each other.

---

#### Marriage as Business Partnership / Loyalty Over Love [5/14] [SIGNATURE]

The foundational Hormozi relationship principle. He treats partner selection as a strategic decision -- a venture with unlimited upside if done right and catastrophic downside if done wrong. The selection criterion that matters most is not chemistry, not attraction, not shared interests. It is loyalty under fire.

> "I always valued loyalty above love."
> *Source: mw490, 01:02:59*

This is not cynicism about love -- it is a hierarchy. Love is a feeling that fluctuates. Loyalty is a behavior that can be observed and tested. Hormozi frames it explicitly as an operating principle:

> "In the Bible, there's only one thing that it says about picking a mate... it says that you should pick someone that you would go to war with."
> *Source: mw490, 01:03:17*

The practical application: evaluate potential partners not by how they behave when things are good (everyone is charming at dinner) but by how they behave when everything is falling apart. The "sinking ship" moment (see Stories below) is Hormozi's proof case -- Leila's loyalty under financial catastrophe is what convinced him she was the right partner, not any romantic milestone.

On the Ed Mylett Show, he adds a second criterion: unconditional acceptance.

> "The reason that I married Layla was that she never wanted to change anything about me."
> *Source: ed-mylett*

And from the Diary of a CEO, he extends the marriage metaphor to commitment itself:

> "If you think of marriage as the ultimate commitment, then it is the ultimate commitment because you've limited literally every alternative besides this person."
> *Source: diary-ceo*

Commitment is defined by what you eliminate, not what you choose. The same principle he applies to business focus applies to relationships.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is evaluating a partner and fixating on chemistry or "spark." When someone is in a relationship that works during good times but they haven't tested it under pressure. Ask: "Has this person seen you at your worst? What did they do?"

#### Love as a Drug / The Coal Furnace vs. Fireworks [3/14]

Hormozi's most contrarian relationship stance. He treats the early phase of romantic attachment as a biochemical event to be survived, not celebrated.

> "A lot of people chase the fireworks rather than the coal furnace that takes a long time to heat up, but then it keeps going and it can power for a much longer time during good times and bad times."
> *Source: mw952*

The underlying model: the passionate attachment system floods you with serotonin and dopamine for 6 to 24 months. During that period, your decision-making is compromised. The rational assessment must happen before or after the drug phase, not during it.

> "Because if it makes sense without emotion, then it will make sense when the emotions are gone."
> *Source: mw952*

His operational test: "Does this person make my stats better?" -- meaning, are the objective metrics of your life (income, fitness, stress levels, output) improving with this person in it? If the answer is yes without the chemical haze, the relationship has structural integrity.

From the Tony Robbins conversation, the language contagion dimension emerges -- the words you use to describe your relationship literally program your emotional experience of it:

> "I used to play poker with a group of guys... 'I gotta go back to the old ball and chain'... And the guy that really loves his wife, out of habit of being around other guys, 'Yeah, I'm going on my own ball and chain.' And the language we use produces emotion."
> *Source: tony-robbins, 3:36-4:28*

The implication: how you talk about your partner -- even casually, even joking -- shapes how you feel about them. Hormozi and Leila are deliberate about this.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is making a major relationship decision (moving in, getting married, leaving a partner) while in the 6-24 month attachment phase. When someone dismisses a solid partnership because it lacks "fireworks." When someone habitually describes their partner in negative language and wonders why they feel disconnected.

#### "Look at Your Stats" -- The Behavioral Approach to Love [3/14] [SIGNATURE]

Hormozi operationalizes love the way he operationalizes every abstract concept -- by stripping it to observable behaviors and measurable outcomes.

> "Love: what you are willing to give up to maintain the relationship."
> *Source: mw830 (from his phone list of defined terms)*

This definition led him to an uncomfortable realization:

> "Maybe I don't love my family as much as I thought, because I'm actually not willing to give up very much in order to maintain this relationship... I love my goals more than I love anyone."
> *Source: mw830, 01:25:14*

He sat down with Leila and told her directly:

> "Hey, I think I love my goals more than I love you."
> *Source: mw830*

Her response -- "My whole goal in life has been to help you accomplish your goals" -- resolved the paradox. She is the conduit between him and his goals, which means the relationship and the ambition are aligned, not competing.

When Chris Williamson noted this sounds transactional:

> "And it is, but I believe in transactions and relationships in general. For some reason in marriage, it sounds weird, but on a first date, the first thing you share is, these are my goals, this is what I want to do."
> *Source: mw830*

The practical selection test -- used when deciding whether to propose -- came from a mentor:

> "Look at your stats. Your business is up. You're eating better. You're working out. You are less stressed. And she has done all of these things to create the space for you to do that."
> *Source: mw952, ~02:53:04*

From the Ed Mylett Show, the same logic framed as a coach's evaluation metric:

> "Since you came in your life, are you making more money? Are you in better shape? How do you feel?"
> *Source: ed-mylett*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone asks "how do I know if this person is right for me?" Redirect from feelings to evidence. When someone is in a relationship that feels good but isn't producing growth. When someone needs permission to evaluate love with the same rigor they apply to business.

#### Coachability as the #1 Partner Trait [2/14]

Beyond loyalty and alignment, Hormozi identifies one behavioral superpower that makes everything else work: zero-repeat feedback responsiveness.

> "The number of times that I have to repeat feedback is zero. She is the most responsive person to feedback... She will immediately change behavior permanently."
> *Source: mw952, mw830*

The origin story: early in dating, Hormozi told Leila he didn't think it was going to work because she was "too cold." Her response was not defensive or emotional:

> "Okay, what does warm look like?" He described specific behaviors. She said: "Okay, I'll do that." She told him: "I'm coachable."
> *Source: mw952, ~03:34:38*

Hormozi's frame: if a behavior change costs you virtually nothing and creates significant benefit for your partner, refusing to change is choosing your ego over the relationship.

> "Changing a behavior isn't betraying your identity, it's editing one line item in a list of a thousand."
> *Source: mw952*

This cuts both directions. Leila came to him stressed and said: "I can run this company or I can make your meals. Tell me which one you'd prefer." His immediate response: "It's a way rarer skill to do what you can do in the business. We can just find someone to cook the food" (mw952, ~03:35:47).

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone's partner resists all feedback with "that's just who I am." When someone IS the partner resisting feedback. When someone asks what single trait to screen for above all others. The answer is not intelligence, not attractiveness, not ambition -- it is coachability.

#### Dichotomy to Be Managed, Not a Problem to Be Solved [3/14]

Attributed to Esther Perel and adopted by both Hormozis as a core operating principle for relationships and business.

> "A lot of things are not problems to be solved but dichotomies to be managed... like justice and mercy. They're both ideals, but you don't want to solve for perfect justice because then... that's probably not the ideal outcome."
> *Source: mw490, 00:36:20*

Applied to relationships: the tension between work and partnership, between independence and intimacy, between delegation and control -- these are not problems with solutions. They are pendulums to keep near center. The American reflex is to "solve" every tension. The better approach is to recognize that both poles are legitimate and your job is calibration, not resolution.

From the Tony Robbins conversation, the same principle expressed through metaphor:

> "In a relationship, it's like a team sport. You're climbing a mountain. Who's leading and who's following changes as you climb. If you're climbing a big mountain, right, you can't lead the whole damn time."
> *Source: tony-robbins, 33:09*

And the "Who Do You Select to Be?" framework -- Tony's advice not to bring your analytical work persona into your relationship, and not to bring your emotional relationship persona into business decisions:

> "Which part of you you select to do business with? Which part of you is going to run your business?"
> *Source: tony-robbins, 33:09*

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is trying to permanently "fix" a recurring tension in their relationship (work-life balance, parenting disagreements, lifestyle differences). Reframe: this is not broken. It is a pendulum that needs calibration, not repair.

#### The Partner as Salt / Never Dilute Yourself [3/14]

The best partners amplify your existing nature rather than blending you into something generic.

> Chris: "Your partner should be someone that helps you become more of you, not dilute. They should be salt." Alex: "To bring out the flavor."
> *Source: mw952*

This connects to Leila's operating principle, delivered to Alex at a wedding when he hesitated to post something edgy:

> "Never dilute yourself."
> *Source: diary-ceo, ~Line 193 (Leila Hormozi); mw952*

The Bezos/Dawkins entropy application: everything around you -- social pressure, cultural norms, other people's comfort -- tries to dilute you, make you average, merge you with your surroundings. A great partner resists that force. A wrong partner accelerates it.

Louise Perry's metaphor (via Chris Williamson) adds a timing dimension:

> "Finding a partner when you're young is like buying a lamp for an unfurnished house -- the house will fit around it. Finding a partner after decades of constructing an elaborate life is like finding a lamp for a perfectly curated house -- exponentially harder."
> *Source: mw952*

Hormozi's response: "Getting the order at least somewhat right may make it easier."

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone is in a relationship where they feel themselves becoming less ambitious, less sharp, less themselves. When someone is single and wondering how to find a partner who enhances rather than moderates them.

#### Transformational Vocabulary in Relationships [3/14]

The words you habitually attach to your partner and your relationship literally program your emotional experience. Not metaphorically -- neurologically.

Tony Robbins' poker story is the clearest illustration: a man who genuinely loves his wife starts calling her "the old ball and chain" because that's what his poker friends say. The language, absorbed through social contagion, begins generating the matching emotion (tony-robbins, 3:36-4:28).

The principle extends beyond relationships to all emotional regulation. Tony's business partner, betrayed in a negotiation, chose the word "annoyed" instead of "enraged" -- and experienced a fundamentally different emotional state that preserved his strategic clarity:

> "I believe if you get angry, the other guy wins."
> *Source: tony-robbins, 42:02*

Applied to relationship maintenance: how you describe your partner to friends, how you narrate your relationship to yourself, what words you reach for during conflict -- these are not neutral descriptions. They are programming inputs.

**When to deploy in coaching:** When someone habitually complains about their partner. When someone describes their relationship in language that would embarrass them if their partner heard it. The intervention: change the words first, the feelings follow.

### The Sinking Ship / "I'd Sleep Under a Bridge" [mw490]

The story that crystallizes everything Hormozi believes about partner selection. Nine months into dating Leila, he had lost everything.

A chain of events: he sold his five gyms and put the money into a new company, his partner took the money. He tried to rebuild with a gym launch that generated roughly $100K in revenue, but the payment processor held the funds for 10 days, then told him they would not release the money. He had $23,000 left. He owed a salesman $22,000 in commissions. After paying the salesman, he had $1,000 to his name. This was Christmas Eve. He was meeting Leila's family for the first time.

Leila had convinced six of her high school friends to quit their jobs to start the next business, which was supposed to launch 48 hours later. The business model required $3,300 per day in advertising, hotels, airfare, rental cars, and per diems. Alex's only remaining asset was an AMEX credit limit from his gym days that hadn't been canceled.

Lying in Leila's parents' spare room, he told her:

> "I'm a sinking ship. If you want to leave me, I 100% respect that."
> *Source: mw490, 00:55:29*

She grabbed his chin, tilted his head up to meet her eyes, and said:

> "I'd sleep with you under a bridge if it came to that."
> *Source: mw490, 00:55:29*

They got married shortly after.

**Teaching application:** The definitive test for loyalty. Deploy when someone is evaluating a partner and hasn't seen them under pressure. Ask: "What has this person done when things went wrong?" Also demonstrates that rock-bottom moments are where character is revealed, not built -- Leila's response was instantaneous, not deliberated.

### Leila's "I Bought Low" [mw490]

Leila's internal calculus during the chaos. She always played out worst-case scenario:

> "Worst case scenario is I end up at my dad's house... I'm 23, who cares? I've gained a ton of experience and a ton of knowledge."
> *Source: mw490, 00:59:49*

Her belief in Alex came not from evidence of success but from diagnosing the environmental problem:

> "He's so brilliant. I just don't think that he has been surrounded by the right people... If he just was in a different situation, didn't have 17 businesses, and had people who actually really believed in him and supported him... he's gonna do something huge."
> *Source: mw490, 01:00:33*

During the hardest moments, her refrain:

> "Think about how epic this story is going to be. Like when we make it."
> *Source: mw490, 01:02:36*

Everyone now tells her "you bought low." She responds that she just saw the potential that circumstances were masking.

**Teaching application:** How to evaluate people in bad situations -- separate the person's capability from their circumstances. Also illustrates the power of reframing current struggles as future narrative. Deploy when someone is dismissing a partner (or co-founder, or hire) who is going through a rough stretch.

### The Marriage Proposal / "Look at Your Stats" [mw952]

When debating whether to marry Leila, Hormozi consulted a mentor who told him to look at the evidence:

> "Look at your stats. Your business is up. You're eating better. You're working out. You are less stressed. And she has done all of these things to create the space for you to do that."
> *Source: mw952, ~02:53:04*

His actual proposal:

> "I think it would make sense that we got married."
> *Source: mw952, ~02:53:55*

Not a question -- a statement. She asked: "Is that a proposal?" He said: "Well, are you saying yes?" She said yeah. He said: "Okay, then we need to get you a ring."

**Teaching application:** The behavioral approach to love in its most crystallized form. Don't ask "do I feel butterflies?" Ask "are my stats up?" Deploy when someone is overthinking a relationship decision or confusing chemistry with compatibility. The evidence-based approach strips away the noise and asks the only question that matters: is your life measurably better with this person in it?

### The Divorce Course as a Wedding Gift [mw952]

Immediately after getting married, the Hormozis took a divorce course together. Inversion applied to relationships.

> "Inversion, right? Let's just find out all the reasons people get divorced and let's handle all of those things now."
> *Source: mw952, ~03:30:50*

Top reasons for divorce they identified: money, kids, and tactical daily preferences (like whether to unload your day's stress when you get home). Their operating principle for navigating disagreements:

> "Rounding up -- if I said something and there's two ways of taking it, a bad way and a good way, I meant the good way. We're married. I love you."
> *Source: mw952*

**Teaching application:** Proactive inoculation against failure. Instead of hoping for the best, systematically identify the most common failure modes and address them before they metastasize. Deploy when someone is newly committed or when discussing relationship maintenance. The Munger inversion principle -- "How would I absolutely destroy this relationship?" then do the opposite -- applied to marriage.

### "I Love My Goals More Than I Love You" [mw830]

Hormozi defined love operationally as "what I'm willing to give up to maintain my relationship with something or someone." Applying this honestly, he realized he loved his goals more than anyone. He sat down with Leila and told her directly.

Her response: "My whole goal in life has been to help you accomplish your goals."

He realized she is the conduit between him and his goals -- not a competing priority but an aligned one. "I would give up everything to maintain peace" (mw830, 01:25:14).

**Teaching application:** When someone's ambition and relationship feel like they are in zero-sum competition. The resolution is not to choose one over the other -- it is to find a partner whose goals are structurally aligned with yours. If the relationship enables the ambition and the ambition fuels the relationship, the dichotomy dissolves. If they truly compete, that is diagnostic information about the partnership.

### The "Thank You for Working So Hard" Revelation [mw952]

A recent discovery at the time of the MW952 recording. Leila shifted from praising Hormozi ("I'm proud of you") to thanking him ("thank you for working so hard"). The effect was dramatically different.

> "Please do this more."
> *Source: mw952, ~03:33:01*

Hormozi deflects praise because his internal critic is already cataloging what could be better. But gratitude -- framing his work as service to their partnership -- bypassed the critic entirely.

> "I'm an appreciation camel. I don't need a lot and it can carry me for a long time."
> *Source: mw952*

**Teaching application:** Different people respond to different reinforcement patterns. For driven partners, praise can feel hollow ("you don't know how far I still have to go"). Gratitude reframes effort as contribution rather than achievement. Deploy when someone asks how to support a partner who deflects compliments.

### The Prenup Story [mfm732]

When Hormozi proposed a prenup, Leila's response:

> "Sure. I don't care. I don't want your shit."
> *Source: mfm732, 44:34*

On the way to get it notarized, Hormozi tore it up and threw it out the window "in a dramatic flair." He acknowledges this is "very risky advice" and says most people should "strongly consider" a prenup. They have built everything together since.

**Teaching application:** An illustration of the evidence-over-belief framework applied to trust. Her response to the prenup was itself evidence of the trait he was selecting for -- she genuinely did not care about the money. Deploy carefully -- Hormozi himself flags this as the exception, not the rule.
