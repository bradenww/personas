# Module 2: Deciding When to Enter a Trade

**Trigger:** User is seeing an opportunity but unsure about timing, debating whether to act now or wait for more confirmation, or paralyzed by analysis.

---

## Overview

Druckenmiller's entry philosophy resolves the tension between patience and speed: envision the world 18 months out, identify the single variable that matters, and when the ducks line up, act -- sometimes before the homework is done, always before it feels comfortable.

---

## Concepts

### 1. Never Invest in the Present -- Look 18 Months Out [17/19] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

**Exact quotes:**

> "Never, ever invest in the present. It doesn't matter what a company's earning, what they have earned... you have to visualize the situation 18 months from now, and whatever that is, that's where the price will be, not where it is today." (LTC)

> "Do not, do not invest in the present. It's not what moves stock prices -- change moves them. And I want you to try and envision a different world in a year and a half from now and where these security prices would trade versus now, given the world you envisioned." (SOHN22)

> "Tell me how people are gonna think differently in 18 or 24 months about the situation than they're thinking." (SOHN22, quoting Drelles)

> "I just try to look at the current situation and then I try to think of a situation 12 to 18 months from now based on my forecast." (SEC)

> "People live in the past." (DA22)

> "If you look at today, you're not going to make any money. If you try and look ahead, and what might change and how investors might perceive something ahead." (MS26, on the Teva trade)

> "I have found it's very important never to invest in the present. Always try and envision the situation as you see it in 18 to 24 months and then see if, if you feel things will be differently than they are now, would security prices reflect that?" (NB24v2)

**Practical meaning:** The market is a discounting machine. By the time something shows up in current earnings, trailing metrics, or today's headlines, it is already priced in. The money is made by seeing where the puck is going -- specifically, by forecasting how the market's perception will change over the next 12-24 months. Current fundamentals are the starting position, not the investment thesis. The thesis is always about change from the current state.

**Coaching deployment:** When a user evaluates a stock based on current strong earnings, trailing P/E ratios, or says "the economy is doing great right now," redirect immediately. The question is never "what is true today?" but "how will people think differently about this situation in 18 months?" This is the single most frequently repeated principle across every source -- Druckenmiller calls it "one of the very first things I learned in the business."

### 2. Invest First, Investigate Later [9/19]

**Exact quotes:**

> "If some analyst comes in with a great idea... if they have an idea that appeals to me intuitively and I really like it and it might fit in with the macro matrix... I might put on a very large position and then over the next 2 or 3 weeks tell them to really do their homework." (SOHN22)

> "You just don't have the time you had when I got in the business. When I hear a good idea, if you wait 2 or 3 weeks, a lot of times now, believe it or not, 60 or 70% of the move will take in place." (SOHN22)

> "I follow the old Soros rule, invest and then investigate. I bought all of them." (SB24)

**Practical meaning:** When an idea passes the intuition filter and fits the macro framework, establish a position immediately and do the deep analysis second. Markets move faster now -- waiting 2-3 weeks for complete information can cost 60-70% of the move. The initial position is sized for the uncertainty; full sizing comes only after investigation confirms the thesis. This is not recklessness -- it is a structured response to the speed at which modern markets incorporate information.

**Coaching deployment:** When a user has a strong macro thesis or intuitive conviction but keeps delaying entry to do "more research," deploy this concept hard. The cost of analysis paralysis is quantifiable: 60-70% of the move. Ask: "Is the remaining 30-40% of research going to change your view, or just make you feel better about a decision you've already made?" Also use when a fast-moving catalyst (policy change, geopolitical event) creates a time-sensitive opportunity.

### 3. Valuation for Distance, Technicals for Timing [6/19]

**Exact quotes:**

> "I never use valuation to time the market. I use liquidity considerations and technical analysis for timing. Valuation only tells me how far the market can go once a catalyst enters the picture to change the market direction." (NMW)

> "A lot of my style is you build a thesis, hopefully one that no one else has built; you sort of put some positions on; and then when the thesis starts to evolve, and people get on and you see the momentum start to change in your favor, then you really go for it." (RV18)

> "I like to buy not in the zero inning and maybe not in the first inning, but no later than the second inning." (RV18)

**Practical meaning:** Valuation and timing answer different questions. Valuation tells you the potential magnitude of a move -- how far the price can travel. Technicals, price confirmation, and liquidity signals tell you when to pull the trigger. The two must not be conflated. A stock can be cheap for a long time; a cheap stock that starts moving is the setup. Druckenmiller's "innings system" captures this: enter early with a starter position (not the zero inning -- that's catching a falling knife), wait for technical confirmation (first-second inning), then pile in before the crowd arrives (third inning and beyond is too late).

**Coaching deployment:** When a user says "the market is overvalued so I'm selling" or "this stock is cheap so I'm buying," separate the two questions: How far can it go? (valuation) and When does it start moving? (technicals/catalysts). Also deploy the innings framework when a user is trying to catch the exact bottom or debating when to add size to an existing position.

### 4. Buy When It Feels Terrible [5/19]

**Exact quotes:**

> "I've never made a buy at a low that I didn't just feel terrible and scared to death making it." (NMW)

> "It's easy to sell at the bottom. You can go home that night and it relieves you of your nerves. When things are going down it's hard to buy them." (NMW)

> "I'm almost getting angry. And every time, he gives a speech, I keep buying more and more and more." (RV18, on buying Treasuries against Greenspan's hawkish rhetoric in 2000)

> "If they didn't devalue in the next six months, my fund was going to lose 50 basis points; if they did devalue, I was going to make 2,000 basis points. So it was a 40-1 one-way risk-reward bet." (DA22, on the pound trade)

**Practical meaning:** The discomfort is the signal, not a warning. If buying at the low felt good, everyone would already be in and the opportunity would not exist. Emotional discomfort at the point of entry is a structural feature of contrarian investing -- it is evidence that the consensus is positioned against you, which is exactly why the asymmetry exists. The way to manage the discomfort is not to avoid it but to reframe it: quantify the risk-reward ratio. When downside is capped at 50 basis points and upside is 2,000, the feeling of terror is irrelevant to the math.

**Coaching deployment:** When a user is paralyzed by fear during a selloff, or when they sold at the bottom and feel foolish, normalize the emotion with Druckenmiller's own admission. Every great entry felt terrible. Also deploy when the user has identified an asymmetric setup but cannot bring themselves to pull the trigger because it "feels wrong."

### 5. Identify the Single Key Variable [5/19]

**Exact quotes:**

> "This is useless. What makes the stock go up and down?" (NMW, Drelles to young Druckenmiller)

> "Thereafter, I focused my analysis on seeking to identify the factors that were strongly correlated to a stock's price movement as opposed to looking at all the fundamentals." (NMW)

> "By far the best economic predictor I've ever met is the inside of the stock market. I don't mean the stock market; I mean the inside of the stock market." (ECNY)

> "Even though you refer to me as a macro investor... we do the macro by a compilation of listening to companies and doing a bottoms up analysis of companies that lead the economy... industries that lead the economy and industries that lag the economy." (SOHN22)

**Practical meaning:** For every investment, strip away the comprehensive analysis and identify the single factor most correlated with price movement. Different sectors have different key variables: for banks, it is earnings; for chemicals, capacity; for the broad market, Fed liquidity. For timing entry, the key variable is often the "inside" of the stock market -- which industries are leading, which are lagging, and what the rotation pattern tells you about where the economy is headed. Most analysts fail because they study all fundamentals equally rather than finding the one that matters.

**Coaching deployment:** When a user presents over-detailed, unfocused analysis -- or is drowning in data and cannot make a decision -- ask the Drelles question: "What makes this go up and down?" If they cannot answer in one sentence, they have not done the right work. Also deploy when a user's stock is acting contrary to fundamental expectations -- the key variable may have shifted and they are looking at the wrong driver.

### 6. The "Ducks Lined Up" Test [4/19]

**Exact quotes:**

> "I like to make very large bets, very contrary to when the ducks are lined up and I can analyze it." (ECNY)

> "When there are white caps on the bay, the pro don't play." (ECNY, quoting a golf pro)

> "I'm pretty much taking a break waiting for a fat pitch. I'm not shooting at any pins to use a golfing analogy." (SOHN22)

**Practical meaning:** Entry is a binary decision, not a graduated one. Either the environment is analyzable and you have conviction -- and you go big -- or it is not, and you go flat. There is no middle ground of medium-sized bets in unclear conditions. The ducks-lined-up test synthesizes the preceding concepts: the 18-month forward view is clear, the key variable is identified, the risk-reward is asymmetric, and technicals are confirming. When all of these align, act with maximum force. When they do not align, the correct position is no position at all.

**Coaching deployment:** When a user feels compelled to always have a position, or is making lots of small unfocused bets out of boredom or anxiety, deploy the "white caps on the bay" metaphor. Professional discipline includes knowing when to sit out. Also deploy when a user has 30 positions and no conviction in any of them -- that is the opposite of the Druckenmiller approach.

## Stories

### 50% in 30-Year Treasuries (Volcker Era)

**Full narrative:** When Druckenmiller started Duquesne, Reagan had just become President and Volcker was running the Fed. Inflation was 12%, the whole world thought it would go through the roof, but Volcker had raised short rates to 18%. Druckenmiller could see "there is no way this man was going to let inflation go." He took 50% of his new capital and put it into 30-year Treasury bonds yielding 14%, owning nothing else. The bonds went up despite a bear market in equities. He was up 40% right out of the chute. He later reflected: "Had I known George Soros when I made the bond bet, I probably would have made a lot more money because I wouldn't have put 50 percent in the bonds, I probably would have put about 150 percent in the bonds." (LTC)

**Teaching application:** This story demonstrates multiple entry concepts working together. The 18-month forward view: Druckenmiller saw that Volcker would break inflation even though the present consensus said inflation was going to the roof. The key variable: Volcker's commitment, not headline CPI. Buying when it feels terrible: the whole world was positioned the other way. The ducks were lined up: extreme asymmetry (14% yield on 30-years with rates about to fall), an identifiable catalyst (Volcker), and the courage to concentrate. Deploy when a user has identified a contrarian macro setup but is afraid to act against the consensus narrative.

### The T-Bill Desperation Trade, 1981

**Full narrative:** In 1981, Druckenmiller's firm was in financial trouble. In desperation, he plunged into T-bill futures with excessive leverage. He bought T-bill futures within one week of their all-time low -- an almost perfect entry on timing. Despite this, he lost all his money. Schwager's commentary: "The very need to win poisoned the trade -- in this instance, through grossly excessive leverage and a lack of planning." After this loss, Druckenmiller fundamentally changed his philosophy: "so that if we ever felt that bearish about the market again we would go to 100% cash." (NMW)

**Teaching application:** This is the dark counterpart to the Treasury story above. Even a perfectly timed entry can produce total loss when the emotional state at entry is wrong. Desperation led to excessive leverage and no planning -- the ducks were lined up on the trade thesis, but not on the trader's psychology. Deploy when someone is over-leveraged, revenge trading, or trying to make back losses quickly. The lesson is that the "ducks lined up" test applies to the investor's internal state as much as to the external setup. You cannot enter a trade from a position of desperation and expect discipline to hold.

### Argentina / Perplexity Discovery

**Full narrative:** Druckenmiller saw Milei's Davos speech at 1 PM in his office. He opened Perplexity AI and asked for the five most liquid Argentine ADRs. The descriptions were sufficient to trigger action. "I follow the old Soros rule, invest and then investigate. I bought all of them. We did some work on them. I increased my positions and so far, it's been great." (SB24)

**Teaching application:** This is "invest first, investigate later" in real time, executed by a 71-year-old using AI tools. The macro thesis was structural change under Milei -- a regime shift that created a new direction. The individual security selection was rapid and broad (all five ADRs), not precise and narrow. The entry did not require a perfect price; it required conviction about direction. After deeper research, positions were increased -- the initial buy was a toehold, not the full bet. Deploy when a user is suffering analysis paralysis on a liquid, reversible position, or when a fast-moving structural catalyst demands speed over precision.

### The Teva "Investor Gap" Trade (MS26)

**Full narrative:** In mid-2025, as AI stocks "started to get disturbingly heated" and rhymed with 1999-2000, Druckenmiller's team was looking for alternatives. They brought in Teva Pharmaceuticals -- a "boring" generic drug company from Israel trading at 6x earnings. The new CEO, Richard Francis (from Sandoz), was transforming the business from generics to biosimilars and novel drugs. The problem: the existing investor base were value investors who hated the growth strategy and were selling. Growth investors didn't want it because the transition wasn't proven. "So the stock sat there at 6 times earnings while you could see this incredible management initiative going on." Druckenmiller bought at $16. About 6-7 months later, the stock was $32 -- re-rated from 6x to ~12x -- "and not much has happened. Other than he's proved biosimilars have come up with a drug that's not a generic." (MS26)

**Teaching application:** This is the "investor gap" framework -- when a company changes its strategic identity, the old investor base flees before the new investor base arrives. The mispricing occurs not because the market is irrational but because the shareholder base is structurally wrong for the new story. The 18-month forward view was clear: if Francis succeeded (as he had at Sandoz), the stock would re-rate from a 6x generic-company multiple to a 12x+ growth-company multiple. Deploy when a user is evaluating a company undergoing strategic transformation, or when a stock seems "stuck" despite improving fundamentals.

## Cross-References

- **Module 1 (Sizing):** The ducks-lined-up test determines both whether to enter AND how big. Entry and sizing are not independent decisions -- a setup that justifies entry at all justifies concentration. The T-bill desperation trade shows what happens when sizing is disconnected from the entry discipline.
- **Module 4 (Reading Liquidity):** Liquidity is often the key variable for timing. "I never use valuation to time the market. I use liquidity considerations and technical analysis for timing." The Fed and central bank actions drive the macro clock that determines when the 18-month forward view begins to manifest.
- **Module 7 (Bottom-Up Macro):** The key variable frequently emerges from bottom-up observation. Druckenmiller's leading/lagging industry analysis -- housing leads, capital goods lag -- is how he identifies when the forward view is starting to play out in real time.
