# Module 13: Thinking About Currencies

**When to load:** The user is trading or evaluating currencies, trying to understand exchange rate dynamics, thinking about the dollar's future, considering gold or Bitcoin as currency alternatives, or asking about how macro forces express themselves through foreign exchange markets. This module covers Druckenmiller's framework for currencies as the purest macro expression, his legendary pound trade, his evolving views on the dollar and its alternatives, and the practical mechanics of identifying unsustainable currency regimes.

---

## 1. Currencies Are the Purest Macro Expression [7/19]

Currencies were Druckenmiller's primary profit center for most of his career. He made approximately 70% of his money in currencies and bonds -- not equities.

> "I made about 70% of my money during that time in currencies and bonds, and that's been pretty much squished." (RV18)

Why currencies? Because a currency is the single cleanest aggregation of everything happening in an economy. Trade flows, capital flows, interest rate differentials, fiscal policy, political stability -- all compressed into one price. When you have a macro view with high conviction, currencies often offer the purest expression because there is no company-specific noise to distort the signal.

Currencies also have a structural advantage for concentrated bets: they trade 24 hours a day in deeply liquid markets.

> "If you're going to make a bet like that, it has to be in a very liquid market, even better if it's a liquid market that trades 24 hours a day. So most of those bets, for me, invariably would end up being in the bond and currency markets because I could change my mind." (RV18)

This is not a minor point. Druckenmiller's entire position-sizing philosophy -- "be a pig," put all your eggs in one basket -- requires the ability to exit quickly when wrong. Currencies and bonds offer that exit in a way that small-cap equities or private credit never can. The optionality to change your mind is a structural requirement for aggressive positioning, not a sign of weakness.

**When to deploy:** When someone is trying to express a macro view and defaulting to equities. Ask: Have you considered whether currencies would be a cleaner expression? Strip out the company-specific noise and ask what the macro signal actually is.

## 2. The British Pound Trade -- The Defining Currency Trade [8/19] [SIGNATURE]

This is the most famous trade of Druckenmiller's career, and arguably the most famous currency trade of the twentieth century. It illustrates every principle he holds -- asymmetric risk-reward, concentrated conviction sizing, the role of mentorship, and the importance of identifying unsustainable political commitments.

**The Setup (Summer 1992):**

Britain was a member of the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM), which linked the pound to the deutsche mark within a narrow band. But the economies were diverging catastrophically. Post-reunification Germany was raising rates to fight inflation. Britain's economy was weakening -- Druckenmiller's housing analyst in Britain reported that rate increases were causing a housing downturn.

> "You cannot have two currencies where one economic outlook is going like this way and the other outlook is going that way." (LTC)

The political commitment to the ERM peg was unsustainable given the economic divergence. This was not primarily an economics trade -- it was a political trade. The question was not whether the peg would break, but when.

**The Initial Position:**

With $7 billion in the Quantum Fund, Druckenmiller put $1.5 billion short the British pound. A significant bet, but not yet the legendary one.

**The Catalyst -- Schlesinger's Editorial:**

A month later, Bundesbank president Helmut Schlesinger published an editorial in the Financial Times that, in Druckenmiller's words, "basically said the British pound is crap." This was the signal. The head of the German central bank was effectively telling the world that the peg was unsustainable from Germany's side.

**The Soros Conversation:**

Druckenmiller went to Soros's office with his plan:

> "George, I'm going to sell $5.5 billion worth of British pounds tonight and buy deutsche marks. Here's why I'm doing it -- that means we'll have 100 percent of the fund in this one trade." (LTC)

Soros had "this unpleasant puzzled look on his face" (NB24v2). Druckenmiller thought: "What does he not understand about this? Because this guy pretty much understood everything." The opposite of what Druckenmiller expected happened:

> "That is the most ridiculous use of money management I ever heard. What you described is an incredible one-way bet. We should have 200 percent of our net worth in this trade, not 100 percent. Do you know how often something like this comes around? Like one in 20 years. What is wrong with you?" (LTC)

They targeted 200% of fund capital -- $15 billion short the pound on a $7.5 billion fund. They never got there, but the ambition had a structural effect:

> "Had it not been for Soros, I probably would have not got to the 7.5 billion because, you know, intending to do 15 billion, I was in a bigger hurry." (NB24v2)

The pound broke. The trade made over $1 billion in a single day. "We broke the Bank of England."

**The Risk-Reward Framing:**

Years later, Druckenmiller distilled the lesson into its purest form:

> "I didn't know whether the pound was going to devalue. What I did know was, if they didn't devalue in the next six months, my fund was going to lose 50 basis points; if they did devalue, I was going to make 2,000 basis points. So it was a 40-1 one-way risk-reward bet." (DA22)

This is the trade's deepest teaching. The question was never "will we be right?" The question was: what do we lose if wrong, and what do we gain if right? At 40-to-1, the position sizing should be enormous even with moderate directional conviction. The asymmetry does the work.

**The Attribution:**

> "Make no mistake about it, shorting the pound was Stan Druckenmiller's idea. Soros's contribution was pushing him to take a gigantic position." (NMW, via Bessent)

The analysis was Druckenmiller's. The sizing was Soros's contribution. This is the difference between batting average and slugging percentage -- between having a good idea and executing it at scale.

> "I had a higher batting average; Soros had a much bigger slugging percentage." (LTC)

**When to deploy:** When someone has identified a genuinely asymmetric setup but is sizing it like a normal trade. When someone is agonizing over whether they will be "right" instead of quantifying the payoff ratio. When teaching that the best currency trades come from identifying unsustainable political commitments backed by irreconcilable economic fundamentals.

## 3. Political Risk Drives Currencies More Than Economics [4/19]

> "If you want to understand currencies, don't start with the economics. Start with the politics." (Taxonomy summary from NB24, ECNY, SB24, DA22)

The pound didn't crash because of a deteriorating UK current account deficit or a tick in inflation data. It crashed because the political commitment to the ERM was unsustainable given the economic divergence between Britain and Germany. The Turkish lira, the Argentine peso -- all political stories first.

This principle inverts how most people analyze currencies. Economists start with trade balances, interest rate differentials, and purchasing power parity. Druckenmiller starts with: Who is making the political commitment? What are they actually willing to defend? And is the economic reality making that commitment untenable?

Currency pegs and fixed exchange rate regimes are where this principle is most powerful. A peg is a political promise. When the economic forces pulling against that promise become overwhelming, the peg breaks -- often violently and suddenly. The investor's job is to identify when the promise becomes unsustainable and position before the break.

**When to deploy:** When someone is analyzing a currency using only economic models and ignoring the political dimension. When evaluating any fixed exchange rate regime or managed currency. When the political commitment to a currency level is diverging from the economic fundamentals.

## 4. Currency Regime Change Is Inevitable [RV18]

> "If there's one thing I've learned, currencies probably being the most obvious, every 15 or 20 years, there is regime change." (RV18)

The drivers of currency markets are not permanent. Currencies traded on current account balances for a while, then interest rate differentials became dominant, then risk-on/risk-off correlations took over. Models built on historical data from a prior regime will fail when the regime shifts.

This principle applies to all asset classes but is "most obvious" in currencies because the regime shifts are so pronounced. A currency model that worked beautifully from 1995 to 2010 might be completely wrong from 2010 to 2025 -- not because the model was bad, but because the underlying driver changed.

Major currency trends, once established, have historical durability:

> "I have never seen a major currency trend last less than two years." (SEC/CMG-2015)

And the magnitude of moves follows a pattern: 50-55% moves are typical for major currency trends. When you identify a new trend, size based on historical pattern duration and magnitude, not on recent volatility.

**When to deploy:** When someone is backtesting a currency strategy using historical data without asking whether the regime has changed. When a previously reliable currency indicator stops working. When someone assumes the current regime is permanent.

## 5. The Dollar Is Not Permanent [6/19]

Druckenmiller is deeply skeptical of dollar permanence. This is not a short-term trading call -- it is a structural concern about the erosion of the most valuable privilege any country has ever held.

> "The dollar's reserve status is the single greatest privilege a country has ever had, and we're squandering it." (ECNY, paraphrased across SOHN16, SB24, SB23)

The threats to the dollar's reserve status come from two directions. First, fiscal irresponsibility -- running 7% deficits at full employment, accumulating $34+ trillion in debt, and failing to reform entitlements. Second, weaponization -- using SWIFT exclusions, sanctions, and financial system access as geopolitical tools.

In 2019, Druckenmiller presciently connected these themes:

> "If we keep weaponizing tariffs and sanctions and everything else we are doing, maybe five or ten years down the road..." (ECNY)

He left the sentence unfinished, but the implication was clear: eventually there will be demand for an alternative to the dollar, and that alternative may be gold, Bitcoin, or something not yet built.

The dollar short has been a recurring position in his portfolio. In 2021, he described being "very, very short dollar" (GS21) as part of a matrix trade expressing the thesis that the US had "borrowed from its future" more aggressively than Asia:

> "Since 2018, M2 in the US has grown 25 percent more than nominal GDP... In China, M2 to nominal GDP is where it was three years ago. So, they haven't borrowed anything from their future." (GS21)

By January 2026, the dollar short was again a core position. Druckenmiller framed it structurally:

> The dollar was "at the top of its historic range in purchasing power" and foreigners were overloaded. "More like if they don't buy American assets on a net basis... the dollar will go down on its own." (MS26)

The dollar short was part of his matrix approach -- long eclectic equities, long Japan/Korea, short USD, long copper, some gold, short bonds -- where the dollar weakness amplifies returns on the international equity positions.

**When to deploy:** When someone assumes dollar hegemony is permanent. When discussing the long-term consequences of fiscal policy for currency values. When evaluating whether sanctions and SWIFT weaponization are accelerating dedollarization.

## 6. Gold as Currency, Not Commodity [6/19]

This is a critical reframing. Druckenmiller does not view gold as a commodity alongside copper and oil. He views it as a currency -- competing with the dollar, the euro, and the yen.

> "Some regard it as a metal, we regard it as a currency and it remains our largest currency allocation." (SOHN16)

At the 2016 Sohn Conference, his largest currency position was gold. He built the entire presentation as a 20-minute case against the monetary system -- then revealed the trade in the final paragraph:

> "On a final note, what was the one asset you did not want to own when I started Duquesne in 1981? Hint: it has traded for 5,000 years and for the first time has a positive carry in many parts of the globe as bankers are now experimenting with the absurd notion of negative interest rates." (SOHN16)

The logic: In a world where central banks are debasing fiat currencies through unprecedented money printing, QE, and now negative interest rates, gold is the natural alternative. It has maintained its value for 5,000 years. It cannot be printed. And with negative real rates, the traditional knock on gold -- that it doesn't yield anything -- is inverted. Gold has positive carry when sovereign bonds have negative yields.

This "gold as currency" framing has implications for portfolio construction. Gold doesn't belong in the commodity allocation bucket -- it belongs in the currency allocation bucket, competing with dollars and euros for portfolio share.

**When to deploy:** When someone dismisses gold because it "doesn't do anything" or "has no yield." When someone puts gold in the commodity sleeve of their portfolio rather than the currency sleeve. When discussing the implications of negative real interest rates for store-of-value assets.

## 7. Bitcoin as an Evolving Currency Thesis [3/19]

Druckenmiller's Bitcoin view evolved significantly, and the evolution itself is instructive. He does not hold positions dogmatically -- when the thesis changes, the position changes.

**2019 (ECNY):** "I look at Bitcoin as a solution in search of a problem."

He saw no use case the dollar couldn't serve better. But he left the door open: "If we keep weaponizing tariffs and sanctions and everything else...maybe five or ten years down the road."

**2021 (GS21):** "I don't really believe in it. I don't really not believe in it. It could be a new asset class. The answer is I don't know."

He owned a small position, describing it as "a plaything." He credited Bitcoin's adoption as an "unbelievable marketing job" -- not technological superiority.

**2022 (SOHN22):** He offered a simple binary framework: Bitcoin for inflationary bull markets, gold for stagflationary bear markets.

> "Tough for me to own anything like that with central banks tightening." (DA22)

With central banks tightening and doing the right thing, the thesis for a dollar alternative was temporarily weaker. But he explicitly held space for a future where cryptocurrency plays a major role: "If things get really bad...I could see cryptocurrency having a big role in a Renaissance because people just aren't going to trust the central banks." (DA22)

The teaching is not about Bitcoin specifically. It is about intellectual flexibility. Druckenmiller went from dismissal to agnosticism to conditional ownership -- not because he became a crypto believer, but because the fundamental conditions changed. Dollar weaponization accelerated. Fiscal deficits worsened. The case for an alternative strengthened.

**When to deploy:** When someone is dogmatic about Bitcoin in either direction. When illustrating that the best investors update their views as fundamentals change. When discussing whether to frame crypto as a technology bet or a currency/store-of-value bet.

## 8. The Swedish Krona Trade -- Pattern Recognition [NB24v2]

A less-known trade that followed identical logic to the pound. Druckenmiller identified a currency peg that was unsustainable given the underlying economic conditions -- and applied the same framework.

The teaching is not about Sweden specifically. It is about pattern recognition. Once you have identified the pattern -- an unsustainable peg combined with economic divergence -- you look for it everywhere. The same structural setup (political commitment to a fixed rate, diverging economic fundamentals) recurs across countries and decades. The pound in 1992, the krona shortly after, the Asian currencies in 1997 -- all variations on the same theme.

**When to deploy:** When teaching that great trades are often variations on a pattern the investor has seen before. When someone has identified one currency opportunity but hasn't asked: "Where else does this same pattern exist right now?"

## 9. The Euro Short at 140 -- Standard Process in Action [RV18]

In 2014, the European Central Bank moved to negative interest rates:

> "I think it was 2014 when the thing was at 140, and they went to negative interest rates. It was very clear they were going to trash that currency, and the whole world was long the euro." (RV18)

He started building a large position at 139, then added significantly more when the euro broke through 135. This is his standard position-building process applied to currencies:

1. Develop the thesis (ECB policy will weaken the euro)
2. Enter a starter position (at 139)
3. Wait for price confirmation (break through 135)
4. Add aggressively on confirmation

This contrasts with the pound trade, where the asymmetry was so extreme that normal confirmation protocols were overridden. The euro short was the normal pattern -- "a more normal pattern for me." The pound was the once-in-20-years exception where sizing had to be immediate and massive.

**When to deploy:** When teaching the standard entry process for currency trades. When contrasting normal conviction sizing versus once-in-a-generation asymmetric setups.

## The Matrix Approach to Currency Positioning [GS21]

When Druckenmiller has a macro thesis with currency implications, he doesn't just trade the currency. He constructs a matrix of positions across asset classes that profit from the thesis under multiple scenarios.

The 2021 inflation matrix included a "very, very short dollar position" alongside short treasuries and long commodities:

> "Because of the juxtaposition of our policy response versus Asia, I have a very, very short dollar position." (GS21)

The dollar short wins under both scenarios -- whether the Fed responds to inflation quickly (short treasuries win, dollar falls on rate expectations peaking) or slowly (commodities win, dollar falls on continued debasement). The matrix is designed so the portfolio profits from the directional thesis regardless of the specific transmission mechanism.

This is the practical application of multi-asset flexibility to currency investing. The currency position is one leg of a broader construction, not a standalone bet.

**When to deploy:** When someone is expressing a macro view through a single currency trade. When teaching that currency positions can be part of a broader matrix that provides internal hedging across scenarios.
