# Module 01: Sizing a Position -- How Much to Bet

**Trigger:** User is deciding how large a position to take, debating whether to add to a winner, or spreading capital across too many ideas without conviction.

---

## Overview

Position sizing is the single most important variable in Druckenmiller's investment framework -- not stock selection, not market timing, not being right about direction. He learned this from George Soros and has repeated it with unusual force across virtually every public appearance for three decades. The core insight: most investors obsess over *whether* they'll be right. Druckenmiller obsesses over *how much* he makes when he's right and *how much* he loses when he's wrong. This module covers his complete sizing philosophy: the conviction to concentrate, the rhythm that earns the right to concentrate, the gates that must be passed before concentrating, and the asymmetry calculation that determines how much to put on.

---

## Concepts

### 1. "Be a Pig" / Eggs in One Basket [19/19] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

This is the concept most identified with Druckenmiller in public discourse. He inverts the Wall Street maxim that "pigs get slaughtered" and treats concentration as the *only* path to superior long-term returns.

> "The first thing I heard when I got in the business, not from my mentor, was bulls make money, bears make money, and pigs get slaughtered. I'm here to tell you I was a pig. And I strongly believe the only way to make long-term returns in our business that are superior is by being a pig."
> -- NMW

> "I think diversification and all the stuff they're teaching at business school today is probably the most misguided concept everywhere."
> -- LTC

> "Only maybe one or two times a year do you see something that really, really excites you... if you really see it, put all your eggs in one basket and then watch the basket very carefully."
> -- LTC

> "I'm very much of the philosophy which also creates its own discipline problem, put all your eggs in one basket and watch the basket very carefully."
> -- GS21

> "I have found time and time again that every investor has three or four big winners a year. And usually, you know what they are. And where you get in trouble is something you're not entirely focused on."
> -- GS21

> "I learned some lessons very early on -- concentration, not to be afraid of concentration -- that's a big reason for my success."
> -- NB24

> "The mistake 98 percent of money managers and individuals make is they feel like they got to be playing in a bunch of stuff."
> -- LTC

> "If you can find the right leader in the right area, bet the ranch on that one thing."
> -- PHIL

> "My advantage is not IQ, it's trigger pulling. I admit it's some kind of intelligence, but Mother-in-law says I'm an idiot savant. I wasn't in the top 10% of my class. A lot of people think I'm smarter than I am 'cause I'm good at our business, but I have a very narrow form of intelligence that allows me to love and play this game."
> -- MS26

> "You're not going to sit there in a lazy position that you're not that sure about anymore. Just clean house."
> -- NB24v2

**Coaching application:** When someone is spreading capital across 15-30 positions without deep conviction in any of them, this is the first concept to deploy. Concentration is not recklessness -- it is the natural consequence of having genuine conviction. If you can't name your top one or two ideas with force, you don't have conviction -- you have diversified ignorance. The second half of "eggs in one basket" is critical: "watch the basket very carefully." Concentration demands vigilance -- massive positions force you to pay attention; small, lazy positions enable negligence. You can only size up if you're willing to monitor obsessively and reverse fast when wrong (see Module 3: Cutting Losses). The edge that enables concentration is not analytical IQ but "trigger pulling" -- the narrow gift for deciding and acting under uncertainty (see Module 10 for how this gift erodes with age and wealth).

### 2. It's Not Whether You're Right or Wrong -- It's How Much You Make When You're Right [17/19] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

The foundational lesson Druckenmiller learned from Soros. He calls it "the most important thing" and "the single most important variable" across multiple decades of interviews.

> "It's not whether you're right or wrong that's important, but how much money you make when you're right and how much you lose when you're wrong."
> -- NMW

> "That's the key to money management. It's making a lot of money when you're right and minimizing it when you're wrong."
> -- DA14

> "I think the major thing I learned with him is it's not whether you're right or wrong, it's how much you make when you're right and how much you lose when you're wrong, and that's what he was probably as good as anybody who's ever been at."
> -- NB24

> "What I learned was sizing is probably 70 to 80% of the equation. It's not whether you're right or wrong. It's how much you make when you're right and how much you lose when you're wrong."
> -- SOHN22

> "For those who follow baseball, I had a higher batting average; Soros had a much bigger slugging percentage."
> -- LTC

> "My advantage is not IQ, it's trigger pulling."
> -- MS26

**Coaching application:** This reframes the entire question of investment skill. Most people evaluate themselves by hit rate -- "I was right on 7 out of 10 trades." Druckenmiller would say that's irrelevant. A 60% hit rate with 5:1 payoff on wins crushes an 80% hit rate with 1:1 payoff. When someone is agonizing over whether they'll be "right" on a trade instead of analyzing the payoff structure, redirect them here. The question is never "will I be right?" -- it is "what's the magnitude of my gain if I'm right, and what's the magnitude of my loss if I'm wrong?" The sizing skill is distinct from analytical skill -- it is the ability to pull the trigger when the asymmetry is clear, which Druckenmiller considers a narrow, savant-like gift rather than general intelligence.

---

### 3. Grind-Then-Swing Rhythm [8/19]

The piggishness has structure. Druckenmiller does not swing for the fences from a standing start. He grinds first -- building a cushion of returns through smaller, high-probability trades -- and only then, when conviction and capital cushion align, does he swing massively.

> "The way to build long-term returns is through preservation of capital and home runs... grind it out until you're up 30 or 40 percent, and then if you have the convictions, go for a 100 percent year."
> -- NMW

> "It's my philosophy, which has been reinforced by Mr. Soros, that when you earn the right to be aggressive, you should be aggressive. The years that you start off with a large gain are the times that you should go for it."
> -- NMW

**Coaching application:** This is how you reconcile "be a pig" with capital preservation. The grind phase is not optional -- it is what funds the swing phase. A trader who is flat or down on the year does not have the financial or psychological capital to take a massive concentrated bet. The grind builds the cushion that makes the swing survivable even if it fails. When someone wants to swing for the fences from a cold start, point them here: you haven't earned the right yet. (See also Module 5: Hot/Cold Streaks for the self-assessment discipline that governs this rhythm.)

---

### 4. Earn the Right to Be Aggressive [6/19]

An explicit gate: you cannot size up unless your recent performance has earned you the right. This is the psychological and financial prerequisite for concentration.

> "If I was down, I had not earned the right to play big. And the little bets you're talking about were simply on to tell me, had I re-established the rhythm and was I starting to make hits again?"
> -- RV18

> "One of my most important jobs as a money manager was to understand whether I was hot or cold."
> -- RV18

**Coaching application:** This principle prevents the most destructive behavioral pattern in money management: getting aggressive after losses to "make it back." When someone is in a drawdown, the instinct is to size up and recover. Druckenmiller does the opposite -- he shrinks to small diagnostic bets ("bunts") until he re-establishes his rhythm. Only when he's demonstrably seeing the ball again does he earn the right to swing big. The small bets during the cold phase are not about making money -- they're a diagnostic tool to test whether his market read is recalibrating.

---

### 5. Two-Part Concentration Gate [5/19]

Before concentrating into a large position, two independent conditions must both be true. Either one alone is insufficient.

> "So believe it or not, that's part of the 'all your eggs in one basket.' Not only do I have to see the investment that really excites me, I also have to see myself sort of being in a good trading rhythm."
> -- SOHN22

**Coaching application:** This is the meta-framework that gates everything above. Gate 1: Is the idea genuinely compelling -- does it excite you, is the thesis robust, is the asymmetry clear? Gate 2: Are you personally in rhythm -- have you been seeing the market well, have you earned the right through recent performance? Both must be yes. A great idea during a cold streak gets moderate sizing at best. A hot streak without a compelling idea means you stay patient. Only when both conditions are met do you go to maximum concentration.

---

### 6. Risk-Reward Ratio Before Entry [5/19]

Before taking any position, Druckenmiller calculates the asymmetry between downside and upside. If the ratio isn't dramatically skewed, the sizing should reflect that -- and the position may not be worth taking at all.

> "The first thing I do is figure out how much money I could lose."
> -- NMW

> "I didn't know whether the pound was going to devalue. What I did know was, if they didn't devalue in the next six months, my fund was going to lose 50 basis points; if they did devalue, I was going to make 2,000 basis points. So it was a 40-1 one-way risk-reward bet."
> -- DA22

> "We weigh risk vs. potential reward and I'm willing to blow it all if I see something with fantastic leverage in terms of results."
> -- PHIL

**Coaching application:** This is the quantitative discipline underneath the bravado. "Be a pig" sounds reckless until you understand that every concentrated position starts with the question "how much can I lose?" The pound trade was not a blind bet on conviction -- it was a calculated asymmetry where the downside was a half percent carry cost and the upside was 2,000 basis points. One-way bets (capped downside, open upside) deserve maximum sizing. Symmetric bets deserve moderate sizing. Negatively skewed bets (small upside, large downside) should be avoided entirely. Always lead with the loss calculation, not the gain calculation.

## Stories

### Story 1: Soros Criticizing for Not Maximizing (The Pound Trade Sizing Lesson)
*Sources: NMW, LTC, NB24, DA22*

Druckenmiller identified that the British pound and Deutsche mark were pegged through the ERM, but with fundamentally diverging economies. Scott Bessent called from London: UK housing was collapsing, the economy was in trouble. Druckenmiller called to find out the carry cost of shorting the pound versus the mark for six months. It was half a percent. He took a $1.5 billion position -- roughly 20-25% of the fund's $7.5 billion -- as an "invest and investigate" trade.

Then Helmut Tietmeyer, head of the Bundesbank, published an editorial essentially saying the mark and pound should no longer be linked. Druckenmiller went to 100% of fund NAV short the pound, long the mark -- "because it's still a half a percent" if he was wrong.

He called Soros to report the trade. The response was not congratulations.

> "He said, 'Is that all? Double it.'"
> -- LTC

In other tellings:

> "That is the most ridiculous use of money management I ever heard. We should have 200 percent of our net worth in this trade, not 100 percent. Do you know how often something like this comes around? Like one in 20 years. What is wrong with you?"
> -- LTC

The trade made billions when the pound broke the peg on Black Wednesday (later renamed White Wednesday).

**Teaching application:** This story teaches that undersizing a high-conviction asymmetric bet is itself a form of risk -- the risk of not maximizing when conditions are as favorable as they'll ever be. Most investors think risk only runs in one direction. Soros showed Druckenmiller that being too small on a once-in-20-years asymmetric setup is a mistake as serious as being too big on a symmetric one. Deploy when someone has identified a genuinely asymmetric trade but is sizing it like a normal position out of caution or habit.

### Story 2: The Las Vegas Gambler Inversion
*Sources: RV18, SOHN22*

Druckenmiller observed that in Las Vegas, 90% of gamblers lose money -- even though the house edge in most games is barely more than 1%.

> "90% of the people that go there lose. And the odds are only 33 to 32 against you in most of the big games, so how can 90% lose? It's because they want to go home and brag that they won money. So when they're winning and they're hot, they're very, very cautious. And when they're cold and losing money, they're betting big because they want to go home and tell their wife or their friends they made money, which is completely irrational."
> -- RV18

Druckenmiller inverts this: bet big when you're hot (your read is calibrated and you've earned the right), bet small when you're cold (your read is off and you need to diagnose, not recover).

**Teaching application:** This story exposes the behavioral trap that destroys most investors. The ego need to report wins causes people to do exactly the wrong thing with sizing -- they get conservative when winning and aggressive when losing. The professional gambler (and professional investor) does the opposite. Deploy when someone is getting aggressive after a losing streak ("I need to make it back") or getting conservative after a winning streak ("time to lock in profits"). Both instincts are wrong.

### Story 3: Shah of Iran 70/30 Bet
*Sources: LTC, NMW*

Early in his career at Pittsburgh National Bank, Druckenmiller was a 25-year-old director of research. Based on his macro view about the Shah's fall and its impact on oil prices, he put 70% of the bank's portfolio into long-term bonds and 30% into defense stocks.

His boss's reaction:

> "Do you know what you've just done? You're a 25-year-old director of research and you've just put 70% of this bank's portfolio in bonds."
> -- LTC [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY]

His mentor Speros Drelles had made him director of research over eight more experienced analysts, explaining:

> "For the same reason they send 18-year-olds to war. You're too dumb, too young, and too inexperienced not to know to charge."
> -- LTC

The trade returned 100% and made Druckenmiller chief investment officer at 26.

**Teaching application:** This story teaches that concentration starts from Day 1 if conviction is there. You don't need seniority, a track record, or institutional permission to size properly. The inexperience that made others cautious was exactly what let Druckenmiller act on what he saw clearly. Deploy when someone is hesitant to size up because they feel underqualified, or when discussing why experienced investors sometimes miss obvious opportunities -- they've accumulated scars that make them too cautious.

## Cross-References

- **Module 3 (Cutting Losses and Changing Your Mind)** -- Concentration only works paired with the discipline to exit immediately when the thesis breaks. Being a pig without being willing to cut is not Druckenmiller's philosophy -- it's gambling. The speed of the exit is what makes the concentration survivable.
- **Module 4 (Reading Liquidity)** -- Liquidity is a prerequisite for concentration. If you're going to put 100% of your fund into a position, it must be in a liquid market where you can get out fast if wrong. This is why the pound trade was in currencies (the most liquid market in the world), not in an illiquid equity. "If you're going to make a bet like that, it has to be in a very liquid market" (RV18).
- **Module 5 (Hot/Cold Streaks)** -- The grind phase funds the swing phase. The hot/cold self-assessment determines whether you've earned the right to concentrate. These modules form a loop: know your rhythm (Module 5) -> grind to build a cushion (Module 1, Concept 3) -> verify both gates are open (Module 1, Concept 5) -> size up in liquid markets (Module 4) -> cut immediately if wrong (Module 3).
- **Module 10 (Aging and Erosion of Edge)** -- The "trigger pulling" gift that enables concentration erodes with age and wealth. Druckenmiller calls himself "Mr. Taco" (Truck Always Chickens Out) -- he has more wisdom and tools now but was a "much better portfolio manager" in his 30s and 40s because "back then I had courage." The sizing philosophy in this module assumes the nerve to execute; Module 10 addresses what happens when that nerve fades.
