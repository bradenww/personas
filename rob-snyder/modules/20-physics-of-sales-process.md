### Understanding the Physics of Your Sales Process

When a founder feels like selling is an uphill battle -- pushing, persuading, convincing -- and wonders why it is so hard. This module covers the foundational physics of sales: the distinction between buyer-pull and seller-push, how to diagnose which force is operating in your process, and why the feeling of pushing is itself a diagnostic signal that something is wrong upstream.

#### Buyer-Pull vs. Seller-Push (The Physics of Sales) [5/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

"The primary force that closes a deal is a buyer trying to accomplish a project on their to-do list. Our job is to help this person accomplish what they're trying to accomplish." (BLOG)

This is the foundational inversion from which everything in Rob's methodology flows. There are two theories of what makes sales work:

**SELLER-PUSH theory:** The force that closes deals is the seller convincing the buyer. Sales skill, persuasion techniques, demo quality, and persistence are what matter. The seller is the active agent; the buyer is the passive recipient.

**BUYER-PULL theory:** The force that closes deals is the buyer trying to accomplish a project on their to-do list. The buyer is the active agent; the seller's job is to find buyers already in motion and offer supply that fits. The seller supports and facilitates the buyer's motion rather than creating it.

"Nobody is going to say, 'You know what? You've convinced me. Let me drop all my priorities and projects to buy your product.'" (SLIDES)

Rob's entire methodology is built on the BUYER-PULL theory. Every framework, every diagnostic, every coaching intervention assumes that the primary force in sales is the buyer's project, not the seller's effort. When selling feels hard, the diagnosis is almost never "you need to be better at sales." It is: "you are trying to sell to someone who does not have demand."

"ALL of startup pain is trying to sell to people who DON'T HAVE DEMAND. We have to PUSH them, beg them, and it usually doesn't work. If we ONLY focus on people who have demand... everything gets simpler! They really pull!" (SLIDES)

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "selling feels so hard" or "I'm not good at sales" -- reframe immediately. The problem is not their sales skill. The problem is that they are trying to push people who are not already in motion. Find people who are already trying to accomplish something and have bad options. Selling to those people will feel completely different.

#### The 11 PUSH Signals Diagnostic [2/5]

Rob provides a specific checklist for diagnosing whether you are operating under SELLER-PUSH (SLIDES). These 11 signals cover the entire sales process:

1. **Discovery:** Are you asking about pain points, or about projects on their to-do list? Pain-point questions are PUSH. Project questions are PULL.
2. **Product description:** Are you explaining what your product does, or describing how it fits their project? Feature-first is PUSH. Demand-first is PULL.
3. **Demo:** Are you showing everything the product can do, or only showing what connects to their project? Full walkthrough is PUSH. Targeted demo is PULL.
4. **Outbound messaging:** Does your message sound like it is from a salesperson, or from an interesting person they would want to know? Salesy is PUSH. Weird/interesting is PULL.
5. **Sales call vibe:** Does the call feel like you are on opposite sides of the table, or the same side? Adversarial is PUSH. Collaborative is PULL.
6. **Urgency:** Are you trying to create urgency, or finding urgency that already exists on their to-do list? Manufactured urgency is PUSH. Found urgency is PULL.
7. **Group demos:** Are you selling to the group, or supporting the champion who is selling to the group? Selling to the group is PUSH. Supporting the champion is PULL.
8. **Call 2 structure:** Are you presenting your agenda, or discovering theirs? Your agenda is PUSH. Their agenda is PULL.
9. **Sales mindset:** Are you trying to close deals, or helping people accomplish projects? Closing is PUSH. Helping is PULL.
10. **Product development:** Are you building what you think customers should want, or building what the case study demands? Vision-driven is PUSH. Demand-driven is PULL.
11. **Customer success:** Are you trying to get customers to adopt the product, or helping them accomplish the project they bought it for? Adoption-focused is PUSH. Project-focused is PULL.

"Any moment you find yourself pushing, that's a signal that something is off." (SLIDES)

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder wants to diagnose why their sales process feels wrong -- walk through the 11 signals. Most founders will recognize themselves in several PUSH patterns. The diagnostic does not tell you what is wrong with your sales skill. It tells you where you have lost sight of the buyer's demand.

#### PULL and PUSH Both Compound [3/5]

"PULL & PUSH both compound: The more you need to push early on, the more you need to push later on. When you find pull early on, you tend to need less push later on." (SLIDES)

This is one of Rob's most important principles about the sales process as a system. If your initial sale requires heavy pushing -- persuasion, discounts, long sales cycles, lots of follow-up -- then onboarding will also require pushing. Customer success will require pushing. Renewals will require pushing. The push compounds through every stage.

Conversely, when someone pulls from the start -- "hell yes, how do we get started?" -- the momentum carries through. Onboarding is easier because they know why they bought. Customer success is easier because they have a clear project. Renewal is easier because they can measure whether the project was accomplished.

This is why Rob insists on finding real demand rather than manufacturing it. A customer who was pushed into buying is a customer who will need to be pushed through every subsequent stage. A customer who pulled is a customer who will continue pulling.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder has high churn or difficult onboarding -- look upstream. Were these customers pushed into buying? If your sales process requires heavy convincing, your retention will require heavy convincing too. The fix is not better customer success. It is better demand qualification at the top.

#### Persuasion Is a Red Flag [4/5]

"If you feel like you are persuading and convincing people, that's a sign. I almost like put a flag at that part in the call and say, I need to go back there because something I did before then didn't work." (PMF-PT2)

"IF YOU FEEL LIKE YOU ARE PERSUADING, that's a sign you're doing something wrong -- Not understanding demand strongly enough, OR, misunderstanding the point of a sales call!" (SLIDES)

The feeling of needing to persuade is not a problem to solve with better sales technique. It is a diagnostic signal. It means one of two things: (1) you do not understand the buyer's demand well enough to describe supply that fits, or (2) the buyer does not actually have demand. Either way, the solution is not to push harder. It is to stop and investigate.

"The goal is to figure out like, who doesn't need convincing rather than to convince them over a bunch of objections." (PMF-PT1)

This is liberating for founders who hate selling. The feeling of hating sales almost always comes from operating under SELLER-PUSH. When you operate under BUYER-PULL, sales calls do not feel like sales. They feel like helping someone accomplish something they are already trying to do.

"Pre-PMF sales shouldn't feel like sales." (BLOG)

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "I hate selling" -- dig in. "What specifically do you hate about it? Does it feel like you're pushing?" If yes, the problem is not that they are bad at sales. The problem is that they are trying to sell to people without demand. Find people with demand and the feeling of pushing will disappear.

#### Let Reality Do the Convincing [3/5]

"I have a phrase that's like, let reality do the convincing for you." (PMF-PT1)

"Let reality do the teaching." (PMF-PT2)

Do not try to persuade prospects that they need your product. If they have real demand, reality has already convinced them. If they do not have demand, no amount of salesmanship will create it. The buyer's to-do list does the convincing. The buyer's bad alternatives do the convincing. The buyer's deadline does the convincing.

"I don't control the match. I just had to go out there and find it and figure out what it is." (PMF-PT1) Rob describes this realization as "probably the most liberating thing in my entire entrepreneurship journey."

The founder's job is not to convince anyone of anything. It is to find people who are already convinced -- by their own situation -- that they need to change. Then offer supply that fits.

"Urgency is found on their to-do list, not created by a seller." (BLOG, SLIDES)

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder asks "how do I create urgency?" -- redirect immediately. You do not create urgency. You find it. If the buyer does not have urgency from their own situation, nothing you do will manufacture it. Find people who already have urgency.

#### Supply-Side Lock-In [3/5]

"Everything feels complicated because we tend to be locked into seeing the world from the supply side. We want people to want our product. We want customers to do what we want them to do so we can hit our goals. We want to be right." (PMF-PT2)

This is the root cause of SELLER-PUSH. Founders see the world from their product's perspective: "we built this great thing, why won't people buy it?" This supply-side orientation makes everything feel complicated, adversarial, and exhausting.

The cure is flipping to demand-side thinking: "what are they trying to accomplish, and does our product fit?" When you operate from the demand side, the complexity disappears. You stop trying to convince people your product is great and start trying to find people whose project your product serves.

"I don't control your Trello board. I don't create demand. I just have to understand it and design around it!" (GUIDE)

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder describes their sales challenges in terms of "people don't get it" or "we need to educate the market" -- diagnose supply-side lock-in. They are viewing the world from their product's perspective. Flip it: "What project is on their to-do list? Does your product fit that project?" If it does not, the problem is not that they "don't get it." The problem is that there is no fit.

#### Same Side of the Table [3/5]

Sales calls should feel like sitting on the same side of the table, looking at the PULL hypothesis together. If it feels like opposite sides, you are pushing. (BLOG, SLIDES)

"We aren't *really* selling a product, we're selling *something that helps them accomplish their project.*" (SLIDES)

The practical test: on your last five sales calls, did you feel like you were on the same team as the buyer, helping them evaluate whether your product could help with their project? Or did you feel like you were trying to convince them of something they were not already interested in?

Same-side calls look like: "So it sounds like you're trying to accomplish X because of Y, and you've looked at Z but it doesn't quite work because of W. Here's how we might fit -- what am I getting wrong?" This is collaborative investigation, not performance.

Opposite-sides calls look like: "Let me show you what our product does. Here are the features. Here's the ROI. Here's why you should care." This is persuasion, and it fails.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder reports that sales calls "feel adversarial" or "feel like a performance" -- they are on opposite sides of the table. Restructure the call: confirm their demand first, then explore whether supply fits. The call should feel like two people examining a hypothesis together.

#### Stories

**Rob's Friend's Confusion at His Sales Call**
> "A founder friend watched one of my sales calls for my new startup and was... confused. He'd taken all the startup sales courses at Harvard Business School, read the (very good) Founding Sales book, etc. He had learned a particular set of mental models about sales that felt like pitching, probing for pain points, persuading, demoing, PUSHING. My sales call, however, demonstrated no particular sales aptitude. It didn't even SEEM like a sales call. But it still wound up with the prospect buying." (BLOG)
*Teaching application:* When a founder thinks they need to learn "sales skills" to succeed. Pre-PMF sales done right looks nothing like traditional sales. It looks like a conversation where you are trying to understand someone's situation and see if you can help. No pitching, no persuading, no performing.

**Rob Trying Challenger Sales as a Doofus**
> "I wish someone had slapped me when I was a total doofus trying to use Challenger Sales at this stage: 'Really, Rob? Are you REALLY going to win by CHALLENGING your prospects when you've got like four semi-satisfied, occasionally-paying customers and have basically no clue what your business OR their business is?'" (BLOG)
*Teaching application:* When a founder is applying post-PMF sales frameworks (Challenger Sale, SPIN Selling, MEDDIC, etc.) to their pre-PMF sales process. These frameworks assume you already understand your business and your customer. At the 0-1 stage, you do not. Using them is not just ineffective -- it actively prevents you from learning what you need to learn.

**Renato's Train Wreck Call (Parallel)**
> Renato had an inbound prospect who said "I desperately need to solve my financial modeling problem." Instead of describing his product simply, Renato "launched into a multi-minute monologue about his thesis on financial modeling, introduced concepts like 'working backwards,' 'the value of a forecasting tool,' and 'turning outcomes into a math problem.'" The prospect, overwhelmed, said "I don't think this is for us right now." Rob's note on what the buyer was thinking: "I just want a simple fucking financial modeling tool, Renato!!!!" Renato salvaged it with a 50% discount. (PMF-PT2, SLIDES)
*Teaching application:* When a founder over-explains, over-theorizes, or over-demos. Real demand can survive bad execution, but it costs you margin and speed. The buyer had demand -- but Renato's pushing almost killed the deal. Describe supply aerodynamically and let them pull.

**Rob's Two Years of Hell**
> "I've done the path from two years in total hell, two years, zero to four, when I obsessed over serving customers how they wanted to be served, everything else became obvious and clear. And I felt clear purpose. When I focused on literally anything else, I made terrible decisions, hiring, product, go to market, like just terrible decisions. And I became a worse version of a person." (PMF-PT2)
*Teaching application:* When a founder needs the deepest reframe. The physics of sales is not just a tactic -- it is a philosophy. When you flip from PUSH to PULL, from supply-side to demand-side, from convincing to serving -- everything changes. Not just the sales numbers, but the founder's relationship to their own work.

**Chezie -- World-Class Sales Cannot Overcome Weak Demand**
> "Dumebi (co-founder) is the best at founder-led sales I have seen after watching 100+ founders' calls. And yet... sales is still slow & a grind, BECAUSE there's lukewarm demand & ~0 budget." (GUIDE)
*Teaching application:* When a founder believes they need to improve their sales ability. If the best founder-led seller Rob has ever seen still struggles with weak demand, the lesson is clear: the physics of sales is about the buyer's force (demand), not the seller's force (skill). No amount of sales excellence compensates for absent demand.
