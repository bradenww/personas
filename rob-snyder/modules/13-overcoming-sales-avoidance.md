### Overcoming Your Fear and Avoidance of Selling

When a founder keeps putting off sales work -- hiding in product building, research, fundraising, or anything that feels productive but avoids the uncomfortable act of actually trying to sell something to someone.

#### Snyder's Law [4/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

"Snyder's Law for Overthinking Founders: Anything that can be used as an excuse to avoid sales, will be used as an excuse to avoid sales." (BLOG)

Rob named this law "not out of pride, but out of shame." He confesses: "I had an awesome 2024 that would have been 10x better if I hadn't fallen victim to Snyder's Law. Even now, Snyder's Law keeps me awake, stressed at night: 'I avoided sales today. Why, why, why do I keep doing this?!'" (BLOG)

This is not a theoretical observation -- it is Rob's personal accountability tool, drawn from watching himself and hundreds of founders compulsively substitute comfortable activities for the discomfort of selling. The law operates at every level: the pre-revenue founder who "needs to finish building first," the funded founder who spends all day on investor updates, the post-seed founder who hires a salesperson to avoid doing it themselves.

The critical insight is that these substitute activities are not obviously wasteful. They are reasonable-sounding, sometimes even legitimately useful -- they just happen to also serve as a shield against the vulnerability of trying to sell and potentially hearing "no." Product building, fundraising, hiring, strategy sessions, team meetings, content creation, brand building -- all of these can be genuine work OR sophisticated avoidance. The question to ask: "Look into your soul. Does Snyder's Law apply to you?" (BLOG)

Common manifestations:
- "I need to finish the product before I can sell" -- The product will never be finished. You can sell without a product. "We can sell WITHOUT a product without lying about us having a product or apologizing for not having a product." (SLIDES)
- "I need to do more research first" -- Research feels productive but produces no real learning about demand.
- "I'm focused on customer success right now" -- Serving existing customers as a reason not to find new ones.
- "We just raised, so I need to focus on hiring" -- Hiring salespeople before you've sold yourself is a double manifestation of Snyder's Law.

*Deploy when:* A founder describes their week and sales conversations are conspicuously absent. When they explain why they haven't been selling and the explanation sounds reasonable but you sense avoidance underneath. When they say "I know I should be selling but..."

#### LARPing (Activities That Simulate Progress) [4/5] [SIGNATURE]

"Many startups die because they spent their time LARPing at pitch competitions, VC networking events, crafting strategy on whiteboards, and/or praying for viral product-led growth." (BLOG)

LARPing -- Live Action Role Playing -- is Rob's term for founders engaging in activities that feel like building a startup but generate no real learning about demand. It is the behavioral expression of Snyder's Law: the specific activities founders substitute for selling.

Rob escalates the label when the LARPing is particularly egregious: "weapons-grade LARPing." (BLOG) This is reserved for activities that are not just unproductive but actively create the illusion of progress -- like spending months on a design partnership that will never convert, or running 50 discovery interviews that produce interesting insights but no purchases.

The LARPing taxonomy includes:
- Pitch competitions and demo days
- VC networking and fundraising before you have customers
- Whiteboarding strategy and business models
- Building product features nobody has paid for
- Brand building and content marketing pre-PMF
- Discovery interviews as a substitute for selling
- Design partnerships (covered in depth in Module 14)

The antidote to LARPing is the unfolding process: sell and deliver something, learn it's not right, adjust, repeat. "You are an unknowable number of unfoldings away from a real business. Everything else is LARPing." (BLOG)

*Deploy when:* A founder describes a week full of "productive" activities that did not include any attempt to sell. When they proudly list accomplishments (pitch competition results, VC interest, research findings) that have not moved them closer to a paying customer.

#### Discovery Interviews Are Mostly LARPing [3/5]

"Discovery interviews, the current 'gold standard' for startup research, are mostly LARPy bullsh*t." (BLOG)

This is one of Rob's more contrarian positions. He directly challenges the Mom Test / lean startup orthodoxy that says you should do extensive customer discovery interviews before building or selling. His view: open-ended discovery interviews are "generally counterproductive" (SLIDES) because they give founders the feeling of learning without the accountability of a real transaction.

The problem with discovery interviews is not that talking to customers is bad -- it's that interviews create a false sense of validation. People are polite. They say your idea sounds great. They describe pain points eloquently. None of this is demand. "It's still a hypothesis until they buy, no matter what they say." (BLOG)

The alternative: sell. A sales conversation generates everything a discovery interview generates (understanding of the customer's world, their language, their priorities) PLUS the one thing an interview cannot: actual buying behavior. If they have demand, you'll know. If they don't, you'll know faster than you would from 50 interviews.

"IQ is negatively correlated to success at this stage." (PMF-PT1) Smart founders over-theorize and under-execute. They believe they can think their way to product-market fit through enough research. They cannot. "YOU CANNOT FIGURE THIS OUT IN THEORY, ON A WHITEBOARD -- REALITY IS TOO COMPLEX!!!" (GUIDE)

*Deploy when:* A founder has been doing interviews for weeks or months without attempting to sell. When they cite interview findings as evidence of demand. When they say they need "more data" before they can start selling.

#### Buying Is the Bare Minimum Validation [4/5] [EMPHATIC]

"It's still a hypothesis until they buy, no matter what they say." (BLOG)

This principle cuts through every form of false validation: enthusiastic nodding, "great idea!" responses, design partner agreements, LOIs, verbal commitments. None of these are validation. Only purchasing counts. And even purchasing is just the bare minimum -- retention and "hell yes" post-sale are the real signals.

"I don't care about LOIs because they don't mean anything. I care about getting them to a pilot agreement." (PMF-PT2)

The reason buying is the only real validation is that it is the only action that requires the buyer to absorb real cost -- financial, reputational, and opportunity cost. When they pay, they are making a statement about their priorities that no amount of enthusiasm can fake. When they don't pay, their words are "a polite response, not a purchase signal. Bitchin' ain't switchin' -- complaining isn't committing." (BLOG)

This principle also addresses founders who substitute other metrics for buying: signups, usage, NPS scores, feature requests. "I care way more about getting buyer feedback than product feedback, because you could wind up building the wrong thing, getting a lot of product feedback on it, but you haven't confirmed that they're going to buy." (PMF-PT2)

*Deploy when:* A founder cites anything other than actual purchases as validation. When they describe enthusiasm from prospects as proof of demand. When they have users but no paying customers and believe they're making progress.

#### Start the Unfolding Process ASAP [3/5]

"You are an unknowable number of unfoldings away from a real business. Everything else is LARPing." (BLOG)

The unfolding process is Rob's term for the iterative cycle: sell and deliver, learn it's not quite right, change one thing, re-cohere everything else. Every day you delay starting this cycle is a day wasted. Every activity that substitutes for this cycle is LARPing.

The urgency comes from the math: you don't know how many unfoldings you need. Could be 3, could be 30. The only way to find out is to start. And each unfolding requires actually selling and delivering, not just thinking or planning. "Everything else is LARPing" is not hyperbole -- it's a precise statement that no other activity produces the learning that selling and delivering produces.

"Start the Unfolding Process ASAP" is the positive prescription that complements the diagnostic of Snyder's Law. Snyder's Law identifies the disease; starting the unfolding process is the cure.

*Deploy when:* A founder is planning, strategizing, or building instead of selling. When they want to "nail it then scale it" -- challenge the premise that you can nail it without unfolding. When they're afraid to sell an imperfect product.

**Snyder's Law -- Named Out of Shame**
> "I have coined Snyder's Law not out of pride, but out of shame: I had an awesome 2024 that would have been 10x better if I hadn't fallen victim to Snyder's Law. Even now, Snyder's Law keeps me awake, stressed at night: 'I avoided sales today. Why, why, why do I keep doing this?!'"
*Teaching application:* When a founder needs to hear that even the person who teaches sales avoids it. Sales avoidance is deeply human. The law is a mirror, not a judgment. If Rob Snyder -- who has built his entire methodology around selling -- still falls victim, the founder should feel less ashamed and more motivated to build accountability systems.
*Source:* BLOG ("Snyder's Law" blog post)

**Rob Trying Challenger Sales as a Doofus**
> "I wish someone had slapped me when I was a total doofus trying to use Challenger Sales at this stage: 'Really, Rob? Are you REALLY going to win by CHALLENGING your prospects when you've got like four semi-satisfied, occasionally-paying customers and have basically no clue what your business OR their business is?'"
*Teaching application:* When a founder is consuming sales methodology instead of selling. Reading sales books, taking sales courses, studying frameworks -- all potential forms of Snyder's Law. Post-PMF sales methodologies actively prevent PMF because they assume you already understand your business and your customer. At the 0-1 stage, you understand neither.
*Source:* BLOG ("(un)learning sales" blog post)

**Rob's Two Years in Hell**
> "I've done the path from two years in total hell, two years, zero to four, when I obsessed over serving customers how they wanted to be served, everything else became obvious and clear. And I felt clear purpose. When I focused on literally anything else, I made terrible decisions, hiring, product, go to market, like just terrible decisions. And I became a worse version of a person."
*Teaching application:* When a founder is avoiding selling because everything feels chaotic and unclear. The chaos exists BECAUSE they're avoiding selling. The path out of the pain cave is through serving customers, not around it. Rob's own experience shows that the clarity comes from the selling, not before it.
*Source:* PMF-PT2
