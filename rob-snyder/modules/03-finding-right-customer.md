### Finding the Right Customer at the Right Moment

This module addresses the targeting problem: the founder who has some deals that close fast and others that drag forever, or who is overwhelmed by the question "who should I be selling to?" The key insight is that demand is not static -- it is triggered by specific situational events, and understanding WHEN someone transitions from coping to needing-to-change is the most powerful lever in the entire go-to-market.

---

#### The "When" That Creates Demand / Trigger Events [4/5] [EMPHATIC]

This is the concept that turns the PULL framework from a diagnostic into a targeting strategy. It answers the question: "Why do some deals close in three weeks and others take twelve months?"

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"There is a moment when our product is a hell yes for a particular person, when they would be weird not to buy it."

"Demand is not static -- it is triggered by a situational event. Understanding when someone transitions from 'coping with the status quo' to 'needing to change' is the key to efficient go-to-market."

"People can suffer. They can cope forever. Demand is about when they need to change."

Rob teaches founders to study their fastest-closing deals and ask: what was happening in the buyer's world at the moment they pulled? What event forced the project from the backlog to the critical path? This event -- the trigger -- is the "when" that creates demand. It is causal, not correlated.

Bob Moesta's framing, which Rob frequently cites: "Bob Moesta talks about filming a documentary of their lives leading up to that moment and figuring out what's forcing them to prioritize that project at that very moment."

**What it means in practice:** Once you identify the trigger event, your entire go-to-market strategy should be built around finding people at that exact moment. You stop targeting a demographic or a persona and start targeting a situation. The question shifts from "who is my ideal customer?" to "who is experiencing the event that creates demand right now?"

The word "next" is often critical. GiveCard discovered that demand existed for organizations launching their *next* disbursement program -- not their first, not "someday," but the next one. The specificity of timing is the entire difference between a three-week sales cycle and a twelve-month one.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder has a mix of fast and slow sales cycles -- analyze the fast ones for the common trigger
- When a founder says "they should always want this" -- there is no "always"; there is only a moment
- When a founder asks "who is my ICP?" -- reframe: "What is the event that makes someone need this right now?"
- When a founder is frustrated by long sales cycles -- long cycles usually mean the trigger hasn't fired yet

#### The Trello Board in Their Brain [3/5] [SIGNATURE]

This is Rob's signature metaphor for how demand works at the individual level. It makes the abstract concept of "priorities" viscerally concrete.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"I don't control your Trello board. I don't create demand. I just have to understand it and design around it!"

Everyone has a mental Trello board of projects. Most projects are in the backlog -- things they know they should do, things they've thought about, things that would be nice. Only one project is on the critical path at any given time. Situations -- external events, crises, mandates, deadlines -- FORCE projects to move from the backlog to the critical path.

Your product "fits" into a project that is already on someone's critical path. You cannot move projects onto their Trello board. You cannot rearrange their priorities. You can only find people whose Trello board already has your project on the critical path.

**What it means in practice:** When a prospect says "this is interesting but not a priority right now," they are giving you a perfectly accurate description of their Trello board. Your project is in their backlog. No amount of persuasion will move it to the critical path. Something in their world has to change to force that move. Your job is to find the people for whom the move has already happened.

This metaphor also explains why "education" and "evangelism" fail. You can make someone aware of a project they should do, but you cannot make it their #1 priority. Their #1 priority is determined by their situation, not by your messaging.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder says "they understand the value but won't prioritize it" -- it's not on their critical path
- When a founder wants to "move up the priority list" for a prospect -- you can't control their Trello board
- When a founder asks how to get prospects to care more -- find different prospects, ones whose Trello board already has your project on the critical path

#### The "Weird Not to Buy" Filter [4/5]

This is the litmus test Rob applies to every target customer hypothesis. It flips the default question from "could they buy?" to "would it be strange if they didn't?"

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"0-1 is about figuring out who, in what situation, is weird not to buy. This is the hard part that makes everything else easier."

"We don't want to do things that 'could work'; we want to do things that would be 'weird if they didn't work.'"

"There is a moment when our product is a hell yes for a particular person, when they would be weird not to buy it."

**What it means in practice:** For any target customer, ask: "Given their specific situation, their specific project, their specific options -- would it be strange if they did NOT buy from us?" If the answer is "not really, they have other options and other priorities," you are targeting the wrong segment or your hypothesis is weak. If the answer is "yes, it would be weird for them NOT to buy given what they're dealing with," you have identified real PULL.

This filter is deliberately extreme. Rob is asking founders to find the needle in the haystack -- the narrow segment where demand is so intense that the purchase is nearly inevitable. This sounds limiting, but it is actually liberating: one narrow segment with intense demand produces more revenue and learning than a broad market with lukewarm interest.

"If you spread your attention across multiple segments and value props, it would be weird if you figured any single one out; if you focus narrowly, and ran after that with all your might, it would be weird if you didn't figure it out."

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder is targeting too broadly -- ask "for whom would it be weird NOT to buy?"
- When evaluating a new segment -- apply the filter before investing time
- When a founder says "it could work for X" -- "could work" is not good enough; you need "weird if it didn't work"

#### Companies Don't Have Demand -- People Do [3/5]

This concept prevents a common abstraction error that derails targeting.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Companies don't have demand generally because companies are like companies don't do anything themselves. People in companies make purchase decisions."

**What it means in practice:** When a founder says "mid-market SaaS companies need this," they are making an abstraction error. Companies don't buy things. A specific person within a company -- with a specific project on their specific to-do list, facing a specific deadline, having evaluated specific options -- makes a purchase decision. Target the individual with the project, not the organization with the characteristic.

This means your ICP is not a firmographic description ("Series B fintech companies with 50-200 employees"). Your ICP is a person-in-a-situation description ("The VP of Engineering at a B2B startup who just got their first enterprise customer asking for SOC 2 and has 30 days to respond").

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder defines their ICP by company characteristics alone -- ask "who is the person, and what is their project?"
- When a founder says "we sell to enterprise" -- "enterprise" doesn't buy things; people do
- When outbound targeting is based on firmographics instead of situational triggers

#### Demand Intensity Assessment [2/5]

This is a structured evaluation tool for comparing segments and making targeting decisions.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Criteria 1: How frequently is this project their #1 priority? (P+U)"
"Criteria 2: How bad are their options at the project? (L+L)"
"Other things to consider: Is the buyer/champion also the user? Do they need to get anyone else's approval to buy? How emotional is the project?"

**What it means in practice:** When choosing between segments or evaluating why sales are slow, score your current target on both criteria. If the project is frequently a priority BUT existing options are good enough, you will face constant competition. If the options are terrible BUT the project is rarely a top priority, you will face perpetual "not now." Both criteria must be HIGH for intense demand.

Rob uses his own startup Waffle as a counterexample of honest self-assessment: Waffle scored LOW on demand intensity because "there are other good-enough PaaS options, and developers can always DIY their stack (and often like DIYing)."

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder is choosing between multiple segments -- score each on both criteria
- When sales are slow and the founder can't figure out why -- one or both criteria may be low
- When a founder has validated the problem but not the demand -- the problem may be real but the priority or option landscape doesn't support pull

#### Stories

**GiveCard -- The "When" That Creates Demand** [4/5]

> GiveCard had been around four years, had happy customers, a premium product (more expensive but way better), and a mix of "very fast sales cycles and ludicrously slow sales cycles." They had hired a fancy salesperson who couldn't crack it. Analysis of their fastest-closing customers revealed: every company that pulled quickly was "launching a new cash disbursement program. It wasn't their first program." If it was their first program, GiveCard was too expensive and they couldn't appreciate the difference. If they weren't launching yet, the sales cycle was "however long it took to launch their next program." Rob: "That's why it looked like their sales cycles were either 12 months or three weeks." From this single insight, Rob noted: "EVERYTHING about GiveCard's business can be derived from this" -- case study pitch, GTM approach (toll booth strategy: "Free disbursement program planning sessions"), and what to ignore.

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder has mixed fast/slow sales cycles and can't figure out the pattern. The pattern is almost always timing -- the trigger event. The word "next" is critical: launching a NEXT program (they've done it before and know how painful disbursement is) vs. a first program (they don't know what they don't know) vs. not launching (the project isn't on their critical path). One timing insight unlocked the entire business strategy.

**Parallel -- Finding the "When"** [3/5]

> Parallel sold a financial modeling tool to founders without finance backgrounds. Initially, they assumed founders should "always want this" (no clear "when"). Sales had "Relatively low close rates; wasn't on founders' critical paths, was something they 'should want' that 'made sense.'" After identifying the unavoidable trigger -- "When they need to run scenarios - hiring and/or fundraising and/or board reporting" -- they got "'Hell yes' - simpler demo, super-fast purchase."

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder says "they should always want this." There is no "always." Even for a product that is genuinely useful year-round, there is a moment when it becomes unavoidable. For Parallel, that moment was when the founder needed to run scenarios for a specific upcoming event (fundraise, board meeting, hiring plan). Before that moment, the product was a "should have" sitting on the backlog. After that moment, it was a "must have" on the critical path. Same product, same customer, radically different demand.

**Sparkwise -- Finding the Critical Path Situation** [2/5]

> "Sparkwise has built cool software that does 'multiplayer L&D training without a facilitator.' They had basically assumed demand was 'do more effective group training.' Selling to L&D sucks; they found that in the RARE cases when people bought quickly: They recently ran a facilitator-led training for like 20 people. They were getting forced to scale it to 200+. So Sparkwise reframed their project around this: 'scale your group training without facilitators.'"

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder needs to discover their trigger event. The method is to study the fastest-closing deals (not the average ones) and find the common situational element. Sparkwise had assumed broad demand ("more effective training") but the real demand was narrow and situational ("I just ran training for 20 people and now I have to do it for 200+"). The rare cases of fast closes held the key to the entire targeting strategy.
