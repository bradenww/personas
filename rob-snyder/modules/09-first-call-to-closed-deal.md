### Getting From a Good First Call to a Closed Deal

When a founder has confirmed case study fit on a first call but doesn't know what happens next -- deals stall, prospects go dark, and nothing converts. The gap between "great call" and "signed contract" is not about sales technique. It is about project management.

#### Call Two Structure: Recap-Status-Agenda-Outcome [4/5] [EMPHATIC]

"MOST DEALS ARE LOST IN THE FIRST 5 MINS OF CALL 2." (SLIDES)

This is the single most dangerous moment in the entire sales process. Most founders either (a) jump straight into a demo the customer requested without confirming context, (b) scramble together a loose agenda, or (c) prepare a careful agenda that the customer immediately rejects. All three patterns played out in Emily's calls at Clearly AI.

The structure that works:

1. **Recap** what you heard about their PULL from call one. "So it sounds like in your specific situation, you have to accomplish this by this time, you're considering these different options and what really matters to you is this... I'm sure I got something wrong in there. What did I miss?" (PMF-PT1)
2. **Status** -- where do things stand since last call? What do they need to understand to decide?
3. **Agenda** based on their status, not yours. "What exactly will move the deal forward is something that's in their heads that we have to get out." (PMF-PT2)
4. **Outcome** -- a project plan to get to sale and success, with next steps scheduled.

The recap is the fulcrum. If you nail it, the customer feels understood and the deal has momentum. If you skip it, you and the customer are speaking different languages for the rest of the call.

"The first part is the most important part where we align on our recap of call one. The deal status... and what's the agenda for today. Then we just follow the agenda... the outcome of this is the project plan to get to sale and success." (PMF-PT2)

*Deploy when:* A founder asks "what do I do on the second call?" or reports that call twos keep going sideways. Also when a founder has confirmed fit but doesn't know how to close. The answer is always: start with the recap.

#### Closing Is Project Management, Not Sorcery [4/5]

"After call 1, the 'sales' part of sales is mostly done... Now we're at the 'project management' part of sales." (SLIDES)

This reframe is critical for founders who feel lost after the first good call. Closing is not a special skill. It is not a technique you learn from a book. It is straightforward project management: build a plan, manage milestones, track progress, do the work.

"'Closing' seems like a big deal. It's not. If they articulate their demand + perceive your supply as good-fit... Everything after this is just 'helping them buy it'. We are on the same team!" (GUIDE)

The project plan has concrete steps: "Step one is we have to go get leadership approval. Step two is we sign a POC agreement. Step three is like during the POC, we kind of like sign a broader agreement." (PMF-PT2)

Pull the project plan out of the buyer's head. Who needs to approve? What do they need to see? What is the timeline? What are the success criteria? Then manage every step.

*Deploy when:* A founder treats closing as some mysterious dark art. Strip it down: you already did the hard part (finding demand and confirming supply fit). Now you just need to help them buy.

#### Assume Your Buyer Will Do Nothing [3/5]

"Assume your buyer will do nothing, and if they do anything, they will do it horribly wrong. It's our job to construct the project plan and micromanage it." (PMF-PT2)

This is not cynicism -- it is operational wisdom. Even proactive, enthusiastic buyers appreciate being managed through a process. They have their own priorities, their own Trello board, their own fires. Your deal is important to them, but it is not the only thing on their plate.

Practically, this means: draft the emails for them, check in with procurement yourself, send weekly status reports, create calendar invites for every milestone, build literal Asana projects with tasks specified. Do all the work. "Time kills all deals" (SLIDES) -- and the antidote to time is proactive effort from the seller.

*Deploy when:* A founder is getting ghosted after call two, or deals are stalling in the pipeline. Ask: "Who is managing this deal? You or them?" The answer should always be the founder.

#### Time Kills All Deals [3/5]

"Time kills all deals." (SLIDES)

Every day between calls is a day the buyer's priorities can shift, their budget can get reallocated, a competitor can show up, or a new fire can consume their attention. Speed is not just a preference -- it is a survival mechanism for deals.

The operational implication: compress timelines wherever possible. Schedule the next call before ending the current one. Send the follow-up email within an hour. Draft the proposal the same day. Check in proactively rather than waiting for them to reach out.

Combined with "assume your buyer will do nothing," this principle means the founder must be the forcing function for deal velocity. You cannot delegate urgency to the buyer.

*Deploy when:* A founder has a deal that's been "in progress" for weeks or months with no clear movement. Diagnose what's actually stalling and inject speed.

#### The Heat Check [2/5]

"Now, it seems to me, maybe I'm totally wrong here, but it seems like this actually could help you solve that employee inquiry problem... But you tell me, I don't know. Do you think this is worth exploring?" (PMF-PT2)

The heat check is a consent moment. After describing supply or completing a demo, before proposing next steps, you create space for the customer to articulate their own interest. This is far more powerful than you asserting fit, because when they say it themselves, they own it.

The phrasing matters: "maybe I'm totally wrong here" and "you tell me" puts them in the driver's seat. If they have demand, they will pull. If they don't, the heat check surfaces that reality before you waste time building a project plan for a deal that doesn't exist.

*Deploy when:* A founder asks how to transition from the demo to next steps without feeling pushy. The heat check is the bridge.

#### The Accordion Sales Process [2/5]

"There's kind of like an accordion here where like call one is the same, contract at the end is the same, and depending on who you're selling to, the middle part could expand." (PMF-PT2)

The sales process has fixed endpoints: the case study fit call on one end and the signed contract on the other. But the middle stretches or compresses based on buyer complexity. A solo founder buying a $500/month tool might close on call one. An enterprise buyer at a bank might need six calls, a POC, leadership approval, procurement review, and a security audit.

The mistake founders make is treating every deal like it requires the same number of steps. The accordion helps them recognize that the variability is in the middle, not the endpoints. Case study fit must always be confirmed first; the contract must always be signed at the end. Everything between is adaptive.

*Deploy when:* A founder is confused about why some deals close instantly and others take months. The accordion explains the variance without suggesting something is wrong.

#### The 48-Hour Forced Trial [2/5]

"They set up a 48-hour trial. And that time period just forces people to use it in that amount of time... How do you design it, so you force them in 48 hours to evaluate it and they cannot mess it up." (PMF-PT2)

When prospects keep asking for extended trials that lead nowhere, compress the evaluation. A 30-day free trial is a 30-day opportunity for the deal to die. A 48-hour trial with guided onboarding at the start and a decision meeting at the end forces focused evaluation.

The design question is: how do you construct 48 hours so they experience the core value and cannot mess it up? This requires thoughtful onboarding, hand-holding, and pre-scheduled touchpoints. It is not "here's your login, good luck."

*Deploy when:* A founder is losing deals to extended pilot periods or "we'll evaluate it and get back to you" responses. Compress the evaluation window and manage it proactively.

#### Dog Who Caught a Car [2/5]

"The second you get a case study right you're gonna feel like a dog who caught a car and isn't sure how to drive." (PMF-PT2)

This metaphor captures the disorientation founders feel when demand suddenly appears and they need to transition from "finding fit" mode to "closing the deal" mode. It is a completely different set of muscles. The emotional shift -- from hoping someone will say yes to suddenly needing to manage a real deal -- catches founders off-guard.

*Deploy when:* A founder is expressing confusion or overwhelm after getting their first strong positive signal. Normalize the feeling: this is exactly what it's supposed to feel like.
