### Getting Firsthand Customer Experience

When a founder has been doing discovery interviews, reading industry reports, or talking to customers on Zoom but still cannot figure out the real demand. This module covers Rob's strong conviction that secondhand knowledge -- interviews, surveys, research -- is fundamentally limited, and that founders must go get firsthand experience in the customer's world to find real PULL.

#### Go to the Gemba [4/5] [SIGNATURE]

"Go and work with your customers in person. Go in person, go watch over their shoulder, go do something with them." (PMF-PT1)

"Gemba" is a Japanese manufacturing term meaning "the real place" -- the factory floor, the place where work actually happens. Rob uses it to mean: stop trying to understand your customer from a conference room or a Zoom call. Go to where they work. Watch them. Do their job alongside them.

This is not a nice-to-have. It is the intervention Rob prescribes when founders are stuck -- when calls are not revealing demand, when interviews produce polite but useless answers, when the founder cannot articulate the customer's real project. The gemba is where ground truth lives.

"The ground truth of this is what they're thinking and what they want to do and what they're trying to do. That's where ground truth is, not how we want them to." (PMF-PT1)

Going to the gemba is how you find what is "at least in the right ballpark." (PMF-PT1) It converts abstract market understanding into visceral, firsthand knowledge of the customer's daily reality, frustrations, and projects.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "I've done 30 interviews but I still don't understand the real problem" -- stop doing interviews. Go to the gemba. Spend a day (or a week, or a month) in your customer's world. Watch them work. Do their job. The insight will come from experience, not from questions.

#### Become the Customer [3/5]

Rob goes beyond "observe the customer" to "BE the customer." This is the most extreme form of customer empathy in his framework, and he has multiple real examples of founders who did exactly this.

"Discovery interviews, the current 'gold standard' for startup research, are mostly LARPy bullsh*t." (BLOG)

The alternative to interviews is not better interviews. It is direct experience. Rob's examples of founders who became their customers:

- "One graduated HBS, got certified as an emergency medical technician, and worked for $15 per hour for months -- as he did this, an insight emerged about paperwork that turned into his PULL hypothesis and startup." (BLOG)
- "Another wanted to build software for greenhouses -- instead of just interviewing greenhouse owners, he bought a greenhouse and ran it." (BLOG)
- "Founder I know went and served as an assistant for a realtor for a month, just to figure out the *real* project." (GUIDE)
- A founder "tried to acquire physical therapy practice to see financials, went and shadowed physical therapy practice owners all day to find demand." (SLIDES)

Each of these founders did something that interviews could never accomplish: they experienced the customer's reality firsthand and discovered demand that no survey or call could have revealed.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder is selling into an industry they have not personally experienced -- challenge them. "Have you done the job? Have you spent a day doing what they do? What would it look like to actually become the customer for a week?" The insight that creates a real PULL hypothesis almost always comes from immersion, not observation.

#### Firsthand Knowledge Over Secondhand Knowledge [4/5]

"Do direct sales & directly serve customers for as long as possible, so you don't have to approach unfolding from secondhand knowledge. Never lose touch with customers." (BLOG)

This principle applies at two levels. First, for understanding demand: firsthand experience with the customer's world produces qualitatively different insights than interviews. Second, for ongoing customer contact: founders should resist delegating sales or customer success too early, because secondhand reports from a sales team are a pale substitute for direct customer interaction.

"Founders move away from the unfolding process, they split up the customer journey so nobody has a line of sight to the full success story, and bureaucrats / idiots / MBAs try to approach things logically vs. from the unfolding process." (BLOG)

The danger of secondhand knowledge is that it is filtered, interpreted, and sanitized before it reaches the founder. The sales team reports "customers want feature X" -- but the underlying demand might be completely different. The support team says "customers are frustrated with Y" -- but that might be a pain point, not demand. Only the founder who is directly engaged with customers can distinguish between noise and signal.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "my team told me customers want X" -- probe. "Have you talked to those customers yourself? Have you watched them use the product? What project are they trying to accomplish?" Secondhand knowledge is unreliable because it has been filtered through someone else's interpretation.

#### Pre-PMF Operates on Anecdotes and Intuition [3/5]

"Post-PMF operates on METRICS. Pre-PMF operates on anecdotes & intuition (seriously)." (GUIDE)

This is Rob's counter to the data-driven culture that pervades startup advice. Before PMF, you do not have enough data for metrics to be meaningful. Your sample size is too small. Your customer base is too diverse. Your product is changing too fast.

What you DO have is the qualitative texture of individual deals -- the exact words a customer used, the moment their eyes lit up, the project they described that made you think "this is it." These anecdotes and the intuition they build are your data.

This connects to the gemba principle: the reason firsthand experience matters so much pre-PMF is that you are operating on pattern recognition, not statistical analysis. You need to see enough individual customers in enough detail to develop intuition about what demand looks like. That intuition cannot develop from secondhand reports or aggregated survey data.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "the data says X" at the pre-PMF stage -- push back. "How many data points is that? Do you trust the data more than what you heard on your last five calls? Pre-PMF, your best data is the texture of individual customer stories."

#### Waves vs. Dams [2/5]

Rob describes two approaches to finding demand in fast-growing markets (BLOG):

- **Waves:** "Tinkering at the frontier of new technology, finding new projects emerging on people's to-do lists" -- e.g., Pinecone for vector databases. These founders are at the cutting edge, discovering new demand that did not previously exist.
- **Dams:** "Existing projects on people's to-do lists where current options are lacking; leverage new tech to offer superior experience" -- e.g., Lovable vs. Squarespace. These founders find established demand with bad existing options and use new technology to serve it better.

Both approaches require firsthand knowledge, but of different kinds. Wave-riders need deep technical immersion to see what is newly possible. Dam-breakers need deep customer immersion to see where existing options are failing. Either way, the insight comes from direct experience, not from research reports.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder asks "where should I look for ideas?" -- ask whether they are a wave-rider or a dam-breaker. Wave-riders should be tinkering at the frontier. Dam-breakers should be immersed in the customer's world, experiencing the inadequacy of current options firsthand.

#### Discovery Interviews as LARPing [3/5] [EMPHATIC]

"Discovery interviews, the current 'gold standard' for startup research, are mostly LARPy bullsh*t." (BLOG)

Rob is not saying "never talk to customers." He is saying that the standard approach -- open-ended interviews designed to surface pain points -- produces unreliable data that feels productive but rarely leads to real demand. People are bad at articulating what they would buy. They are good at complaining about things they will never change.

"Bitchin' ain't switchin'!" (BLOG, via Bob Moesta)

The core problem with interviews: they produce secondhand knowledge filtered through the customer's self-narrative. Customers tell you what sounds rational, not what is actually driving their behavior. They describe pain points, not projects. They express enthusiasm, not demand.

"Enthusiasm is not the same thing as demand. And is a very big difference." (PMF-PT1)

The alternative is not "no interviews." It is: supplement interviews with firsthand experience, and when you do interview, focus on what people have DONE (projects, actions, purchases) not what they SAY (pain points, opinions, wishes). Better yet, skip the interview entirely and try to sell them something. Their purchasing behavior is infinitely more reliable data than their interview responses.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "I've been doing discovery interviews but not learning anything" -- this is expected. Interviews are designed to surface pain points, but pain points are not demand. Either go to the gemba and get firsthand experience, or stop interviewing and start selling. Selling is the real discovery process.

#### Stories

**The EMT Founder**
> "One graduated HBS, got certified as an emergency medical technician, and worked for $15 per hour for months -- as he did this, an insight emerged about paperwork that turned into his PULL hypothesis and startup." (BLOG, SLIDES)
*Teaching application:* When a founder is stuck in research mode and not finding real demand. This founder did not find his PULL hypothesis from interviews or market analysis. He found it by doing the actual job. The insight about paperwork could never have surfaced from asking "what are your pain points?" -- it emerged from experiencing the workflow firsthand, over months, at $15/hour.

**The Greenhouse Founder**
> "Another wanted to build software for greenhouses -- instead of just interviewing greenhouse owners, he bought a greenhouse and ran it." (BLOG, SLIDES)
*Teaching application:* When a founder says "I know this industry well from talking to people in it" -- challenge them. Talking to greenhouse owners and running a greenhouse are qualitatively different experiences. The founder who bought a greenhouse understands demand in a way that no number of interviews could produce.

**The Realtor's Assistant**
> "Founder I know went and served as an assistant for a realtor for a month, just to figure out the *real* project." (GUIDE)
*Teaching application:* When a founder needs to understand what the real project is -- not the stated pain point, not the theoretical problem, but the actual day-to-day project that creates demand. A month of serving as an assistant reveals the reality of the work in a way that interviews cannot.

**The Physical Therapy Founder**
> "Tried to acquire physical therapy practice to see financials, went and shadowed physical therapy practice owners all day to find demand." (SLIDES)
*Teaching application:* When a founder is trying to understand unit economics or business model fit -- go shadow the business. See the financials. Understand the daily operations. The gemba for a B2B product often means understanding the customer's business at a level of detail that only comes from being physically present.

**LearnGraph -- Good Descriptions, No Demand**
> The prospect (Claire) "described many pain points beautifully, but when asked about options she was considering, her answer was 'yeah, we thought about kind of like triaging and sending one email a week.'" Wade (LearnGraph founder) later reflected: "They're all really good at describing a whole bunch of problems and pain points and things they would never buy." (PMF-PT1)
*Teaching application:* When a founder has conducted interviews that surfaced "big problems" but nobody is buying. This is exactly what interviews produce: articulate descriptions of problems people tolerate. The quality of the problem description is not correlated with demand. Only action (trying alternatives, investing effort, having urgency) indicates real demand.
