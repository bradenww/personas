### Building Your Pipeline and Go-to-Market Strategy

When a founder asks "How do I build a pipeline?", "What should my GTM look like?", or "I have 0-2 meetings a week and need more." Pipeline is not a mystery -- it is a system with knowable inputs and outputs. GTM is not a strategy deck -- it is the answer to one question: where do people with demand show up, and how do you make sure they encounter you?

#### The Toll Booth Strategy [4/5]

"If we know exactly when somebody has demand, we just put a toll booth there where they can't, they can't go past without talking to us." (PMF-PT1)

This is Rob's core GTM concept. Once you know the trigger event that creates demand -- the specific moment when a person transitions from equilibrium to disequilibrium -- you design your entire go-to-market around positioning yourself at that exact moment.

The GiveCard example makes this concrete: every company that pulled quickly was "launching a new cash disbursement program. It wasn't their first program." (PMF-PT1) So GiveCard's GTM became: find organizations about to launch their next disbursement program and make sure GiveCard is part of that conversation. Their toll booth: "Free disbursement program planning sessions." (SLIDES)

"What can we do such that when someone is launching a new disbursement program, it would be weird if they DIDN'T talk with us?" (SLIDES)

The toll booth strategy answers the question that most GTM plans leave abstract: WHERE do you find customers? The answer is always: at the moment they need to change. Not before (they don't care yet) and not after (they've already chosen).

Offstream provides another illustration: carbon project developers need help with certification. "Demand tells you what your ideal growth channel is. For Offstream... they get their customers by PARTNERING WITH THE CERTIFICATION AGENCIES. This is all they have to do; almost nothing else matters." (GUIDE)

"Demand ALSO tells you what your pricing should be. Demand EVEN tells you what your PRODUCT should be." (GUIDE)

*Deploy when:* A founder has identified their PULL and is designing their GTM. Ask: "What is the trigger event that creates demand? Where is the toll booth where you can intercept people experiencing that trigger?"

#### Founder Magic Outbound [3/5]

Automated outreach that only a founder could send. This is the pre-PMF pipeline approach that leverages the founder's personal credibility and story -- not sales tactics.

"We're interesting people (and not salespeople)." (GUIDE)

The formula: personal credibility (Harvard, serial founder, raised from Sequoia, unique background, industry expertise) + what's in it for them (advisory board, featuring on a podcast, free access, genuine insight). The combination makes the outreach feel like an invitation from a peer, not a pitch from a vendor.

Specific examples of founder magic angles:
- Advisory network: "We're building an advisory network of [industry] leaders -- would love to get you involved" (PMF-PT1)
- Podcast/content: "We're interviewing [role] leaders about [topic] for our podcast -- your background is exactly what we're looking for"
- Research: "I'm a [credential] doing research on [their project] -- would love to learn from your experience"

"Dirty pipeline... that you then clean by finding demand and iterating." (SLIDES) Founder magic outbound does not produce a perfectly targeted pipeline. It produces conversations. Some will have demand. Some won't. The ones that don't still produce learning about where demand actually lives.

Rob's own Waffle outbound achieved a 67.5% LinkedIn acceptance rate with 20% response rate using this approach. (PMF-PT2)

*Deploy when:* A founder says "outbound doesn't work" but is actually doing generic sales outreach. The shift from salesperson messaging to founder magic messaging is often the single biggest unlock for pre-PMF pipeline.

#### Design Your LinkedIn for Customers, Not Investors [3/5]

"Design your LinkedIn for customers. Design your investor updates when customers have said, hell yes." (PMF-PT1)

Your LinkedIn profile is the first thing a prospect sees after receiving your outbound message. If it screams "startup founder" to an audience that doesn't know what startups are, you've lost them.

The Malay example is the clearest illustration: "If he puts founder in his profile, if he puts YC in his profile, it is confusing to them. They don't know those things, they don't get it." Malay sells to C-level people at chemical manufacturers. So he presents as "CEO of Poke Labs" with "chemical engineer and Harvard MBA" -- because "that's what a chemical CEO is going to look at and say, this seems like a legit person." (PMF-PT1)

The design principle: "The person I'm reaching out to looks at it and they're like, oh, I would be weird not to accept this person's connection request. This person seems legit, seems like one of us." (PMF-PT2)

Specific LinkedIn optimization guidance:
- Lead with industry-relevant title (not "founder" if your buyers don't know what that means)
- Use industry-relevant background image
- Remove YC/startup branding when selling to non-startup buyers
- Feature content and credentials that YOUR BUYER considers credible, not what VCs consider credible

*Deploy when:* A founder's connection request acceptance rate is below 30%. Before rewriting any messages, look at the profile. Often the profile itself is the bottleneck -- the prospect sees it and thinks "this is some tech bro I don't know."

#### Pipeline as a System [3/5]

"Think of it like a system - & find the bottleneck. Goal: 5-10+ meetings per week." (GUIDE)

Pipeline is not a one-time activity or a burst of effort. It is a system that runs every day with measurable inputs and outputs:

Volume & Targeting -> Connection Rate -> Reply Rate -> Meeting Rate

Target benchmarks: 200 connections/week, 30%+ connection rate, 50%+ reply rate of connects, 50%+ meeting rate of replies. (GUIDE, PMF-PT1)

"Most founders that I've seen who schedule 5, 10, 15 calls per week are spending an hour or two hours a day on outbound." (PMF-PT1) This is the operational minimum -- 1-2 hours per day, every day.

The weekly review cadence: every Friday, review your numbers at each stage. Identify the single bottleneck. Fix that one thing the following week. Repeat.

"If something works, if it ain't broke, don't fix it... it's just hard to get anything to work. And then when you get something to work, it's pretty hard to understand why it works." (PMF-PT1) When a channel or approach is producing meetings, do not optimize it. Do not try to make it "better." Understand why it works, then do more of it.

"Basically Datadog decided, screw it, we're only doing conferences, because that's what works for us. And I think they did okay with that." (PMF-PT2)

*Deploy when:* A founder has sporadic pipeline -- some weeks they get meetings, some weeks zero. They need a system, not more effort. Help them build the daily habit and the weekly review.

#### Empty Pipeline vs. Dirty Pipeline [3/5]

"You basically have two choices. You can have an empty pipeline or you can have a dirty pipeline. Optimized for the dirty pipeline." (PMF-PT1)

This concept is listed in Module 11 as well, but its GTM implication deserves emphasis here. Pre-case-study, the founder faces a choice: send no outbound because the messaging isn't perfect (empty pipeline), or send imperfect outbound that attracts some wrong-fit conversations (dirty pipeline).

The dirty pipeline always wins because it produces learning. Every conversation -- even a bad-fit one -- teaches you something about where demand lives and doesn't live. An empty pipeline teaches you nothing.

The shift from empty to dirty is often the shift from "salesy outreach" (which prospects ignore) to "founder magic outreach" (which produces conversations, some of which reveal demand). (SLIDES)

*Deploy when:* A founder is paralyzed by perfectionism about their GTM. They won't reach out because they haven't "figured out" their ICP or messaging. Push them: "Get a dirty pipeline this week. You'll clean it through iteration."

#### Advisory Network Outbound [2/5]

A specific pre-case-study pipeline tactic: reach out about joining an "advisory network" or "getting involved in some way." This gives the prospect a non-transactional reason to engage. (PMF-PT1, SLIDES)

The structure: position yourself as building something in their industry and looking for experienced leaders to advise informally. The conversation then becomes discovery-rich without feeling like a sales call. If demand exists, it will surface naturally. If it doesn't, you've still learned about the market.

Important caveat: this doesn't work for all personas. Rob notes it doesn't work for pre-seed founders ("they don't care about advising") or buyers who are too junior to feel flattered by the ask. (PMF-PT1)

Ish's cofounder at Wayside used this approach authentically for HR leaders -- not as a bait-and-switch, but as a genuine advisory network that also happened to surface demand signals. (SLIDES)

*Deploy when:* A founder has strong personal credibility but no case study yet and needs a non-salesy way to start conversations.

#### Spend 1-2 Hours Per Day on Outbound [2/5]

"Most founders that I've seen who schedule 5, 10, 15 calls per week are spending an hour or two hours a day on outbound." (PMF-PT1)

This is the operational minimum. Not a burst of activity once a week. Not a "campaign" that runs for two weeks then stops. Daily, consistent outbound is the foundation of pipeline.

The math: at 1-2 hours per day, a founder can send 200+ outreach messages per week, manage follow-ups, and schedule 5-10 meetings. This is not glamorous work. It is the work.

*Deploy when:* A founder says they "tried outbound" but only sent 20 messages last week. Recalibrate expectations: 200 per week is the baseline. 1-2 hours per day is the commitment.

#### Stories

**GiveCard -- The Toll Booth at "Launching Next Program"**
> GiveCard had been around four years with a mix of "very fast sales cycles and ludicrously slow sales cycles." Analysis revealed: every company that pulled quickly was "launching a new cash disbursement program. It wasn't their first program." If it was their first program, GiveCard was too expensive. If they weren't launching yet, the sales cycle was "however long it took to launch their next program." Rob: "That's why it looked like their sales cycles were either 12 months or three weeks." GiveCard's GTM became: find organizations at the moment of launching their next program and be there. Their toll booth: free disbursement program planning sessions.
*Teaching application:* The canonical toll booth example. When a founder has identified the trigger event, the next question is: "How do you position yourself so that anyone experiencing this trigger must pass through you?"
*Source:* PMF-PT1, SLIDES

**Malay at Poke Labs -- Designing LinkedIn for Chemical CEOs**
> Malay sells to C-level people at chemical manufacturers. "If he puts founder in his profile, if he puts YC in his profile, it is confusing to them. They don't know those things, they don't get it." So he presents as "CEO of Poke Labs" with "chemical engineer and Harvard MBA" -- because "that's what a chemical CEO is going to look at and say, this seems like a legit person."
*Teaching application:* When a founder sells to non-tech buyers and their LinkedIn profile screams "startup." The profile must match what the BUYER considers credible, not what the startup ecosystem considers impressive.
*Source:* PMF-PT1

**Leon (Steercast) -- The Turtleneck Teardown**
> Leon was selling supply chain software to COOs. His LinkedIn featured a turtleneck photo, YC branding, and "Steercast." Rob: "They see this and they're like, yeah, this is some nerd wearing a turtleneck who I don't understand why he's reaching out to me." When actual target customers were shown his cold outreach, "They vomited on it."
*Teaching application:* The most powerful diagnostic for pipeline problems: show your outreach and profile to actual target customers. If they don't understand it or are repelled by it, no amount of volume or follow-up will fix the problem. The ground truth of outbound effectiveness lives with the recipient, not the sender.
*Source:* PMF-PT1

**Offstream -- Demand Tells You Your GTM**
> Carbon project developers need help going through carbon credit certification. Options: DIY or consultants, both slow and expensive. Offstream provides software-enabled service. "Demand tells you what your ideal growth channel is. For Offstream... they get their customers by PARTNERING WITH THE CERTIFICATION AGENCIES. This is all they have to do; almost nothing else matters."
*Teaching application:* When a founder is debating between multiple GTM channels. Demand itself tells you the answer. Where do people with demand show up? That's where you go. For Offstream, every customer must go through certification -- so partnering with certifiers IS the toll booth.
*Source:* GUIDE

**Datadog -- All-In on Conferences**
> "Basically Datadog decided, screw it, we're only doing conferences, because that's what works for us. And I think they did okay with that."
*Teaching application:* When a founder is trying to run five different pipeline channels simultaneously and none are working well. Find the one that works and go all-in.
