### Deciding Whether to Charge or Give It Away Free

When a founder is debating whether to offer their product for free to get feedback, set up a design partnership, or charge from day one -- and is rationalizing the free path as "strategic."

#### Design Partners Are a Trap [4/5] [EMPHATIC]

"When we 'convert' a design partner, we think we've validated demand; they think we are children." (BLOG)

This is one of Rob's strongest and most emotionally delivered warnings. Design partnerships are not just inefficient -- they are actively destructive to the 0-1 process because they create a false sense of progress that delays real learning.

The core problem: "Something happens in customers' minds when they hear the words design partnership... They're in people's minds when they hear the word design partnership, which is like the product isn't ready. I'm not gonna pay." (PMF-PT2) The phrase itself poisons the relationship. It signals to the buyer that this is a product in development, not a product to buy. It gives them permission to treat the engagement as optional, low-priority, and non-committal.

Design partnerships let potential customers "pretend I'm a startup person without risking anything." (BLOG) The customer gets free access to a tool and the feeling of being innovative. The founder gets user feedback and the illusion of validation. Neither party gets what actually matters: proof that someone will pay for this.

The time cost is devastating. Months get spent building to feedback from people who never had real demand. The feedback is real -- they genuinely do want certain features -- but the underlying question ("Would you pay for this?") remains unanswered. And the answer, when it finally comes, is almost always no.

The alternative is simple: charge. Even a small amount. Even a discounted amount. The act of paying -- even $100 -- changes the customer's seriousness and the quality of feedback you receive. A paying customer gives you buyer feedback. A design partner gives you product feedback. "I care way more about getting buyer feedback than product feedback, because you could wind up building the wrong thing, getting a lot of product feedback on it, but you haven't confirmed that they're going to buy." (PMF-PT2)

If a founder feels their product "isn't ready" to charge for, Rob's response: "We can sell WITHOUT a product without lying about us having a product or apologizing for not having a product. In many cases we can just sell a service that gets their project done, and they're willing to pay MORE for that than they would pay for a product." (SLIDES)

*Deploy when:* A founder proudly announces design partner arrangements. When a founder has been iterating with a design partner for months with no purchase. When a founder asks whether to pursue a design partner arrangement vs. selling. When a founder says "our product isn't ready to charge for."

#### Free to Paid Conversion Is Nearly Impossible [2/5]

"I have seen that happen then maybe once effectively. It's just very hard to actually convert people who are currently free to paid." (PMF-PT2)

This is the empirical punchline to the design partner trap. Once someone is using your product for free, converting them to paid is a fundamentally different (and much harder) problem than selling to someone new. The free user has already established a mental model: this is something I use for free. Asking them to pay feels like a price increase from zero, which is psychologically much larger than any percentage increase from a non-zero price.

The only tactic Rob has seen work with existing free users: "set a hard date and threaten to remove access." (PMF-PT2) And even this is brutal -- it feels adversarial, often kills the relationship, and rarely results in a paying customer who becomes a "hell yes" success story.

The lesson: never give the product away for free expecting to convert later. The conversion problem is nearly unsolvable. Charge from the start, even if the price is low. The act of charging establishes the right relationship from day one.

*Deploy when:* A founder asks how to convert free users to paid. When a founder has users with great feedback but no revenue. When a founder is considering a "freemium" strategy at the pre-PMF stage.

#### Buyer Feedback Over Product Feedback [3/5]

"I care way more about getting buyer feedback than product feedback, because you could wind up building the wrong thing, getting a lot of product feedback on it, but you haven't confirmed that they're going to buy." (PMF-PT2)

This distinction is the analytical core of why charging matters. There are two fundamentally different types of feedback:

**Product feedback** comes from users -- people who interact with the product. It tells you what features work, what's confusing, what they'd like added. It is useful for building a better product. But it does not tell you whether you're building the RIGHT product, for the RIGHT person, solving the RIGHT project.

**Buyer feedback** comes from people who make purchasing decisions. It tells you whether your product fits their demand, whether the price makes sense relative to alternatives, whether the project is important enough to justify the investment. It tells you what really matters.

Design partnerships generate product feedback. Sales conversations generate buyer feedback. The tragedy of the design partnership is that a founder can spend months collecting product feedback -- improving the product in real ways -- while the underlying question of demand remains unanswered. Then they emerge with a better product that still nobody will buy, because they optimized for the wrong signal.

"It's still a hypothesis until they buy, no matter what they say." (BLOG) This applies to design partners too. Their usage, their feature requests, their enthusiasm -- none of it is buying. And buying is the only validation that matters.

*Deploy when:* A founder is collecting user feedback and interpreting it as demand validation. When a founder has built features based on design partner requests and is confused why they can't close deals. When a founder equates user engagement with product-market fit.

#### Discounts Kill Conversion [2/5]

"MOOC tests recently went through an exercise where they tried to offer some of their customers discounts on the proof of concept, and any customer they wound up offering a discount on the proof of concept did not convert. Any customer they didn't offer a discount, converted. It's like black and white on their data." (PMF-PT2)

This is a striking empirical finding that reinforces the principle of charging real money. Discounting doesn't just reduce revenue -- it changes the buyer's psychology. A discounted price signals that the product isn't worth its stated price, which undermines the seriousness of the engagement.

There is also a deeper dynamic at play: when a buyer pays full price, they have skin in the game. They need the product to work because they've committed real resources. When they get a discount (or free access), they have no urgency to make it work, no incentive to prioritize adoption, and no real cost to walking away.

This connects to Rob's broader principle that "PULL and PUSH both compound." (SLIDES) If the relationship starts with a discount (a form of push), you'll need to push through onboarding, through adoption, through renewal. If the relationship starts with the buyer paying full price because they have real demand (pull), that pull continues through the entire lifecycle.

*Deploy when:* A founder is considering offering a discount to close a deal. When a founder has offered POC discounts and can't convert to full contracts. When a founder instinctively wants to lower the price to reduce friction.

#### The 48-Hour Forced Trial [2/5]

"They set up a 48-hour trial. And that time period just forces people to use it in that amount of time... How do you design it, so you force them in 48 hours to evaluate it and they cannot mess it up." (PMF-PT2)

When prospects ask for extended trials or pilots, the default outcome is death by neglect. They sign up, use it once, get busy with other priorities, and six weeks later when you follow up, they've forgotten about it. The trial period is not actually evaluating anything -- it's just creating time for the deal to die.

The 48-hour forced trial is the structural antidote. Compress the evaluation into a short window with guided onboarding at the start and a decision meeting at the end. The constrained timeframe forces actual usage and prevents the deal from drifting into limbo. The founder designs the trial so that within 48 hours, the prospect has experienced enough to make a decision -- and cannot mess up the experience.

This tactic is also an alternative to the open-ended design partnership. Instead of months of unstructured free access, offer a tight, time-boxed evaluation that leads to a purchase decision. The buyer gets to verify the product works; the founder gets a decision in days, not months.

*Deploy when:* Prospects keep requesting extended trials that lead nowhere. When deals are stalling because the buyer "needs more time to evaluate." When a founder is designing a pilot program and doesn't know how to structure it.

#### Stories

**Braden's Design Partnership Nightmare**
> "I am so failing with a design partnership that it is costing me tens of thousands of dollars. I have the guy, I said hey design partner cool. He wrote me an angel check. Now he's been dragging me along using my product for almost six months free. Open ended, no milestone, offered the discount, getting user feedback, no buyer feedback. I consider and now I really like stuck in this thing that I can't get out of."
*Teaching application:* The canonical cautionary tale. Braden's situation illustrates every failure mode simultaneously: open-ended, no milestones, discounted, collecting user feedback instead of buyer feedback, and now emotionally and financially trapped. The angel check created a sense of obligation that made it harder to push for payment. The "design partner" label gave the customer permission to use without buying. Six months of real cost (time, engineering, opportunity) with no validation of demand. Use this story when a founder is considering or already in a design partnership arrangement.
*Source:* PMF-PT2 (PMF7 AMA Session)

**MOOC Tests -- Discounts Kill Conversion**
> "MOOC tests recently went through an exercise where they tried to offer some of their customers discounts on the proof of concept, and any customer they wound up offering a discount on the proof of concept did not convert. Any customer they didn't offer a discount, converted. It's like black and white on their data."
*Teaching application:* When a founder is instinctively reaching for discounts to close deals. This is real data from a real company showing the counterintuitive result: discounts don't help conversion, they kill it. The mechanism is psychological -- the discount signals that the product isn't worth paying for, and the buyer internalizes that signal. Use this story to push back against the "let's lower the price to reduce friction" instinct.
*Source:* PMF-PT2 (PMF7 AMA Session)

**Renato's Train Wreck Still Closed -- At 50% Off**
> Renato from Parallel had real demand from an inbound prospect who said "I desperately need to solve my financial modeling problem." Instead of a clean description, Renato launched into a multi-minute monologue about his thesis on financial modeling. The prospect, overwhelmed, said "I don't think this is for us right now." Renato salvaged it by offering a 50% discount, and the deal closed.
*Teaching application:* Real demand can survive bad execution -- "but it costs you margin and speed." (PMF-PT2) The 50% discount was the price Renato paid for over-explaining supply. This story shows that discounting is often a symptom of poor execution elsewhere in the sales process. The real fix is not to discount -- it's to fix the upstream problem (in this case, the supply description). Use when a founder is using discounts to compensate for weak sales calls.
*Source:* PMF-PT2 (Debugging/Analyzing Calls)
