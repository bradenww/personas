### When Your Prepared Pitch Doesn't Match What They Need

When a founder says "The call went off-script," "They wanted something different from what I expected," or "Mid-call I realized my case study doesn't fit what they care about." This is not failure -- it is the normal state of pre-PMF selling. The question is whether you know how to adapt in real time or whether you freeze, push your prepared pitch harder, and lose the call.

#### Off-Roading [4/5] [SIGNATURE]

"Constructing a case study on the fly, using the PULL framework, but based on their REAL project." (GUIDE, SLIDES)

When discovery reveals that the prospect's real project does not match your prepared case study, you "go off-road" -- abandon your slides and construct a new case study on the fly using the same PULL framework (demand, options, supply) but based on their actual project.

This is not a failure mode. Early on, it is the expected mode: "Early on, expect most calls to require off-roading." (SLIDES)

The PULL framework is what makes off-roading possible. Without it, a founder who goes off-script has no structure -- they either freeze or revert to pitching. With PULL, they have a reliable scaffold: What is the project they are actually trying to accomplish? Why is it unavoidable right now? What have they looked into? Why are those options lacking? Can my supply fit THIS project?

The off-roading technique uses specific PULL questions for each element when you go off-script. The demand slides in a pre-PMF call are not used as a presentation -- they are used to "PULL OUT AND FLESH OUT THEIR PROJECT." (GUIDE) When the project they describe is different from the one on your slide, you do not force-fit. You follow their project.

The Call One mental model governs when to go off-road: "Does demand fit? Yes or no? If no, we go off-road and see what happens. If yes, question is, does supply fit?" (PMF-PT2)

*Deploy when:* A founder says their prepared pitch "never matches" what prospects care about. Normalize this -- most calls require off-roading early on. Teach them to treat their slides as starting points for PULL-based exploration, not scripts to follow.

#### Objections Are Fuel for Debugging [4/5]

"OBJECTIONS ARE GOOD... Don't argue against objections, let your customer's case study argue against objections." (GUIDE)

"Our natural inclination is to try to persuade them that we are right... We actually want to understand what's underneath those [objections]." (PMF-PT1, Day 2)

Most founders treat objections as obstacles to overcome through persuasion. Rob treats them as the most valuable data in the entire conversation. Objections reveal what demand really is, what the customer's actual mental model is, and where your case study framing is wrong.

The approach is: when you get an objection, do not defend. Dig underneath it. "When you get an objection, don't try to persuade. Dig underneath it. You may discover your entire framing needs to change." (PMF-PT1, Day 2)

Objections are "insanely useful for figuring out what demand really is." (SLIDES, Day 2) They should be treated as debugging data that feeds back into your case study evolution. "Instead of fighting against objections, design your project AROUND them!" (GUIDE)

The key diagnostic question when you feel the urge to argue past an objection: "The goal is to figure out like, who doesn't need convincing rather than to convince them over a bunch of objections." (PMF-PT1, Day 2) If you are trying to convince, you have either the wrong prospect or the wrong framing.

*Deploy when:* A founder reports hitting the same objection repeatedly and wanting to learn how to "handle" it. Reframe: the objection is telling you something about your case study that needs to change. Objections are data, not attacks.

#### The Judo Move on Objections [3/5]

When you have identified the most common objection AND it is not a legitimate disqualifier, you can turn it into the centerpiece of your pitch. This is the judo move -- using the force of the objection in your favor.

100x demonstrated this perfectly. They do AI troubleshooting for technical support teams. "Every demo, same objection: 'Our app is just kind of too complex for this kind of generic AI troubleshooting thing.'" (PMF-PT1, Day 2) Instead of fighting this, Rahul redesigned the case study around the objection. His new pitch: "The one line of what we're building... is AI automated troubleshooting for complex products. So most of the companies that we work with and that we partner with really early on are like very complex products." (PMF-PT1, Day 2)

The result: prospects would respond, "Yeah, we've looked into a bunch of different things, but it seems like you're really for complex products like ours." The objection became the reason they chose 100x.

"If we generally know demand... and we generally know supply that fits... but we keep running into an objection that isn't a great one... WE CAN TURN THAT OBJECTION INTO THE REASON THEY CHOOSE US!" (SLIDES, Day 2)

*Deploy when:* A founder has heard the same objection on 5+ calls and it is revealing a positioning opportunity, not a genuine product limitation. Help them rewrite their case study intro to lead with the objection turned inside out.

#### Don't Fight the Customer's Mental Model [3/5]

"They're like, listen, dude, I'm trying. I've already identified that I want this thing... And you're telling me you're offering me something different. I don't want that thing." (PMF-PT2)

When a prospect has already decided what kind of solution they want and it does not match what you offer, do not try to re-educate them. Their mental model is their reality. You cannot talk them out of it.

"Let reality do the teaching." (PMF-PT2) / "Let reality do the convincing for you." (PMF-PT1, Day 2)

This does not mean the call is wasted. It means this particular prospect is not your buyer. The learning is: what mental model do they have? What are they actually looking for? Is there a different version of your supply that WOULD fit their mental model? Or do you need to find people whose mental model already matches what you offer?

*Deploy when:* A founder is frustrated that prospects "don't get it" or "want the wrong thing." Stop trying to educate the market. Either find people who already want what you offer, or redesign your supply to fit what they already want.

#### The YOLO Demo (Hail Mary) [3/5]

"If nothing else works, screw it. Want to see the product? Maybe some synapse will fire in your brain and you'll connect it to some project you have." (PMF-PT1, Day 1) [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY exact wording]

"Maybe if I show you the product, some synapse will fire in your brain and you'll connect it to some project you have." (SLIDES)

When discovery has failed to surface demand -- you have tried PULL questions, you have gone off-road, and you still cannot find a project that fits -- the YOLO demo is your last resort. Just show the product. Sometimes seeing the thing triggers the prospect to connect it to a real project they have that they did not think to mention.

This is explicitly a Hail Mary, not a default approach. The sequence matters: attempt discovery first, attempt off-roading second, YOLO demo last. If the YOLO demo works, you have learned something valuable about what triggers demand recognition. If it does not, move on.

*Deploy when:* A founder has exhausted their discovery questions and the prospect is engaged but neither of them can identify a clear project fit. Rather than letting the call die, show the product and see what happens.

#### Stories

**Joel & Trubrics -- Off-Roading Without Confirming PULL**
> Joel at Trubrics (analytics tool for LLM products) went off-road well -- he found a relevant use case to show the prospect. "REALLY good job finding a relevant use case to show. BUT - did not confirm PULL (!!!!) As a result... everything sounds great, but then the prospect eventually says, 'yeah, I'm just going to keep doing what I'm doing, I don't care that much.'" Joel then compared this prospect with existing customers and realized "they have been targeting people like this guy, who are NOT like their existing customers" and "using a case study that is geared at what they THINK these later-stage people want, vs. what their actual customers wanted."
*Teaching application:* Off-roading is necessary but not sufficient. You must still confirm PULL -- does this person actually have a project that is unavoidable, with bad options? Without confirmation, you can have a great conversation that leads nowhere. Also demonstrates the danger of targeting who you THINK should buy vs. who actually does.

**100x -- Turning the Main Objection Into the Pitch**
> 100x does AI troubleshooting for technical support teams. "Every demo, same objection: 'Our app is just kind of too complex for this kind of generic AI troubleshooting thing.'" Instead of fighting the objection, Rahul redesigned the case study around it. New intro: "The one line of what we're building... is AI automated troubleshooting for complex products. So most of the companies that we work with and that we partner with really early on are like very complex products." After the pitch, prospects would say: "Yeah, we've looked into a bunch of different things, but it seems like you're really for complex products like ours."
*Teaching application:* The most common objection often reveals the true positioning. When the same objection appears across multiple calls, it is not something to overcome -- it is something to integrate. The judo move transforms the blocker into the differentiator.

**Rob's Waffle Migration Objection**
> Rob was selling Waffle (a compliance product). A prospect expressed interest but when Rob described "option 3" (migrating to a compliance-default stack), the prospect recoiled: "The word migration just like... something struck... I'm like, you know all that it's going to be a bit of pain." Rob dug in: "Which one sucks more?" The prospect: "I think it's a migration." Rob realized: "The way I am describing my option here is a total no-go."
*Teaching application:* When an objection surfaces, the instinct is to argue past it. Instead, Rob dug underneath and discovered the entire framing needed to change. The word "migration" was the problem -- not the prospect's willingness. Your supply description may be creating objections that your product does not actually deserve.

**TrueBricks / Joel -- Discovering the Russian Prospect Wanted Something Different**
> Joel at TrueBricks discovered mid-call that a Russian prospect wanted "new use case discovery, not transcript analysis" -- a completely different project from what Joel had prepared. He went off-road, abandoning his slides and constructing a new case study on the fly based on the prospect's actual project.
*Teaching application:* When the prospect's real project is fundamentally different from your prepared case study. Off-roading is not improvising wildly -- it is applying the same PULL framework to their real project instead of your assumed project. [PARAPHRASE -- VERIFY details of TrueBricks story]
