### Scaling After Finding Something That Works

When a founder has early traction -- a few customers who love the product -- and is asking "now what?" This module covers how to think about repeating success, when to hire, and how to avoid the premature scaling trap that kills most startups at this inflection point.

#### The Case Study Factory [4/5] [SIGNATURE]

"Our business is just a FACTORY that repeats this case study." (GUIDE)

This is Rob's core mental model for what a post-traction startup actually is. Not a product company. Not a sales organization. A factory with three stages: Pipeline -> Sales Process -> Delivery Process. The founder's job is to apply manufacturing theory: identify bottlenecks, reduce cycle time, improve throughput, reduce variance.

The factory framing matters because it tells you what to optimize. You are not optimizing "the product" or "the sales team" or "the brand." You are optimizing the speed and reliability with which you can repeat one specific case study -- the story of one real person who had to accomplish something, had bad options, and was helped by your supply.

"One case study can get you to $1M, $10M, even $100M+." (GUIDE)

This is counterintuitive for founders who think they need to expand to new segments, add features, or diversify. The factory model says the opposite: get extraordinarily good at repeating one thing.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "We have 5 customers, should we go after a different segment too?" -- No. Your job right now is to make this factory faster and more reliable. New segments are a distraction from the generative work.

#### 1x1x1 Simplicity [3/5]

"PMF requires only three ingredients: one kind of customer, one thing you sell them, one way to reach them. Everything else is the RESULT of PMF, not the CAUSE." (GUIDE)

This is the discipline that prevents premature complexity. Founders who have early traction often feel pressure to expand -- more customer types, more products, more channels. Rob's position is that this impulse is exactly backwards.

"Fast-growing companies DO a lot of things... but most of the things they do are the RESULT of PMF, not the CAUSE of it. These 3 things are CAUSAL / GENERATIVE. Nail them, and other stuff emerges." (GUIDE)

"If you spread your attention across multiple segments and value props, it would be weird if you figured any single one out; if you focus narrowly, and ran after that with all your might, it would be weird if you didn't figure it out." (SLIDES)

The 1x1x1 principle is the antidote to the "everything matters" trap. It tells founders exactly what to focus on and, more importantly, what to ignore.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder lists four customer segments they're "exploring" -- challenge them. Which one has the most intense demand? Go all-in on that one. The others are distractions disguised as opportunities.

#### Everyone Reports to the Case Study [3/5]

"Everyone reports to the case study." (BLOG)

The primary successful customer use-case should guide the entire team -- product, marketing, sales, hiring. One "hell yes" case study generates everything else: ICP, product roadmap, differentiation, GTM, pricing.

This is Rob's causal model in action. The case study is the single generative cause. Everything else is a generated effect. When founders feel overwhelmed by the "everything matters" trap or the "tail-wags-dog" trap, this principle strips everything back to the one thing that actually matters.

The practical implication: before making any decision -- what feature to build, who to hire, what channel to invest in -- ask "Does this help us repeat the case study faster and more reliably?" If the answer is no, it does not matter right now.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder is debating product strategy, hiring plans, or marketing channels -- redirect to: "What is the one case study you want to repeat? Does this decision serve that?"

#### Don't Hire Salespeople to Avoid Selling [4/5]

"We look to hire and delegate the hard parts of sales when we know deep down it won't work." (BLOG)

This is one of Rob's strongest warnings. Hiring salespeople before PMF is a form of Snyder's Law -- using it as an excuse to avoid the uncomfortable work of founder-led sales. The founder MUST personally sell pre-PMF because hired salespeople cannot discover demand; they can only execute a known playbook.

"It is the founder's job to get the 3 key ingredients right (and demonstrate this by repeatedly selling)... THEN can hire people WHEN it works to really scale!" (GUIDE)

GiveCard provides the cautionary example: they "tried to hire in sales, didn't work." They had hired a "very fancy" salesperson who "couldn't figure it out." (PMF-PT1) The fancy salesperson failed because the job at that stage is not executing a playbook -- it is discovering and refining the playbook through direct customer contact.

The rule of thumb: hire salespeople only after the founder has repeatedly sold and the process is repeatable. If the founder cannot articulate exactly why customers buy, with what case study, through what channel, no salesperson will figure it out for them.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "I need to hire a salesperson" -- probe hard. Is this because the process is working and they need to scale it? Or is it because selling is uncomfortable and they want someone else to do it? If the latter, that is Snyder's Law, and hiring will waste money and time.

#### Factory A vs. Factory B [2/5]

Rob presents a concrete comparison of the same company one year apart (GUIDE):

- Factory A: 2 meetings/week, 4-month sales cycles, <10% close rate, 20% churn = 10 customers/year in 2000 hours.
- Factory B: 5 meetings/week, 1-month sales cycles, 30% close rate, -5% churn = 75 customers/year in 500 hours.

That is 7.5x the customers in 25% of the effort. The difference is not a better product or more salespeople. It is relentless iteration on the factory -- tightening the case study, improving pipeline targeting, reducing sales cycle friction, and improving delivery so customers retain.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder thinks scaling means "more inputs" (more salespeople, more marketing spend), show them the Factory A vs. Factory B math. Scaling is about making the existing factory dramatically more efficient, not just throwing more resources at an inefficient one.

#### If It Ain't Broke, Don't Fix It [2/5]

"If something works, if it ain't broke, don't fix it... it's just hard to get anything to work. And then when you get something to work, it's pretty hard to understand why it works." (PMF-PT1)

This principle applies specifically when founders have found something that generates traction and feel the urge to optimize or change it. The instinct to tinker is strong, especially for smart founders, but it is dangerous. When outbound or any tactic is generating results, resist the urge to optimize. Understand why it works before changing anything.

"Don't break that." -- Rob to Bert, who was getting 15 meetings/week and considering changing his outreach approach. (PMF-PT2)

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder has a working channel or process and wants to "improve" it -- pump the brakes. Understand why it works first. Optimization without understanding is how you destroy the thing that is actually working.

#### Premature Scaling as the Killer [3/5]

"Suffering = premature optimization." (GUIDE)

"When you don't have a single customer, NOTHING ELSE MATTERS. Not 'how to build a scalable pipeline engine.' Not 'how will this work at scale.' Not 'what is the ideal sales process.'" (GUIDE)

This applies even after finding early traction. Founders who have 5-10 happy customers often jump to "How do we scale to 500?" when the real question is "How do we make the next 10 as good as the first 5?" Premature scaling -- hiring ahead of repeatability, investing in infrastructure before the case study is nailed, expanding to new segments before dominating one -- is how founders destroy early traction.

"Trying to outsource this / hire someone to do it" is listed explicitly as a way NOT to get to PMF. (GUIDE)

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder with early traction starts talking about "scaling" -- slow them down. Are they at PMF Level 3 (can replicate but not always "hell yes") or Level 4 (consistent "hell yes," just need a growth lever)? The answer determines whether the work is still tightening the case study or actually scaling.

#### Stories

**Datadog -- All-In on Conferences**
> "Basically Datadog decided, screw it, we're only doing conferences, because that's what works for us. And I think they did okay with that." (PMF-PT2)
*Teaching application:* When a founder is trying to do everything -- LinkedIn outbound, content marketing, partnerships, events, paid ads -- and spreading thin across all of them. The lesson: find the one channel that works, go all-in, and ignore the rest. Datadog committed to one distribution channel and built a massive business. 1x1x1 in action.

**GiveCard -- Hiring Sales Before PMF**
> GiveCard had been around four years, had happy customers, a premium product, and had "hired a very fancy salesperson who couldn't figure it out." The salesperson failed because the job was not executing a playbook -- it was discovering that "every company that PULLED seemed to be launching a new cash disbursement program... and it wasn't their first program." (PMF-PT1)
*Teaching application:* When a founder wants to hire sales to "take the next step." If the founder has not identified the exact demand pattern (the "when" of PULL), no salesperson will discover it for them.

**Rob's Causal Model**
> "Selling & delivering using a case study GENERATES the 'hell yes' case study. Customer demand GENERATES the case study. One 'hell yes' customer case study GENERATES everything that matters (ICP, product, differentiation, GTM, pricing). Viewing business as a system to replicate case studies GENERATES your highest-leverage focus." (BLOG)
*Teaching application:* When a founder feels overwhelmed by everything they need to do. Strip it down to the one generative cause: repeat the case study.
