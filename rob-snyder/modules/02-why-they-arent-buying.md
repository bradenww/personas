### Understanding Why People Who "Should" Buy Aren't Buying

This module addresses the specific and painful situation where founders have identified real problems, gotten agreement from prospects that the problem matters, and still can't close deals. The gap between "they agree it's important" and "they won't pull the trigger" is explained by a small number of powerful concepts about human inertia, the nature of equilibrium, and the impossibility of manufactured urgency.

---

#### Equilibrium vs. Disequilibrium [5/5] [SIGNATURE]

This is Rob's primary mental model for understanding why people who acknowledge a problem still don't act. It draws on the physics metaphor that runs throughout his methodology.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"It's *weird* for anyone to buy anything; the default state is that people are in equilibrium and don't need / want to change. Our job is to figure out who is in disequilibrium and is trying to change."

"We need to look for people who are in disequilibrium -- they need to change, are trying to change... they are blocked by something."

"We don't have to PUSH at all... Something in their world has changed - no longer in equilibrium!"

Rob describes this visually as a ball on a hill: in equilibrium, the ball sits in a valley -- even a shitty valley. There is a mountain between the customer and change. In disequilibrium, the ball is already rolling toward change but is blocked by something. Your job is to remove the blocker, not push the ball uphill.

**What it means in practice:** People in equilibrium -- even terrible, painful equilibrium with massive inefficiencies and daily frustration -- will not buy. They have adapted. They cope. The pain is real but it is also familiar, and familiar pain is almost always less scary than unfamiliar change. Only people who have been knocked out of equilibrium by some external event (a new mandate, a regulatory change, a competitor move, a leadership change, a crisis) are actually in motion.

The critical insight: the same person can be in equilibrium for one priority and disequilibrium for another. A CTO who is perfectly content with their shitty CRM might be in disequilibrium about compliance because a major customer just demanded SOC 2. You must find the specific project where they are in disequilibrium.

"ALL of startup pain is trying to sell to people who DON'T HAVE DEMAND. We have to PUSH them, beg them, and it usually doesn't work. If we ONLY focus on people who have demand... everything gets simpler! They really pull!"

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder is selling to people who agree the problem exists but don't act -- explain shitty equilibrium
- When a founder can't understand why an obviously-beneficial product isn't selling -- the buyers aren't in disequilibrium
- When a founder says "what triggers someone to buy?" -- the trigger is entering disequilibrium
- When a founder reports calls that feel productive but go nowhere -- they are likely talking to people in equilibrium who are happy to discuss problems they will never solve

#### Bitchin' Ain't Switchin' [5/5] [SIGNATURE]

This phrase, attributed to Bob Moesta, is one of Rob's most frequently deployed concepts. He uses it as a sharp diagnostic for the single most common mistake in early-stage sales.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Bitchin' ain't switchin'!" (attributed to Bob Moesta)

"People can suffer. They can cope forever. Demand is about when they need to change."

"People can complain about pain points for years, but that doesn't mean they'll change."

The concept is simple but founders resist it because it challenges a deeply held assumption: that if someone is suffering, they will want relief. In reality, complaining is the default state. People complain about their CRM, their workflow, their tools, their processes -- and then continue using them for years without ever talking to a single vendor.

**What it means in practice:** When a prospect describes a problem eloquently and with genuine frustration, the founder's natural response is to think "they clearly need this." But the correct response is suspicion: how long have they been bitching about this? And what have they done about it? If the answer is "three years" and "nothing," that is not demand. That is coping.

The diagnostic question is not "do they have a problem?" but "are they actively trying to change?" The difference is the distance between a complaint and a purchase.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder reports that prospects describe the problem perfectly but don't convert -- "bitchin' ain't switchin'"
- When reviewing a call transcript full of pain-point descriptions and no action language
- When a founder says "they clearly need this" -- ask "what have they done about it? How long has it been?"
- When a founder confuses discovery (finding problems) with demand (finding projects)

#### Nobody Buys Because You Convinced Them [5/5] [EMPHATIC]

This is the philosophical foundation beneath the tactical advice. If you internalize this one principle, nearly every other concept follows logically.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Nobody is going to say, 'You know what? You've convinced me. Let me drop all my priorities and projects to buy your product.'"

"Most people won't buy your product, no matter how hard you push, persuade, beg, evangelize."

"We can't force anyone to do anything they don't want to do; we mostly can't even convince or persuade them. When someone is trying to change, we need to offer them something that THEY PERCEIVE fits what they're trying to do better than their alternative options."

"The primary force that closes a deal is a buyer trying to accomplish a project on their to-do list. Our job is to help this person accomplish what they're trying to accomplish."

**What it means in practice:** Stop designing sales calls, demos, outbound, and marketing around persuasion. Design them around discovering and fitting what's already on the buyer's to-do list. If you are trying to convince someone to care about a problem, to prioritize a project, or to see the value in your approach, you have already lost. The job is to find people who are already convinced by reality and need help accomplishing what they have already decided to do.

"I have a phrase that's like, let reality do the convincing for you."

This principle transforms the entire emotional experience of sales. When you understand that you are not responsible for creating motivation -- that motivation either exists or it doesn't -- the weight lifts. You are exploring, not performing.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder says "How do I convince them?" -- redirect: you don't. Find people who don't need convincing
- When a founder is frustrated that prospects "don't get it" -- they get it fine; they just don't have a project
- When a founder is trying to "educate the market" -- the market doesn't need education; you need different people
- When a founder feels crushed by rejection -- reframe: they didn't reject you; they just don't have PULL right now

#### You Can't Create Urgency -- Urgency Is Found [4/5]

This is the tactical corollary of "nobody buys because you convinced them," applied specifically to the common sales tactic of manufacturing urgency.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Urgency is found on their to-do list, not created by a seller."

"If it doesn't solve their #1 problem right now, they won't freaking care."

**What it means in practice:** If you find yourself trying to "create urgency" through limited-time offers, artificial scarcity, or emotional pressure, you are treating a demand problem as a sales problem. Real urgency comes from the buyer's situation: a deadline, a mandate, a crisis, an opportunity window. The founder's job is to find people who already have urgency -- whose project is unavoidable right now -- not to manufacture it in people who don't.

When a prospect says "this is interesting, let's revisit in Q3," that is not a sales-technique problem. That is a PULL problem. The project is not unavoidable for them right now. No amount of clever follow-up will change that. Find someone for whom the project is unavoidable today.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder asks "how do I create urgency?" -- redirect: "urgency is found, not created"
- When a founder is designing discounts or time-limited offers to push prospects to decide -- diagnose whether the real problem is weak demand
- When deals keep slipping to "next quarter" -- the "U" in PULL is missing

#### People Would Be Insane to Buy Your Product (Usually) [3/5]

This concept sets the default assumption that reframes all targeting and positioning work.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Most people, most of the time, would be insane to buy your product, given what's on their to-do list."

"0-1 is about figuring out who, in what situation, is weird not to buy. This is the hard part that makes everything else easier."

**What it means in practice:** The default assumption should always be no-demand. Most people, most of the time, have other priorities. They are not broken, they are not ignorant, they are not irrational -- they just have a to-do list that does not include your project. Instead of trying to broaden appeal, the founder's job is to find the narrow segment where buying would be "weird NOT to" -- where the project is unavoidable, the options are lacking, and your supply fits. This is the hard part that makes everything else easier.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder is casting too wide a net and getting low conversion -- narrow to "weird not to buy"
- When a founder takes non-purchases personally -- most people would be insane to buy anything, ever; this is normal
- When a founder is trying to make their product appeal to everyone -- the answer is to find the few, not convince the many

#### Stories

**Campground/Shruti -- Three Years of Salesforce Complaints** [4/5]

> Shruti built Campground, an all-in-one CRM/program management tool for nonprofits. She had ~20 customers but sales calls felt good yet rarely converted -- or took 6-12 months. In a recorded sales call with Davina (a director at a nonprofit), Davina spent several minutes complaining about Salesforce: "We have been dealing with issues for my nearly three years here... it just doesn't quite reflect the story that we're trying to tell two funders." However, Davina's boss had asked her to look into alternatives over a year ago, and "we haven't talked to anyone yet." Rob's analysis: "You've been struggling for three years. Why can't you just struggle for 30?" The real demand was not "replace Salesforce" but "we need better reporting because we're about to fundraise." Shruti's pitch of a full CRM migration was the wrong case study. When Shruti pivoted to pitching the reporting module in a second call (different prospect), the dynamic changed immediately. "She could start feeling them pulling."

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder confuses pain-point articulation with purchase intent. Three years of Salesforce complaints with zero vendor conversations is the textbook example of "bitchin' ain't switchin'." The pain was real; the demand was not for a CRM replacement -- it was for reporting. Getting the project wrong meant nothing else could stick, no matter how obvious the pain seemed.

**Cobu -- Pushing a Backlog Project** [3/5]

> "Cobu's mission/vision is to create community in multifamily apartments. Cobu got to millions in ARR, but it was HARD to sell & retain. Felt like pushing uphill, even when following all the best practices. Why? Multifamily owners talk a big game about community & resident retention... but only spend $$$ and time on resident acquisition (e.g., Google ads)." Evolution: "'Build community' - backlog at best. 'Retain your residents' - backlog at best. 'Acquire new residents (at lower cost to Google ads)' - I'm listening."

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder is selling something buyers talk about but never prioritize. Cobu proves that even millions in ARR can feel like suffering if the project you're selling is on the buyer's backlog. The buyers weren't lying when they said community matters -- they were in equilibrium around it. The project that was actually on their critical path was acquisition, not retention. The product was the same; the framing had to change to match where the buyer was actually in disequilibrium.
