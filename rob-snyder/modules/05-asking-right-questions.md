### Asking the Right Questions on Calls

When a founder says "I don't know what to ask," "I keep pitching instead of listening," or "My discovery calls go nowhere" -- the problem is almost always that they are asking about pain points and current state instead of projects, priorities, and what's blocking progress. The right questions surface demand. The wrong questions surface interesting-but-useless conversations.

#### Ask About Projects, Not Pain Points [5/5] [EMPHATIC]

"THE MAIN REASON WE DON'T FIND DEMAND IS BECAUSE WE DON'T LOOK FOR IT." (SLIDES, Day 2)

This is the single most important discovery principle in Rob's entire methodology. Founders default to asking about the current state -- "Tell me about your role," "What are your biggest challenges?" -- and then wonder why pleasant conversations never convert. These questions surface pain points. Pain points are not demand.

"Pain points and demand are two separate things... I like to think of it as projects on people's to-do lists. They have a project on their to-do list with bad alternatives." (PMF-PT1, Day 1)

The fix is deceptively simple: ask about what they are trying to accomplish, not what hurts.

Bad questions (about current state and pain):
- "Tell me about your company"
- "Tell me about your team"
- "What is hard?"
- "What are your pain points?"
- "Tell me about your role"
- "What's high-priority on your team's roadmap for the next 6-12 months?"

Good questions (about projects, change, and what's blocking progress):
- "What are you trying to accomplish?"
- "Why do anything?"
- "What's missing?"
- "Was there a particular initiative you think we could help out with?"
- "What are your team's main projects, initiatives, goals?"
- "What would be a 'holy sh*t' result?"

The difference between these two sets of questions determines whether you find demand or get lost in a pleasant conversation that goes nowhere. "Good calls -> yeah, super interesting, thanks I'll circle back (ghosted)." (SLIDES, Day 2)

*Deploy when:* A founder's discovery calls produce lots of head-nodding and "that sounds interesting" but no forward motion. Review their question list -- they are almost certainly asking about state rather than projects.

#### Discovery as Detective Work [3/5]

"It's really a lot of detective work and like this interrogating and questioning, not over-imposing yourself, but really being on the listening side and being really attentive to the minute signals." -- Bert Ji, participant. Rob affirmed: "I think the best way to describe it is detective work." (PMF-PT1, Day 2)

Discovery is not an interview. It is not a checklist. It is not a conversation where you politely ask questions and wait for your turn to pitch. It is detective work -- following leads, probing inconsistencies, listening for what they are actually trying to do underneath the words they use.

The detective metaphor matters because it reframes the founder's role. You are not performing a sales call. You are investigating whether this person has real demand. You follow the evidence wherever it leads. You listen more than you talk. You get curious about the details that don't add up.

"The ground truth of this is what they're thinking and what they want to do and what they're trying to do. That's where ground truth is, not how we want them to." (PMF-PT1, Day 2)

*Deploy when:* A founder treats discovery as a scripted checklist or feels pressure to get through their questions. Reframe: you are a detective. Follow the thread.

#### The Demand Hairball [2/5]

"Buyers usually hack up a demand hairball, where we have to (ew) get our hands dirty to untangle what demand is." (GUIDE, SLIDES)

Buyers do not articulate their demand cleanly. It comes out as a tangled mess of complaints, aspirations, context, half-formed priorities, and organizational politics. The founder's job is to untangle this hairball using the PULL framework -- separating the project from the pain points, finding the unavoidable trigger, identifying what options they have already considered, and discovering why those options are lacking.

This is why scripted discovery fails. You cannot predict what the hairball will look like. You need the PULL framework as your untangling tool and the detective mindset to follow the threads.

The demand alignment moment is the outcome of successfully untangling the hairball: "So it sounds like in your specific situation, you have to accomplish this by this time, you're considering these different options and what really matters to you is this... I'm sure I got something wrong in there. What did I miss?" (PMF-PT1, Day 1)

*Deploy when:* A founder says the call was "confusing" or "all over the place." They encountered a demand hairball and did not know how to untangle it. Walk them through applying PULL to what they heard.

#### Filming a Documentary of Their Life [2/5]

"Bob Moesta talks about filming a documentary of their lives leading up to that moment and figuring out what's forcing them to prioritize that project at that very moment." (PMF-PT1, Day 1)

This is about reconstructing the causal sequence, not just asking "why now?" The question "why now?" elicits rationalizations and justifications. Instead, reconstruct the timeline: What happened six months ago? What changed in their organization? What event forced this project onto their critical path?

The documentary approach reveals the WHEN of demand -- the specific trigger that moved this project from their mental backlog to their critical path. Understanding the trigger is what allows you to find more people at that same moment.

*Deploy when:* A founder can identify that some prospects have demand but cannot explain WHY those specific people have it right now. Use the documentary technique to reconstruct the causal chain.

#### Equilibrium vs. Disequilibrium Discovery Questions [2/5]

"It's *weird* for anyone to buy anything; the default state is that people are in equilibrium and don't need / want to change. Our job is to figure out who is in disequilibrium and is trying to change." (SLIDES)

The purpose of discovery is to determine whether the prospect is in equilibrium (no matter how much they complain) or in disequilibrium (actively trying to change and blocked by something). This maps directly to question design:

Equilibrium-revealing questions (bad): "What are your pain points?" -- lets them describe suffering they are comfortably enduring.

Disequilibrium-revealing questions (good): "What are you doing about that today?" -- forces them to reveal whether they are taking action or just complaining.

The four questions from Nuooly/Newly demonstrate this perfectly: (1) Why did you take the call? (2) Why did you leave the big firm? (3) What do you miss about the big firm? (4) What are you doing about that today? "From these four questions, you can see demand." (PMF-PT1, Day 2)

*Deploy when:* A founder reports that prospects describe problems beautifully but never buy. Their questions are surfacing equilibrium, not disequilibrium. Redesign questions to probe for action, not just pain.

#### Stories

**Ish & Wayside -- Iterating Discovery Questions**
> Wayside is AI for HR teams. Ish's original discovery questions -- "Tell me about your job," "What's high-priority on your team's roadmap for the next 6-12 months?" -- were directionally correct but "VERY RARELY led them to explain demand when they had demand... so they are bad questions!" The actual demand was: "Our HR team needs to get to our strategic projects but we're being held back because we're spending 50%+ of our time just handling employee inquiries." Ish updated his questions to include: "How is your team balancing that with day-to-day work like employee support?" and "What are you doing about that?" He eventually discovered that HR people "very infrequently had real demand" but PEOs (outsourced HR providers) had demand because "using Wayside, could make more $$$."
*Teaching application:* When a founder's questions are "directionally correct" but rarely surface demand. Shows the iterative process of refining questions -- AND how better questions can reveal that the right buyer is a completely different persona than expected.

**Jimmy & Rubie -- Wrong Questions, Then Pivot**
> Jimmy at Rubie (AI for customer onboarding) asked current-state questions: "Tell me about your company," "Tell me about your team," "What is hard?" These led nowhere because they are about pain points, not projects. Rob's recommended questions: "You took a look at our website -- was there a particular initiative you think we could help out with?" / "What are your team's main projects, initiatives, goals?" / "What would be a 'holy sh*t' result?" Jimmy eventually pivoted from "faster customer onboarding" to "rip and replace your competition" when a competitor of one of their customers reached out saying "if XYZ is doing this, I need to do this."
*Teaching application:* Contrast between current-state questions and project-focused questions. Also shows how demand can arrive from unexpected angles -- a competitive trigger revealing a completely different project frame.

**Nuooly/Newly -- 75% Close Rate from 4 Questions**
> Newly is a paid community for independent lawyers and accountants (Austin-based, friends of Rob). They do only cold email outbound and have a 75% close rate from first meeting. Their demand: independent professionals who left a big firm, are past survival stage, and "miss the perks they had when they were at the big firm." They ask four questions: (1) Why did you take the call? (2) Why did you leave the big firm? (3) What do you miss about the big firm? (4) What are you doing about that today? "From these four questions, you can see demand."
*Teaching application:* The gold standard. When you truly know demand, you need only 2-4 killer questions to predict with ~90% confidence whether someone will buy. These questions do not feel salesy or pushy -- they feel like a real conversation. "This is only possible because Nuooly knows the EXACT demand their customers have."
