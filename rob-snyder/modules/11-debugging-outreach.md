### Debugging Why Nobody's Responding to Your Outreach

When a founder is sending hundreds of messages and hearing crickets -- 0% reply rates, no meetings booked, outbound that feels futile. The problem is almost never "I need to send more messages." It is that the messages sound like every other salesperson in the prospect's inbox.

#### Be Weird, Not Good [4/5] [SIGNATURE]

"Be so weird they can't not respond." (BLOG)

This is Rob's core outbound principle, derived from his own HBS interview experience: "I learned that weirdness wins during my HBS interview. My interviewer spent the interview just trying to figure me out: 'Let me get this straight... you have no work experience, played in a metal band, danced Bhangra, and spent your free time wandering around rural Tanzania. Uh... what are you?' That's when it clicked: They interviewed me - this barely-above-average middle-class kid from a state school - because I was too weird not to interview." (BLOG)

Applied to outbound: stop optimizing for "good" and start optimizing for "weird." Traditional messaging fails because it sounds like sales. Every inbox is flooded with messages that follow the same templates -- the "Sales Haiku" (ultra-short "clever" messages that only work for salespeople selling to salespeople), the "Hubspot Standard" (formal pitch based on a 2017 template), the "Predictable Revenue Bank Shot" ("who's the right person?" delegation messages). (BLOG)

The fix: "We have to compete on a different plane. We can't sound like we're salespeople." (PMF-PT1)

The Snoop Dogg message is a perfect example: "Hey -- Snoop Dogg invested in us, would love to get you involved too. We're working on X, open to chat?" It works because it is too weird not to respond to. It is not persuasive. It is interesting. (BLOG)

"Going from: Why would I respond? To: Why wouldn't I respond?" (SLIDES)

*Deploy when:* A founder shows you their outbound messages and they sound like templates from a sales blog. Rip them up. Ask: "What about you or your story is so weird that a stranger couldn't not respond?"

#### The Outbound System Bottleneck Analysis [4/5]

"We can look at it as a system that has a goal of getting 5 to 10 meetings per week... when we look at our own stats... we can come back and say, OK, given this... what's the part of the system that's not working this week?" (PMF-PT1)

Outbound is a measurable system with four stages and specific benchmarks:

1. **Volume & Targeting** -- target: 200 outreach per week
2. **Connection Rate** -- target: 30%+
3. **Reply Rate** -- target: 50%+ of connections
4. **Meeting Rate** -- target: 50%+ of replies

Result: approximately 15 meetings per week at target benchmarks. (PMF-PT1, GUIDE)

The critical discipline: find the SINGLE bottleneck and fix ONLY that. Do not try to improve everything at once. "When we look at our own stats... what's the part of the system that's not working this week?" (PMF-PT1)

Common misdiagnosis: a founder rewrites all their messaging when the real bottleneck is volume. Or a founder sends more messages when the real bottleneck is their connection rate (because their LinkedIn profile looks wrong to the target buyer).

"I recommend Friday afternoons, w/ beer" for the weekly review. (SLIDES)

Over time, the optimization shifts: "First we optimize for do they actually have demand on call one... Do they buy... Product market fit is about retention and hell yes, post sale." (PMF-PT1) Early on, optimize for meetings. Later, optimize for demand quality, then close rate, then retention.

*Deploy when:* A founder says "outbound isn't working." Do not accept this vague diagnosis. Ask for numbers at each stage. Find the bottleneck. Fix that one thing.

#### Sell the Conversation, Not the Product [3/5]

The outbound message is not a pitch. It is an invitation to a conversation. "It's only a sales call if THEY have demand; otherwise it's a conversation with someone interesting." (GUIDE)

The entire frame of outbound should be: "I'm an interesting person doing interesting work, and I think you'd find a conversation with me valuable." Not: "I have a product that solves your problem."

"The whole email should come across as something a human would send. Something about automated sequences just makes us write the worst things." (PMF-PT2)

Specific tactics that support this:
- Blank LinkedIn connection requests (no message) generally outperform connection requests with messages. (PMF-PT1)
- 2-3 follow-up messages after connection. "Lower case 'bump' actually works. I should not, it shouldn't work. And it's kind of weird to send, but lower case bump actually works, again, it feels like a human is sending it." (PMF-PT1)
- The goal of a subject line: "to not look like the other subject lines that are in their inbox." (PMF-PT2) Go look at your target customer's actual inbox to see what competing emails look like, then differentiate.

*Deploy when:* A founder's outbound messages read like mini pitch decks. Strip out the pitch entirely. Make the message about them and what's in it for them.

#### Outbound Messaging Rubric (6 Criteria) [2/5]

Score every outbound message against these six criteria:

1. **Something cool in it for them** -- advisory board, featuring on podcast, free access, genuine insight
2. **Something cool about us** -- founder credibility, interesting story, unique background
3. **Doesn't sound like from a salesperson** -- would a friend send this?
4. **Doesn't bury the lede** -- the interesting thing is in the first line, not buried after a preamble
5. **Doesn't sound automated** -- feels like a human wrote it for them specifically
6. **Wastes no words** -- every sentence earns its place

(GUIDE, SLIDES)

"Going from: Why would I respond? To: Why wouldn't I respond?" (SLIDES)

The rubric is diagnostic. When a message scores low, the founder can identify exactly which criteria are failing rather than vaguely trying to "write better messages."

*Deploy when:* Reviewing a founder's outbound messaging in a teardown session. Score each message against the six criteria. Most failing messages score zero on criteria 1 and 2 -- nothing in it for the prospect and nothing interesting about the sender.

#### Pre-Case-Study vs. Post-Case-Study Outbound [3/5]

Rob makes a critical distinction between outbound approaches depending on where you are:

**Before a validated case study:** Use advisory network outreach ("we're building an advisory network of people like you"), ego-driven messaging (leverage credentials, unique background), or research-framed calls ("I'm researching how X leaders approach Y"). The purpose is to get conversations, not to sell. (PMF-PT1, GUIDE)

**After a validated case study:** Use direct, case-study-first messaging. Lead with the story of a customer like them who had a project like theirs and achieved a result they would want. The case study does the selling. (PMF-PT1, GUIDE)

The transition is gradual: "chiseling down a directionally correct case study" into sharper and sharper messaging over time. (PMF-PT1)

This distinction matters because founders often try to use post-case-study messaging before they have a case study, resulting in vague claims about value that sound like every other startup. Or they keep using pre-case-study messaging after they have real customer success stories, missing the chance to let proof do the work.

*Deploy when:* A founder asks "what should my outbound message say?" The first question back is: "Do you have a case study?" The answer determines the entire approach.

#### Dirty Pipeline vs. Empty Pipeline [3/5]

"You basically have two choices. You can have an empty pipeline or you can have a dirty pipeline. Optimized for the dirty pipeline." (PMF-PT1)

Before you have a nailed case study, your outbound will inevitably attract some wrong-fit prospects. Some conversations will go nowhere. Some people will have no demand. This is not a failure of your outbound -- it is the nature of early-stage selling.

The alternative -- sending nothing because you haven't perfected your messaging -- is far worse. An empty pipeline produces zero learning. A dirty pipeline produces some learning, some real demand signals, and occasionally a customer.

"Salesy outreach" leads to an empty pipeline. "Founder magic outreach" leads to a "dirty pipeline... that you then clean by finding demand and iterating." (SLIDES)

*Deploy when:* A founder is paralyzed by the fear of reaching out to the wrong people or saying the wrong thing. Give them permission: a dirty pipeline is infinitely better than no pipeline. You will clean it through iteration.

#### Stories

**Rob's Hundreds of Emails at 0.5% Reply Rate**
> "I recall sending hundreds of emails manually and getting a 0.5% reply rate (0% if you don't count out-of-office replies); I did a ton of work to send what I now see was spammy sales outreach ('Did you know AI agents can get you 10x ROI?'), then to send messaging in the uncanny valley between sales and begging for feedback ('Would love to pick your brain on our AI agent that can 10x ROI'); no matter what I did, I heard crickets."
*Teaching application:* When outbound feels futile and a founder is ready to give up on it. This is Rob's own experience. The problem was not volume or effort -- it was that the messages optimized for "good" instead of "weird." The messaging sounded like every other startup pitch in the inbox.
*Source:* BLOG

**The Snoop Dogg Outbound Message**
> From a Finding Pull workshop: "Hey -- Snoop Dogg invested in us, would love to get you involved too. We're working on X, open to chat?" Rob cited this as a perfect example of "weird" outbound.
*Teaching application:* A concrete example of what "be weird" looks like in practice. The message works because it is too interesting not to respond to. It makes no attempt to explain the product, pitch a value prop, or "create urgency." It just makes you curious. Not every founder has Snoop Dogg as an investor -- the point is to find whatever YOUR version of that weird, interesting hook is.
*Source:* BLOG

**HBS Founder Scheduling 5 Calls in 30 Minutes**
> "A HBS founder messaged me saying, 'Wait, so with like 30 mins of work I scheduled 5 sales calls this week?!' Yeah, great, that's pretty normal when you're not overthinking it."
*Teaching application:* When a founder is overthinking outreach and treating each message like a dissertation. Scheduling conversations is not the hard part -- the hard part is WHAT you're looking for on those calls. When the messaging is weird and interesting, conversations happen easily.
*Source:* BLOG

**Leon (Steercast) -- The Turtleneck Teardown**
> Leon was selling supply chain software to COOs and supply chain leaders. His LinkedIn featured a turtleneck photo, YC branding, and "Steercast" -- none of which meant anything to supply chain managers. Rob: "They see this and they're like, yeah, this is some nerd wearing a turtleneck who I don't understand why he's reaching out to me." Rob also revealed that they had previously had Leon show his cold outreach to actual customers: "They vomited on it."
*Teaching application:* When a founder's outreach is falling flat and they can't figure out why. The most powerful diagnostic: show your outreach to actual target customers and let them react. Their reaction IS the ground truth. If they vomit on it, you know what to fix.
