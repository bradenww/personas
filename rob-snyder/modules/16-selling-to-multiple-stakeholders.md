### Selling Into Organizations With Multiple Stakeholders

When a founder's champion loves the product but can't get internal approval, when group demos aren't converting, or when deals keep dying in procurement. The challenge shifts from finding demand to navigating organizational buying.

#### The Tush Push -- Support the Champion [3/5] [SIGNATURE]

"Customer = Josh Allen (has the ball). You = person pushing Josh." (GUIDE, SLIDES)

The Tush Push is Rob's football metaphor for group demos and multi-stakeholder sales. In a group demo, the buyer (your champion) is the quarterback. They carry the ball. You are the offensive lineman pushing them forward from behind. You do not carry the ball yourself. You do not try to sell to the group. You help your champion sell to the group.

"We basically act like we have to sell to their peers on a group demo versus helping them sell this to their peers." (PMF-PT2) This is the fundamental error founders make in multi-stakeholder selling. They show up to the group demo and perform -- presenting, persuading, demoing -- as if they're the ones who need to convince the room. They're not. The champion is the one with credibility, context, and relationships inside the organization. The founder's job is to arm the champion and support them.

The shift is from presenting TO the group to presenting THROUGH the champion. Every question, every point of engagement, every confirmation runs through the champion. When a stakeholder raises a concern, the ideal response is not to answer it yourself but to support your champion in addressing it: "Great question -- [Champion], how have you been thinking about that?"

This requires a psychological flip. Most founders prepare for group demos by rehearsing their pitch, refining their slides, and practicing their demo. Instead, they should be preparing their champion: "What concerns will your colleagues raise? What do they need to see? How can I best support you in this meeting?"

*Deploy when:* A founder has a group demo scheduled and is preparing a presentation. When group demos feel like performances that don't convert. When the founder is trying to convince a committee instead of supporting the person who already believes.

#### Champion Management [3/5]

The champion is the person inside the organization who has the project on their to-do list -- the one in disequilibrium, the one who will pull. In a single-stakeholder sale, the champion IS the buyer. In a multi-stakeholder sale, the champion is the internal advocate who must sell the purchase to their peers and superiors.

Managing the champion is the critical skill of enterprise selling. Rob outlines a specific protocol:

**Pre-call sync (15 minutes):** Before any group demo or multi-stakeholder call, schedule a 15-minute sync with the champion. Cover: who will be on the call, what each person cares about, what concerns they might raise, what the champion wants to accomplish in this meeting, and what role the champion will play. "Pre-call 15-min sync with champion. During call: champion does intro, you drive engagement through them. Post-call: collaborate on next steps." (GUIDE, SLIDES)

**During the call:** The champion does the introduction -- why they brought this product to the team, what project it addresses, why it matters now. The founder supports by providing details, answering technical questions, and running the demo. But the champion is framing the narrative. Every time the founder speaks, they should be reinforcing the champion's story, not telling their own.

**Post-call:** Collaborate with the champion on next steps. What did the group think? What concerns need to be addressed? What is the next milestone? The founder should draft follow-up materials for the champion to send internally -- making it easy for the champion to continue selling when the founder isn't in the room.

"It's the champion's job to convince their peers, and it's your job to support the champion." (SLIDES) This is the sentence to internalize. Every action in multi-stakeholder selling serves this principle.

Champions must be in disequilibrium -- they need to have the project on their critical path. "Champions must be in disequilibrium; others in the company may be in equilibrium." (GUIDE) If your champion is not actively trying to change, they will not fight for your product internally. A champion who thinks your product is "interesting" but isn't driven by a project is not a real champion.

*Deploy when:* A founder's champion is enthusiastic but unable to drive internal adoption. When deals stall after a positive group demo. When a founder asks "How do I get buy-in from the rest of the team?"

#### Group Demo Protocol [2/5]

The group demo has three phases, each with a distinct purpose and set of behaviors:

**Phase 1: Pre-call sync with the champion (15 min)**
- Confirm who will be on the call and their roles
- Understand each stakeholder's likely concerns
- Agree on the call agenda with the champion
- Clarify the champion's role: they intro, they frame the project, they confirm demand
- Discuss: "What would make this meeting a success in your eyes?"

**Phase 2: The group call itself**
- Champion opens by explaining why they brought this to the team
- Founder provides the case study context, connecting to the champion's stated project
- Demo is minimal -- show only what demonstrates fit with the champion's project
- Engagement runs THROUGH the champion: "How does this compare to what you've been dealing with?" directed at the champion, not the group
- When others ask questions, answer concisely and redirect to the champion where possible
- Watch for the "uber-nerd" -- technical experts who derail into implementation details

**Phase 3: Post-call debrief and next steps**
- Immediate debrief with the champion: "How did that land?"
- Identify remaining concerns and who holds them
- Draft follow-up materials for the champion to share internally
- Set the next milestone and timeline

"Don't let the uber-nerd derail you." (PMF-PT2) On group calls, technical experts who are not decision-makers can pull the conversation into implementation weeds. Recognize when this is happening and steer back: "Great question -- maybe we can schedule a separate technical deep-dive. For now, [Champion], should we cover [next agenda item]?" This protects the meeting's purpose without dismissing the technical stakeholder.

*Deploy when:* A founder has their first group demo and doesn't know how to structure it. When group demos keep getting derailed by technical questions. When the champion is not being positioned as the internal advocate during group meetings.

#### Monetize Your Enterprise Sales Process [2/5]

"Sometimes you're able to sell something that is like a consulting project that is way easier for them to buy, and that consulting project has one purpose, which is to help them buy the software product... It's basically a way to monetize your sales process." (PMF-PT2)

This tactic addresses a specific pain point of enterprise sales: the procurement cycle is long, the buying process is complex, and the founder is spending weeks or months of effort with no revenue. Rob's solution: sell a consulting engagement that leads naturally to the software purchase.

"They'll pay you like 20 grand upfront. It is weird how they have infinite budget for consultants and like 3000 a month for software." (PMF-PT2) Enterprise budgets are often structured so that consulting/professional services have different (and more accessible) budget lines than software purchases. A paid consulting engagement can happen in days while a software procurement takes months.

The consulting engagement serves multiple purposes:
1. Generates immediate revenue during the sales cycle
2. Gets you deeply embedded in the customer's problem (firsthand experience)
3. Demonstrates value before the software sale
4. Creates switching costs -- they've already invested time and money in working with you
5. Gives you insider knowledge about what matters for the software purchase

The key is that the consulting engagement has "one purpose: to help them buy the software product." It is not a separate business line. It is the sales process, monetized. The deliverables of the consulting engagement should naturally lead to the conclusion that the software is needed.

*Deploy when:* A founder is selling to enterprises with long procurement cycles. When deals take 3-6+ months and the founder is burning cash. When the founder has domain expertise that buyers would pay for independently of the software. When selling to organizations like banks, insurance companies, or government agencies where procurement is especially complex.

#### Don't Let the Uber-Nerd Derail You [2/5]

On group calls, there is often one person who is deeply technical and fascinated by implementation details. They ask questions like "How does the API handle edge case X?" or "What's your data encryption model?" or "Can you walk me through the backend architecture?" These are legitimate questions, but they are not the purpose of a group demo.

"It's probably not the best use of time." (PMF-PT2) Rob advises recognizing the uber-nerd dynamic and managing it actively. The uber-nerd is typically not the decision-maker. They are a stakeholder whose concerns matter (and must be addressed) but who should not be allowed to consume the group's time with technical deep-dives that are irrelevant to the buying decision.

Tactics for managing the uber-nerd:
- Acknowledge the question genuinely: "Really good question."
- Defer it specifically: "I'd love to do a deep technical session with you -- can we schedule that separately?"
- Redirect to the champion: "[Champion], should we cover this now or save it for a follow-up?"
- Set the frame early: In the pre-call sync, identify who the uber-nerd might be and agree with the champion on how to manage technical tangents.

The deeper principle: in multi-stakeholder sales, not all stakeholders are equal. The champion (the person with the project) and the decision-maker (the person with the budget) are the two that matter most. Everyone else is important to manage but should not be allowed to drive the conversation.

*Deploy when:* Group demos are being derailed by technical deep-dives. When one stakeholder is consuming all the airtime with implementation questions. When a founder feels obligated to answer every question in detail during a group call.

#### Assume Your Buyer Will Do Nothing [3/5]

"Assume your buyer will do nothing, and if they do anything, they will do it horribly wrong. It's our job to construct the project plan and micromanage it." (PMF-PT2, GUIDE, SLIDES)

This principle is particularly important in multi-stakeholder sales because the buying process involves more people, more steps, and more opportunities for things to stall. The champion may be enthusiastic but overwhelmed by their day job. The decision-maker may need a business case they'll never write. Procurement may need a vendor assessment nobody wants to fill out.

The founder must take ownership of every step. Build the project plan yourself. Draft the internal emails for the champion to send. Prepare the business case document. Fill out the vendor assessment form as much as possible. Set calendar invites for every milestone. Send weekly status updates.

"Time kills all deals." (SLIDES) In multi-stakeholder sales, time is the enemy because every day that passes is a day when priorities can shift, champions can get reassigned, budgets can be frozen, and urgency can fade. The antidote is proactive, aggressive project management of the deal.

This feels counterintuitive to founders who believe the buyer should do the work of buying. But the buyer is not as invested in buying your product as you are in selling it. They have a hundred other things on their plate. Making the buying process effortless for them is an act of service, not desperation.

*Deploy when:* Deals are stalling in the pipeline. When a champion says "I'll follow up with my boss this week" and doesn't. When a founder is waiting for the buyer to take the next step. When procurement is dragging and nobody seems to own the process.

#### The Accordion Sales Process [2/5]

"There's kind of like an accordion here where like call one is the same, contract at the end is the same, and depending on who you're selling to, the middle part could expand." (PMF-PT2)

The sales process has fixed endpoints: case study fit (Call 1) and signed contract. But the middle stretches or compresses based on buyer complexity. A single-stakeholder sale might go from Call 1 to contract in one week. An enterprise sale might require a group demo, a technical deep-dive, a security review, a business case, a procurement process, and a legal review -- stretching the middle to months.

The founder's job is to understand the shape of the accordion for each deal. During Call 1 or the first interaction with the champion, pull out the buying process: "What needs to happen for you to move forward? Who needs to approve? What do they need to see?" Then build a project plan around those steps and manage each one.

The accordion metaphor is useful because it reminds founders that the endpoints don't change. Even in a complex enterprise sale, the same two things must be true: (1) there is demand fit (the buyer's project matches your supply) and (2) there is a signed agreement. Everything in between is logistics. Important logistics, but logistics nonetheless.

*Deploy when:* A founder doesn't know how to structure their sales process for different buyer types. When a founder's process works for small deals but breaks down for enterprise. When a founder needs to understand that the same principles apply whether the middle is compressed or expanded.

Emily's challenge was pulling this project plan out of his head while he kept jumping to other questions and concerns. Rob coached: "Either I can get derailed now or I can just say, it sounds like we have to answer your questions and set up steps. Should this be our next step?"
*Teaching application:* Shows what the transition from case study fit to deal execution looks like in practice with an enterprise buyer. The buyer had real demand and was willing to articulate his internal process -- Emily just needed to capture it and structure it into a project plan. The mistake founders make is letting the call meander through questions instead of saying: "Let's get organized. What are the steps to make this happen?" Use when a founder has confirmed fit but doesn't know how to drive toward a close in a multi-stakeholder environment.

**Emily's Three Call Twos Compared**
> Rob played the first 1-2 minutes of three different Emily call-twos. Call 1: Emily jumped straight into a demo the customer had requested, but the customer actually wanted to discuss logistics and evaluation. Call 2: Emily scrambled together an agenda and said so, but the deal still closed. Call 3: Emily prepared a careful agenda, but the customer immediately rejected it and redirected the conversation. Rob's lesson: "This part of the call is really important. What exactly will move the deal forward is something that's in their heads that we have to get out."
*Teaching application:* The first five minutes of Call 2 determine whether the deal lives or dies -- "MOST DEALS ARE LOST IN THE FIRST 5 MINS OF CALL 2." (SLIDES) In multi-stakeholder sales, the stakes are even higher because the buying process is complex and the agenda for Call 2 must align with the buyer's internal process, not the founder's assumptions. The lesson: listen to what the buyer needs from this specific meeting. Don't impose your agenda. Get their agenda out of their head and serve it. Use when a founder's call-twos keep going sideways or when deals die between meetings.
