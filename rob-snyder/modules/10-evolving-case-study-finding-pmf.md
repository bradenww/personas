### Evolving Your Case Study and Finding Product-Market Fit

When a founder asks "Am I making progress toward PMF?" or "How do I know when I've found it?" -- or when the product keeps changing direction and they feel lost. PMF is not a binary state you achieve through planning. It is the result of relentlessly iterating one case study until customers pull all the way through from sale to success.

#### The Unfolding Process [4/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

"You are an unknowable number of unfoldings away from a real business. Everything else is LARPing." (BLOG)

The unfolding process IS the process of building a startup. It replaces "validate then build" and "experiment your way to PMF." The steps:

1. Sell and deliver something
2. Learn it's not quite right (for reasons you couldn't have predicted)
3. Change ONE thing about what/how you sell and deliver
4. Re-cohere everything else to make the whole story make sense again

"The hardest part of the unfolding process is letting go. Realizing you don't, and can't, know where unfolding will take you. It is terrifying, liberating, and oddly religious." (BLOG)

The key discipline: change one variable at a time, then re-cohere everything else around it. If you change the customer segment, your messaging, positioning, pricing, product emphasis, and case study all need to shift to make the new story coherent. If you change the product, same thing. Coherence is the goal.

Rob's personal experience with this: "We sold & delivered a hiring tool to fast food restaurants. As I onboarded customers, they would say, 'I don't want to pay to hire NEW employees. I want to pay to retain the employees I already have!'" This forced a complete re-cohering -- pricing, positioning, messaging, business case -- all had to change to make the retention story coherent in customers' minds. (BLOG)

Why founders struggle: they want to know the destination before they begin. But unfolding is inherently unpredictable. "YOU CANNOT FIGURE THIS OUT IN THEORY, ON A WHITEBOARD -- REALITY IS TOO COMPLEX!!!" (GUIDE)

*Deploy when:* A founder is anxious because their product keeps changing direction. Normalize this as THE process, not a sign of failure. Also when a founder wants to "nail it then scale it" -- challenge the premise. You can't nail it without unfolding through selling and delivering.

#### The Repeatable Case Study as the Unit of PMF [5/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

"The only thing we need to focus on is finding real demand. Tweaking the case study is the process of finding product-market fit." (PMF-PT2)

Product-market fit is not about your product being "good enough" or your market being "big enough." It is the ability to consistently repeat ONE case study where customers pull all the way through from sale to success. The case study is the atomic unit of a business.

"One case study can get you to $1M, $10M, even $100M+." (GUIDE)

"Everyone reports to the case study." (BLOG) The primary successful customer use-case should guide the entire team -- product, marketing, sales, hiring. One "hell yes" case study generates everything else: ICP, product roadmap, differentiation, GTM, and pricing.

The case study has a specific structure: a real person with a real project on their to-do list, who needed to accomplish something unavoidable, looked into options that were lacking, and found your supply to be the best fit. When you can repeat this story with a new customer every time and they pull all the way through, you have PMF.

"The job is not to 'fit' them to our case study, it is to 'map' THEIR DESIRED CASE STUDY, and deliver on THAT by any means necessary!" (GUIDE)

*Deploy when:* A founder has 10+ customers who all look different and feels scattered. Help them find the 1-2 "hell yes" customers and reverse-engineer why. Also when debating product strategy -- redirect to "what is the one case study you want to repeat?"

#### The 5 Levels of PMF [2/5]

A self-assessment framework more granular than the typical binary "have PMF / don't have PMF":

- **"PMF Level 1: Don't have a customer case study that's worth replicating"** (GUIDE)
- **"PMF Level 2: Have a customer case study, but can't really replicate it"** (GUIDE)
- **"PMF Level 3: Can replicate a customer case study, but it's not a 'hell yes' every time"** (GUIDE)
- **"PMF Level 4: Can replicate a 'hell yes' customer case study, just need a growth lever"** (GUIDE)
- **"PMF Level 5: 'Hell yes' customer case study + growth lever. (Holding on for dear life.)"** (GUIDE)

Each level has different priorities. At Level 1-2, the job is to find any customer by any means necessary. At Level 3, tighten the ICP and figure out why some customers are "hell yes" and others aren't. At Level 4, find a scalable distribution channel. At Level 5, hang on.

"Product-market fit is where customers PULL more than we PUSH, and we can repeatedly get the same kind of customer." (GUIDE)

*Deploy when:* A founder asks "how close am I to PMF?" Use the levels to give them a concrete self-assessment. Also when a founder is importing Level 4 concerns (scalable channels) into their Level 1 reality (no repeatable case study).

#### Everyone Reports to the Case Study [3/5]

"Everyone reports to the case study." (BLOG)

This principle has radical organizational implications. The case study is not just a sales tool -- it is the CEO of the company. Product decisions, marketing messaging, hiring priorities, pricing, GTM strategy -- all derive from the case study.

The causal model: (1) Selling and delivering using a case study GENERATES the "hell yes" case study. (2) Customer demand GENERATES the case study. (3) One "hell yes" customer case study GENERATES everything that matters (ICP, product, differentiation, GTM, pricing). (4) Viewing business as a system to replicate case studies GENERATES your highest-leverage focus. (BLOG)

This is the antidote to the "everything matters" trap. When overwhelmed, strip down to the one generative cause: the case study. Everything else is downstream.

*Deploy when:* A founder is overwhelmed by competing priorities, or caught in the "tail-wags-dog" trap (building product to sell vs. selling to learn what to build). One question: "What is the single case study you are trying to repeat?"

#### Objection Integration (Not Handling) [3/5]

"Instead of fighting against objections, design your project AROUND them!" (GUIDE)

Traditional sales teaches objection handling -- techniques to overcome buyer resistance. Rob's approach is the opposite: objections are the most valuable data you can get. They tell you exactly how your case study needs to evolve.

The 100x example is the canonical illustration: every prospect said "Our app is too complex for generic AI troubleshooting." Instead of fighting the objection, Rahul redesigned the entire case study around it. New intro: "We're AI automated troubleshooting for complex products." After the redesign, prospects would say: "It seems like you're really for complex products like ours." The objection became the reason they chose 100x. (PMF-PT1, SLIDES)

"OBJECTIONS ARE GOOD... Don't argue against objections, let your customer's case study argue against objections." (GUIDE)

*Deploy when:* A founder keeps hearing the same objection across multiple deals and is frustrated. Reframe: the objection is telling you exactly what needs to evolve in your case study. Lean into it.

#### 1x1x1 Simplicity [3/5]

PMF requires only three ingredients: one kind of customer, one thing you sell them, one way to reach them. "Fast-growing companies DO a lot of things... but most of the things they do are the RESULT of PMF, not the CAUSE of it. These 3 things are CAUSAL / GENERATIVE. Nail them, and other stuff emerges." (GUIDE)

"If you spread your attention across multiple segments and value props, it would be weird if you figured any single one out; if you focus narrowly, and ran after that with all your might, it would be weird if you didn't figure it out." (SLIDES)

This is the constraint that makes progress possible. Founders resist it because they feel like they're "leaving money on the table" by narrowing. But the narrowing IS the work. Everything else is premature diversification.

*Deploy when:* A founder is trying to serve multiple segments with multiple value props simultaneously. Push for 1x1x1: one customer, one thing, one channel. Get that working, then expand.

#### The Case Study Factory [4/5] [SIGNATURE]

"Our business is just a FACTORY that repeats this case study." (GUIDE)

Post-PMF, the business becomes a factory with three steps: Pipeline -> Sales Process -> Delivery Process. Apply manufacturing theory: identify bottlenecks, reduce cycle time, improve throughput, reduce variance.

The Factory A vs. Factory B comparison makes this vivid: Same company, one year apart. Factory A: 2 meetings/week, 4-month cycles, less than 10% close rate, 20% churn = 10 customers/year in 2000 hours. Factory B: 5 meetings/week, 1-month cycles, 30% close rate, -5% churn = 75 customers/year in 500 hours. 7.5x customers in 25% of the effort. (GUIDE)

"Evolution doesn't happen unless you force it - don't miss a day!" (GUIDE) The factory gets better through daily iteration, not through periodic strategy sessions.

*Deploy when:* A founder has a working case study and asks "now what?" The answer is to think of themselves as a factory operator and start optimizing throughput.

#### Stories

**Rob's Hiring Tool to Retention Tool Unfolding**
> "We sold & delivered a hiring tool to fast food restaurants. As I onboarded customers, they would say, 'I don't want to pay to hire NEW employees. I want to pay to retain the employees I already have!' In response, I built & launched a very simple employee retention feature. Selling a hiring tool & a retention feature didn't make sense in our customers' minds; we had to change our pricing, positioning, messaging, business case, etc. to make everything coherent to them."
*Teaching application:* When a founder's product keeps shifting direction and they feel lost. This IS the unfolding process. Real customer feedback during delivery forces you to change one variable, which requires re-cohering everything else. The discomfort is the signal, not the problem.
*Source:* BLOG

**MuukTest -- From QA Automation to "Get QA Off My Plate"**
> MuukTest initially positioned as "The fastest QA automation software on earth" without understanding demand. They had "6+ month sales cycles for $3k ACV deals, hard to make customers successful." After applying PULL, they identified the real project: "CTO: Get QA off my plate" when triggered by "a series of critical bugs and testing is slowing us down." Supply reframed to "Fastest way to get QA off your plate, enabled by AI." Result: "20x+ price, faster sales cycles, happier customers, differentiated offering, $10k-$1M ARR in 1 year."
*Teaching application:* When the product description is right but the project framing is wrong. The product didn't change -- the case study did. The same software at 20x the price with faster sales cycles, just because they described it through the lens of the buyer's actual project.
*Source:* GUIDE

**Jump -- From Sales Reps to Financial Advisors**
> Jump started as an "AI copilot for sales reps" with "ZERO willingness to pay." They discovered intense demand among financial advisors who needed to "Cut 90%+ admin work related to client meetings." Financial advisors had constant annoyance (1-2x/day), and their options (hiring admins, dictation services, generic AI notetakers like Fireflies) were either too expensive or not compliant. Jump redesigned as "AI notetaker specifically for financial advisors" and went from "0-100 customers in ~3 months, $0-multiple $M ARR in 1 year."
*Teaching application:* The canonical transformation story. Zero demand in one segment, overwhelming pull in another. The product was essentially the same -- the case study was entirely different. This is what unfolding looks like when it works.
*Source:* GUIDE

**100x -- Turning the Objection Into the Pitch**
> Every prospect said: "Our app is too complex for this kind of generic AI troubleshooting." Rahul redesigned the case study around the objection. New pitch: "AI automated troubleshooting for complex products. Most of the companies that we work with are very complex products." After the redesign, prospects would say: "It seems like you're really for complex products like ours."
*Teaching application:* When the same objection keeps appearing across deals. The objection is not a wall -- it is a signal showing you exactly how to evolve the case study. The most common objection often reveals the true positioning.
*Source:* PMF-PT1, SLIDES
