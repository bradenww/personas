### Running a Demo That Actually Converts

When a founder says "My demos don't lead to deals," "I show everything and they say 'cool' and leave," "Prospects say 'this is amazing but complicated,'" or "My demos run 15-20 minutes and nothing happens." The problem is almost always showing too much. Demos are not product tours. They are not education sessions. They are the moment where the buyer connects your supply to their demand -- and that moment requires the absolute minimum of screens and clicks.

#### Every Extra Click Reduces Conversion [4/5] [EMPHATIC]

"Every additional click you take in a demo, every additional screen you show reduces their likelihood to buy by some percent. We'll say 10%, 15%, 25%, whatever percent you want. Because the more you show, the more confusing and complicated it seems." (PMF-PT2)

"Every additional click you make in a demo REDUCES their likelihood to buy by X%. Why? Confusing, seems more complicated." (SLIDES, Day 4)

"The more product they see, the less likely they are to buy." (SLIDES, Physics of Sales pt 2)

Rob states this principle with unusual force and repetition across multiple sources. It is the single most important demo principle in his methodology. The math is simple: if every additional click reduces conversion by some percentage, then a 15-click demo is exponentially worse than a 2-click demo.

The reason is psychological: each new screen, feature, or capability makes the product feel bigger. A bigger product means a bigger buying decision. A bigger buying decision means more risk, more stakeholders needed, more time to evaluate, more reasons to delay. The buyer walked in trying to accomplish one specific project. If you show them a platform that does 20 things, they have to evaluate all 20 things -- or decide they cannot, and leave.

Demo only the one or two things that create the "aha moment" connecting supply to demand. "How do we change our description of supply / demo so they get this 'hell yes' moment and pull in call 1, consistently?" (SLIDES, Day 4) The "hell yes" moment is not when they see all your features. It is when they see the one thing that fits their project.

*Deploy when:* A founder's demos run longer than 2-3 minutes or include more than 2-3 screens. Ask: "What is the ONE thing you show that makes them say 'this is exactly what I need'? Show only that." If they cannot identify it, they do not yet understand what demand they are serving.

#### Stop Selling Past the Close [3/5]

"It's called selling past the close. When they're like, yes, I want this. Boom, done." (PMF-PT2)

When a customer says "this is exactly what I want" during a demo, stop the screen share immediately. Transition to next steps. Do not continue demoing additional features.

This sounds obvious. In practice, almost every founder violates it. The buyer has confirmed fit. The deal is ready to progress. And the founder, nervous or excited, keeps showing more features -- "but wait, you'll also love this..." -- and each additional thing creates new questions, new doubts, and new reasons to evaluate further rather than move forward.

Renato at Parallel demonstrated this painfully. The buyer said "this is exactly what I want" -- and Renato kept demoing for 2+ more minutes. "HALF THE DEMO COMES AFTER THIS." (SLIDES, Day 4) The deal closed, but only with a 50% discount. The extra demo time did not add value -- it subtracted it.

The instinct to keep showing is rooted in supply-side thinking: "I spent months building these features and I want them to see how much we can do." But the buyer does not care about your features. They care about their project. Once they see that your product fits their project, anything else you show is noise.

*Deploy when:* A founder reports that demos "go well" but deals stall afterward, or that they have to discount heavily to close. Listen for the moment in their call recordings where the buyer signals fit -- then check whether the founder kept demoing after that moment. If yes, this is the diagnosis.

#### Demo Structure: Prepare, Show, Confirm [3/5]

"When we're demoing supply, first prepare them for what you're going to show them... Then demo and just deliver on the promise above... Then confirm supply fits their demand." (PMF-PT2)

The demo structure has exactly three steps:

**Step 1: Prepare.** Tell them what you are about to show. "What I'm going to show you is two things..." (PMF-PT2) This sets expectations and focuses their attention. They know what to look for. They know when the demo is "done."

**Step 2: Show.** Demo only what you promised to show. Deliver on the setup and nothing more. 30-60 seconds is the target. "Demos should be 30-60 seconds, showing only what fits their PULL." (SLIDES, Day 4) Do not teach them how to use the product. Do not prove the product works at scale. Do not show the admin panel. Show the thing that connects to their project.

**Step 3: Confirm.** Ask whether it fits: "How could you see using this with [their specific situation]?" and "Does this seem like it fits what you're looking for?" (PMF-PT2) Let them articulate the fit in their own words. Their confirmation is far more powerful than your assertion.

The entire demo -- prepare, show, confirm -- should take 3 minutes or less. If the demo takes longer than that, you are showing too much, not preparing them well enough, or not checking for fit.

*Deploy when:* A founder asks "what should I show in my demo?" or reports that demos feel unstructured. Give them the three-step framework and insist on the 3-minute constraint.

#### Demos Are Often Counterproductive [3/5]

"There's really no such thing as a sales call, demos are often counterproductive." (PMF-PT2)

This is the meta-principle: a demo is not inherently valuable. A demo that teaches the prospect how to use the product is counterproductive. A demo that proves the product works is counterproductive (they will evaluate that in a trial). A demo that shows all capabilities is counterproductive (it overwhelms them).

The only demo that works is one that creates the moment where the buyer connects your supply to their demand. This is "confirmation, not education." The buyer should walk away knowing one thing: "this fits my project." Not: "I understand how this product works."

"If you feel like you are there persuading and convincing people, that's a sign. I almost like put a flag at that part in the call and say, I need to go back there because something I did before then didn't work." (PMF-PT2) The feeling of needing to persuade during a demo means either you did not understand their demand well enough, or your supply description is not aerodynamic. The demo cannot fix a demand alignment problem. Going back and doing better discovery is the fix, not a better demo.

*Deploy when:* A founder treats the demo as the centerpiece of their sales call -- spending 15+ minutes walking through the product. Reframe: the demo is a brief confirmation step, not the main event. The main event is demand alignment. If demand alignment is done well, the demo is almost unnecessary.

#### Persuasion During a Demo Is a Red Flag [3/5]

"IF YOU FEEL LIKE YOU ARE PERSUADING, that's a sign you're doing something wrong -- Not understanding demand strongly enough, OR, misunderstanding the point of a sales call!" (SLIDES, Day 4)

"If you feel like you are there persuading and convincing people, that's a sign. I almost like put a flag at that part in the call and say, I need to go back there because something I did before then didn't work." (PMF-PT2)

This is the real-time self-diagnostic. During a demo, if you catch yourself trying to convince the buyer that the product is good, that it will solve their problem, that they should be excited -- stop. That feeling is a signal. It means one of two things: (1) you do not understand their demand well enough and are guessing at what matters, or (2) they do not actually have demand and you are trying to create it.

Either way, the answer is not "push harder." The answer is "go back." Go back to demand. Re-confirm their project. Re-confirm what is lacking in their alternatives. Then show -- and only show -- the thing that addresses what is lacking.

"Any moment you find yourself pushing, that's a signal that something is off." (SLIDES, Physics of Sales pt 2)

*Deploy when:* A founder says something like "I really had to sell them on it" or "I spent a lot of time explaining why they should be excited." This is the red flag. Real demand does not need to be sold. If you are persuading, debug the demand alignment, not the demo.

#### Stories

**The Two-Screen Demo Story**
> "My friends ran a company, a bootstrap software company, like $10 million, and they had the same product. They just demoed it two different ways. The before was they showed them all the different screens in the product, basically taught them how to use it. And people would come out of that saying, wow, this is amazing. It can do so much, but man is it complicated. And then they wouldn't buy. Then, they did a demo where they showed two screens and described all the things they could do just from those two screens and only clicked the thing twice. And people would come out of that and say, wow, that can do so much and it's so simple and then they would buy."
*Teaching application:* This is the most powerful demo story in Rob's arsenal because it eliminates every excuse. Same product. Same company. Same prospects. The ONLY variable is the demo approach. All screens = "amazing but complicated" = no buy. Two screens = "so much and so simple" = buy. Use this story every time a founder says "but I need to show them everything so they understand what we can do." No. You need to show them less so they understand it fits.

**Renato's Continued Demo After "This Is Exactly What I Want"**
> Renato from Parallel had a buyer who said "this is exactly what I want" during the demo. Instead of stopping and transitioning to next steps, Renato continued demoing for 2+ more minutes -- including showing "how the model is built" (overwhelming) when the buyer only needed to see "the scenario stuff." Rob's commentary: "2+ more minutes of demo, aka HALF THE DEMO COMES AFTER THIS." The deal closed, but Renato had to offer a 50% discount to get there. Rob added: "I have been guilty of way more times than Renato has been guilty of."
*Teaching application:* Selling past the close is one of the most expensive mistakes a founder can make. The buyer was ready. The extra demo introduced complexity and doubt that cost Renato 50% of the deal value. The lesson is visceral and specific: when they say "this is what I want," STOP. Close your screen share. Ask about next steps. Every additional second of demo is costing you money.

**Emily & Clearly AI -- The "Holy Shit" Moment**
> Emily at Clearly AI (security review tool) found that by call #4, the buyer finally "gets it" and says "Holy shit" -- pulling for more: "Can *I* customize this?" (vs. "Can someone customize this"). Rob's coaching: "How do we change our description of supply / demo so they get this 'hell yes' moment and pull in call 1, consistently? (And not in call #4)." The challenge is redesigning the demo to reach that moment in minutes rather than weeks.
*Teaching application:* When a founder reports that prospects eventually get excited but it takes multiple calls. The "holy shit" moment exists -- but the demo is not reaching it fast enough. The work is identifying exactly what triggers that moment and designing the demo to reach it immediately. If it takes 4 calls, something about the first demo is either showing the wrong thing or showing too much.

**Renato's Co-founders Banned Him from Demoing**
> "Renato's co-founders do not let him even show the screen, the product screen anymore, because he does that too much. So now he can only use slides." (PMF-PT2)
*Teaching application:* A humorous but real operational solution. If a founder cannot stop themselves from over-demoing, use constraints. Slides-only forces brevity. No screen share forces verbal description. The constraint itself becomes the cure.
