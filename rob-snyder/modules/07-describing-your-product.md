### Describing Your Product Without Losing the Buyer

When a founder says "People don't get what we do," "My pitch is too long," "I describe features and their eyes glaze over," or "Prospects seem confused after I explain what we do." The problem is almost always that the founder is describing supply from the supply side -- explaining what the product does, how it works, and why it is impressive -- instead of describing it as the most obvious response to what the buyer is already trying to accomplish.

#### The Aerodynamic Supply Description [4/5] [SIGNATURE]

"We basically just want to describe it in the way that is really aerodynamic. What is the most obvious response to demand with the fewest different concepts?" (PMF-PT2)

This is Rob's signature concept for how to talk about your product. "Aerodynamic" means zero drag -- no theory, no persuasion, no non-sequiturs, no tangential concepts. Just the most direct response to their stated demand, delivered with the fewest possible words.

"You want to describe yours in a way that is the MOST AERODYNAMIC for them to PULL -- the most direct response to PULL; the fewest non sequiturs; the least amount of words and concepts so they 'get it' and can compare it to their options." (GUIDE)

The aerodynamic description does three things and nothing more:
1. States plainly what the product IS (category / form factor)
2. Explains how it enables the customer's specific project
3. Positions it against their known alternatives

"When we describe supply, we give an overview and then bring it back to project success." (PMF-PT2) There is no "our thesis on the market" or "our unique approach to the problem space." There is: here is what it is, here is how it fits your project, here is why it is better than what you have been considering.

The antidote to over-explaining is Rob's directness: when someone has said "I need a simple financial modeling tool to run scenarios" and you start talking about "working backwards" and "the value of a forecasting tool" and "turning outcomes into a math problem" -- "What's going on in this buyer's mind? -- I just want a simple fucking financial modeling tool, Renato!!!!" (SLIDES, Day 4)

*Deploy when:* A founder's product descriptions run longer than 60 seconds, introduce more than 2-3 concepts, or include words like "thesis," "approach," "paradigm," or "we believe." Strip it down to: what is it, how does it fit their project, why is it better than their alternatives.

#### Supply Positioning Formula (What Is It + Why Better) [3/5]

"Supply answers two questions: 1. What is it? (What is the category, or form factor?) 2. Why does it fit my demand better than my alternatives?" (GUIDE)

This is the structural formula for any product description. It has exactly two components:

**What is it?** -- The category or form factor. This tells the buyer what mental bucket to put you in. "AI notetaker" tells them it is like Fireflies or Otter. "Financial modeling tool" tells them it is like Excel or Causal. If they cannot categorize you, they cannot evaluate you.

**Why is it better than alternatives?** -- The differentiation, derived directly from what is LACKING in their current options. For Jump: "AI notetaker for financial advisors." The differentiation ("for financial advisors") comes directly from what is lacking in generic notetakers -- they are not compliant, not configured for advisory workflows.

"For Jump, the answer is: 'AI notetaker for financial advisors'. What it is: 'AI notetaker' (category / form factor). Why it's better than alternatives: 'it is specifically made FOR financial advisors' (differentiation)." (GUIDE)

The critical insight: differentiation is derived FROM demand, not FROM the product. You do not describe what makes your product special. You describe what makes it a better fit for their specific project than what they have already considered. The buyer's lacking options tell you what to emphasize.

*Deploy when:* A founder describes their product with a long paragraph of features and benefits. Compress it to the two-part formula: What is it? Why does it fit their demand better than alternatives? If they cannot answer in one sentence, their positioning is not clear yet.

#### The "Space" Demand Creates for Supply [2/5]

"Demand creates a 'space' that supply must fit into. The space is defined by what is LACKING in the buyer's current options. Your product description must fit precisely in that space -- nothing more, nothing less." (GUIDE)

This metaphor makes the supply description feel less like a creative exercise and more like a puzzle. The buyer has a project. They have considered options. Those options are lacking in specific ways. That lacking creates a "space" -- a gap shaped exactly like what they need. Your job is to describe your product in a way that fits that space precisely.

If your description is bigger than the space (introducing concepts, features, or capabilities beyond what they need), you overwhelm them. If your description is shaped differently than the space (emphasizing the wrong differentiation), they cannot see the fit. If it fits perfectly, they say "this seems like it fits." (PMF-PT2)

*Deploy when:* A founder is agonizing over how to position their product in the abstract. Redirect: positioning is not an abstract exercise. It is determined by what is LACKING in the specific buyer's alternatives. Once you understand their demand, the space is defined, and your description either fits or it does not.

#### "The User Heard a Project That Wasn't His" [2/5]

When you introduce too many concepts, the customer cannot find themselves in your description. They hear a product that solves someone else's problem.

The participant Ndonga articulated this: "The user heard a project that wasn't his. So he was like, oh, this sounds like a cool product, but it's for someone else. And when I am that someone, I'll come back." (PMF-PT2)

Rob highlights this as the key failure mode of over-explaining supply. When Renato talked about "working backwards" and "turning outcomes into a math problem" and "the value of forecasting," the buyer heard a sophisticated analytics philosophy when all he wanted was a simple tool to run scenarios. The buyer concluded this product was for someone smarter, more analytical, more strategic than him -- someone else.

This is the inverse of aerodynamic. An aerodynamic description lets the buyer see themselves immediately. An over-explained description makes the buyer think "this is for someone else."

"Everything feels complicated because we tend to be locked into seeing the world from the supply side. We want people to want our product." (PMF-PT2) The cure is flipping to demand-side thinking: what are THEY trying to do? Describe only what fits THAT.

*Deploy when:* A founder reports that prospects say things like "that's cool but I'm not sure it's for us" or "I can see how that would be useful for someone." The description is making the buyer feel like a bystander, not the protagonist. Simplify until the buyer sees their own project in your words.

#### Friction Compounds, Complexity Compounds [2/5]

"Friction compounds. Complexity compounds. Then kills us." (GUIDE)

"Friction is a design challenge -- we can design our supply to minimize friction!" (GUIDE)

Every unnecessary concept in your description adds friction. Every extraneous detail makes the buying decision feel bigger and scarier. This compounding effect means that a description with 5 concepts is not 2.5x worse than one with 2 concepts -- it is exponentially worse, because each concept introduces questions, doubts, and confusion that compound on each other.

This principle connects supply description directly to demo behavior (Module 8) and to the broader sales process. The same compounding applies everywhere: every extra meeting, every extra stakeholder, every extra feature shown compounds confusion and reduces the likelihood of a deal.

*Deploy when:* A founder wants to "be thorough" or "make sure they understand everything we can do." Thoroughness kills deals. Simplicity closes them.

#### Stories

**Renato's Train Wreck vs. Ish's Clean Description**
> Renato from Parallel (financial modeling for founders) had an inbound prospect who said "I desperately need to solve my financial modeling problem. I need to be able to run scenarios. And this other tool is too complex." This buyer was practically handing Renato the sale. Instead of saying "We're the simplest financial modeling solution, designed specifically for founders, and we build the model for you," Renato launched into a multi-minute monologue about his thesis on financial modeling, introduced concepts like "working backwards," "the value of a forecasting tool," and "turning outcomes into a math problem." The prospect, overwhelmed, said "I don't think this is for us right now." Renato salvaged it by offering a 50% discount, and the deal closed. Rob's comment: "I have been guilty of way more times than Renato has been guilty of."
>
> In the same session, Ish from Wayside (AI for people operations teams) described his product in under one minute: "This is essentially an AI assistant for people operations that can answer any question, do any HR task, lives on top of your existing systems... when inquiries come in, it drafts a personalized response that the team can review." No theory, no persuasion, just plain description. The buyer said "this seems like it fits." Rob's analysis: "He's just explaining the thing. And having her say, yeah, this seems like it fits our demand."
*Teaching application:* The A/B comparison makes it visceral. Same session, opposite approaches, dramatically different outcomes. Renato had real demand and nearly killed the deal by over-explaining. Ish had a clean description and got immediate confirmation. Use this story whenever a founder is over-explaining -- "Are you being Renato or Ish right now?"

**Gigit -- Getting Out of MBA-Land**
> Gigit started with "Humanized AI to help ecommerce brands improve inbound ads conversion with 1:1 lead nurturing." Rob breaks this down: "Humanized AI + 1:1 lead nurturing = SUPPLY (how). Improve inbound ads conversion = MBA-land." After examining one real customer: a skincare company "Doing 1:1 skincare consultations with humans, on their website, but only able to do limited volume & hours. Wanted to scale this." Demand reframe: "Scale 1:1 skincare consultations to work 24/7 for infinite customers on any channel."
*Teaching application:* When a founder's product description is abstract supply-side jargon that sounds like it was written for a pitch deck, not a buyer. The shift from "Humanized AI to improve inbound ads conversion" to "Scale 1:1 skincare consultations 24/7" is the shift from MBA-land to demand-land. One is a thesis. The other is a project the buyer recognizes immediately.

**MuukTest -- From Product Description to Project Description**
> MuukTest initially positioned as "The fastest QA automation software on earth" -- a product description. They had "6+ month sales cycles for $3k ACV deals, hard to make customers successful." After applying PULL and understanding the buyer's project, they reframed: "Fastest way to get QA off your plate, enabled by AI." Result: "20x+ price, faster sales cycles, happier customers, differentiated offering, $10k-$1M ARR in 1 year."
*Teaching application:* The shift is from describing the product ("fastest QA automation software") to describing the project outcome the buyer wants ("get QA off your plate"). The product is the same. The description changed. 20x price increase, faster cycles. That is the power of aerodynamic supply description.
