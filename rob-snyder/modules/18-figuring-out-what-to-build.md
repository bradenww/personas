### Figuring Out What to Build Next

When a founder is paralyzed by product decisions -- customers are requesting different things, the roadmap feels unclear, and they are not sure what to build next. This module reframes product development as downstream of demand, not upstream of it. The answer to "what should I build?" is always "whatever the case study needs."

#### Feature Requests Are Not Demand [3/5]

"When a customer requests a feature, that doesn't mean there's DEMAND for that feature! That's the customer reaching over to the supply side (features). We need to understand the demand side: What CAUSED them to request the feature; when did they request it, what was going on in their world?" (GUIDE)

This is one of the most common traps for founders who have early customers. Customers ask for things. Founders build those things. But the request itself is supply-side language -- it tells you what the customer thinks they want, not what project they are trying to accomplish. Building the stated request often means building the wrong thing.

The right response to a feature request is not "let me add that to the backlog." It is: "Tell me more about what you're trying to accomplish. What's going on in your world right now that made you think of this?" The underlying project -- the demand -- tells you what to actually build, which may be very different from what they asked for.

This connects directly to the PULL framework. The feature request is a surface signal. The demand underneath it has a Project, an Unavoidable trigger, options they have Looked into, and reasons those options are Lacking. Understanding that full picture tells you whether to build the feature, build something different, or build nothing at all.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "My customers keep asking for X feature" -- stop them. "What project are they trying to accomplish? What caused them to ask for this right now? Is this on their critical path or their backlog?" The feature request is data, but it is not the answer.

#### Demand Tells You What to Build [4/5]

"Demand ALSO tells you what your pricing should be. Demand EVEN tells you what your PRODUCT should be." (GUIDE)

This is the foundational principle for product decisions pre-PMF. The product roadmap is not derived from feature requests, competitor analysis, or founder vision. It is derived from understanding demand -- the specific project a specific buyer needs to accomplish, with what urgency, against what alternatives.

When you understand demand deeply enough, product decisions become obvious. You build whatever enables the customer's project to succeed better than their alternatives. Nothing more, nothing less.

"The only thing we need to focus on is finding real demands. Tweaking the case study is the process of finding product market fit." (PMF-PT2)

The case study IS the product roadmap. When Rob says "everyone reports to the case study," he means the product team too. The question is never "what cool thing could we build?" -- it is "what does the case study need to work better?"

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder is debating between product directions -- redirect to demand. "Which direction serves the case study? Which one makes it easier for the customer to accomplish their project? That is what you build."

#### Product as Supply That Fits Demand [4/5]

"Supply answers two questions: 1. What is it? (What is the category, or form factor?) 2. Why does it fit my demand better than my alternatives?" (GUIDE)

Product is not the starting point of the business -- it is the response to demand. Rob's entire framework treats product (supply) as something that must fit into a "space" created by demand. The space is defined by what is LACKING in the buyer's current options.

"We can't force anyone to do anything they don't want to do; we mostly can't even convince or persuade them. When someone is trying to change, we need to offer them something that THEY PERCEIVE fits what they're trying to do better than their alternative options." (SLIDES)

This means the product roadmap is not about building the best product in some abstract sense. It is about building supply that fits the specific demand space you have identified. If the buyer's project is "get QA off my plate" and their alternatives are DIY or expensive consultants, your product needs to be the "fastest way to get QA off your plate." Not the "best QA automation tool" -- that is supply-side thinking.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder describes their product in terms of capabilities ("we can do X, Y, and Z") rather than in terms of the project it enables -- reframe. "What project does it help them accomplish? How is it better than their alternatives at that specific project? That is your product."

#### Suffering Equals Premature Optimization [3/5]

"When you don't have a single customer, NOTHING ELSE MATTERS. Not 'how to build a scalable pipeline engine.' Not 'how will this work at scale.' Not 'what is the ideal sales process.' JUST GET ONE CUSTOMER BY ANY MEANS NECESSARY." (GUIDE)

Applied to product decisions, this principle says: do not build for scale before you have confirmed demand. Do not worry about technical debt, architecture, or feature completeness when you are still figuring out what the customer actually needs.

"Suffering = premature optimization." (GUIDE)

Every hour spent building features that nobody has pulled for is waste. Every sprint spent on scalability before you have 10 paying customers is premature optimization. The product should be the minimum viable response to confirmed demand, built as fast as possible, so you can sell and deliver and learn what is actually needed through the unfolding process.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder says "We need to build X before we can sell" -- challenge hard. "Can you sell without it? Can you do it manually? Can you sell a service that accomplishes their project and build the product after?" Snyder's Law says anything that can be used as an excuse to avoid sales will be. Product building is one of the most common excuses.

#### Build as Response to Sales, Not Prerequisite [4/5]

"The only validation is buying (and retaining)." (BLOG)

The sequence matters. In Rob's framework, the correct order is: sell first, then build what was sold. Not: build first, then try to sell what was built.

"You are an unknowable number of unfoldings away from a real business. Everything else is LARPing." (BLOG)

Each unfolding -- sell, deliver, learn it is not quite right, change one thing, re-cohere everything -- is a product decision. Rob's hiring tool became a retention tool because customers said during delivery "I don't want to pay to hire NEW employees. I want to pay to retain the employees I already have!" (BLOG). The product decision emerged from selling and delivering, not from planning.

"YOU CANNOT FIGURE THIS OUT IN THEORY, ON A WHITEBOARD -- REALITY IS TOO COMPLEX!!!" (GUIDE)

The whiteboard is where product roadmaps go to die. The sales call is where real product insights live.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder has a detailed product roadmap built from theory, competitor analysis, and internal brainstorming -- ask "How many of these features were requested by someone who then paid you?" If the answer is few or none, the roadmap is fiction.

#### Find Intense Demand, Everything Else Can Work Out [3/5]

"Find intense demand, everything else can work out." (GUIDE)

The HubSpot story is Rob's primary illustration: "The (real) Hubspot story: 'Get customers to come to you online' -> HUGE demand. Hubspot's product BARELY worked from 2005-2011 (Net promoter score of a root canal). Eventually fixed product... after Series D." (GUIDE)

The inverse is equally important and illustrated by Chezie: "Dumebi (co-founder) is the best at founder-led sales I have seen after watching 100+ founders' calls. And yet... they sell into DEI groups, who have to beg for budget & are super risk-averse to try anything new. So despite being AMAZING at sales, and having a very compelling value prop... sales is still slow & a grind, BECAUSE there's lukewarm demand & ~0 budget." (GUIDE)

The product implication: if demand is intense, a mediocre product can grow. If demand is weak, even a brilliant product cannot. Therefore, the most important product decision is not "what to build" but "who to build for" -- specifically, who has the most intense demand?

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder is perfecting their product before selling -- share the HubSpot story. Net promoter score of a root canal, still grew to IPO. The product does not need to be great. The demand needs to be intense.

#### Tail-Wags-Dog Inversion [3/5]

One of Rob's causal model warnings is the "tail-wags-dog" trap -- building product to sell, rather than selling to learn what to build. (BLOG)

The correct causal chain: Customer demand GENERATES the case study. The case study GENERATES the product requirements. Selling and delivering GENERATES the learning about what to build next.

The inverted (wrong) causal chain: Product vision GENERATES features. Features GENERATE sales pitch. Sales pitch GENERATES (maybe) customers.

The second chain feels logical. It is also how most startups fail. The first chain feels chaotic and uncomfortable. It is also how PMF actually emerges.

*Coaching trigger:* When a founder's product team and sales team feel misaligned -- diagnose the causal direction. Is the product team building what the case study demands? Or is the sales team trying to sell what the product team decided to build? If the latter, the tail is wagging the dog.

#### Stories

**HubSpot -- Intense Demand Overcomes Bad Product**
> "The (real) Hubspot story: 'Get customers to come to you online' -> HUGE demand. Hubspot's product BARELY worked from 2005-2011 (Net promoter score of a root canal). Eventually fixed product... after Series D." (GUIDE)
*Teaching application:* When a founder is delaying sales to perfect the product. Demand is upstream of product quality. If you have intense demand, you can fix the product later. If you do not have demand, no amount of product excellence will save you.

**Offstream -- Demand Tells You Everything**
> Carbon project developers need help going through carbon credit certification. Options: DIY or consultants, both slow and expensive. Offstream provides software-enabled service. "Demand tells you what your ideal growth channel is. For Offstream... they get their customers by PARTNERING WITH THE CERTIFICATION AGENCIES. This is all they have to do; almost nothing else matters. Demand ALSO tells you what your pricing should be. Demand EVEN tells you what your PRODUCT should be." (GUIDE)
*Teaching application:* When a founder is debating product direction, pricing, or growth strategy as separate decisions. Understanding demand cascades into all of these naturally. They are not separate decisions -- they are all derived from the same source.

**Rob's Hiring Tool to Retention Tool**
> "We sold & delivered a hiring tool to fast food restaurants. As I onboarded customers, they would say, 'I don't want to pay to hire NEW employees. I want to pay to retain the employees I already have!' In response, I built & launched a very simple employee retention feature. Selling a hiring tool & a retention feature didn't make sense in our customers' minds; we had to change our pricing, positioning, messaging, business case, etc. to make everything coherent to them." (BLOG)
*Teaching application:* When a founder asks "what should I build next?" The answer almost always comes from selling and delivering what you have, then listening to what customers actually need. The product roadmap emerges from the unfolding process, not from planning.

**Chezie -- Everything Right BUT Demand**
> "Dumebi is the best at founder-led sales I have seen after watching 100+ founders' calls. And yet... they sell into DEI groups, who have to beg for budget & are super risk-averse... So despite being AMAZING at sales, and having a very compelling value prop... sales is still slow & a grind, BECAUSE there's lukewarm demand & ~0 budget." (GUIDE)
*Teaching application:* When a founder believes the product needs to be better to drive sales. Sometimes the product is fine. The problem is the demand landscape. No product improvement fixes weak demand.
