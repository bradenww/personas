### Running Your First Sales Calls

This module addresses the founder who has calls scheduled and is terrified. They may hate sales, feel like an impostor, not know what to say, or be carrying mental models from traditional sales training that will actively sabotage them at the 0-1 stage. The central reframe: pre-PMF sales calls are not performances where you convince someone to buy. They are collaborative investigations where you and the prospect figure out together whether there is a fit.

---

#### Pre-PMF Sales Shouldn't Feel Like Sales [5/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

This is the foundational reframe of Rob's entire approach to early-stage selling. He considers it the single most important mindset shift for founders who are afraid of or bad at sales.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"If you feel like you're pitching, persuading, or presenting... you're failing."

"Pre-PMF sales shouldn't feel like sales."

"There's really no such thing as a sales call, demos are often counterproductive."

"Someone either HAS demand or they don't. We don't control this. This is liberating: If they have demand, it's a sales call. If they don't have demand, it's not a sales call. AKA: WE DON'T DECIDE IF IT'S A SALES CALL OR NOT."

Rob's definition of a pre-PMF sales call is fundamentally different from what most founders imagine. It is not a pitch. It is not a demo. It is not a presentation with slides and a close. It is a conversation in which you are trying to understand whether this specific person has a project on their to-do list that your product could help with. If they do, you explore that together. If they don't, you learn something and move on.

"It's only a sales call if THEY have demand; otherwise it's a conversation with someone interesting."

**What it means in practice:** The pressure to "perform" on a sales call comes from the wrong mental model. Under the SELLER-PUSH model, the call is a test of your sales ability. Under the BUYER-PULL model, the call is an investigation of their situation. You cannot fail an investigation -- you can only learn from it. This reframe removes the fear that paralyzes most technical founders.

"IF YOU FEEL LIKE YOU ARE PERSUADING, that's a sign you're doing something wrong -- Not understanding demand strongly enough, OR, misunderstanding the point of a sales call!"

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder says "I hate sales" or "I'm not a salesperson" -- reframe: you're not selling, you're investigating
- When a founder is anxious about upcoming calls -- remove the performance pressure; it's not a test
- When a founder reports that calls feel adversarial or awkward -- diagnose whether they're operating under SELLER-PUSH
- When a founder is studying sales methodologies (Challenger, SPIN, etc.) -- redirect: those are for post-PMF; they will hurt you here

#### Selling Is Learning, Not Convincing [5/5] [EMPHATIC]

This is the purpose-level reframe. If pre-PMF sales shouldn't feel like sales, what IS the point? The answer: learning.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"I'm not trying to sell -- I'm trying to figure out what they'll buy, and why. The difference matters."

"The only thing we need to focus on is finding real demand. Tweaking the case study is the process of finding product-market fit."

"It's really hard to find something when you don't know what you're looking for."

Rob frames every pre-PMF sales call as a hypothesis test. You have a PULL hypothesis: "I believe that [this person] in [this situation] with [this unavoidable project] considers [these unworkable options] and when they see [our product], they will PULL." The call tests that hypothesis. Sometimes the hypothesis is confirmed and you make a sale. Sometimes it is disproven and you learn something that makes the next hypothesis better. Both outcomes are progress.

"The ground truth of this is what they're thinking and what they want to do and what they're trying to do. That's where ground truth is, not how we want them to."

**What it means in practice:** Each call teaches you one of three things: (1) your hypothesis is right and you have demand, (2) your hypothesis is wrong in a specific way you can debug, or (3) you've found something unexpected that changes your entire approach. None of these is failure. All of them are the work.

Rob's Sales Sprint process operationalizes this:
1. Craft a PULL hypothesis: Who would be weird not to buy?
2. Schedule 5 conversations with people fitting the hypothesis
3. Execute those 5 conversations (testing the PULL hypothesis, not pitching)
4. Analyze across calls with compare-and-contrast; adjust hypothesis; repeat

"It's a four-step process you run as fast as you can schedule 5 sales calls."

The sprint produces three layers of learning: insights from individual calls, patterns across calls, and directional decisions ("so what?").

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder says "my sales calls go nowhere" -- reframe: every call goes somewhere if you're learning
- When a founder is paralyzed by not knowing what to say -- give them the PULL hypothesis framework and tell them to test it
- When a founder has done 5-10 calls and is discouraged -- help them do the compare-and-contrast analysis: which calls had demand? Which didn't? What was different?
- When a founder is avoiding calls because they don't have a "perfect pitch" -- the pitch doesn't matter; the hypothesis does

#### Same Side of the Table [4/5]

This is the experiential description of what good pre-PMF selling feels like from the inside.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Sales calls should feel like you're on the same side of the table, helping them assess whether your product can help them accomplish the thing they're trying to accomplish."

Rob contrasts two experiences. Under SELLER-PUSH, the founder sits on one side of the table and the prospect sits on the other. The founder pitches; the prospect evaluates. The dynamic is adversarial. Under BUYER-PULL, both sit on the same side of the table, looking at the PULL hypothesis together. The founder says: "Here's what I think might be going on for you. Am I right?" The prospect says: "You're close, but actually it's more like this." They explore together.

**What it means in practice:** If a call feels like a performance or a pitch, something is wrong. Good pre-PMF calls feel like a collaborative investigation. The founder is curious, not persuasive. The prospect is revealing, not defending. The energy is mutual problem-solving, not salesmanship.

This also changes the emotional weight of "rejection." When you're on the same side of the table, a "no" is just information: this person doesn't have the project, or their options are good enough, or the timing is wrong. It's not a judgment of you or your product. It's a finding.

"We aren't *really* selling a product, we're selling *something that helps them accomplish their project.*"

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder says their calls feel adversarial or awkward -- check if they're on opposite sides of the table
- When a founder is preparing a pitch deck for a sales call -- redirect: don't present; investigate
- When a founder asks "how do I handle objections?" -- on the same side of the table, objections are shared diagnostic findings, not attacks to defend against

#### Demand Alignment Before Anything Else [4/5]

This is the structural principle for what happens on the call. Before any demo, any product discussion, any supply description -- you must first align on demand.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"The most important part of any sales call is to align on demand with the buyer."

"So it sounds like in your specific situation, you have to accomplish this by this time, you're considering these different options and what really matters to you is this... I'm sure I got something wrong in there. What did I miss?"

"If we don't do this we might wind up thinking we're speaking the same language as them, but speaking totally different languages."

**What it means in practice:** The first portion of every call is spent understanding their project, what's forcing it to the top of their list, what options they've looked into, and why those options aren't working. Only after you and the prospect agree on this picture do you introduce your product. If you skip this step and jump to a demo or a pitch, everything afterward is built on an unverified assumption.

The demand alignment has a specific form -- the demand recap:
"Ok, sounds like you're trying to accomplish X right now because Y, and considering Z option. Maybe I'm wrong here -- what am I missing?"

This recap is the fulcrum of the call. It is where the conversation pivots from discovery to supply. Getting it right means the rest of the call flows naturally. Getting it wrong -- or skipping it -- means you're pitching blind.

"Your demand slides are not used as presentation -- but to PULL OUT AND FLESH OUT THEIR PROJECT."

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder jumps straight to demoing without understanding the buyer's project -- prescribe demand alignment first
- When calls run long without an outcome -- the founder likely never aligned on demand
- When a founder gets ghosted after Call 1 -- they may have never confirmed that the buyer actually has the project

#### The Sales Sprint Process [4/5]

This is the operational rhythm Rob prescribes for pre-PMF founders. It is deliberately simple -- "brutally simple by design" so there are "zero excuses for not doing it."

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"It's a four-step process you run as fast as you can schedule 5 sales calls."

**Steps:**
1. **Craft a PULL hypothesis:** Who would be weird not to buy? Define the person, the situation, the project, the trigger, and why their current options are lacking.
2. **Schedule 5 conversations** with people who fit the hypothesis. Do not overthink outreach. Just get the meetings.
3. **Execute those 5 conversations.** Test the PULL hypothesis on each call. Don't pitch -- investigate. See if the hypothesis holds.
4. **Analyze across calls.** Compare and contrast. Why did Steve have demand but Jane didn't? What was different about their situations? Adjust the hypothesis and repeat.

Each sprint produces three layers of output: learning from individual calls, patterns across calls, and a directional "so what?" decision.

**What it means in practice:** The Sprint is the antidote to analysis paralysis. Instead of spending weeks researching the market, building personas, or perfecting a pitch, the founder schedules 5 calls and runs the experiment. Each sprint takes days, not months. And each sprint produces real data that no amount of desk research could generate.

"IQ is negatively correlated to success at this stage." Smart founders over-theorize and under-execute. The Sprint forces execution.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder is stuck in analysis paralysis about their ICP -- prescribe a Sprint to generate real data
- When a founder has been doing research/interviews for weeks without progress -- shift to selling as the learning mechanism
- When a founder just pivoted and needs to test a new direction fast -- structure a Sprint around the new PULL hypothesis
- When a founder says "I don't know enough yet to sell" -- they know enough to form a hypothesis and test it in 5 conversations

#### You Can Sell Without a Product [2/5]

This concept removes the last excuse for not selling.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"We can sell WITHOUT a product without lying about us having a product or apologizing for not having a product. In many cases we can just sell a service that gets their project done, and they're willing to pay MORE for that than they would pay for a product."

**What it means in practice:** If the buyer has a project and bad options, you can offer to solve the project by any means necessary -- manually, with consulting, with a cobbled-together service. The buyer doesn't care about your product; they care about their project. Selling a service that gets the project done is a perfectly valid way to start the unfolding process, generate revenue, and learn what the product should actually be.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder says "I can't sell yet, the product isn't built" -- you can sell the outcome
- When a founder is using product incompleteness as a reason to delay selling (Snyder's Law) -- call it out
- When a founder has demand but no product -- sell a service, learn, then build

#### Stories

**Rob's Friend Who Was Confused by His Sales Call** [3/5]

> "A founder friend watched one of my sales calls for my new startup and was... confused. He'd taken all the startup sales courses at Harvard Business School, read the (very good) Founding Sales book, etc. He had learned a particular set of mental models about sales that felt like pitching, probing for pain points, persuading, demoing, PUSHING. My sales call, however, demonstrated no particular sales aptitude. It didn't even SEEM like a sales call. But it still wound up with the prospect buying."

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder thinks sales calls need to look like traditional sales -- with a pitch structure, a demo flow, objection handling, and a close. Rob's call looked like a conversation. It didn't have a polished structure. It didn't demonstrate "sales skills." But the prospect bought, because the call was oriented around PULL, not PUSH. The friend's confusion is the exact confusion most founders have: they think sales is a performance, when at the 0-1 stage, it is an investigation.

**Newly/Nuooly -- 75% Close Rate from 4 Questions** [4/5]

> Newly is a paid community for independent lawyers and accountants. They do only cold email outbound and have a 75% close rate from first meeting. Their demand: independent professionals who left a big firm, are past survival stage, and "miss the perks they had when they were at the big firm." They ask four questions: (1) Why did you take this call? (2) Why did you leave the big firm? (3) What do you miss about the big firm? (4) What are you doing about that today? "From these four questions, you can see demand." Rob calls these "killer discovery questions" that predict with ~90% confidence whether someone will buy.

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder says "I don't know what to ask on calls." Newly's example shows that when you truly understand your demand -- when you know the exact PULL your customers experience -- you need only 2-4 questions to determine whether someone has it. The questions are not about pain points. They are about projects, triggers, and actions taken. Notice the structure: question 1 checks for self-selection (why are you here?), questions 2-3 reconstruct the trigger event and demand, question 4 checks the "L" (what options have they looked into?). This is the PULL framework disguised as a natural conversation.

**Rob Trying Challenger Sales as a Doofus** [3/5]

> "I wish someone had slapped me when I was a total doofus trying to use Challenger Sales at this stage: 'Really, Rob? Are you REALLY going to win by CHALLENGING your prospects when you've got like four semi-satisfied, occasionally-paying customers and have basically no clue what your business OR their business is?'"

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder is studying post-PMF sales methodologies and trying to apply them at the 0-1 stage. Challenger Sales, SPIN Selling, Sandler -- these frameworks are designed for salespeople at established companies with known products and known markets. At the 0-1 stage, you don't know enough to challenge anyone. You don't know enough to diagnose their pain with SPIN questions. You barely know your own business. The right approach at this stage is humility and curiosity, not methodology and technique.
