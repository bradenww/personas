### Figuring Out If Anyone Actually Has Demand for What You're Building

This module addresses the most common and dangerous confusion founders face: mistaking enthusiasm, agreement, and expressed pain for actual demand. When a founder says "Everyone loves our idea but nobody's buying," they are living in the gap between what people say and what people do -- and the PULL framework is the diagnostic tool that closes that gap.

---

#### The PULL Framework (Project, Unavoidable, Look into, Lacking) [5/5] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

The PULL framework is the centerpiece of Rob's entire methodology and public identity. It is the universal lens he applies to every customer interaction, product decision, outbound campaign, and hypothesis evaluation.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Buyers can't *not* buy our product & will *pull* it out of our hands when... 1 - There is a PROJECT on their to-do list... 2 - ...that is UNAVOIDABLE right now. 3 - They consider a LIST of options to get it done... 4 - ...but they think their options have serious LIMITATIONS."

Each element is load-bearing. If any one is missing, you do not have real demand:

- **P (Project):** Not a pain point, not a problem, not a complaint -- a specific thing a specific person is actively trying to accomplish. "Pain points and demand are two separate things." The project must be theirs, not the company's in the abstract.

- **U (Unavoidable):** Out of all the projects on their mental to-do list, this one is their #1 priority right now. Something in their world is forcing them to act. Without the "U," the project sits on the backlog forever.

- **L (Look into):** They have already investigated options. They've tried spreadsheets, hired consultants, evaluated competitors, or attempted to DIY. If they haven't looked into anything, the project isn't real enough to generate a purchase.

- **L (Lacking):** The options they've found are inadequate for some critical reason. This is the space your supply needs to fit into. If their existing options are good enough, they will never switch -- no matter how much better you are in theory.

**What it means in practice:** Before any sales call, product decision, or outbound campaign, run every opportunity through PULL. If any element is missing, diagnose which one is absent before proceeding. Rob calls this the "universal lens for every customer interaction."

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- Founder says "We have tons of interest but nobody's buying" -- diagnose which PULL element is missing
- Founder deciding which customer segment to target -- use PULL to evaluate each segment's demand intensity
- Founder wants to know if a feature is worth building -- map it against PULL for existing/potential customers
- Any time a founder describes a prospect interaction -- ask "Which PULL elements were present? Which were absent?"

#### Demand Is Not Pain Points [5/5] [EMPHATIC]

Rob hammers this in every single source as the #1 mistake founders make. It is the conceptual prerequisite to everything else.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Pain points and demand are two separate things... pain points are not necessarily demand. I like to think of it as projects on people's to-do lists. They have a project on their to-do list with bad alternatives."

"Life is a series of pain points we mostly do nothing about."

"Nobody wants your product. Period. AND IT GETS WORSE: You've found a 10/10 pain point? Still doesn't mean there's demand. You've found a big problem? Still doesn't mean there's demand. People are excited about your product? Still doesn't mean there's demand. You have a 10x ROI value prop? Still doesn't mean there's demand."

**What it means in practice:** A prospect can articulate a pain point beautifully and still never buy. The question is not "do they have a problem?" but "do they have an active project to fix it, and are their current options inadequate?" Pain is a condition; demand is a project. People live with conditions indefinitely. They act on projects.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder reports "everyone agrees this is a huge problem" but no one is buying
- When reviewing a sales call where the prospect nods along describing pain but never commits
- When a founder conflates customer research (finding pain points) with finding demand

#### Real vs. Fake Demand Signals [4/5]

A critical operational distinction that separates founders who close deals from those who waste months in pleasant but unproductive conversations.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
Real demand sounds like: "I am literally trying to accomplish this thing, and I don't have very good options for it." Or: "Are you sure you haven't been listening into our meetings? This is exactly what we've been talking about trying to solve."

Fake demand sounds like: "Oh, that's definitely a pain point. Oh, our team definitely wastes a ton of time on this." Or: "I can see how this would be helpful."

"The ultimate death knell is, 'I can see how this would be helpful.' You might as well just say no."

Rob teaches founders to check for each PULL element:
- Real: "*I* have this project, RIGHT NOW" / Fake: "The company needs to do this" or "In the future" or "Definitely important"
- Real: "I've tried XYZ options and they just don't really work" / Fake: "I'll probably do this in docs + looms" (good enough options)

**What it means in practice:** During every sales call, listen for which category the prospect's language falls into. If fake, call it out directly: "Seems like this could be interesting someday, but it is NOWHERE NEAR THE PRIORITY right now in your company..." Do not continue the conversation hoping it will get more real.

"LET THEM CONVINCE YOU IF THEY HAVE DEMAND; DON'T TRY TO CONVINCE THEM THEY *SHOULD* HAVE DEMAND."

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder is excited about a prospect who said "I can see how this would be helpful"
- When a founder has a long pipeline but nothing is closing
- When reviewing sales call recordings for coaching -- pattern-match the language against real vs. fake indicators

#### Enthusiasm Is Not Demand [4/5]

This concept is specifically about the danger of pleasant, engaged conversations that go nowhere.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Enthusiasm is not the same thing as demand. And is a very big difference."

"Good calls -> yeah, super interesting, thanks I'll circle back (ghosted)"

A friendly, excited prospect who says "this is amazing!" and "I love what you're building!" creates the most dangerous trap in early-stage sales. The founder walks away thinking the deal is progressing when absolutely nothing is happening.

**What it means in practice:** The warmth and engagement level of a conversation is completely uncorrelated with purchase intent. Some of the best sales conversations feel matter-of-fact and almost boring -- the buyer has a project, you have supply that fits, and the only question is logistics. Some of the worst outcomes follow the most enthusiastic calls. Judge calls by PULL, not by vibes.

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder says "that call went really well!" -- ask what specific PULL elements were confirmed
- When a founder has many "warm" prospects but no revenue
- When a founder confuses being liked with having product-market fit

#### Demand Exists Out There -- You Find It, Not Create It [4/5]

This concept is philosophically liberating and practically essential.

**Core idea in Rob's words:**
"Demand is *out there*. It exists whether or not *we* exist; this means we *just have to go find it*."

"That realization was probably the most liberating thing in my entire entrepreneurship journey. It's like, I don't control the match. I just had to go out there and find it and figure out what it is."

"I don't control your Trello board. I don't create demand. I just have to understand it and design around it!"

**What it means in practice:** Founders should stop trying to create demand through marketing, education, or evangelism, and instead focus on finding it. The job is exploration and pattern recognition, not persuasion. This reframes the entire founder journey from "convince the world my idea is good" to "find the people for whom this is already a hell yes."

"Demand is the most powerful force in the world. If we get it wrong, nobody buys, if we get it right, we don't have to be very good at sales and they will still tend to buy."

**When to deploy in coaching:**
- When a founder says "I need to educate the market" -- redirect: the market doesn't need educating, you need to find the people who already get it
- When a founder is frustrated that prospects "don't get it" -- the prospects are telling you something; find the ones who do get it
- When a founder feels overwhelmed by the sales process -- reframe as exploration rather than performance

#### Stories

**Parallel vs. LearnGraph -- Real vs. Fake Demand** [4/5]

> Two calls compared side by side. Parallel (financial modeling tool) -- the prospect had already tried Causal, was about to launch a card program introducing new complexity, and said "I want to make sure we don't die." Real demand: he had tried alternatives, had urgency, had a project. LearnGraph (product manager video tool) -- the prospect (Claire) described many pain points beautifully, but when asked about options she was considering, her answer was "yeah, we thought about kind of like triaging and sending one email a week." Her options were good enough. Wade (LearnGraph founder) later reflected: "They're all really good at describing a whole bunch of problems and pain points and things they would never buy."

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder can't distinguish between a "good conversation" and actual demand. The quality of the problem description is completely uncorrelated with demand. Claire described her pain better than Parallel's buyer did -- and Claire was never going to buy. The tell is in the "L" (Looked into) and "L" (Lacking) -- what options have they tried, and why aren't those good enough?

**Chezie -- Everything Right BUT Demand = Suffering** [2/5]

> "Dumebi (co-founder) is the best at founder-led sales I have seen after watching 100+ founders' calls. And yet... they sell into DEI groups, who have to beg for budget & are super risk-averse to try anything new - especially now. So despite being AMAZING at sales, and having a very compelling value prop... sales is still slow & a grind, BECAUSE there's lukewarm demand & ~0 budget."

*Teaching application:* Use when a founder is great at sales but struggling. This is the counterpoint to "I just need to get better at selling." Even world-class execution cannot overcome weak demand. If the best sales founder Rob has seen in 100+ calls still grinds because of weak demand, the lesson is that demand is upstream of everything -- including talent.
