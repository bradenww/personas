# Extraction: Charlie Rose Interview — February 2009

- **Source:** Charlie Rose Interview: Jeff Bezos — February 26, 2009
- **Type:** Television Interview (1-on-1)
- **Duration:** ~54 minutes
- **Context:** Kindle 2 launch week. Amazon stock had fallen 94% from peak by 2001 but was rebounding strongly. Interview covers Kindle 2, Amazon strategy, AWS/cloud computing, Blue Origin, global economy, social networking, and Bezos's personal philosophy. Recorded during the global financial crisis.

---

## 1. Key Themes

### Customer Obsession Over Competitor Obsession
The foundational operating principle. Bezos explicitly contrasts Amazon's customer-focused approach with competitor-focused strategies (close following). He acknowledges competitor-focused strategies can work but says "it's not ours. It's not what we do. It's not how we are made." Customer obsession drives improvement even when Amazon is already in the lead.

### Long-Term Orientation (Even at the Cost of Being Misunderstood)
This is the single quality Bezos identifies as most important to his success. He explicitly names the pair: "willingness to be long-term oriented and willingness to be misunderstood. Those two things go together." Kindle took 4+ years of investment before meaningful financial impact. Blue Origin is even longer-horizon ("People who tease me about being too long-term oriented at Amazon would be horrified by Blue Origin").

### Strategy Built on Stable Customer Needs
Bezos's key strategic insight: base strategy on what WON'T change, not what will. Low prices, vast selection, fast delivery — these are permanent customer desires. "If you base your business strategy on things that are going to change, then you have to constantly change your strategy."

### Invention Requires Willingness to Fail
Experimentation is central. "If you want to be inventive, you have to be willing to fail, because you have to be willing to do experiments. And it's not an experiment if you know in advance it's going to work." Cites A-9.com (search) and Amazon Auctions as failures that either taught lessons or led to new successful businesses (third-party marketplace).

### Purpose-Built vs. Swiss Army Knife
Kindle is deliberately NOT a general-purpose device. Bezos uses the Swiss army knife metaphor: "If you build a Swiss army knife, it's never the best thing to eat dinner with." Every feature decision on Kindle is filtered through whether it compromises the core reading experience.

### No Short-Term / Long-Term Customer-Shareholder Misalignment
Bezos asserts there is "never a misalignment between customers and shareholders" in the long term. In the short term, Amazon will do "expensive" and "shareholder-unfriendly" things to stay aligned with customers, believing it all converges.

### Global Optimism Through Innovation
Despite the 2009 financial crisis, Bezos is deeply optimistic. Source of optimism: the global pipeline of inventive, educated people working on hard problems (clean energy, medicine, IT). "We live in an idea economy, where ideas generate wealth."

### Simplicity as the Ultimate Value
"We live in a complex world. And if you can figure out how to make things simpler for people, they will value it." Applied to Kindle's no-contract, no-computer-required, 60-second delivery model.

## 2. Principles & Beliefs

1. **Base strategy on what's stable, not what's changing.** "What is going to stay the same?" is a more important question than "what's going to change?" Low prices, vast selection, fast delivery will always matter.

2. **Customer obsession > competitor obsession.** "We have always had the mentality that we're going to be obsessed over our customers and not obsessed over our competitors." This drives continuous improvement even from a leading position.

3. **Long-term orientation requires willingness to be misunderstood.** These are paired traits. Investments that take 5-7 years to pay off will draw criticism. You have to accept that.

4. **Invention requires willingness to fail.** "It's not an experiment if you know in advance it's going to work." Failures are the cost of experimentation. Amazon Auctions failed but led to the third-party marketplace.

5. **Trust is hard earned and easily lost.** Applies to companies and individuals equally. Amazon extends trust into new categories by consistently delivering on low price, selection, and speed.

6. **Me-too strategies don't work on the internet.** "If somebody has a different way of doing something, that can be successful. But if you try to just do it as a me-too offering, probably if customers were already doing it with somebody, why would they switch to you?"

7. **Technology should disappear.** The #1 design objective for Kindle was to "get out of the way and disappear" so the reader enters the author's world. "The key feature of a physical book is that it gets out of the way."

8. **Never compromise the core experience for secondary features.** "What we will never do with this device is add anything to it that compromises the long-form reading experience." Applied to rejecting color screens (eyestrain), limiting mp3 to background music, etc.

9. **Your passions choose you; you don't choose your passions.** Said in context of Blue Origin and his lifelong interest in space.

10. **Non-zero-sum worldview.** Global innovation is additive. "It's not a zero-sum game. It is happening all over the world." More smart, educated people globally means a better world for everyone.

11. **People are fundamentally the same everywhere.** In every country Amazon enters, customers want the same things: low prices, vast selection, fast delivery. Companies "over-exaggerate the differences in different geographies."

12. **If you're not experimenting, you eventually get run over.** Said about publishing industry and Stephen King's willingness to experiment with Kindle-first content.

13. **Short-term grasping can lead astray.** Worries about isolationism (trade restrictions, immigration restrictions) during macroeconomic crisis as examples of short-term thinking with long-term damage.

## 3. Decision Frameworks

### The "What's Not Going to Change?" Framework
Instead of predicting the future, identify the permanent customer needs and build strategy around them. Three for Amazon: low prices, vast selection, fast/convenient delivery. This eliminates the need to constantly change strategy as the landscape shifts.

### Purpose-Built vs. Multi-Purpose Decision Filter
For product design: every proposed feature must pass the test of "does this compromise the core use case?" If yes, reject it even if it's technically easy or cheap. Example: could easily put a cheaper color LCD on Kindle, but it would compromise the reading experience, so they don't.

### The "Would Customers Say the Opposite?" Test
Implied but present: test whether a customer need is permanent by asking if anyone would ever say the opposite. "Nobody is going to say I wish Amazon delivered my products a little more slowly." "You never go into a new country and they say, oh, I love the Amazon, I just wish the prices were a little higher."

### Failure-as-Input Model
Failures are not dead ends but inputs to future successes. Amazon Auctions → third-party marketplace. The question isn't "did it work?" but "what did it lead to?"

### Safety Threshold Framework (Blue Origin)
For non-essential, high-risk activities (space tourism vs. work-critical): the safety bar must be very high. Bezos won't fly on existing orbital vehicles as a tourist because "it's still quite dangerous" and as "a tourist mission, for me it has to be very safe." Different from if it were "important" — risk tolerance is contextual.

## 4. Mental Models & Metaphors

### Electric Grid Analogy (for Cloud Computing)
100 years ago, factories built their own electric power generators. But making your own electric power "didn't make your beer taste any better. It wasn't part of their secret sauce." As soon as the grid came online, they dumped their generators. Cloud computing (AWS) is the same transformation for infrastructure computing.

### Swiss Army Knife Metaphor (Product Focus)
"If you build a Swiss army knife, it's never the best thing to eat dinner with. You would rather have a knife and a fork." Applied to Kindle — purpose-built reading devices beat general-purpose devices for the core use case.

### Digital Camera / Cell Phone Camera Analogy
The dedicated device (Kindle) is like a digital camera; the phone version is like the camera on your cell phone. You want both. The phone camera is for when you don't have the dedicated device (grocery store line). The dedicated device is the primary experience.

### "Flashlight Shining in Your Eyes" (LCD vs. E-Ink)
Emissive displays (LCD) cause eyestrain "because it is like reading while somebody's shining light in your eyes." E-Ink is reflective like real paper — fundamentally different and superior for sustained reading.

### Books on Dead Trees
"It is not written anywhere that books shall forever be printed on dead trees." The current form factor is not sacred; it's just the technology that existed.

### College Students as Optimism Reset
"If I ever get discouraged, all I have to do is spend half an hour with a group of college students, and it will completely fix me." Young, inventive, passionate people are the leading indicator of the future.

## 5. Management & Leadership

### Cultural Traits as Competitive Advantage
Bezos frames Amazon's success as primarily about *cultural traits*, not individual brilliance: "willingness to be misunderstood, a willingness to fail, and a willingness to be long-term oriented... those cultural traits are an important contributor to what we have built at Amazon."

### Operating Efficiency as Recession Advantage
14 years of relentless focus on operating efficiency means "your value proposition to customers shows itself a little better in this kind of economy." The discipline of low prices isn't just customer-friendly — it's a structural moat during downturns.

### Assume Similarity, Adapt for Differences (Global Expansion)
Starting assumption when entering new countries: "let's just assume that people are generally very similar all over the world." Then adapt for local business conditions (cash on delivery in China, bicycle couriers, regional fulfillment centers). But don't over-engineer for differences that may not exist (Japan customer reviews worked fine despite predictions they wouldn't).

### Missionaries vs. Mercenaries (Implied)
"We really feel very missionary" about Kindle and reading. The passion is about the mission (every book ever printed in any language, within 60 seconds), not just the business.

### No Number Disclosure Culture
Amazon has "a long tradition" of not disclosing granular metrics. Bezos deflects Charlie Rose's persistent questioning about Kindle sales numbers with humor and firmness. Described as "a curious case of shyness" but the principle is clear: don't give competitors data points.

### Embrace Industry Disruption, Don't Fight It
On publishers: "future-leaning publishers... want to get ahead of this change." Music industry is the cautionary tale. "You don't want to wait so long that you are fighting after it's gotten away from you."

## 7. Biographical Details

- **Family:** Married to Mackenzie, four young children (as of 2009). Charlie Rose describes asking about family as a ritual every interview.
- **Blue Origin:** Working on second development vehicle. First flew for ~90 seconds (vertical takeoff and vertical landing). At least a third development vehicle planned. Operating from "a few hundred thousand acres" in Texas.
- **Space safety stance:** Won't fly on existing orbital vehicles (Soyuz etc.) because he doesn't think it's safe enough for a tourist mission. Had this conversation with Charles Simonyi (who went twice). "I have four little kids" as a factor.
- **Amazon at time of interview:** 50% revenue outside US. 14 years old. Selling shoes, dry goods, auto parts, electronics in addition to media. Kindle 1 sold out two Christmases in a row (2007 and 2008). Kindle 2 launched Feb 24, 2009 at $359.
- **Recent travel:** Just returned from India, toured a Belgian beer facility. Meets college students globally.
- **Personal style:** Characteristically deflects with humor (Kindle sales numbers dodging), deeply engaged on product details, genuinely enthusiastic about technology possibilities. Famous laugh present throughout.
- **Stock history:** Charlie Rose notes Amazon stock fell 94% by 2001 but was "rebounding fast" by 2009.
- **AWS at this time:** Already launched Elastic Compute Cloud, Simple Storage Service, Simple Queue Service. Growing "very rapidly" but Bezos won't disclose size — same policy as Kindle.

## 8. Unique Content

### Failures Discussed in Detail
- **A-9.com:** Licensed Google web search, built a different front end. Ran it for ~3 years, "nobody came." Shut it down. Bezos jokes he couldn't even get his mother to use it.
- **Amazon Auctions:** Tried to compete with eBay on auctions. "We could never get auctions to work." But led to the third-party fixed-price marketplace, which became hugely successful. Classic example of failure leading to adjacent success.

### Kindle as Disruptive Force on Publishing Economics
Bezos makes the economic case that e-books are structurally better for publishers and authors: no printing, no inventory risk ("you never print exactly the right amount"), no distribution cost, books never go out of stock. The challenge is rights disputes and royalty negotiations between publishers and authors, not technology.

### Kindle Shrinks Publishing Cycles
Stephen King's "Ur" as evidence: the novella references Obama as president and other current details. Traditional publishing has 18-month cycles; Kindle enables near-real-time currency. Bezos sees potential for serialized novels (Dickens-style installments), integrated music, and eventually integrated video.

### Cloud Computing Explained (Pre-Mainstream)
This is 2009 — cloud computing is still so new Bezos has to explain it from scratch. He explicitly says "you need to always explain it, because right now it is so early in the development of this that people use the term cloud computing to mean different things." Defines Amazon's offering as "infrastructure computing" — "pay by the drink, compute on tap."

### Alternative Energy Enthusiasm
If Bezos were coming out of college in 2009, he'd go into alternative energy. Specifically excited by "advanced bioengineering approaches" — genetically engineering algae to be more efficient at photosynthesis, converting solar energy to fuel. "Very clean, scalable solution for virtually unlimited amounts of energy."

### Social Networking + Mobile Prediction
In 2009, Bezos predicts "a blending of social networking and mobile that will be very useful to people." Also predicts smartphones won't replace laptops, and laptops didn't replace desktops, and desktops didn't replace mainframes: "In high tech, things generally don't go away. They get added to."

### Immigration as Economic Issue
During the financial crisis, Bezos worries about isolationism — specifically reduced immigration and restricted visas. Frames welcoming immigration as essential to America's inventive energy. "Some of the grasping for short-term solution could lead us astray."

### Amazon's China Operations (2009)
Cash on delivery instead of credit cards (low credit card penetration). Regional fulfillment centers. Amazon employs its own bicycle couriers. Significant operational adaptation, but customer needs remain identical.

### The "What If the Opposite?" Test (Implicit)
Bezos repeatedly validates strategic choices by testing if anyone would ever want the opposite: "Nobody is going to say I wish Amazon delivered my products a little more slowly." "You never go into a new country and they say, I just wish the prices were a little higher." This is a practical heuristic for identifying truly permanent customer needs vs. temporary preferences.

### Product Pricing Philosophy
On Kindle pricing: "We can afford to sell this device for $359, and so we want to." Amazon's mission is to lower prices. They deliberately avoided the cell phone subsidy model (cheap device + monthly contract) in favor of a clean one-time purchase. Charlie Rose advocates strongly for keeping it this way: the simplicity of "buy it and you're done" is a feature.
