# Module 2: Making Hard Decisions

**Load when:** The user is stuck on a difficult decision, facing ambiguity, dealing with irreversible choices, navigating disagreement within a team, or debating which path to take. This module covers Bezos's complete decision-making toolkit — from categorizing decisions by reversibility, to breaking deadlocks, to knowing when you have enough information to act.

---

## 1. Type 1 / Type 2 Decisions (One-Way vs Two-Way Doors) [10/22] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

The foundational Bezos decision framework. Every decision falls into one of two categories based on reversibility.

**Type 2 decisions (two-way doors)** are reversible. Walk through, and if you don't like what you see, walk back. These should be made quickly — by individuals or small groups with good judgment. Most decisions are Type 2.

**Type 1 decisions (one-way doors)** are irreversible or very costly to reverse. These require slow, deliberate, careful analysis.

> "Some decisions are consequential and irreversible or nearly irreversible — one-way doors — and these decisions must be made methodically, carefully, slowly, with great deliberation and consultation... But most decisions aren't like that — they are changeable, reversible — they're two-way doors." (SL2015)

The critical organizational failure: as companies grow, they default to treating all decisions as Type 1. Junior leaders observe senior leaders making slow, deliberate decisions on irreversible matters and conclude that ALL decisions should be made that way.

> "As organizations get larger, there seems to be a tendency to use the heavy-weight Type 1 decision-making process on most decisions, including many Type 2 decisions. The end result of this is slowness, unthoughtful risk aversion, failure to experiment sufficiently, and consequently diminished invention." (SL2015)

> "So now even trivial decisions end up going through a lot of extra unnecessary process." (AFA2018)

**Bezos's role:** He calls himself the "chief slowdown officer" on Type 1 decisions — insisting on seeing things one more way when the team thinks they've exhausted all angles. But he actively pushes speed on Type 2 decisions, explicitly delegating them down.

> "I'm the chief slowdown officer. The team that's working on it rolls their eyes. I've already seen it 18 ways, but I thought of a 19th way and I want to see it." (AFA2018)

> "Senior leadership can set the tone on that. They can say, 'Why are so many people involved in this decision? I want Jill to make this decision, and whatever Jill decides is fine. And guess what, Jill, if you get it wrong, it's OK. We're just going to back up and do it again. I don't want you to talk to anybody about this. Just go make the decision.'" (AFA2018)

**The quick-drying cement variant** — some technical decisions look reversible but harden fast. Propellant selection for rockets is one example. These masquerade as Type 2 but are actually Type 1.

> "Some technical decisions are like quick-drying cement. Once you make 'em, it gets really hard." (Lex)

## 2. Disagree and Commit [7/22] [EMPHATIC]

When teammates disagree and neither side can prove they're right, someone commits to the other's path — not grudgingly, but actively. No second-guessing, no sniping, no "I told you so." This avoids the two worst decision mechanisms: compromise and attrition.

> "Use the phrase 'disagree and commit.' This phrase will save a lot of time. If you have conviction on a particular direction even though there's no consensus, it's helpful to say, 'Look, I know we disagree on this but will you gamble with me on it? Disagree and commit?'" (SL2016)

**Crucially, this goes both ways — the boss does it too.** Bezos tells a vivid story of personally disagreeing with an Amazon Studios green-light decision:

> "I told the team my view: debatable whether it would be interesting enough, complicated to produce, the business terms aren't that good, and we have lots of other opportunities. They had a completely different opinion and wanted to go ahead. I wrote back right away with 'I disagree and commit and hope it becomes the most watched thing we've ever made.'" (SL2016)

He's emphatic that this is not passive delegation:

> "Note what this example is not: it's not me thinking to myself 'well, these guys are wrong and missing the point, but this isn't worth me chasing.' It's a genuine disagreement of opinion, a candid expression of my view, a chance for the team to weigh my view, and a quick, sincere commitment to go their way." (SL2016)

On the Lex Fridman podcast, Bezos describes how he frames it to his reports:

> "I would often say, you know what, I disagree. I don't think you're right, but I'm going to gamble with you. And you're closer to the ground truth than I am." (Lex)

## 3. The 70% Rule [5/22]

Don't wait for perfect information. Act when you have roughly 70% of the data you wish you had.

> "Most decisions should probably be made with somewhere around 70% of the information you wish you had. If you wait for 90%, in most cases, you're probably being slow. Plus, either way, you need to be good at quickly recognizing and correcting bad decisions. If you're good at course correcting, being wrong may be less costly than you think, whereas being slow is going to be expensive for sure." (SL2016)

This principle is tightly coupled to the Type 1 / Type 2 framework. On two-way doors, the cost of being wrong is small and recoverable. Being slow, however, is always expensive — because slowness compounds. Every day you don't decide is a day you don't learn from the consequences of having decided.

The 70% rule also connects to Bezos's early Amazon fundraising. He told investors there was a 70% chance they'd lose their money, and in retrospect thinks even that was generous:

> "I think I was giving myself better than the odds were." (DealBook2024)

Despite those odds, he acted. The regret of not trying weighed more than the risk of failure.

## 4. Bad Decision Mechanisms: Compromise and Attrition [4/22]

Bezos is explicit about what NOT to do when disagreements arise. Two mechanisms are singled out as destructive.

**Compromise** is low-energy but not truth-seeking. It produces outcomes nobody actually wants.

> "Compromise, the advantage of compromise as a resolution mechanism is that it's low energy, but it doesn't lead to truth." (Lex)

Bezos uses a vivid example: if two people disagree on a ceiling height, the answer isn't to split the difference. The answer is to get a tape measure. Compromise substitutes agreement for accuracy.

**War of attrition** is when whoever gets exhausted first capitulates. This is demoralizing and slow.

> "'You've worn me down' is an awful decision-making process. It's slow and de-energizing. Go for quick escalation instead — it's better." (SL2016)

> "Sometimes teams have different objectives and fundamentally different views. They are not aligned. No amount of discussion, no number of meetings will resolve that deep misalignment. Without escalation, the default dispute resolution mechanism for this scenario is exhaustion. Whoever has more stamina carries the decision." (SL2016)

The prescribed antidote: **escalate early.** Escalation is not a failure — it's a feature. When true misalignment exists, forcing the decision up to someone who can break the tie is faster and healthier than grinding through the conflict.

## 5. Math vs Judgment Decisions [3/22]

Some decisions can be computed. Others require taste, instinct, and conviction. Knowing which is which is the senior leader's core skill.

> "Many of the important decisions we make at Amazon.com can be made with data... These are our favorite kinds of decisions." But the most important decisions — pricing strategy, Amazon Prime, letting third-party sellers onto product pages — went against what the short-term math clearly said. (SL2005)

> "Any institution unwilling to endure controversy must limit itself to decisions of the first type." (SL2005)

Math-based decisions command broad agreement because the data settles them. Judgment-based decisions are inherently controversial because reasonable people disagree. The key insight: institutions that avoid controversy automatically limit themselves to math decisions, which means they'll never make the biggest, most transformative bets.

Bezos draws a clear line about when to trust math and when to override it:

> "When you can make a decision with analysis, you should do so, but it turns out in life that your most important decisions are always made with intuition, taste, heart." (EC2018)

## 6. Senior Executive Decision Quality [3/22]

Senior leaders are not paid to make thousands of decisions per day. They're paid to make a small number of high-quality decisions.

> "If I make, like, three good decisions a day, that's enough. Warren Buffett says he's good if he makes three good decisions a year." (EC2018)

This belief drives Bezos's entire daily structure. He sleeps eight hours, has no meetings before 10 AM, schedules mentally demanding work before lunch, and by 5 PM defers hard problems to the next day.

> "I think better, I have more energy, my mood is better." (EC2018)

The logic: sleeping four hours gives you 33% more time but degrades every decision you make. For someone whose job is three great decisions per day, that trade is catastrophic.

> "Senior leaders don't have to do a lot of things. They have to pick a few big ideas, and enforce tough execution against those big ideas." (AFA2018)

## 7. Conviction Splits (98/2 vs 51/49) [3/22]

Not all disagreements are created equal. Bezos thinks about conviction on a spectrum and adjusts his behavior accordingly.

> "I'm actually personally very easy to influence... But a couple of percent of the time, no force in the world can move me because I'm so sure of something." (DealBook2024)

When the split is 98/2 — when the team overwhelmingly agrees — stop debating and go. The decision is clear.

When it's 51/49 — a genuine toss-up — that's when you need mechanisms: get more data, escalate, or use disagree-and-commit. Don't pretend you know the answer when you don't.

Bezos is unusually transparent about signaling his conviction level. When he has an immovable view (rare, ~2% of decisions), he says so upfront rather than making people go through a charade:

> "Nothing here is gonna change my mind." (DealBook2024)

The Fulfillment by Amazon decision was one such case — Bezos was certain despite internal resistance. But he emphasizes that 98% of the time, new information readily changes his view.

> "People who are right a lot change their mind a lot. People who are wrong a lot are very stubborn on the details." (ITW2025)

This creates a practical heuristic: if you find yourself unable to change your mind on something, ask whether that's because the evidence is overwhelming — or because you've confused stubbornness with conviction.
