# Module 3: Building and Scaling a Company

Load this module when the user is running a company and dealing with growth challenges, organizational design, culture, scaling teams, maintaining quality at scale, or asking how to keep a large company innovative.

This is Bezos's richest operational module. It covers the daily mechanics of running Amazon -- the systems, rituals, and cultural disciplines that prevent a company from calcifying as it scales. Where Module 1 is about starting and Module 2 is about deciding, Module 3 is about sustaining.

---

## 1. Customer Obsession (Over Competitor Obsession) [22/22] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

The single most frequently cited principle across all sources. Every interview, every letter, every speech.

> "The number-one thing that has made us successful by far is obsessive-compulsive focus on the customer as opposed to obsession over the competitor." (EC2018)

> "Customers are always beautifully, wonderfully dissatisfied, even when they report being happy and business is great. Even when they don't yet know it, customers want something better." (SL2016-2020)

Why customer obsession over competitor obsession? Competitor-focused companies stop innovating when the competitor stops. Customer-focused companies never stop, because customers are "divinely discontent" -- expectations only go up.

> "One thing I love about customers is that they are divinely discontent. Their expectations are never static -- they go up. It's human nature. We didn't ascend from our hunter-gatherer days by being satisfied." (SL2016-2020)

Applied everywhere: at Amazon the customer is the buyer, at The Washington Post the reader, at his Day One schools the child. The principle is universal, not domain-specific.

## 2. Day 1 vs Day 2 [14/22] [SIGNATURE] [EMPHATIC]

Bezos named his office building "Day 1." When he moved buildings, he took the name with him. This is not a slogan -- it is Amazon's central operating philosophy.

> "Day 2 is stasis. Followed by irrelevance. Followed by excruciating, painful decline. Followed by death. And that is why it is always Day 1." (SL2016-2020)

Four defenses against Day 2 (from the 2016 shareholder letter):
1. **Customer obsession** -- "by far the most protective of Day 1 vitality"
2. **Skepticism of proxies** -- "The process is not the thing. It's always worth asking, do we own the process or does the process own us?"
3. **Eager adoption of external trends** -- "If you fight them, you're probably fighting the future. Embrace them and you have a tailwind."
4. **High-velocity decision-making** -- most decisions should be made at 70% information, not 90%

The Day 1 test is simple: is the rate of change still accelerating?

> "It's still day one. The alarm clock hasn't gone off yet. You haven't even hit the snooze alarm once." (CR2012)

Every shareholder letter closes: "As always, I attach a copy of our original 1997 letter. It remains Day 1."

## 3. Experimentation / Willingness to Fail [18/22] [EMPHATIC]

> "To be innovative you have to experiment. If you want to have more invention, you need to do more experiments per week, per month, per year, per decade. It's that simple. You cannot invent without experimenting." (AFA2018)

> "If you know it's going to work in advance, it is not an experiment." (AFA2018)

Failure and invention are "inseparable twins." Bezos doesn't just tolerate failure -- he demands it scale with the company:

> "As a company grows, everything needs to scale, including the size of your failed experiments. If the size of your failures isn't growing, you're not going to be inventing at a size that can actually move the needle." (SL2016-2020)

Fire Phone (billions lost), Amazon Auctions, zShops, Destinations -- all cited as the cost of doing business. But the Fire Phone team went on to build Alexa and Echo. Failed experiments recycle into future wins.

> "If you double the number of experiments you do per year, you're gonna double your inventiveness." (Reinvent2012)

## 4. "What's NOT Going to Change" Framework [10/22] [EMPHATIC]

> "I very frequently get the question, what's going to change in the next 10 years? I almost never get the question, what's not going to change in the next 10 years? And I submit to you that that second question is actually the more important of the two, because you can build a business strategy around the things that are stable in time." (Reinvent2012)

The rhetorical proof:

> "It's impossible to imagine a future 10 years from now where a customer comes up to me and says, Jeff, I love Amazon. I just wish the prices were a little higher." (Reinvent2012)

For Amazon: low prices, fast delivery, vast selection. For AWS: reliability, low cost, fast-evolving APIs. For Blue Origin: lower cost, higher availability, more reliability. Every dollar invested in stable needs compounds indefinitely.

> "The great thing about the big ideas is you don't have to launch a research project to figure them out. You know what they are in your head already. That's how big they are." (AFA2018)

## 5. Stubborn on Vision, Flexible on Details [9/22] [EMPHATIC]

> "If you're not stubborn, you'll give up on experiments too soon. If you're not flexible, you'll pound your head against a wall." (Lex)

Amazon Marketplace is the canonical example. The vision (third-party sellers on the platform) never changed. The execution evolved through three failures: Auctions (failed), zShops (failed), then Marketplace (succeeded). By 2018, third-party sellers were 58% of Amazon's sales.

> "Be stubborn on the vision and flexible on the details." (ITW2025)

> "People who are right a lot change their mind a lot. People who are wrong a lot are very stubborn on the details." (ITW2025)

## 6. Six-Page Memo / Narrative Culture [8/22] [SIGNATURE]

No PowerPoint at Amazon. Six-page narratively structured memos, read in 30 minutes of silence at the start of every meeting.

> "When you have to write your ideas out in complete sentences and complete paragraphs it forces a deeper clarity of thinking." (CR2012)

> "My perfect meeting starts with a crisp document... I like a crisp document and a messy meeting." (Lex)

Why it works: PowerPoint is designed to persuade (a sales tool). Memos force rigorous thinking -- "you can't hide sloppy thinking behind bullet points." Study hall ensures the memo is actually read, not bluffed. Questions that arise on page 2 are often answered by page 4.

> "A good 6-page memo might take 2 weeks to write. You have to write it. You have to rewrite it. You have to talk to people about it. They have to poke holes in it for you." (Lex)

Quality insight: most memo failures come from misunderstanding scope, not inability. People think a great memo takes two hours when it really takes two weeks.

> "By tradition at Amazon, authors' names never appear on the memos -- the memo is from the whole team." (SL2016-2020)

## 7. Flywheel Economics [8/22]

Lower prices -> more customers -> more sellers -> better selection -> lower costs -> lower prices. Each investment strengthens all others.

> "Third-party sellers are kicking our first party butt. Badly." (SL2016-2020)

Amazon deliberately built tools for independent sellers to compete against its own first-party retail. The result: third-party sellers grew from 3% to 58% of sales, at 52% CAGR vs Amazon's 25%.

Prime Video winning Golden Globes drives Prime membership, which drives shoe sales. The flywheel means each component accelerates the others -- it's hard to say which is pushing which.

## 8. Working Backwards (from the Customer) [7/22]

> "Start with the customer, figure out what they want, work backwards, figure out how to give it to them." (CR2007)

Contrasted with "skills-forward" thinking ("We are really good at X. What else can we do with X?"). Skills-forward is useful but leads to skill atrophy if used exclusively. Working backwards demands acquiring new competencies -- Amazon learned hardware engineering from scratch for Kindle.

> "Working backwards from customer needs often demands that we acquire new competencies and exercise new muscles." (SL2004-2010)

The press release method: write the press release first, then build the product. Forces clarity about who benefits and why.

## 9. Wandering / Loose Agendas [7/22]

> "Wandering in business is not efficient... but it's also not random. It's guided -- by hunch, gut, intuition, curiosity, and powered by a deep conviction that the prize for customers is big enough that it's worth being a little messy and tangential to find our way there." (SL2016-2020)

AWS, Echo/Alexa, and Prime all came from wandering -- none were requested by customers. The Pringles Can test:

> "If you had gone to a customer in 2013 and said 'Would you like a black, always-on cylinder in your kitchen about the size of a Pringles can that you can talk to and ask questions, that also turns on your lights and plays music?' I guarantee you they'd have looked at you strangely and said 'No, thank you.'" (SL2016-2020)

> "Wandering is so important because wandering is a kind of humility... the only way to go straight to your destination is if you know where you're going. And sometimes you don't." (ITW2025)

Bezos keeps meeting agendas deliberately loose, except for operational reviews, because tight agendas prevent discovery.

## 10. High Standards Treatise [5/22]

From the 2017 shareholder letter -- a full management essay on organizational quality. Four elements:

1. **Teachable** -- not innate; people learn through exposure to high-standards teams
2. **Domain-specific** -- mastery in one area doesn't transfer automatically
3. **Recognition** -- you must be able to identify what "good" looks like
4. **Realistic scope** -- you must understand how much effort quality actually requires

> "Unrealistic beliefs on scope -- often hidden and undiscussed -- kill high standards." (SL2016-2020)

The handstand story: a friend hired a coach to learn a perfect handstand. Most people think it takes two weeks; it actually takes six months of daily practice. If you think two weeks, you just quit. Most quality failures come from misunderstanding scope, not lacking talent.

> "High standards are fun! Once you've tasted high standards, there's no going back." (SL2016-2020)

## 11. Kaizen / Continuous Improvement [4/22]

Amazon brought in a 70-year-old Japanese consultant from the Toyota school for week-long kaizen training in fulfillment centers. The consultant called himself an "insultant."

On day 4, Bezos was personally sweeping the fulfillment center floor when the consultant challenged him:

> "Mr. Bezos, I am all in favor of a clean fulfillment center, but tell me, why do you sweep? Why do you not instead eliminate the source of dirt?" (Reinvent2012)

The andon cord: any customer service agent can pull a product off the website when they identify systemic defects, triggering upstream investigation. Borrowed from Toyota's production line -- authority pushed to the front line, not reserved for management.

> "Every day is day one. Every day you're deciding what you're gonna do. And you are not trapped by what you were or who you were." (Lex)
