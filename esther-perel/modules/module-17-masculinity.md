# Module 17: Understanding Masculinity and Men's Emotional Lives

**Trigger:** A man struggling to express emotions, dealing with performance pressure, feeling stuck in a narrow definition of manhood, or hearing from a partner "you never open up." Also triggered when someone asks about men and vulnerability, or when a man presents with shame about low desire, caregiving, or nontraditional roles.

**Sources contributing:** 9/25 (SXSW 2018, Hussey Part 1, Hussey Part 2, Diary CEO 2025, Diary CEO 2023, cluster-05, Huberman Lab, Rich Roll 872, and additional supporting material from cluster-05 essays "What Does It Mean to Be a Man?", "A Man's Secret: Performance Anxiety", and "Breaking Free from Gender Expectations")

---

## Overview

This module addresses what Perel frames as the great unfinished project of the gender revolution: men's emotional lives. Her core argument is that forty years of feminist work expanded women's identities -- giving them permission to be powerful, assertive, ambitious -- but no equivalent project was undertaken for men. Men remain locked in a definitional void where masculinity must be constantly proven, vulnerability is structurally forbidden, and emotional expression is channeled almost exclusively through sexuality. The result is not that men lack emotional depth but that they lack permission and vocabulary to access it.

Perel approaches this topic with explicit tenderness. She has deliberately sought out male clients for decades and consistently frames male emotional constriction as a systemic product, not a character flaw. The coaching posture here is never "men need to be fixed" but rather "men have been denied something essential, and that denial hurts everyone."

## Concepts

### 1. Men's Incomplete Gender Project (8/25)

The most frequently occurring concept in this module. Perel argues that the feminist movement expanded women's range of acceptable identities but left men behind.

**Exact quotes:**
- "Forty years of work has helped women find power and voice, but men have been left stuck in a complete definitional void of manhood." (SXSW 2018)
- "The construction of gender identity for men is more fragile than for women. In many cultures, one is born a woman -- and one becomes a man." (cluster-05, "What Does It Mean to Be a Man?")
- "The status of men is at once so valuable and so precarious that it must be won over and over again." (cluster-05, "A Man's Secret")
- "We are born women and we become men. We start with XX and then we go XY." (Hussey Part 2)
- "Emasculation has no feminine equivalent." (Diary CEO 2025)

**The structural argument:** Masculinity is not a stable identity but a perpetual audition. It must be constantly re-earned, and it can be lost in an instant. There is no feminine equivalent of "emasculation" -- no word for a woman whose womanhood has been stripped from her -- because femininity is not constructed as something that must be proven. Similarly, "loser" has no feminine form. These linguistic gaps reveal the asymmetry: masculine identity is uniquely fragile because it is uniquely contingent on performance.

**Practical application:** When a man presents with shame about vulnerability, caregiving, low desire, or emotional expression, name the system that produced the shame before addressing the behavior. The move is always: "This is not about what you are. This is about what you were made to be." Separating the man from the script he inherited is the prerequisite for any other work.

**Coaching triggers:**
- A man says "I feel like a loser" for not being the breadwinner, for caregiving, for showing emotion
- A man cannot name what he feels beyond "fine" or "frustrated"
- A partner says "he won't open up" and interprets it as indifference
- A man is defensive about his masculinity in ways that seem disproportionate to the situation

### 2. "When a Man Talks to You, He's Hearing Himself for the First Time" (6/25) [EMPHATIC]

This concept captures the lived experience of men's emotional constriction -- not as a stereotype about male reticence but as a structural consequence of how men are socialized.

**Exact quotes:**
- "When a man talks to you, you know that he's hearing himself for the first time." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "But that's not because that's what men are. That's because that's what we make men to be." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "Men are allowed to talk about sex. Women are allowed to talk about intimacy. But that's not the same as saying men and women want other things." (Diary CEO 2023)

**The structural argument:** The cultural script permits men to speak about sex but not intimacy. Women are permitted to speak about feelings but not desire. This creates a false impression that men care about sex and women care about feelings -- when in reality, both want both. Men's emotional lives are not absent; they are channeled through the one domain where emotional expression is permitted: sexuality. When a man finally speaks about what he actually feels -- his self-doubt, his shame, his longing -- he is often literally hearing those words come out of his own mouth for the first time.

**Practical application:** When working with a man who is speaking about his feelings, recognize the magnitude of that act. Do not rush past it. Do not immediately redirect to solutions. He may be discovering language for experiences he has carried wordlessly. Meet the disclosure with silence, with receiving, not with interpretation. The fishbowl technique (see Stories below) formalizes this: create a structure where men speak and others listen without judgment, without opinions -- just receive.

**Coaching triggers:**
- A man shares something emotionally vulnerable and immediately deflects with humor or changes the subject
- A couple where the woman says "he talks about sex but never about feelings"
- A man who equates emotional expression with weakness
- Any moment where a man reveals something he has clearly never said aloud before

### 3. Performance Anxiety and the Connoisseur Myth (6/25)

Perel traces a specific historical narrative about male sexual identity that explains why men experience sexual guidance as criticism.

**Exact quotes:**
- "There is an entire history that it's the man who uncovers the woman's sexuality. He deflowers her. And with that, he also brings her whole experience of herself. And he knows and she doesn't have to tell him anything because he's the connoisseur." (Hussey Part 2)
- "The man who is deflowering his bride has this very important notion of knowing things. And you don't ask directions when you're a man who knows. It's a sign of weakness." (Hussey Part 2)
- "Male identity and masculinity is often a fragile identity. Difficult to acquire and easy to lose." (Hussey Part 2)
- "If something has to prove itself so much all the time, maybe it's not that strong to begin with." (Hussey Part 2)
- "The popular culture does not support it, and wants men, especially young men, to really think that there is nothing internal involved as long as they have unprompted, spontaneous erections." (Hussey Part 1)

**The structural argument:** The myth positions men as sexual "connoisseurs" -- they are supposed to already know. If a woman tells him what she wants, that by definition means he does not know, which collapses the entire mythological structure of his sexual identity. This is why men experience a partner's feedback not as guidance but as criticism, not as intimacy but as indictment. The myth also erases men's interior sexual lives entirely -- performance anxiety, fear of rejection, fear of inadequacy, depression, self-doubt -- because acknowledging these would violate the script that says men are always ready, always capable, always in command.

**Practical application:** When working with a man around sexual performance, the first move is to name and dismantle the myth. The question is never "what's wrong with you?" but "what story were you told about what a man is supposed to be in bed, and what has that story cost you?" When working with a couple where the woman wants to communicate her needs, frame her guidance not as criticism of his competence but as an act of trust -- she is inviting him into her experience rather than evaluating his.

**Coaching triggers:**
- A man struggles with performance anxiety but frames it as a mechanical problem
- A man reacts defensively when his partner tries to communicate sexual preferences
- A man is ashamed of low desire or occasional difficulty with arousal
- A woman is confused about why her sexual feedback is received as attack

### 4. Women's Lives Won't Change Until Men's Do (5/25)

Perel's macro-level argument about why masculinity is everyone's concern.

**Exact quotes:**
- "The lives of women will not change until the men come along. The lives of women will not change until the men get the opportunity to also examine and define and change their lives." (SXSW 2018)
- "Patriarchy doesn't just hurt women; it hurts us all." (SXSW 2018)
- "Often, masculinity is defined as the disavowal of the feminine inside of us." (cluster-05, "A Man's Secret" / "What Does It Mean to Be a Man?")

**The structural argument:** Gender equality is not a zero-sum conversation. Excluding men from the work of gender liberation stalls the entire project. The 20th century was about expanding women's identities; the 21st century requires an equivalent expansion for men. Masculinity defined as the disavowal of the feminine creates men who cannot access caregiving, vulnerability, or emotional intimacy -- not because they lack capacity but because the identity structure demands they suppress it. This harms men directly and harms women indirectly, because women cannot receive what men are not permitted to give.

**Practical application:** When gender conversations become exclusionary or adversarial -- "men are the problem" or "feminism has gone too far" -- redirect toward the shared project. Both framings are dead ends. The productive question is: what would it look like for men to have the same expansiveness of identity that the feminist movement created for women? What would men gain, and what would everyone gain?

When Perel encounters the gender wars frame directly, she refuses it. In the Diary CEO 2025 interview: "I don't really want to participate in that. The women are talking about the uselessness and the men are talking about the bitch's death. And I don't find that helpful."

**Coaching triggers:**
- Someone frames gender equality as women vs. men
- A man asks whether he is "allowed" to be part of the conversation about relationships and vulnerability
- A couple where rigid gender roles are creating resentment on both sides
- An organizational context where "soft skills" or relational competence is dismissed as feminine

## Stories

### The Fishbowl (Diary CEO 2025)

**Full narrative:** At relationship retreats, Perel puts all the men in a "fishbowl" -- a circle in the center of the room -- while women sit around the outside and listen silently. For an hour or more, men discuss "their challenges, their frustrations, their hopes, their aspirations, their losses, their self-doubt, their shame, their shadows." Women receive this "without judgment, without opinions -- just receive the gift of having somebody be willing to expose themselves like that."

The effect is profound: "Things become softer. And people start to weep in front of total strangers, and you realize humanity is bigger than gender." The women learn "how much of your projections are standing in the way, how much you make assumptions without really knowing."

**Teaching application:** This story demonstrates two principles simultaneously. First, men will speak about their emotional lives when given structured permission and the right container -- the problem is not male incapacity for vulnerability but the absence of environments that invite it. Second, listening without reaction is itself therapeutic. The fishbowl works not because of what is said but because of how it is received -- without judgment, without rebuttal, without defensiveness.

**Deploy when:** A partner says "he never opens up" -- the fishbowl suggests the problem may be the receiving environment, not the man. When facilitating difficult conversations between groups with entrenched positions. When demonstrating that vulnerability is not gender-specific; it is permission-specific. When a man needs to see that other men share his inner experience.

### The "I'm Not Made for This" Story (SXSW 2018)

**Full narrative:** When asked about loss of identity in her own marriage, Perel shares her personal practice: "In 35 years with Jack with the same partner I have two sons 21 and 24 and when I felt like that sentence that would come up in me was 'I'm not made for this,' I would literally leave for a week which I thought was very good. It made it very clear that I'm not merely so needed and that important and everybody seems to do quite well when I'm not around. So that is very good for the grandiosity. And then the second part was I went to meet people, friends that gave me back, they are the parts of myself that were not the ones that live at home." Her husband's response: "Have a great time."

**Teaching application:** While this story is Perel's own (not a male client's), it is relevant to this module because it models what men are culturally forbidden to do: acknowledge the feeling of being "not made for this," temporarily withdraw to restore identity, and return without the absence being treated as betrayal. For men, admitting "I'm not made for this" risks activating the "loser" script. The story normalizes periodic retreat as a sign of self-awareness, not failure, and demonstrates that the partner who does not panic about the departure enables it.

**Deploy when:** A man feels trapped but loves his partner. When normalizing the distinction between needing space and wanting out. When someone needs permission to restore the parts of self that domestic life has compressed.

### Zach: The Stay-at-Home Father (cluster-05, "What Does It Mean to Be a Man?")

**Full narrative:** Zach writes to Perel: "I had to quit my job because of a health issue, and now I'm home taking care of our kids. My wife has become the breadwinner. I know I should be happy that we could make it all work, but I feel like a loser." Perel responds: "I have worked with many men in your position -- lead dads shunned by moms on the playground. Men who feel inadequate because they're not financially providing for their family (even though they are raising children). For some, pent-up frustration even causes angry lash-outs at their children." She paraphrases Viktor Frankl: "You cannot always control the conditions you find yourself in, but you have the freedom to choose your reaction to them." Her prescription: seek out other men who are parenting, explore part-time work, speak to your partner about how you feel so you can help one another find a balance.

**Teaching application:** This story illustrates the gap between intellectual acceptance and emotional experience. Zach knows rationally that caregiving is valuable. He still feels like a loser -- because the masculine script equates worth with earning, not nurturing. The phrase "I know I should be happy" is the diagnostic signal: it reveals a man fighting against his own conditioning with insufficient support.

**Deploy when:** A man is struggling with a nontraditional role and feels shame he cannot rationalize away. When exploring why "knowing better" does not eliminate internalized gender expectations. When a man's frustration in a caregiving role is manifesting as anger.

### David: The Man Who Faked Back Pain (cluster-05, "A Man's Secret: Performance Anxiety")

**Full narrative:** David writes: "When I'm not interested in sex, it often makes me feel like less of a man. In fact, my wife wants it more than me so I came up with the excuse of chronic back pain. I think it's easier for her to accept. What's wrong with me?" Perel names the cultural assumption: "Men are under pressure in life, and in the bedroom, to be untiring, masterful and dominant. It's assumed men are always up for sex and women's interest is much less, and subjective. It's time we stop this oversimplification of men." She reveals the hidden cost: "Your wife might buy your story about back pain but underneath she is wondering about her lack of desirability. You are not the only one feeling insecure." Her advice: open the conversation without blame, reveal how you feel, start talking about what turns you on and what blocks you. Also: "Put yourself more into your body and do less ruminating, which takes us out of the experience of pleasure."

**Teaching application:** This story debunks the "men always want sex" myth and shows how shame drives deception. David's cover story does not protect his wife; it compounds the problem by creating a second layer of insecurity -- she begins questioning her own desirability. The lie that was meant to spare her feelings creates precisely the wound it was designed to prevent.

**Deploy when:** A man expresses shame about low desire. When discussing desire discrepancy where the woman has higher drive. When someone is hiding something from a partner "to protect them." When illustrating how gender stereotypes damage intimacy for both people.

### The Three Emotional Vulnerabilities Porn Addresses (Diary CEO 2023)

**Full narrative:** When Steven Bartlett describes his fear of sexual rejection, Perel explains: "This fear of rejection is probably one of the most important emotional or sexual vulnerabilities for many men. It's part of what is so alluring in porn. You're never rejected in porn. She always says, me too, more, more, more." Then: "The next one would be you're also never incompetent. You never have performance anxiety. It doesn't matter." And third: "You never have to wonder is she enjoying it because she only screams and makes sure that she lets whoever the actor is know how phenomenal he is." Conclusion: "It takes care of three major emotional dilemmas around sex. It's not that it just takes care of the sex, it takes care of the vulnerabilities, the emotional vulnerabilities around sex."

**Teaching application:** This reframes porn consumption from a moral issue to an emotional coping mechanism. The real question is not "what are you watching?" but "what emotional vulnerabilities is this addressing?" -- rejection, incompetence, and uncertainty about the partner's experience. When these three vulnerabilities are named and addressed in the relationship itself, the compensatory behavior becomes less necessary.

**Deploy when:** A man or couple presents with conflict about porn use. When a man expresses fear of sexual rejection. When exploring men's emotional needs around sex without judgment or moralization. When helping a partner understand what drives porn use beyond "he just wants variety."

### "Slow Down" -- The One Piece of Advice to 200 Men (Hussey Part 1)

**Full narrative:** Perel did a session with 200 men. "I finally had someone who said, so what's your one thing?" After everything she could say: "In the end, out of everything I could say, I just say, slow down. Slow down."

**Teaching application:** The distillation of everything in this module into a single prescription. The masculine script demands speed, decisiveness, performance, conquest. Slowing down is a radical act for a man -- it means being present rather than performing, feeling rather than achieving, connecting rather than completing. Two words that challenge the entire edifice of performance-based masculinity.

**Deploy when:** A man asks for the "one thing" he can do. When anchoring complex concepts into actionable simplicity. When a man is focused on technique, performance, or outcomes rather than presence.
