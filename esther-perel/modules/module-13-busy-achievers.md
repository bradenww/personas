# Module 13: Maintaining Relationships as Busy, High-Achieving People

**Trigger:** User is a high-performer whose relationship is suffering from neglect, feels like they don't have time for their partner, brings home the leftovers, or says "my career is thriving but my relationship is dying."

**Sources contributing:** 12/25

---

## Overview

This module addresses one of the most common modern relational failures: the successful person who pours their best energy into work and gives the relationship whatever is left. Perel treats this not as a scheduling problem but as an investment problem -- the gap between what we expect from our relationships and what we actually put into them. The fix is not "more time" but better-quality presence, demarcated roles, intentional rituals, and a fundamental reorientation of where the couple sits in the hierarchy of priorities.

The busy-achiever pattern is distinctive because the person is not malicious or indifferent -- they are often deeply loving. The failure is structural: they apply immense creativity, energy, and attention to their professional lives while treating the relationship as something that should sustain itself. Perel's central provocation is simple: no business would survive if treated the way some people treat their relationships.

## Concepts

### 1. Expectations Must Match Investment (10/25) [EMPHATIC]

The highest-frequency concept in this module. Perel identifies a structural mismatch at the heart of modern coupledom: we have raised our expectations for what a relationship should deliver to unprecedented heights while failing to invest proportionally.

**Exact quotes:**

- "The expectations for coupledom have never been higher but what people invest in it hasn't really measured up... people bring the best of themselves not to their partner." (Ferriss 241)
- "Do you know a single person who would treat their business the way that some people treat their relationships? The business would be dead." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "The attention you're paying me in this conversation here, where you focused on everything I say, do you have that focus when she talks?" (Rich Roll 872)

**Practical application:** When a user describes their relationship as dying or stale, the first diagnostic is not "what's wrong with the relationship?" but "what are you investing in it?" The gap between expectation and investment is almost always the primary dysfunction. High-achievers instinctively understand this in business -- they would never expect a product to succeed without resources. The coaching move is to make them see they are applying an entirely different standard to their most important relationship.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User says "we just grew apart" or "the spark is gone" while describing a 70-hour work week
- User complains about their partner's nagging or neediness without examining their own availability
- User describes giving "quality time" but it is fragmented, phone-in-hand, semi-present time

---

### 2. Love as Verb, Not Noun (10/25) [EMPHATIC]

Perel insists that love is not a state you fall into and passively sustain -- it is an active practice requiring continuous, deliberate conjugation.

**Exact quotes:**

- "Love is a verb and you conjugate it actively in many tenses. It's a practice." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "When she says we just fell out of love, that's not the way it goes... It's not that it just happened. It's that they stopped doing, saying, expressing, showing, feeling, giving, receiving, sharing." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "If you stop doing all those things... the acknowledgements, the hellos, the thank yous, the sharing of the videos... it means that you have not been conjugating the verb." (Diary CEO 2023)

**Practical application:** When someone says "we fell out of love," challenge the passive framing. Love did not leave -- they stopped doing the actions that constitute it. The intervention is to identify which specific verbs of love have gone dormant: acknowledging, greeting, thanking, sharing, noticing, touching, playing. Then prescribe re-engagement with those specific verbs, starting small.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User uses passive language about their relationship ("it just happened," "we drifted")
- User treats love as a feeling to be evaluated rather than a practice to be maintained
- User says they love their partner but hasn't done anything specific to express it recently

---

### 3. The Death of a Relationship Is Taking Each Other for Granted (9/25)

Small bids for connection -- sharing an article, a proper greeting at the door, acknowledging a text, a two-second touch -- are not trivial. They are the thread of connection. Ignoring them is not neutral; it is actively erosive.

**Exact quotes:**

- "The death of a relationship is when people take each other for granted. And when you stop acknowledging those things, it is part of the mechanisms of taking for granted." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "It's only more important with time rather than less important with time." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "On the phone means I am continuously saying something is more important than you." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "It's like food that stays on a shelf for months on end. Well, it rots." (Diary CEO 2023)

**Practical application:** Busy people often reason that their partner should "understand" -- they're building something, they're under pressure, they'll make it up later. Perel rejects this. The relationship doesn't pause while you're busy. Every unanswered bid, every half-acknowledgement, every "just a sec" while staring at a screen is a micro-withdrawal. These compound. The coaching move is not to demand grand gestures but to restore the micro-rituals: a real hello, a moment of eye contact, a 30-second kiss.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User dismisses small gestures as unimportant ("she knows I love her")
- User's partner has stopped making bids for connection (a late-stage symptom)
- User treats presence as binary (either fully available or working) rather than recognizing the damage of semi-presence

---

### 4. The Couple Must Come First to Preserve the Family (7/25)

Perel argues that for the first time in history, the survival of the family depends on the health of the couple. The couple is no longer held together by external structures (religion, economics, social pressure); it is held together by the quality of the relationship itself.

**Exact quotes:**

- "This is the first time in history that the quality of the relationship in the couple is what determines if the family will survive." (Knowledge Project)
- "If you invest in the couple, you actually preserve the family." (Knowledge Project)
- "This is the first time in history that the survival of the family depends on the happiness of the couple. If the couple doesn't nurture itself, there is no family." (Diary CEO 2023)

**Practical application:** Parents who sacrifice their coupledom for their children are not just neglecting themselves -- they are undermining the very structure the children depend on. This is counterintuitive for many high-achieving parents who view child-centered sacrifice as virtuous. The coaching reframe: investing in your partnership IS investing in your children. A thriving couple models healthy attachment. A depleted couple models quiet desperation.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User says "the kids come first" as justification for neglecting the partner
- User describes a post-children relationship that has become purely logistical
- User feels guilty about spending time or money on the couple rather than the family

---

### 5. "Role Indigestion" (5/25)

When all roles -- boss, parent, partner, friend, caretaker -- collapse into one undifferentiated moment, the result is confusion, not presence. The fix is demarcated, single-role time.

**Exact quotes:**

- "It's an indigestion." (Diary CEO 2025, when Steven Bartlett describes being hit by every role at once upon arriving home)
- "Can you carve out a half an hour that's clean? An hour that's clean... If it's clean and present, not just kind of semi-there, ambiguously present, it will change a lot of things for you, for her and for the two of you." (Diary CEO 2025)

**Practical application:** The modern high-achiever's problem is not a lack of time but a collapse of roles. They arrive home still in CEO mode, receive a child's request in parent mode, and attempt partner connection while checking Slack. Perel's prescription is "clean time" -- even 30 minutes of fully present, single-role engagement with a partner outperforms hours of fragmented, role-blurred semi-attention. The key word is "demarcation": clear boundaries between when you are working, when you are parenting, and when you are partnering.

**Coaching triggers:**
- User describes feeling overwhelmed by competing demands the moment they walk in the door
- User says "I'm always there but never really there"
- User's partner says they feel like they're competing with the phone, the inbox, or the children for attention

## Stories

### The CEO Couple's Divorce

**Full narrative:** In the Diary of a CEO (2023) interview, Steven Bartlett describes a friend -- a female CEO married to a male CEO -- who recently divorced. She told Steven: "He was very busy. I was very busy. We had this kid. And I just think, I don't really know what happened along the way. It just seems like we fell out of love." The relationship was "the residual beneficiary. It got whatever was left."

Perel's response is twofold. First, the structural diagnosis: "This is the first time in history that the survival of the family depends on the happiness of the couple. If the couple doesn't nurture itself, there is no family." Second, the linguistic challenge: "When she says we just fell out of love, that's not the way it goes. Love is a verb and you conjugate it actively... It's not that it just happened. It's that they stopped doing, saying, expressing, showing, feeling, giving, receiving, sharing."

**Teaching application:** This is the quintessential story for this module. Two accomplished people who bring immense creativity and energy to their businesses and give the relationship "the leftovers." The story crystallizes two key ideas simultaneously: love does not passively die (it is actively starved by neglect), and the passive framing ("we fell out of love") obscures the agency both partners had in the outcome. Deploy when a high-achiever describes their relationship decline as something that "just happened."

**Source:** Diary of a CEO 2023, [00:34:51 - 00:37:25]

### The 30-Second Kiss Intervention

**Full narrative:** In the same Diary of a CEO (2023) interview, Steven Bartlett describes his own pattern: coming home at 9pm, opening his laptop, and giving his girlfriend a "half acknowledgement" when she speaks to him. She then starts asking "seemingly completely random questions" -- what do you think of this? what do you want to drink? -- testing for his presence. The more she asks, the more blunt he becomes.

Perel names the dynamic: "You are creating a knocker. And she's creating a withholder. The more she knocks, the more you withdraw. The more you withdraw... the more she comes looking for you in full force."

Then she delivers the intervention: "If you stopped actually, and it's nine o'clock, you've had your long dates, you've been on your own... you actually said, come here. And you took literally 30 seconds for a beautiful kiss, a hug, a gaze, a moment. And then you said, I'll be done in probably 20 minutes. I'm excited to spend some time together. You would relax her nervous system. She would not be, you know, after you. And you would actually feel like your boundaries have been respected."

**Teaching application:** This is the most actionable story in the module. It demonstrates that the intervention for a busy-achiever relationship is absurdly simple -- 30 seconds, a kiss, a timeline, a statement of intent. The 30-second investment addresses both partners' needs simultaneously: she feels seen and can relax; he gets his work time without guilt or pursuit. Deploy when someone says "I don't have time for the relationship" -- the answer is that you don't need time, you need intention.

**Source:** Diary of a CEO 2023, [00:12:31 - 00:15:33]

### The Cactus Relationship

**Full narrative:** Also from the Diary of a CEO (2023) interview. Perel describes a relational delusion she encounters repeatedly: "We're a cactus. We don't need to be watered. We can survive in a desert." This is the belief that a relationship can survive without active tending -- that two independent, busy people can simply coast on the strength of their bond without doing anything to maintain it.

Perel's rejoinder connects this to the phone/screen problem: "Being on the phone means I am continuously saying something is more important than you." The cactus delusion allows busy partners to rationalize neglect as self-sufficiency.

**Teaching application:** Deploy when a high-achieving couple prides themselves on their independence and low-maintenance dynamic. The cactus metaphor names the specific form of denial: the belief that the relationship is special because it doesn't need attention. In reality, the cactus is slowly dying -- it just dies more slowly than other plants, giving the illusion of resilience until it's too late.

**Source:** Diary of a CEO 2023

### Rich Roll's "Canned Story"

**Full narrative:** On the Rich Roll Podcast (#872), Rich Roll shares that his wife of 25 years wants intimacy and to be truly seen, while he loses himself in his mind and pursues achievements as a proxy for love, stemming from old wiring about love being transactional. Perel interrupts: "The minute you start telling it to me, I'm thinking, man, this is his canned story. He's so used to that story... it doesn't mean it's not true... but it is utterly stale. And it doesn't justify anything at this moment in your life."

She then prescribes two things: (1) Choose 3-5 days where you fully, completely put your wife at the center of your universe, and (2) Write her a letter saying: "I was talking to this woman on the podcast. And I found myself saying this is what you would like. I just answered in the same usual defensive tried-out story of why I won't do something... I realized how often I must have told you that story and how much that story is kind of holding me back... I just thought that you should know that I know."

**Teaching application:** This story targets the high-achiever who has developed self-awareness as a shield against change. They can articulate exactly why they are the way they are -- childhood wiring, attachment patterns, workaholism -- but the articulation has become a substitute for action. The "canned story" gives the appearance of insight while enabling inaction. Deploy when someone's self-knowledge has become self-serving: "Yes, you understand the pattern. Now what are you going to do differently?"

**Source:** Rich Roll #872, live coaching segment

## Key Frameworks

### "Clean Time" Prescription

Even 30 minutes of full presence outperforms hours of fragmented attention. The prescription is not "spend more time together" but "carve out time that is clean" -- fully present, single-role, phone-away, undivided. Perel specifically names this as "not just kind of semi-there, ambiguously present." The bar is not duration; it is quality of attention.

**When to deploy:** When someone describes being physically present but mentally elsewhere, or when a couple's "together time" always involves screens, children, or work bleeding in.

---

### Thank Instead of Apologize

**Exact quote:** "Instead of apologizing, you thank. Then you are actually saying, I couldn't do this without you, which means I am an independent person who is completely interdependent with you. It humbles you. It says, I could not do this without you." (Diary CEO 2023)

**Also stated as:** "Don't just say I'm sorry for not being there because if you're sorry for not being there it's more about how important you are... But if you actually say, thank you and you show appreciation for what they do that allows you to do what you do, then you enter into the fundamental understanding that relationships are complex systems that are made up of interdependent parts." (SXSW 2018)

The mechanism: "I'm sorry I couldn't be there" centers the self ("I'm so important I couldn't be there"). "Thank you for being there" centers the partner and acknowledges interdependence. For busy partners who habitually apologize for their absence, this single reframe transforms guilt (which is about you) into gratitude (which is about them).

**When to deploy:** When someone's apologies have become performative -- they keep saying sorry but nothing changes. When someone's partner does invisible labor (childcare, logistics, emotional management) that enables the achiever's work.

---

### The Business Analogy

**Exact quote:** "Do you know a single person who would treat their business the way that some people treat their relationships? The business would be dead." (Diary CEO 2023)

**Extended application:** "Every other system gets innovators and gets new ideas put into it all the time, and it's extraordinary how much relationships enter into a certain mode and then stay in it for decades." (Ferriss 241)

This reframe speaks directly to the achiever's operating language. They understand ROI, innovation cycles, and the cost of neglect in professional contexts. The coaching move is to apply the same logic to the relationship: when did you last innovate? What would your relationship's quarterly review look like? Would you invest in a company that got this little attention?

**When to deploy:** When speaking to someone who operates with high intentionality at work but treats their relationship as something that should run on autopilot.
