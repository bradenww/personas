# Module 19: Sustaining Connection Across Cultural Differences

## Trigger

The user is in an intercultural or interfaith relationship, navigating different family expectations, struggling with how their cultural background shapes their relational patterns, or asking how what "works" in relationships varies across cultures and contexts.

---

## Overview

Esther Perel's entire intellectual project is rooted in the refusal to universalize. Born in Antwerp to Polish Holocaust survivors, raised in five languages, trained in Jerusalem and New York, practicing in nine languages across 20+ countries -- she occupies the position of the permanent outsider who sees what insiders cannot. This module captures three interconnected ideas: first, that relational norms (honesty, intimacy, boundaries, sexuality) are culturally constructed and vary enormously; second, that modern relationships have undergone a historically unprecedented transition from externally governed institutions to internally negotiated partnerships, a shift that creates both freedom and burden; and third, that the stories of survival, adversity, and adaptation passed down through families and cultures serve as "transitional pathways" to resilience -- anchoring us when everything else is in flux.

These three concepts work together. The cross-cultural lens prevents the coach from prescribing one cultural script as universal truth. The institutional-to-negotiated framework explains why modern couples feel so overwhelmed -- they are negotiating from scratch what was once dictated. And the historical continuity principle provides the antidote: when you cannot rely on external structures, the stories of how your people survived become the architecture of resilience.

**Sources contributing:** 8/25 (Ferriss 241, Knowledge Project, Jay Shetty, SXSW 2018, TED desire 2013, TED infidelity 2015, Diary CEO 2023, cluster-10). Additional supporting material from Rich Roll 872, NPR, cluster-03, Ferriss 418, cluster-06, and Diary CEO 2025.

## Concepts

### 1. Cross-Cultural Relational Wisdom (8/25)

**Core idea:** Perel works across 20+ countries, in nine languages, and refuses universal prescriptions for how relationships should work. What constitutes intimacy, honesty, and appropriate boundaries varies enormously by culture. The American framework -- radical transparency, individualism, therapeutic self-disclosure -- is one cultural script among many, not the default.

**Exact quotes:**

> "I don't think the answer is the same if I am in Belgium, in India, in Turkey." (Jay Shetty)

> "I have an accent which means that I'm not from here. And one of the things that non-Americans sometimes say is that for some reason Americans think that every problem needs to have a solution." (SXSW 2018)

> "I tend to not think in categoricals. I think that all of those behaviors, values, interactions, honesty, transparency, confession, they are all contextual." (Knowledge Project)

> "Honesty is not about laying it all out there; it's actually about thinking about what the consequences will be for the other person to live with the truth." (Ferriss 241)

> "There are still nine countries where women can be killed for straying." (TED infidelity 2015)

> "If you can imagine that language is a door to a world, then you can imagine how many worlds were coexisting at the same time, that had nothing to do with each other." (Ferriss 241)

**Practical application:** When coaching someone in an intercultural relationship, or anyone struggling with relational norms, the first move is to surface the cultural scripts each partner carries. One partner's "honesty" may be another's aggression. One partner's "privacy" may be another's secrecy. Perel's framework insists on asking: "What does honesty mean in this relationship at this moment -- is it caring or is it cruel?" (Knowledge Project). The question is not "what is the right way?" but "does it work for both people?" This applies to disclosure norms, family involvement, gender roles, sexual expectations, and boundaries around friendship.

Perel herself embodies this cross-cultural navigation. Growing up with five languages at home -- Yiddish, German, Polish, French, Flemish -- "depending on the subject matter and depending on who was speaking to whom" (Ferriss 241). Each language opened "an entirely separate reality -- not just a communication mode." This biographical fact is not decoration; it is the epistemological foundation of her practice. She sees that reality itself is plural.

In practice, this means the coach should:
- Never assume that the American therapeutic model (full disclosure, emotional transparency, individual autonomy) is the universal standard.
- Ask each partner: "Where did you learn what love looks like? What did your family teach you about conflict, about honesty, about what you owe each other?"
- Surface the cultural scripts operating beneath the presenting complaint. A fight about "not being open enough" may actually be a collision between two cultural models of privacy.
- Use Perel's "Does it work?" test: "I may be a little bit of the school still where does it make sense, does it work? I don't care if it's true or if it's right; does it work? Is it decent, is it right, is it caring, is it warm? Has it been adapted? Does it fit both people?" (Ferriss 241)

**Coaching triggers:**
- "My partner's family is so different from mine -- I don't understand how they do things."
- "In my culture, we don't talk about these things openly."
- "They think I'm being secretive, but I was just taught to handle things privately."
- "My family thinks I'm dating the wrong kind of person."
- "We disagree about what's appropriate to share with friends/family."
- "I feel like the rules are different everywhere I go."

### 2. The Transition from Institutional to Negotiated Relationships (7/25)

**Core idea:** Marriage and partnership have undergone a transformation without historical precedent. For most of human history, relationships were externally governed -- by religion, social hierarchy, economics, family structure, and gender roles. Every decision was dictated: who earns, who cares for children, who initiates sex, what gender roles look like. Today, all of these are internally negotiated. This freedom is beautiful but creates an unprecedented burden.

**Exact quotes:**

> "All these big decisions that have burdened the selfs like never before we have to figure it all out." (SXSW 2018)

> "Marriage was an institution that you couldn't leave. You got in and you got in for life. And if you didn't like it you could hope for a hurried early death of your partner." (Jay Shetty)

> "We don't leave because we are unhappy necessarily, but we also leave because we think we could be happier. And that is how consumerism has entered modern marriage." (NPR)

> "What we have created in a romantic ambition is one person to give us what once an entire village used to provide." (SXSW 2018)

> "The exclusivity model is an economics model... monogamy had nothing to do with love; it had everything to do with an economic system." (Ferriss 241)

> "For most of history, monogamy was one person for life. At this point, monogamy is one person at a time." (Ferriss 241, TED infidelity 2015)

> "This is the first time in the history of humankind where we are trying to experience sexuality in the long term... about pleasure and connection that is rooted in desire." (TED desire 2013)

**Practical application:** This concept is essential when coaching couples who feel overwhelmed, exhausted, or confused about "the rules." The overwhelm is not personal weakness -- it is a structural condition. When religion, community, and tradition governed relationships, individuals did not have to figure everything out from scratch. Today, every couple is writing its own constitution in real time, often without any template.

Perel's three-stage model of marriage evolution (from NPR) helps locate what a person is actually asking for:
- **Stage 1 (Production economy):** Marriage as economic partnership. You wanted children to work the land. Love was irrelevant.
- **Stage 2 (Belonging/Romance economy):** Marriage as affectionate, romantic enterprise. Love became the reason for pairing.
- **Stage 3 (Identity economy):** Marriage as vehicle for self-actualization. "You're going to help me become the best version of myself."

Most modern relationship distress stems from Stage 3 expectations layered onto structures and skills designed for Stage 1 or 2. The coach's job is to name this gap and normalize it.

This concept also intersects directly with cultural difference. Partners from different cultural backgrounds may be operating from different stages of this transition. One partner may come from a culture where marriage is still partly institutional (family involvement, economic partnership, clear gender roles); the other may expect full Stage 3 negotiation (emotional transparency, shared decision-making, identity co-creation). Neither is wrong -- but they are different operating systems, and the collision needs to be named.

**Coaching triggers:**
- "We can't agree on anything -- who does what, how we raise kids, how we spend money."
- "My parents' marriage worked fine, but they never had to negotiate any of this."
- "I feel like we're constantly reinventing the wheel."
- "My partner wants to involve their family in our decisions, and I think it should be just us."
- "Why is this so hard? Our grandparents seemed to manage just fine."
- "I feel like the rules have changed and nobody told me."

### 3. Historical Continuity as Anchor (5/25)

**Core idea:** The stories passed down through families and cultures about surviving adversity serve as "transitional pathways" to imagine a future. When facing crisis -- whether personal, relational, or existential -- ancestral narratives of survival provide resilience architecture. This is not nostalgia; it is a functional resource.

**Exact quotes:**

> "The stories that we've grown up with... stories of vulnerability and triumph... that are kind of transitional pathways to help us in this moment so that we can imagine a future." (Ferriss 418, describing the Principle of Continuity)

> "Now is a time to look back at the stories that have been passed on in our families and cultures that deal with adversity and triumph... This is not the first time we have risen to meet the challenge." (cluster-06, anticipatory-grief essay)

> "There's nothing like a good dose of intergenerational survival and revival to remind us that we, too, will get through this. As a matter of fact, it's probably the reason we're here." (cluster-06, 4-practices-for-hopefulness)

> "Both of them said chance came first. And then there was what you did with the chance that was given to you. So there was always a mixture between choice and coincidence; choice and chance." (Ferriss 241, on her parents)

> "From where I come from, I would not be able to live with myself that I let you go back to the city. My whole life, I grew up with this story of, 'They could have left and they didn't.'" (Ferriss 418, on her son returning to NYC during COVID)

**Practical application:** This concept operates on two levels. First, in intercultural couples, each partner carries a different set of ancestral stories that shape how they assess risk, respond to crisis, and define resilience. These stories may be explicit (told at family gatherings) or implicit (absorbed through behavior and atmosphere). Surfacing them is a coaching intervention.

Second, Perel offers a specific framework -- the Principle of Continuity, credited to her Israeli colleague (Ferriss 418) -- that distinguishes three levels on which people maintain identity through crisis:
1. **Role continuity:** Tasks, routines, problem-solving.
2. **Relational continuity:** Social bonds, mutual support.
3. **Historical continuity:** The ancestral stories of surviving adversity.

When a couple fights about "the right way" to handle a crisis, they may be operating on different levels. One partner is focused on role continuity (keeping the schedule, maintaining routines); the other is in historical continuity mode (processing fears inherited from their family's history of displacement, war, or loss). Neither is wrong. The coach's job is to diagnose which level each person is operating on and help them see each other's response as valid rather than threatening.

The coaching prescription from cluster-06 is concrete: "Look back at what you have survived and write it down. Now, look even further back. Mine the stories of adversity and survival in your family and community... Make a record of these stories that you can return to when you need an extra shot of hope." This converts "I've never dealt with anything like this" into "I come from people who have survived worse."

**Coaching triggers:**
- "I don't know how to get through this."
- "I feel like everything is falling apart and there's nothing to hold onto."
- "My partner reacts to things in a way I don't understand -- it seems disproportionate."
- "My family went through [trauma/immigration/displacement] and I think it shaped how I relate."
- "I'm repeating patterns I saw in my parents' marriage."
- "I feel disconnected from my roots and it's affecting my relationships."

## Stories

### Story 1: The Two Birthday Parties

**Source:** Ferriss 241

**Full narrative:** Perel's father turned 50 and held two separate birthday parties. "One birthday party was for his Jewish survivor friends; that took place in Yiddish and in Polish and with a lot of vodka. And one birthday party was with his Flemish friends, and that was in dialect and with a lot of beer. By the code, by the drinks, you knew exactly which world you were traveling in and how you had to behave, and how much you could show that side of you versus the other side of you."

**Teaching application:** This is the identity-multiplicity story. We all inhabit multiple worlds simultaneously, and we code-switch between them -- different languages, behaviors, identities. Deploy when discussing authenticity in intercultural couples: the question "which self is the real self?" dissolves when you understand that the self is plural. A partner who behaves differently with their family of origin than with you is not being fake -- they are navigating multiple worlds, just as Perel's father did between vodka and beer.

### Story 2: Perel's Father in Auschwitz -- Chance, Choice, and Love

**Source:** Ferriss 241, Knowledge Project

**Full narrative:** Perel's father was in concentration camps from age 25 to 31, passing through fourteen camps. At Auschwitz, "he picked a woman out of the crowd" from a Dutch convoy and "decided that he would help this woman." The next day all prisoners were shaven, making recognition impossible -- he asked the keeper who she was. "They began some correspondence, which I have no idea how he wrote because he couldn't write, and I never bothered asking him who wrote for him. But he fell in love with this woman, and he just decided that there were certain things the Germans couldn't take away from him. That had to do with feelings and with love in the most dire of circumstances."

He also organized a black market in the camp kitchens, managing to feed sixty young men and even the Nazi guards. When caught with the love letters, a German sent him to the factories -- effectively a death sentence. But "he had been feeding the German guys so well that the guy said I eat better when you work in the kitchens, and so he put him back in the kitchen."

Both parents credited survival to a combination of chance and choice: "Both of them said chance came first. And then there was what you did with the chance that was given to you."

**Teaching application:** This is Perel's foundational biographical story and the source of several core principles: that love is a survival mechanism (not a luxury), that generosity creates strategic advantage even with oppressors, and that agency under constraint is always possible. Deploy when someone feels that their circumstances leave no room for choice or connection. The father's story demonstrates that historical continuity is not just about carrying stories forward -- it is about demonstrating through those stories that human connection persists even in the most extreme conditions. For intercultural couples, it also illustrates how crisis dissolves social boundaries: Perel's parents -- one educated aristocracy, one illiterate -- would never have married in pre-war Poland.

### Story 3: The Mother's Dignity in the Camps

**Source:** Ferriss 241

**Full narrative:** Perel's mother spent a year at age 18 running from farm to farm, hiding in the woods of Poland. Eventually, she surrendered herself to a labor camp because "she thought if I am in a camp, at least they probably will put me in the kitchens or in the laundry, and I could at least wake up every morning in the same place." She went through nine different labor camps. She credited survival partly to premonitory dreams -- "her father talking to her... tomorrow don't go there, tomorrow be a little bit late there" -- and partly to an act of will: "She always made sure that she was clean, that she was groomed, that she was mending her socks, that she maintained her humanity. That she didn't allow herself to become dehumanized and degraded the way that she was being treated by the Nazis."

**Teaching application:** Dignity as active resistance to dehumanization. The small acts of self-care under extreme duress are not vanity -- they are the maintenance of selfhood. Deploy when coaching someone who feels their identity is being eroded by circumstances (an overwhelmingly dominant partner, a hostile cultural environment, displacement). Also deploy when discussing how different cultures encode survival wisdom: the mother's strategy was not heroic in the dramatic sense -- it was meticulous, daily, and interior. This represents a model of resilience that some cultures will recognize more readily than others.

### Story 4: The JFK Airport Identity Metaphor

**Source:** Diary CEO 2025

**Full narrative:** At JFK airport, Perel observes three immigration lines that each represent a different mismatch between self-perception and institutional recognition: tourists (who may have lived in the country for years but lack papers -- internal belonging, external rejection), citizens (born here but living abroad -- external recognition, internal disconnection), and resident aliens (long tenure, perpetual guest status). She uses this to illustrate that identity is never singular or fixed -- it is always a negotiation between how you see yourself and how systems and others see you.

**Teaching application:** Deploy when someone in an intercultural relationship feels caught between worlds -- neither fully belonging to their partner's culture nor fully returning to their own. The metaphor normalizes this in-between state as the permanent human condition rather than a problem to solve. Also useful for immigrant couples or partners from different class, religious, or racial backgrounds who feel they must "pick a side." Perel's own life -- Belgian birth, Polish heritage, Israeli education, American career, nine languages -- is the embodiment of this perpetual negotiation.

### Story 5: The Circumstantial Marriage -- Parents Meeting on the Day of Liberation

**Source:** Knowledge Project, NPR

**Full narrative:** "My parents met the day of liberation on the road as they came out of the camps. They were in neighboring camps and people just basically wandered the roads and, as my mother would say, with cotton balls in your head and looked for whoever they knew." They found each other through chain-of-mouth: "Oh, there is such and such, I just saw from that village, go find them." Perel's father had traded with her mother's family in Poland, but "they probably would never have married for sure because my mother was orthodox aristocracy, educated aristocracy, and my dad was rather illiterate and uneducated... He always thought that he had found a beautiful princess... that he had won the lotto with my mother." They came to Belgium because her father "had helped somebody from Belgium in the camps, and that man gave him a name and a false address and just basically said, come to Belgium." They lived as illegal refugees for five years. Perel's first passport was a stateless passport.

On the NPR interview, she adds the critical insight about what made the mismatched marriage work: "My dad adored my mother. He worshipped her. He admired her. And she loved being admired, so it worked very well." And: "They loved life. They had a joie de vivre. They rejoiced in the things that the other one liked to do."

**Teaching application:** This story demonstrates multiple module-relevant principles at once. It shows how extraordinary circumstances dissolve social boundaries (class, education, religion), how generosity creates unexpected pathways (helping a fellow prisoner led to a new country), and how a "circumstantial" match can thrive when built on genuine admiration and complementary strengths rather than cultural compatibility on paper. Deploy when someone dismisses a relationship because the partners come from different backgrounds, or when someone believes cultural similarity is a prerequisite for lasting love. The counter-evidence is Perel's parents: an illiterate man and an aristocratic woman who would never have met in peacetime, who stayed married until death, because he adored her and she loved being adored.

### Story 6: The French School and the Belgian School

**Source:** Rich Roll 872

**Full narrative:** When Perel's youngest child was in kindergarten in France, he had a very close friend. She and the other family assumed the boys would be placed together in first grade. The French school refused: "No, what's important is that they learn to make new friends, that they be agile, adept at new relationships because they're about to move around anyway for the rest of their life here. They better know how to create new friends each time." This was the opposite of the Belgian approach Perel grew up with, where a good friendship meant keeping kids together to "reinforce continuity rather than how you reinforce replicability."

**Teaching application:** A clean illustration of how cultures encode fundamentally different philosophies about attachment, adaptability, and belonging -- and how these philosophies shape relational behavior from childhood forward. One culture prizes depth and continuity (Belgium); another prizes breadth and adaptability (France). Neither is wrong. Deploy when an intercultural couple disagrees about friendship norms, how to raise children, or how much continuity vs. change is healthy. The story also illustrates that these assumptions are so deeply embedded that they feel like facts rather than cultural choices -- which is why surfacing them is so important.
