# Module 15: Navigating Relationships at Work

**Trigger:** User has conflict with a colleague, wants to improve team dynamics, struggles with a boss or direct report, brings relationship patterns into the workplace, or asks about boundaries between professional and personal life.

**Sources contributing:** 9/25 (cluster-10, NPR, Diary CEO 2025, Diary CEO 2023, SXSW 2018, Ferriss 418, Rich Roll 872, cluster-04, Knowledge Project)

---

## Overview

Perel's workplace practice is not a separate discipline from her couples work -- it is the same discipline applied to a different stage. Her foundational claim is that relational dynamics are universal: the figure-eight loops, the pursuer-distancer patterns, the three hidden dimensions of conflict (power, trust, recognition) all operate identically whether the relationship is romantic, familial, or professional. What makes the workplace distinct is not that the dynamics differ, but that people pretend they do not apply.

The central provocation of this module is the "unofficial resume" -- the idea that every person carries their entire relationship history into every professional interaction, whether they acknowledge it or not. Attachment wounds from childhood, breakup patterns, family loyalty structures -- none of these stop at the office door. Perel's workplace consulting begins from this premise: you cannot fix organizational dysfunction without understanding the relational patterns of the people inside it.

Her expansion into workplace consulting (through her "How's Work?" podcast, her partnership with Culture Amp, and her corporate advisory practice) represents the logical extension of a career-long thesis: "The quality of your relationships determines the quality of your life" -- and that includes work life. She frames this not as a soft-skills addendum but as the actual bottom line.

## Concepts

### 1. Relational Dynamics Apply Everywhere (9/25)

This is the gateway concept for the entire module. Perel insists that the frameworks she developed for couples therapy -- the figure-eight feedback loop, the pursuer-distancer pattern, the form-over-content principle -- are not romantic-specific but universal to all relational systems.

**Exact quotes:**

- "This is not just for romantic love. These are foundations of relational systems. Feedback loop, it's called in cybernetics." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "If the people don't like each other, your system is not going to work very well." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "Leadership is relationships. It's vision, it's efficiencies, et cetera. It's operations, but it is also relationships. If those don't work, you can have the best product. You can have the most exquisite system in place. If the people don't like each other, don't want to work together, don't know how to rely on each other, don't want to share the information, your system is not going to work very well." (Diary CEO 2023)
- "Whether I'm consulting for a company or conducting sessions between colleagues... I want to meet the people before I meet their problems." (cluster-10, from "conflict-in-the-workplace")

**Practical application:** When coaching someone through a workplace conflict, the first move is always to look past the content of the disagreement (the missed deadline, the meeting that happened without them, the credit that was not given) and identify the relational pattern underneath. Is this a pursuer-distancer dynamic? Is one person chronically over-functioning while the other withdraws? Has the workplace relationship calcified into a figure-eight where each person's defensive reaction triggers the other's vulnerability? The same systemic lens Perel uses for couples -- "once you've noticed the loop, it doesn't matter what they're talking about" (Knowledge Project) -- applies directly.

**Coaching triggers:** User describes a co-worker who "always" does something, or says "we keep having the same argument." User presents one person as the problem and themselves as reasonable. User describes a dynamic that sounds exactly like a romantic pattern (stonewalling, pursuing, controlling).

### 2. The Unofficial Resume (6/25)

This concept names the invisible force that governs most professional behavior: every person's full relational history -- attachment patterns, family dynamics, breakup wounds, childhood survival strategies -- travels with them into every workplace interaction.

**Exact quotes:**

- "Nobody's asking you about your unofficial resume, and your unofficial resume is your relationship history, and that relationship history does not stop at the door when you go into the office." (NPR)
- "When people say you should bring your whole self to work, my response is always this: They already do. But it's in conscious and unconscious ways." (cluster-10, from "conflict-in-the-workplace")

**Practical application:** When someone's reaction at work seems disproportionate to the triggering event -- a colleague who crumbles at mild feedback, a manager who cannot delegate, a team member who hoards information -- the unofficial resume is almost always the explanation. The colleague who crumbles may have spent their childhood being "good" for a depressed parent (see the "little prince" case, Knowledge Project). The manager who cannot delegate may be replaying a family role of hyper-responsibility. The information-hoarder may be reenacting a survival strategy from an environment where knowledge was the only source of power.

The coaching move is not to psychoanalyze the person but to recognize that professional behavior has personal roots. Asking "What is your story with this?" (Jay Shetty) -- the same question Perel uses in couples work -- opens a different conversation than asking "Why can't you just do your job?"

**Coaching triggers:** User describes a colleague whose behavior seems irrational or disproportionate. User notices they themselves react to a boss or coworker in ways that feel familiar from personal life. User says "I don't know why this gets to me so much."

### 3. Four Workplace Relational Pillars (4/25)

From Perel's collaboration with Culture Amp (a people analytics company with "1.5 billion experience survey points"), she identifies four relational foundations that sustain workplace quality. These are not perks, benefits, or compensation structures -- they are relational conditions.

**Exact quotes (Diary CEO 2025):**

1. **Trust** -- "I can rely on you, on my team, on my manager, that you have my back."
2. **Belonging** -- "We are part of a group, of a company, and I get a certain sense of who I am by virtue of my sense of connection to this group."
3. **Recognition** -- "I feel valued, that I contribute and that it is recognized."
4. **Collective Resilience** -- "How we are able to respond creatively and adaptively and flexibly to all the changes that are happening."

She calls these "the new bottom line" and explicitly rejects the dismissal of these as soft skills: "Relationships have been dismissed in business as soft skills, fluff, feminine skills or feminine concerns. Feminine concerns you can always hold in high regard, but then disregard in reality." (Diary CEO 2023)

Supporting quote from NPR: "Relationships become the new bottom line because no amount of free food or money... compensation, benefits is going to compensate for a poisonous relationship."

**Practical application:** Use the four pillars as a diagnostic framework when someone describes workplace dissatisfaction. Ask which pillar is missing. A person who feels unrecognized needs something fundamentally different from a person who lacks trust in their manager. A team struggling with collective resilience (common post-layoffs or during rapid change) needs a different intervention than one struggling with belonging. The pillar framework prevents the generic prescription of "better communication" and directs attention to the specific relational deficit.

Perel emphasizes collective resilience as the most critical and most overlooked: "Resilience isn't an individual matter only." (Diary CEO 2025). This pushes back on the corporate wellness trend of teaching individuals to be more resilient, when the real issue is whether the team can adapt together.

**Coaching triggers:** User says their workplace "looks good on paper" but feels empty. User has good compensation but is miserable. User describes a team that performs but does not connect.

### 4. Engagement Drives Performance, Not the Reverse (4/25)

This concept inverts the standard corporate assumption that performance should be measured and managed first, with engagement as a nice-to-have.

**Exact quote (Diary CEO 2025):**

"If the engagement is low, on what basis do we think people are going to perform? The performance is here, but the performance is a response to the engagement, which is a response to the culture, which is a response to the quality of the relationships between the people who work together."

**Practical application:** This is Perel's causal chain for organizational health: relationships --> culture --> engagement --> performance. When leaders try to improve performance directly (through targets, incentives, accountability structures) without addressing the relational substrate, they are, in her framing, "trying to change the kitchen when the bedroom is the problem" (adapted from Diary CEO 2023). The intervention point is always upstream: fix the relationships, and performance follows.

The parallel to couples is precise. Just as she tells couples that improving the sexual relationship transforms the entire relationship ("when you change the bedroom, it changes the people who walk into the kitchen" -- Diary CEO 2023), she tells leaders that improving relational quality transforms organizational output.

**Coaching triggers:** User is a manager frustrated by underperformance. User describes a team that "should" be performing but is not. User is implementing performance improvement plans without examining relational conditions.

### 5. Work Friendships as Essential Infrastructure (cluster-04)

From her essay series (Letters from Esther #71), Perel frames workplace friendships not as pleasant bonuses but as critical scaffolding.

**Exact quotes:**

- "They are the quiet scaffolding that holds us up in our work and, sometimes, in our lives." (cluster-04, Letters #71)
- "I believe that the quality of our relationships at work determines the quality of our work." (cluster-04, Letters #71)
- "Some colleagues stay with us for life, long after we've both moved on from where and how we met. These 'forever colleagues' remind us that work friendships are not bound by office walls, nor a particular company or position, nor a chapter in one's life." (cluster-04, Letters #71)
- "Our work is not meant to be done in isolation. It is sustained by the friendships we form with one another." (cluster-04, Letters #71)

**Practical application:** Work friendships provide nine specific functions that Perel taxonomizes: loyalty in strained moments, recognition when labor is unseen, honesty when stakes are high, relief when the load is heavy, perspective when we lose our way, protection when we need a witness, support when things are tough at home, collaboration, and comedic relief. When any of these is missing, the person is operating without support infrastructure.

The "forever colleague" concept is worth deploying when someone is mourning a work transition. Not all workplace relationships are circumstantial -- some transcend organizations and chapters, carrying institutional memory and perspective across contexts.

**Coaching triggers:** User dismisses work relationships as superficial. User is starting a new role and feels isolated. User has lost a key work ally to departure or reorganization.

### 6. The Three Hidden Dimensions of Workplace Conflict (cluster-10, Jay Shetty, Knowledge Project)

Adapted from Howard Markman's research and integrated into Perel's clinical framework, these three dimensions operate beneath the surface of every interpersonal conflict -- professional and personal alike.

**Exact quotes:**

- "There are hidden dimensions at play underneath the majority of interpersonal issues... I tend to focus on these three: Power and Control; Care and Closeness; Respect and Recognition." (cluster-10)
- "What hurts you more? The fact that he did it in the first place -- or that he did it without you? The former is an issue of power, the latter is an issue of care and closeness." (cluster-10)
- "It's not what you fight about. It's what you fight for." (Jay Shetty)
- "What people fight for when they argue whatever thing they're in a spat about. They fight for power and control -- who makes the decision and whose priorities matter most. They fight for trust, for care and closeness -- can I trust you, do you have my back, can I lean on you? And they fight for respect and recognition -- do you value me, do I matter?" (Diary CEO 2025)

**Practical application:** When mediating any workplace conflict -- between co-founders, between a manager and report, between peers -- decode the surface complaint through these three lenses. A dispute about who gets included in a meeting is rarely about the meeting; it is about recognition ("Am I valued?") or trust ("Do you have my back?") or power ("Whose priorities matter?"). Naming the hidden dimension aloud often dissolves the surface conflict because people feel seen at the level where they actually hurt.

**Coaching triggers:** User describes a workplace conflict that keeps recycling through different topics. User feels disproportionately upset by something that "shouldn't" bother them. User says a colleague's behavior "makes no sense."

### 7. From Making a Living to Making Meaning (cluster-10, SXSW 2018)

Perel traces a historical arc in which work has shifted from production economy to identity economy -- a shift that mirrors the transformation of marriage.

**Exact quote:**

- "We used to go to work to make a living. Now we go to work to make meaning." (cluster-10, from "conflict-in-the-workplace" / Letters #4)

**Practical application:** This shift explains why workplace dissatisfaction feels so personal. When work was purely transactional, a bad boss was an inconvenience. Now that work is an identity project -- where people seek belonging, purpose, recognition, and self-actualization -- a bad boss is an existential threat. The same dynamic that overloads romantic partners ("one person to give us what an entire village used to provide" -- SXSW 2018, NPR, and others) overloads the employer-employee relationship.

Use this concept when someone is agonizing over a job the way they might agonize over a romantic partner: "Should I stay or should I go? Am I settling? Could I be happier somewhere else?" The consumerist logic Perel identifies in modern romance -- "we don't leave because we are unhappy necessarily, but we also leave because we think we could be happier" (NPR) -- applies equally to modern careers.

**Coaching triggers:** User is devastated by workplace rejection in a way that seems disproportionate. User treats every job dissatisfaction as a sign they should leave. User expects work to fulfill all their needs for meaning and connection.

## Stories

### The Mother and Son in Business (NPR)

**Full narrative:** A mother (61) and son (late 20s) run a real estate firm together. The mother ran the firm alone for 25 years before hiring her son at 22. Six years later, they cannot separate their professional and family roles. The mother confesses: "I don't know how I will go on and for how long I will go on... I don't know where I stand anymore." She catches herself mothering at work: "I see that he's bad-tempered. I said, oh, maybe he's hungry. I'm going to the supermarket -- the mama again." She asks her son directly: "Do you still need me? This I want to know. Do you still need me, honestly?" The son responds: "I still learn every day from you, but... I don't need you to hold my hand as much anymore... For my years to come, it's more knowing that you have my back more than you hold my hand." The mother then voices her existential fear: "There will come a moment where I will not be there anymore. You will have to be alone. I'm thinking a lot about it."

**Teaching application:** This story crystallizes three dynamics that show up in all mentorship and leadership transitions: (1) the mentor's identity crisis when the mentee outgrows them ("Do you still need me?"), (2) the mentee's need to redefine the relationship without severing it ("have my back" vs. "hold my hand"), and (3) the existential dimension that gives workplace transitions their emotional charge (mortality, legacy, being replaced). The son's reframe -- from hand-holding to having his back -- is a precise articulation of what mature professional support looks like.

**Deploy when:** A manager is struggling to let go of a direct report who has grown. A founder is navigating a succession. A mentorship has evolved past its original form and needs renegotiation. Any situation where professional and family roles are entangled.

### The Card Game at the Corporate Offsite (Jay Shetty)

**Full narrative:** Someone told Perel they organized a corporate offsite and brought a card from her "Where Should We Begin?" game. The card prompt was: "Somebody who impacted your life and doesn't know it." The whole group went through this question. "Basically, people spoke that you knew nothing about. People who never talk. And people who you thought you knew came up with stories that you had no idea about."

**Teaching application:** This story demonstrates the power of oblique questions in professional settings. Direct questions ("tell me about yourself," "what are your development areas") produce rehearsed answers. Oblique prompts ("someone who impacted your life and doesn't know it") bypass professional armor and invite people to show up as full human beings. The result is not therapy -- it is the revelation that colleagues have interior lives, and that seeing those lives creates bonds that improve professional collaboration.

**Deploy when:** User wants to build team trust but direct approaches feel forced. User is planning a retreat or offsite and wants genuine connection, not corporate exercises. User has a team where people perform well together but do not actually know each other.

### The CEO Who Laid Off 200 of 204 People (Ferriss 418)

**Full narrative:** During early COVID, a friend's husband had to lay off 200 of his 204 employees. Perel heard about it from his wife and called him directly: "I just want you to know, I know how much you care about your workers. I can't imagine what a hard thing that must've been for you to do." She reports: "I haven't seen this man cry ever. This is on the phone. I didn't see it. I heard it. I knew it." He then opened up -- he had called every one of his managers personally. His wife texted afterward: "You don't understand what you just did. It's like you just knew exactly what to say." Perel reflects: "After that you stay quiet. Like you and me, I stay quiet. It's not like this, you just let it sink for a minute."

**Teaching application:** The most powerful intervention for a leader who has made a painful decision is the simplest: naming the difficulty of what they have done, without trying to solve or minimize. "I know how hard this must have been for you" is the entire script. Then silence. Leaders are chronically under-witnessed -- everyone looks to them for strength, and no one asks what the decision cost them. This story models what it looks like to be an empathic witness for someone in a position of authority.

**Deploy when:** User has made a painful leadership decision (layoffs, restructuring, ending a partnership) and needs acknowledgment, not advice. User is supporting a leader through a crisis. User is a leader who feels isolated by the weight of their decisions.

### The Colleague Who Overslept (Ferriss 418)

**Full narrative:** A colleague at Perel's company (EPGM -- Esther Perel Global Media) overslept a morning meeting. Rather than moving straight to business, Perel asked: "You don't go to sleep or you wake up?" He said he woke up. She asked: "What happens at four o'clock in the morning? What was it like for you? What were you thinking about? What do you think got you up? It's kind of caught up with you. You've been trying to kind of think, 'I have it all under control,' and then suddenly, the night terror arrives." They chatted for three minutes. "Once we had gotten that addressed, then we could actually talk about what we needed to talk about rather than him rallying up and just kind of showing up totally disheveled, ready for work."

**Teaching application:** Three minutes of emotional acknowledgment enables productive work. Skipping the check-in does not save time -- it costs it. Insomnia is often the body expressing what the mind will not. This story models the micro-intervention: brief, precise emotional recognition that does not turn the meeting into a therapy session but does not pretend the person is a productivity unit either.

**Deploy when:** User notices a colleague is off but everyone is pushing through. User is a manager wondering whether to "get personal" with a struggling team member. User is the one who overslept and needs to understand why the body is protesting.
