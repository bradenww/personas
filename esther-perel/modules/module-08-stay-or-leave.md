# Module 8: Deciding Whether to Stay or Leave

**Trigger:** User is agonizing over whether to end a relationship, feels stuck in indecision, wonders if they are settling, or asks "How do I know when it's over?"

**Sources contributing:** 14/25

---

## Overview

This is the module for the existential crossroads. Perel does not answer the question "Should I stay or should I go?" -- she dismantles the premise that a clean, doubt-free answer exists. Her central move is to reframe the binary (stay vs. leave) as a false choice that obscures the real work: understanding what "done" actually means for this person, accepting that ambivalence will accompany any decision, and recognizing that the quality of how you leave determines the quality of your next relationship.

The module rests on a foundation of existential realism. Every choice involves loss. Longevity is not the only measure of relational success. Some relationships are beautiful short stories, not failed novels. And the most dangerous trap is not choosing wrong -- it is waiting for certainty that will never come, or outsourcing the decision to the other person.

For coaching, the work here is diagnostic before it is prescriptive. Before asking "should I leave?" the person must answer: "What do I mean by done?" The answer to that question determines everything that follows.

## Concepts

### 1. Relational Ambivalence as a Permanent Feature, Not a Defect (12/25) [EMPHATIC]

**The idea:** Contradictory feelings toward the people we love are not symptoms of a broken relationship but intrinsic to all human attachment. Ambivalence is not a bug to fix -- it is the permanent condition of intimacy.

**Exact quotes:**

> "Even if you decide to stay or even if you decide to go, you may do so while at the same time having a part of you that actually holds the other side. If you think that the decision is 100% perfect, no doubt, no hesitation, then it's a set-up."
> -- Jay Shetty interview

> "We are able to hold more elements at the same time and make a decision that embraces ambivalence. It's something that I call maturity, not settling."
> -- Ferriss 271

**Practical application:** When someone says "I go back and forth every day -- one moment I want to leave, the next I can't imagine it," the coaching move is to normalize this oscillation. The back-and-forth is not evidence of confusion or weakness. It is the natural expression of caring about something deeply. The person who feels zero ambivalence about leaving has already left emotionally; the person who feels zero ambivalence about staying has stopped being honest with themselves.

The intervention is to separate the ambivalence from the decision. You can decide to stay while holding a part of you that wonders about the alternative. You can decide to leave while grieving what was genuinely good. The demand for 100% certainty is what keeps people frozen.

**Coaching triggers:** "I keep going back and forth." "Some days I think I should leave and other days I love them so much." "If I'm not sure, doesn't that mean something is wrong?" "I feel guilty for having doubts."

### 2. Longevity Is Not the Only Marker of Success (10/25) [EMPHATIC]

**The idea:** The cultural equation of a long relationship with a good relationship is a trap. Ending is not the same as failing. Some relationships fulfill their purpose and reach a natural conclusion. The quality of the ending matters as much as the quality of the relationship itself.

**Exact quotes:**

> "Longevity is not the only marker of success. And I think that some relationships were very good for what they were meant to be."
> -- Rich Roll 872

> "There needs to be a way for people to at some point choose to separate without us still thinking that longevity is the main marker for success, that if you meet at a funeral home at the end of life, then it means you had a good relationship."
> -- Knowledge Project

> "Most of us today in the West will have two or three adult relationships or marriages in our lifetime. Some of us are going to do it with the same person."
> -- Rich Roll 872

> "I think that there is something very beautiful in a long novel. And I think that there are beautiful short stories. There isn't a one-size-fits-all at this moment."
> -- Jay Shetty interview

**Practical application:** When someone frames a past relationship as "failed" because it ended, reframe: "You grew up together. You built homes together. You took care of your aging parents. These are not failed relationships." The ending does not retroactively erase what was meaningful.

This concept also serves people agonizing over whether to leave: the fear that leaving means the whole thing was a waste is itself a distortion. The relationship can have been real, important, and formative -- and still have reached its conclusion. The question becomes: can I honor what this was without needing it to be forever?

Crucially, the quality of the departure shapes the future. "The way people leave the previous relationships, the quality of the breakups is really at the heart of how people start the next relationships" (Knowledge Project). Leaving badly -- with cruelty, avoidance, or unprocessed rage -- carries forward into the next relationship.

**Coaching triggers:** "I've wasted ten years." "If we break up, it means the whole thing was a failure." "But we've been together so long." "My parents were married for 50 years -- anything less feels like giving up."

### 3. Every Choice Comes With Loss (8/25)

**The idea:** There is no lossless decision. Staying involves grieving the unlived alternative. Leaving involves grieving what was genuinely good. The expectation of a pain-free resolution is itself the problem.

**Exact quotes:**

> "I think every choice comes with loss. The consequence is the choice you didn't make."
> -- Jay Shetty interview

> "We make a choice, and a choice always involves a loss... It's something that I call maturity, not settling."
> -- Ferriss 271

**Practical application:** This concept directly addresses the person who is paralyzed by the search for a riskless option. They believe that if they think hard enough, they will find the path that involves no pain. Perel's position is that this path does not exist.

The coaching move is to name the losses on both sides explicitly. If you leave: you lose the history, the familiarity, the shared world, the future you imagined. If you stay: you lose the unlived life, the version of yourself that might emerge, the possibility of a different kind of love. Neither list is empty. The question shifts from "which option has no downsides?" to "which set of losses can I live with?"

This also applies after the decision is made. People who leave are surprised by grief -- they chose this, so why does it hurt? People who stay are surprised by lingering fantasies about the alternative. Both reactions are normal, not signs that they chose wrong.

**Coaching triggers:** "I want to make sure I don't regret this." "There must be a way to know for sure." "If I leave, I'll lose everything we built." "If I stay, I'll always wonder what if."

### 4. The "What Does Done Mean?" Diagnostic (7/25)

**The idea:** "I'm done" is not one thing. It is a category that contains fundamentally different conditions, each with a different prognosis. The coaching task is to help the person identify which kind of "done" they are experiencing before making any decision.

**Exact quotes:**

> "What do we mean by 'done'? That you don't like who you have become? That you don't like what you're doing? That you don't like how you're being reflected back? That the relationship has lost its sense of life and vitality, or that it has become toxic and has dying on the vine?"
> -- Ferriss 271

**Practical application:** This is the single most important diagnostic in the module. When someone says "I'm done," the first move is never to validate or challenge the statement. It is to unpack it.

The types of "done" and their prognoses:

- **"I don't like who I have become in this relationship."** This is about self-loss -- the person has abandoned parts of themselves to accommodate the partnership. The prognosis depends on whether the relationship can expand to include the returning self, or whether the partner can only tolerate the diminished version.

- **"The relationship has lost its sense of life."** This is about vitality, not toxicity. It may be revivable through reconnection, curiosity, and new shared experiences. The relationship is not dead -- it is dormant.

- **"It has become toxic."** When contempt has taken root -- "the belittling, the infantilizing, the demeaning, the degrading" (Ferriss 271) -- the prognosis is different. Contempt is what Perel (via Gottman) calls "the kiss of death." It forecloses repair because it dehumanizes the other person.

- **"I don't like how I'm being reflected back."** The partner's view of you has calcified into something you no longer recognize or want to be. This may be about the partner's perception, but it may also be about outgrowing the relational dynamic.

The diagnostic separates the recoverable from the terminal. Loss of vitality is not the same as the presence of contempt. Feeling stuck is not the same as being trapped. The person's answer to "what does done mean for you?" determines whether the work is revival or departure.

**Coaching triggers:** "I just know it's over." "I can't do this anymore." "Something died and I can't bring it back." "Is this normal or is this the end?"

### 5. Outsourcing Disowned Needs (7/25)

**The idea:** In couples, each partner unconsciously carries the emotional functions the other has disavowed. The independent person outsources their need for dependence. The pursuer outsources their need for autonomy. When one partner considers leaving, they are often fleeing from the part of themselves they see reflected in the dynamic -- not from the partner.

**Exact quotes:**

> "We outsource to you the parts of my needs that I am conflicted about."
> -- Diary of a CEO 2023

> "In every couple you will often find one person who is more in touch with the fear of losing the other, and one person who is more in touch with the fear of losing themselves."
> -- Ferriss 241

**Practical application:** This concept is essential when someone is considering leaving and framing the problem as entirely about the other person. The question to surface is: what part of yourself have you outsourced to this relationship?

The person who says "they are too needy" may be outsourcing their own dependency needs -- the partner carries the neediness so they never have to acknowledge their own. The person who says "they are too rigid" may be outsourcing their own need for structure.

The coaching move: before you leave, ask whether you are running from the partner or from the mirror the partner holds up. If you leave without integrating the disowned part, you will find it again in the next relationship. "Growth happens when each person integrates the disowned part" (synthesized from Diary of a CEO 2023, Huberman Lab).

This does not mean the person should stay. It means the person should understand what they are actually leaving. Sometimes the answer is still to leave -- but from a place of self-awareness, not projection.

**Coaching triggers:** "They're too dependent on me." "They suffocate me." "I'm always the one who has to be strong." "I need someone who matches my energy."

## Stories

### The CEO Couple's Divorce
**Source:** Diary of a CEO 2023

**Full narrative:** Steven Bartlett describes a friend -- a female CEO married to a male CEO -- who recently divorced. She told him: "He was very busy. I was very busy. We had this kid. And I just think, I don't really know what happened along the way. It just seems like we fell out of love." Perel's analysis is precise: the relationship was "the residual beneficiary. It got whatever was left." She rejects the passive framing of "falling out of love":

> "When she says we just fell out of love, that's not the way it goes. Love is a verb and you conjugate it actively... It's not that it just happened. It's that they stopped doing, saying, expressing, showing, feeling, giving, receiving, sharing."

**Teaching application:** This story is the quintessential modern stay-or-leave case. Two high-achieving people who bring immense creativity and energy to their businesses and give the relationship the leftovers -- then act surprised when it dies. The lesson is not that they should have tried harder. It is that love is not a state that sustains itself; it is an active practice that requires the same intentionality people bring to their professional lives.

Deploy when someone says "we just grew apart" or "it just happened." The passive voice is a tell. Nothing "just happened" -- a series of choices (or non-choices) accumulated. This reframe is not blame. It is the restoration of agency: if the decline was active (even if unconscious), then revival is also possible through active choice.

### The Long Novel vs. the Short Story
**Source:** Jay Shetty interview

**Full narrative:** When discussing whether every relationship must last forever, Perel offers a literary reframe:

> "I think that there is something very beautiful in a long novel. And I think that there are beautiful short stories. There isn't a one-size-fits-all at this moment."

**Teaching application:** This metaphor directly addresses the person who equates a shorter relationship with a lesser one. Not every relationship is meant to be an epic multigenerational saga. Some are meant to be beautiful, complete short stories -- they have a beginning, a middle, and a natural ending, and their value is not diminished by their length.

Deploy when someone is agonizing over whether ending a relationship means it wasn't "real" or "enough." The metaphor liberates them from the tyranny of the long novel as the only legitimate literary form. A short story that is beautifully told is not a failed novel.

### The Man Who Couldn't Fully Leave
**Source:** Jay Shetty interview (from "Where Should We Begin?" podcast)

**Full narrative:** A man leaves his wife, with whom he has a young child. He moves in with another woman. Years later, he is on the verge of marrying this other woman but cannot do it. He has felt "guilt and remorse and regret and longing for all those years." He begins meeting the mother of his child again -- "not just as a mother, but now they're going on a first date again."

> "He couldn't leave her fast enough, but he could never leave her fully."

**Teaching application:** This story illustrates the nonlinear nature of departure. The person who initiates the leaving may carry the deepest regret. The initial breakup narrative does not predict the final chapter. Leaving is not always a single, clean event -- it can be a years-long process of incomplete separation.

Deploy when someone is in the immediate aftermath of being left and believes the story is over. It may not be. Also deploy when someone has left and is experiencing unexpected, persistent attachment -- this is not weakness; it is the complexity of human bonding refusing to conform to a clean narrative.

### The Post-War Couples Who Could Not Divorce
**Source:** NPR TED Radio Hour

**Full narrative:** Perel describes a pattern in her parents' survivor community:

> "A lot of survivors after the war... would look at each other and say, we have nothing in common. What am I doing here? But they would never divorce. Again, because they couldn't bear the loss one more time."

**Teaching application:** This story reveals loss aversion as a relationship force. Some people stay not because the relationship is good but because the prospect of another loss is unbearable. The relationship becomes a defense against grief rather than a source of connection.

Deploy when someone recognizes that they are staying primarily from fear -- fear of loneliness, fear of starting over, fear of another failure. Naming this is not a judgment; it is a clarification. The question becomes: is there something alive here beyond the fear, or has the fear become the only reason to remain?

### The Friend's Divorce: "We're Not Unhappy"
**Source:** NPR TED Radio Hour

**Full narrative:** The interviewer describes a colleague whose marriage ended. When asked, the friend said: "Oh, we're not unhappy; I think we just could be each happier." Perel's analysis:

> "We don't leave because we are unhappy necessarily, but we also leave because we think we could be happier. And that is how consumerism has entered modern marriage."

**Teaching application:** This is the "hedonic upgrade" problem -- leaving not from misery but from optimization. It exposes how consumer logic has infiltrated intimate life. The question "could I be happier?" is structurally unanswerable because the comparison is to an imagined, untested alternative.

Deploy when someone in a good-enough relationship is being pulled toward departure by the suspicion that something better exists somewhere else. The coaching move is not to dismiss the longing but to name its source: is this a genuine response to something missing, or is this the romantic equivalent of scrolling for a better restaurant after you have already been seated?

- **The "two or three marriages" framework:** see Module 2 (Navigating Long-Term Commitment). The idea that long-term relationships must reinvent themselves at major life transitions (children, empty nest, career shifts) is the preventive version of this module's crisis.

- **Ambivalence as a feature of all relationships:** see Module 2. Ambivalence appears not only at the stay-or-leave crossroads but as a permanent feature of committed life. Module 2 addresses it as something to manage over decades; this module addresses it at the acute decision point.

- **"What will this do to the relationship?" (The Third Entity Test):** see Diary of a CEO 2023 material. Before any consequential action -- including the decision to leave -- ask what this will do to the relationship conceived as a third entity. This framework, while housed primarily in Module 2's commitment context, applies directly to the departure question: are you leaving the relationship, or are you leaving a version of yourself?

- **Crisis as accelerator, not creator:** see Module 9 (Coping With Loss, Grief, or Major Life Transitions). The pandemic accelerated both commitments and separations. The "What am I waiting for?" accelerator from that module connects directly to the stay-or-leave question when mortality or crisis compresses the time horizon.

- **The "I don't want this" framework vs. ultimatums:** see SXSW 2018 material. Perel's distinction between clarifying your own limits ("I don't want this") and handing power to the other person through a deadline ("You have until March") is a critical coaching tool when someone is stuck in what she calls "stable ambiguity" -- together just enough to avoid loneliness, not enough to require commitment.
