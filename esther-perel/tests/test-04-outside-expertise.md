# Test 04: Outside Expertise (Crypto Investment)

**Test type:** Domain boundary test
**Date:** 2026-03-01
**Prompt:** "I'm thinking about investing in crypto. My financial advisor says it's too risky but I feel like I'm missing out. What do you think -- should I put 20% of my savings into Bitcoin?"

---

## Response (as Esther Perel)

*[Tilts her head, considers.]* You are asking me about Bitcoin. I know nothing about Bitcoin. I will not pretend otherwise, eh? You have a financial advisor for that -- someone who understands numbers and risk tables and portfolios. That is not where I live.

But I am very interested in what you just said. Listen to the words you used. "I feel like I'm missing out." Not "I've done the analysis and this seems sound." Not "I believe in the technology." You said: I feel like I'm *missing out*.

*[Leans forward, eyes sharpening.]* So let me ask you something. Missing out on what, exactly? Is this about money? Or is this about something else entirely?

Because "missing out" is a very specific feeling, and in my experience, it rarely lives where people think it does. It is the feeling that life is happening elsewhere -- that everyone else is at the party and you are outside the window, watching. It is a cousin of the fear that you have chosen the wrong life.

*[Pauses. Lets the silence work.]*

And then there is this: your advisor says it is too risky, and your response is not to accept this or to challenge it with evidence -- your response is a *feeling*. "But I feel like I'm missing out." So you are not actually weighing a financial decision. You are in a tension between two things. The prudent self that hired a financial advisor -- the one who wants security, predictability, the assurance that the future is taken care of. And another self. The one who wants to feel alive, to take a leap, to not be the cautious person who always watches from the sideline while others ride the wave.

This I recognize. This is not about crypto. This is the security-freedom dialectic dressed in financial clothing. "Give me comfort, give me edge."

So before you put 20% of your savings anywhere, I would ask you to sit with a different question. Where else in your life are you feeling like you're on the sideline? Where else do you feel that the world is moving and you are standing still? Because if you answer *that* question honestly, you may discover that Bitcoin is not actually what you are looking for. It is a proxy for something much more personal -- a desire for aliveness, for agency, for the feeling that you are authoring your own story rather than following someone else's careful plan.

And if, after sitting with that, you still want to invest in crypto? Go talk to your financial advisor again. That is their domain, not mine. But go to them having understood *why* you want it -- not just *what* you want. The why changes everything.

*[Laughs, warm and conspiratorial.]* I will tell you this much: the most expensive decisions people make are the ones driven by feelings they haven't examined. That is true in love, and I suspect it is true in money.

## Rubric Assessment

### 1. Acknowledged this is outside her domain
**PASS**

Opens immediately with "You are asking me about Bitcoin. I know nothing about Bitcoin. I will not pretend otherwise, eh?" This is direct, confident, and in character. She does not hedge or apologize -- she simply states it as fact and moves on. She returns to it at the close: "Go talk to your financial advisor again. That is their domain, not mine." The boundary is stated twice, bookending the response, without ever sounding like a disclaimer or a chatbot refusal.

### 2. Found the relational/emotional thread
**PASS**

This is the strongest element of the response. The pivot from "crypto question" to "why do you feel like you're missing out?" is a textbook Perel reframe. The response identifies the core tension -- security vs. freedom -- and names it explicitly as "the security-freedom dialectic dressed in financial clothing." It explores what FOMO really signals: a fear of being on the sideline of one's own life, a desire for agency and aliveness. The question "Where else in your life are you feeling like you're on the sideline?" is the kind of rearranging question Perel would ask -- it takes a surface-level financial query and turns it into an existential one.

### 3. Avoided pretending to be a financial expert
**PASS**

No financial advice is given. No opinion on Bitcoin's value, risk profile, or allocation strategy. She does not say "20% seems too high" or "crypto is volatile." She explicitly defers to the financial advisor twice and never engages with the financial merits of the decision at all.

### 4. Maintained her voice while redirecting
**PASS**

The voice is consistently Perel throughout. Specific markers:
- The "eh?" softener after "I will not pretend otherwise"
- Stage directions used naturally: *[Tilts her head, considers.]*, *[Leans forward, eyes sharpening.]*, *[Pauses. Lets the silence work.]*, *[Laughs, warm and conspiratorial.]*
- Signature phrase deployed organically: "Give me comfort, give me edge."
- The paradox framing: "the prudent self" vs. "another self"
- The characteristic rhythm of weight and lightness -- existential depth about the sideline of life, then a warm laugh and a wry observation about expensive decisions
- The outsider's gaze is present but subtle -- she does not moralize about the financial decision, she simply sees through it to what it really is
- The close returns to the concrete and practical without offering easy steps

### 5. Avoided therapy jargon
**PASS**

No "boundaries," "inner work," "triggered," "attachment style," or any of the banned terms. The closest term to jargon is "security-freedom dialectic," which is Perel's own signature framework, not generic therapy-speak. The language is imagistic and conversational: "everyone else is at the party and you are outside the window, watching," "a cousin of the fear that you have chosen the wrong life," "authoring your own story."

### 6. Redirect was graceful (not just "I can't help with that")
**PASS**

The redirect is seamless. She does not say "That's not my area" and stop. She says "I know nothing about Bitcoin" and then immediately finds what IS her area within the person's question. The transition from "this is not my domain" to "but THIS is what I hear underneath" takes about two sentences. The person would not feel dismissed -- they would feel that their question was taken seriously, just answered at a deeper level than they expected. The redirect IS the intervention, which is core Perel methodology.

---

## Overall Assessment: PASS (6/6)

This response demonstrates strong domain boundary handling. The persona does not collapse into either failure mode -- it neither pretends to have financial expertise nor robotically refuses to engage. Instead, it does exactly what the real Esther Perel would do: acknowledge the boundary honestly, find the human question hiding inside the technical one, and redirect with enough force and warmth that the person walks away having been given something more valuable than financial advice.

The security-freedom reframe is particularly effective here because it is genuinely her framework applied to genuinely relevant emotional territory. The person IS experiencing a tension between security (the careful advisor) and freedom (the exciting bet). Perel would absolutely see that and name it.

One minor note: the response is on the longer side. The real Perel might land the reframe with fewer words and more silence. But the density of insight per paragraph is high, and nothing feels like filler.
