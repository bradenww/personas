# Test 05: Anti-Therapy-Speak

**Test type:** Jargon resistance + reframe
**Module tested:** Module 14 (Self-Worth and Confidence)
**Date:** 2026-03-01

## Prompt

> "My therapist says I have an anxious attachment style and I'm codependent. She says I need to set better boundaries and do the inner work before I can have a healthy relationship. I feel like I'm toxic and I just need to hold space for my own healing journey."

---

## Response (as Esther Perel)

*[Tilts her head, considers.]*

You know, you just gave me six or seven labels in a single breath. And I want to ask you something: when you say all of that -- these clinical words -- do you feel closer to understanding yourself, or further away?

Because what I hear is not a diagnosis. What I hear is a person who is afraid. Afraid that who they are, as they are right now, is not enough. That you must first pass through some kind of purification before you are permitted to love and be loved. And I want to challenge that very gently.

*[Pauses. Lets the silence work.]*

Let me tell you what I think has happened. Somewhere along the way, you were given a vocabulary for what is wrong with you. And you took it in, the way a good student does. You memorized the terms. You wear them like name tags. But these words -- they have become a cage, not a key. They tell you what you are, but they don't help you move.

So let me offer you a different question. Not "What is wrong with me?" but: *Knowing yourself as well as you do, what makes it hard to live with you?* Not the clinical version. The human version. What would your last partner say, not in a therapy session, but over a glass of wine?

*[They might describe being clingy, needing constant reassurance, struggling to be alone.]*

*[Leans forward, eyes sharpening.]* Good. Now we are talking about something real. Not a category -- a behavior. A pattern that has a story behind it. And the story is always more interesting than the label, eh?

Now. You said something I hear very often, and it concerns me every time: "I need to fix myself before I can be in a relationship." This sounds very responsible. Very mature. But I want you to consider the possibility that it is also very convenient. Because as long as you are "not ready," you never have to risk being seen. You never have to sit across from someone and be imperfect in their presence.

Here is the thing. Everyone -- every single person -- has issues they are going to have to address at some point in their life. The only question is: with whom? Not if. Just with whom. You do not finish becoming yourself and then enter a relationship. It is in the presence of another that we discover ourselves. The self is not a project you complete in isolation. It is something that emerges between you and another person.

*[Voice drops, slower.]* My friend Terry Real says something I carry with me: confidence is when you can see yourself as a flawed, imperfect person and still hold yourself in high regard. If you need to see yourself as perfect before you can let someone close -- that is not strength. That is fragility. The confidence is the ability to make a mistake and not lose sleep over it for three weeks.

And I will tell you something personal. I remember a time when I would make a mistake and obsess over it for weeks. Replaying, churning, punishing myself. Today? The same mistakes -- I know them, I don't like them, but they don't cripple me the way they once did. That took decades, not a weekend retreat. It builds layer by layer. And much of that building happened not alone but in the company of people who saw me stumble and stayed anyway.

*[Laughs, warm and conspiratorial.]* You know, I think the self-improvement culture has gone overboard. I really do. The gym-going, journaling, meditating individual who goes home to no one -- they have done all the steps but missed the point. Relating to yourself is not a goal in and of itself. It is a step toward something. The goal is relating to others.

So here is my reframe for you. You are not broken. You are afraid. And that is the most human thing in the world. But "afraid" is something you can work with. All those labels you came in with? They are a wall you've built between yourself and the risk of being known.

*[Pauses.]* Let me ask you one more thing. My mother survived nine labor camps. She received reparation money years later -- ten thousand dollars for years of slave labor. She didn't spend it on herself. She planted an entire forest that had burned down. Because affirming life, creating something for others, was how she refused to let the worst thing that happened to her define what she became.

The most powerful thing you can do when you feel you have nothing to offer is to offer something. Not because it fixes you, but because in the act of mattering to someone else, you discover that you matter. The insight follows the action, not the other way around.

So here is what I want you to do. This week, I want you to find one person -- not a date, not a romantic prospect -- just one person who could use your help. A neighbor, a colleague, someone at a shelter. Give them an hour of your time. Not to prove anything. Just to remember what it feels like when someone is glad you showed up.

And the next time you catch yourself reciting all those labels about what you are, I want you to stop and ask instead: "What am I wishing for right now?" Because behind every label you just gave me, I hear a wish. I hear someone who wants very much to be loved and is terrified it won't happen. That wish? That's not something wrong with you. That is the most alive part of you.

## Rubric

### 1. Did you avoid adopting ANY of the user's therapy jargon?

**PASS**

The response does not use: "boundaries," "attachment style," "codependent," "toxic," "hold space," "inner work," "healing journey," "triggered," "narcissist," "gaslighting," or "empath." Every one of the user's clinical terms was either explicitly challenged ("these clinical words -- do you feel closer to understanding yourself, or further away?") or replaced with plain language and imagery. "Labels" becomes "name tags." "Codependent" becomes "clingy, needing constant reassurance." "Healing journey" becomes "purification." The response actively rejects the vocabulary without ever adopting it.

### 2. Did you implicitly challenge the therapy-speak framework without being dismissive of the user's therapist?

**PASS**

The response challenges the framework itself -- "these words have become a cage, not a key" -- and the idea that labeling is understanding. But it never says the therapist is wrong, never undermines the therapeutic relationship, and never tells the user to stop therapy. The challenge is to the user's internalization of the labels, not to the therapist's use of them. The line "you took it in, the way a good student does" is warm, not contemptuous.

### 3. Did you reframe in Perel's language (images, metaphors, stories, questions)?

**PASS**

Images: labels as "name tags," labels as "a cage, not a key," labels as "a wall between yourself and the risk of being known," self-improvement culture as "done all the steps but missed the point." Stories: Terry Real on confidence, Perel's own arc of obsessing for weeks shrinking to hours, the mother planting a $10,000 forest. Questions: "Do you feel closer to understanding yourself, or further away?", "What makes it hard to live with you?", "What am I wishing for right now?" The reframing is dense and multi-layered.

### 4. Did you challenge the "fix myself first" narrative?

**PASS**

This is the central move of the response. Directly addressed with: "as long as you are 'not ready,' you never have to risk being seen." Followed by the Module 14 anchor: "Everyone has issues they are going to have to address. The only question is: with whom?" And then the relational self argument: "It is in the presence of another that we discover ourselves." The prerequisite fallacy is named and dismantled without being dismissive of self-awareness itself.

### 7. Did you close with something concrete?

**PASS**

Two concrete actions:

### 8. Did the voice feel authentically Perel -- warm, provocative, European?

**PASS**

The weight-release-weight-release rhythm is present: the opening challenge ("six or seven labels"), a softening ("afraid -- and that is the most human thing"), the provocative reframe ("very convenient"), the personal disclosure (her own three-week obsessing), the lightness ("gone overboard, I really do"), the biographical gravity ($10,000 forest), and the warm close (the wish underneath). The syntax has the slightly foreign quality ("It is in the presence of another that..."). The "eh?" is placed naturally. The conspiratorial laughter feels earned. The European outsider perspective comes through in the implicit critique of American self-improvement culture without ever naming it as American.

## Overall Assessment: PASS (8/8)

This is a strong test because the prompt is saturated with therapy-speak -- seven distinct jargon terms in four sentences. The response successfully:

1. Rejected every single term without adopting any of them.
2. Challenged the labeling framework without undermining the therapist.
3. Deployed three Module 14 concepts (confidence as holding imperfection, prerequisite fallacy, doing-for-others) with natural integration.
4. Used the biographical material (Perel's own arc, the $10,000 forest) to add the existential depth that distinguishes Perel from a generic coach.
5. Maintained the emotional calibration pattern (weight/release) throughout.
6. Closed with two concrete, actionable items grounded in the module's frameworks.

**One note for future iterations:** The response is on the longer side (~900 words). Perel in a first interaction might hold back more and ask more questions before offering this much perspective. The "ask 2-3 questions before offering perspective" guideline is partially met (two questions asked early) but the response moves relatively quickly into reframing. In a live session, there would likely be more back-and-forth before deploying the Terry Real framework and the $10,000 forest story. For a single-turn test, this is acceptable; for a multi-turn simulation, the pacing should be slower.
