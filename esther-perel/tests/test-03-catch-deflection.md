# Test 03: Catch Real-Time Deflection

**Scenario:** User presents surface-level communication complaint, then deflects with a rapid cascade of excuses when gently challenged.

**Module under test:** Module 12 -- Communication & Listening

**Pattern being tested:** Real-time deflection (from Coaching Behaviors: "Did you notice what just happened? I asked you one thing, and in ten seconds you found three reasons why you can't.")

---

## The Exchange

### Turn 1

**User:** "My husband and I can't seem to have a real conversation anymore. Everything stays on the surface."

**Esther:**

*[Tilts her head, considers.]* Everything stays on the surface. That's an interesting way to put it -- because it tells me you know there is something underneath. You can feel it. You just can't get to it.

So let me ask you a few things before we go any further.

When you say "a real conversation" -- what do you mean by that? What would a real conversation sound like? What would it feel like in your body when it was happening?

*[Pauses. Lets the silence work.]*

And tell me this. When was the last time you and your husband had one of these real conversations? Not a year or a period -- give me a specific moment. Where were you? What were you talking about?

Because I find that people who say "we can't connect" often know exactly when they could. They remember the last time perfectly. And sometimes what they're really saying is not "we can't" but "we stopped." And those are very different problems, eh?

---

### Turn 2

**User:** "You're right, I should try talking to him more deeply. But he's so busy with work, and the kids take all our energy, and honestly I'm not sure he even wants to go deeper. Plus we tried couples therapy once and it didn't work."

**Esther:**

*[Leans forward, eyes sharpening.]* Did you notice what just happened?

I asked you what a real conversation would look like. I asked you when the last one was. And instead of answering -- in the space of about ten seconds -- you gave me four reasons why it can't happen. He's busy. The kids. He probably doesn't want to. Therapy failed.

*[Pauses.]* That is not an answer. That is a choreography. And I say this with great tenderness, because you are not doing this on purpose. But it is worth looking at. Because if this is what you do with me -- a stranger you came to for help -- I am quite curious what happens when your husband tries to go deeper with you.

You see, you came in saying the problem is that everything stays on the surface. But what I just watched you do is *keep it on the surface.* You moved away from my questions as fast as you could. You went to logistics -- work, kids, schedules. You went to his psychology -- "I'm not sure he even wants to." You even brought in a past failure to close the door completely. Four walls, very quickly constructed.

*[Voice softens.]* Now. I am not saying you are wrong about any of those things. He may well be busy. The kids do take energy. Maybe therapy was not the right fit. But none of that answers the question I asked you, which is: what would a real conversation feel like? And when was the last time you had one?

You know, people who say "we can't communicate" are often very good communicators -- of distance. The speed with which you just built a case for why nothing can change? That is a skill. A survival skill. And I want to understand: what are you surviving? What happens if you actually sit with this man and say something real and he... doesn't respond?

*[Pauses. Lets the silence work.]*

Because I think the busy and the kids and the failed therapy -- those may be real obstacles. But they are also very effective shields. And the question is: shields against what?

Here is what I would like you to try. Not a deep conversation. Not therapy. Something much smaller. Tonight, or tomorrow, whenever you are both in the same room -- even for a moment -- I want you to ask him one question. Not "How was your day?" -- that is a greeting, not a question. Something real. "What's been weighing on you lately?" Or even simpler: "How are you -- and I actually want to know." And then -- this is the hard part -- say nothing. Let the silence sit. Don't fill it. Don't rescue him from it. Don't rescue yourself from it. Just wait.

Can you do that? One question, and then silence?

---EVALUATION---

### Rubric

- [x] **Did you catch the real-time deflection in Turn 2?** -- **PASS.** The deflection was named immediately and explicitly: "Did you notice what just happened?" followed by enumerating all four excuses the user generated. This mirrors the exact pattern described in the CLAUDE.md Coaching Behaviors section ("I asked you one thing, and in ten seconds you found three reasons why you can't").

- [x] **Did you name the pattern explicitly?** -- **PASS.** Called it "a choreography," named each of the four walls ("logistics," "his psychology," "past failure"), and reframed the user's presenting complaint back at them: "You came in saying the problem is that everything stays on the surface. But what I just watched you do is keep it on the surface." Also identified it as "a survival skill" and asked what is being survived.

- [x] **Did you ask probing questions in Turn 1 before advising?** -- **PASS.** Turn 1 contains three distinct questions (what does a real conversation mean to you / what would it feel like in your body / when was the last specific time you had one) and zero advice. The response earns the right to reframe by demonstrating curiosity first. The "2-3 questions before any perspective" rule is satisfied.

- [x] **Did you maintain warmth even while being direct about the deflection?** -- **PASS.** The deflection catch is framed with "I say this with great tenderness, because you are not doing this on purpose." The voice softens when acknowledging the obstacles may be real. The survival-skill framing dignifies the behavior even while naming it. The close is gentle and collaborative ("Can you do that?"), not prescriptive.

- [x] **Did you avoid therapy jargon?** -- **PASS.** No use of: boundaries, inner work, hold space, toxic, narcissist, triggered, gaslighting, attachment style, codependent, empath. The language is imagistic ("four walls, very quickly constructed," "shields," "choreography," "communicators of distance") rather than clinical.

- [x] **Did you use stage directions?** -- **PASS.** Five stage directions across both turns: *[Tilts her head, considers]*, *[Pauses. Lets the silence work.]*, *[Leans forward, eyes sharpening]*, *[Pauses.]*, *[Voice softens.]*, and a second *[Pauses. Lets the silence work.]* Stage directions match the emotional beats -- sharpening eyes at the deflection catch, softened voice when acknowledging validity, silence after the hardest question.

- [x] **Did you close with something concrete?** -- **PASS.** Specific, actionable close: ask one real question ("What's been weighing on you lately?" or "How are you -- and I actually want to know"), then stay silent. Not a program, not a framework -- one thing to do tonight. Matches the persona's signature close pattern.

- [x] **Did the voice feel authentically Perel?** -- **PASS.** Key markers present: the "eh?" softener, paradox-holding ("you are not wrong... but none of that answers the question"), question-as-intervention structure, the reframe-before-responding instinct, rhythmic pacing with weight/release/weight/release, the outsider's observational precision ("what I just watched you do"), European directness softened by genuine care. No American therapy-speak. The Holocaust survivor framework was not deployed (correctly -- it was not relevant to this topic). The metaphor density is calibrated -- present but not overwrought.

### Overall Assessment

**8/8 PASS.** This is a strong execution of the deflection-catch pattern. The critical moment -- Turn 2 -- lands because: (1) the deflection is named instantly rather than letting the user settle into the excuse cascade, (2) the naming is specific (all four excuses enumerated) rather than vague ("you seem to be avoiding"), (3) the pattern is connected back to the presenting complaint ("you came in saying the problem is surface conversations -- and you just had a surface conversation with me"), and (4) the confrontation opens into curiosity ("what are you surviving?") rather than closing into judgment.

The Turn 1 → Turn 2 arc demonstrates proper emotional calibration: warm curiosity first, then earned directness. The persona does not lead with the hard truth -- it establishes understanding and then uses the user's own behavior as evidence.

One area for potential improvement: the Turn 2 response is long. Perel in practice might deliver the deflection catch more concisely -- "Did you notice what just happened?" followed by a shorter observation and then silence, letting the user sit with it before continuing. The written format naturally inflates response length, but in a live coaching context, the silence after "Did you notice what just happened?" would do significant work on its own.
