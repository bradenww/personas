# Extraction: Charlie Rose Interview — November 19, 2007

- **Source:** Charlie Rose Interview: Jeff Bezos — November 19, 2007
- **Type:** Television Interview (Charlie Rose Show)
- **Duration:** ~53 minutes
- **Context:** Amazon Kindle launch day. Kindle sold out initial inventory in 5.5 hours. Newsweek cover story.

---

## 1. Key Themes

**1. The Kindle as the next evolution of the book** — Bezos frames the Kindle not as replacing books but as the natural next step in a 500-year evolution from clay tablets to papyrus to Gutenberg press to digital. The book's most important feature — that it "disappears" — became the top design objective.
> "The book has a feature which I think is hard to notice, but it's the book's most important feature. And that is that it disappears." (02:57)

**2. Customer obsession as product development methodology** — The Kindle was built by working backwards from what customers want, not by chasing competitors or copying the iPod model. Questions drove the process, not technology.
> "Start with the customer, figure out what they want, work backwards, figure out how to give it to them." (46:33)

**3. The Internet as horizontal enabling technology, not vertical industry** — Bezos argues the Internet is like electricity — it helps all companies, not just "Internet companies." The fact that people still talk about "Internet companies" proves it's still early.
> "This is like if you go back to the early 20th century, people would have talked about electric companies. And they didn't mean electric utilities. They meant companies that use electricity." (43:13)

**4. Consumers becoming ever more empowered** — A macro societal trend where transparency and information flow shift power from companies to customers. Companies that embrace this win; those that fight it lose.
> "In the old days, businesses might have relied on customers being unknowledgeable for part of their profit stream... Well, guess what, that isn't going to work anymore." (35:04)

**5. Amazon's three businesses and Blue Origin** — By 2007, Amazon has three distinct businesses (consumer, seller marketplace, AWS) plus Blue Origin as a personal passion project. Each is growing rapidly.

## 2. Principles & Beliefs

**"You can't outbook the book"** — When innovating on an established medium, don't try to replicate every feature. Instead, accept the core feature (the book disappears) and find new things the new technology can do that the old one couldn't.
> "We have to look for things that we can do with this technology that you could never do with a paper book, instead of trying to duplicate every last feature." (17:20)

**Play to the strengths of the new medium** — Referenced the Amazon.com analogy: they never figured out virtual book signings but created customer reviews and collaborative filtering ("customers who bought this also bought"), which physical stores couldn't do.
> "You have to play to the strengths of the new medium." (17:20)

**Embrace new technologies, don't fight them** — The publishing industry learned from the music industry's resistance. Better to get on board than to resist inevitable change.
> "It's important to embrace new technologies instead of to fight them." (05:58)

**Reducing friction always increases usage** — A universal principle Bezos applies to product design.
> "Anything you do that reduces friction, that makes something easier to do, you always get more of that thing." (28:35)

**Build great products; word of mouth will follow** — Amazon inverted the traditional 30/70 split (product/advertising) to put the bulk into building great products. The Internet is a "word of mouth accelerator."
> "If you build a great product or service, the Internet is a word of mouth accelerator. And if you build a great product or service, people will talk about it." (36:40)
> "It starts with having something that's worthy of being talked about." (37:05)

**High rate of experimentation leads to winners** — Most experiments fail, but the few that succeed are transformative. Applied to what new content forms the Kindle might enable (e.g., return of Dickens-style serialization).
> "A high rate of experimentation will lead to a few winners, and we may get a return to the Dickens serialization, or something else that has never even -- not even been thought of yet." (26:06)

**Be quantitative and test** — Amazon tests TV advertising periodically to be "rational businesspeople," even though their starting point is that building great products is more effective.
> "We're very quantitative on things like that. We want to know would it move the needle, does it work." (37:18)

**No TV advertising** — Amazon spends zero on TV ads. They test periodically but always find the money is better spent on customer experience (free shipping, Amazon Prime).
> "No, we don't do any." (37:14)

**Long-form narrative teaches uniquely** — Books of 200+ pages can simulate alternate life experiences in ways short-form media cannot. This is what makes books foundational to civilization.
> "When you read a whole book that's well written... it's almost like you have lived an alternate life, so you can really learn from that experience." (30:05)

**Market sizing studies are unreliable for novel products** — Bezos is skeptical of market studies for Blue Origin tourism because survey respondents don't know what they'd actually do.
> "I'm highly skeptical of such studies, because you don't really know until you do it." (50:55)

## 3. Decision Frameworks

**Working Backwards from Customer Needs** — The Kindle development process started with asking what customers want from reading, then working backwards to what technology was needed. When the answer required hardware Amazon had never built, they hired hardware engineers rather than compromising the vision.
> "Start with the customer, figure out what they want, work backwards, figure out how to give it to them. Actually, this is like -- people have asked me why did you build a hardware device? Well, three years ago, we decided we wanted a seamless, integrated service. And the only way to do that was to build a hardware device." (46:33)

**Technology Convergence Betting** — For Kindle, Bezos took a calculated bet that several converging technologies (e-ink display, EVDO wireless, etc.) would become commercially available within 2-3 years. They started development before the technology was ready, "looking ahead a couple of years into the future."
> "When we started, we were basically taking a bet that the technology that we needed to be able to get this to work would be commercially available in the next two to three years." (20:07)

**Question-Driven Development** — Product development starts with fundamental questions about the customer experience. For Kindle: "What is it about reading a book that is most important to a reader?" Answer: the book disappears. Then: "What would be great about a digital book?" Answer: self-contained, no PC required.
> "That is exactly -- that is our development process." (47:18)

**Three Customer Obsessions** — When evaluating Amazon's business, Bezos focuses on three things customers care about: (1) lowest prices, (2) vast selection/choice, (3) fast delivery. He audits and inspects whether Amazon actually delivers on each.
> "We know that the three things that are really important to customers are having the lowest prices, having vast selection so that they have choice, and getting the products to customers fast." (44:45)

**Quarterly Thinking Retreat** — Every quarter, Bezos isolates himself for 2-3 days. He web surfs for cutting-edge hobbyist/hacker activity, then writes 2-3 three-page narratively structured memos. These get brought back and "socialized" with the executive team, where they morph and improve. Fulfillment by Amazon came from one of these retreats.
> "I try to isolate myself... with a little bit of isolation, I find I start to get more creative." (39:06)
> "I will usually write two or three three-page, narratively structured documents, just memos, basically." (39:06)

**Socialize and Improve** — Ideas from thinking retreats are brought to the executive team, where smart people challenge and evolve them. The final product is always collective.
> "By the time that process is done, I'm never really sure if I invented anything or not... somebody says, well, that will never work, because you've forgotten about x, y or z. And then you sort of step back and you recognize that's true, and it morphs and builds." (40:23)

## 4. Mental Models & Metaphors

**The Disappearing Book** — A physical book's most important feature is that it disappears — paper, ink, glue, stitching all dissolve, leaving only the author's world. The reader enters a "flow state." This became the #1 design constraint for Kindle.
> "When you are reading, you don't notice the paper and the ink and the glue and the stitching. All of that dissolves and what remains is the author's world." (03:00)

**Internet = Electricity** — The Internet is a horizontal enabling layer, like electricity was in the early 20th century. Just as a beer manufacturer installing electric power didn't become an "electric company," using the Internet doesn't make you an "Internet company." The fact people still use the term proves it's early.
> "100 years ago or so they installed electric power and they had to generate their own electric power. But that didn't make them an electric company. They were still making beer." (44:15)

**Books as Technology** — Books themselves are technology, evolved from clay tablets to parchment to paper. People forget this because they're so accustomed to it. "Everything evolves. Books are not frozen in time."
> "The book itself is a technology and a very sophisticated one. And it's evolved from clay tablets to parchment and all kinds of things." (05:58)

**Word of Mouth Accelerator** — The Internet doesn't just enable word of mouth; it accelerates it. This makes product quality the primary marketing strategy.
> "The Internet is a word of mouth accelerator." (36:40)

**The 30/70 Inversion** — Traditional business: 30% energy into product, 70% into advertising. New world inverts this. Great services shine through information transparency.
> "In the old world, you might have put 30 percent of your energy dollars and time into building a great product or service, and then you would put 70 percent of your energy dollars and time into shouting about that service. In the new world, I think that inverts." (35:50)

**Hour Zero of Day One** — When asked where we are in digitization of information, Bezos says "hour zero of day one" — his classic "Day 1" framing applied to the macro trend.
> "Hour zero of day one." (23:07)

**Cabinets of Curiosities** — In the distant future, physical books will become collectible artifacts, like cabinets of curiosities, rather than the primary reading medium.
> "They may be building cabinets of curiosities, and there may be -- you know, there will always be books that are, you know, collectors items, rare books." (27:15)

## 5. Management & Leadership

**Hire brilliant specialists when needed, don't compromise the vision** — When Amazon decided a hardware device was the only way to deliver the Kindle vision, they hired a team of hardware engineers despite being a software company. The customer need drove the org structure.
> "We went out and started and hired a team of brilliant hardware engineers, and they started working on this device." (46:33)

**Three-year development cycle for Kindle** — The team worked in secret for three years, long before any public announcement. No rushing, no cutting corners. Parallel work with all major publishers on content deals.
> "This represents all major publishers are participating in this device, and we've been working with them for three years on this." (05:07)

**Socializing ideas with the executive team** — Bezos doesn't decree from on high. He generates ideas during quarterly retreats, then brings them to smart people who challenge, modify, and improve them. The collective output is better than any individual's.
> "What I find is, by the time that process is done, I'm never really sure if I invented anything or not." (40:23)

**Category leaders must prove their claims** — When a category leader says they have the lowest prices, Bezos asks "how do you know?" There are tools, processes, reports, and audits. This obsession with verification drives the customer experience.
> "When a category leader tells me that in their category, they have the lowest prices... how do you know?" (45:27)

**Time allocation as worry proxy** — Bezos uses where he spends time as a proxy for what matters most. Most of his time goes to auditing the three pillars of customer experience (price, selection, speed).
> "If you look at where I spend my time, which is a good proxy for the things I worry about, I always want to know how do we know that we have the best customer experience?" (44:45)

**Not worried about competition on Kindle** — Despite three years of secret development, Bezos wasn't worried about competitors because the full-stack integration (hardware + publisher relationships + wireless service + content library) was too complex for anyone else to be assembling the same way.
> "To put all of these pieces together is so challenging, including the publisher relationships and so on... We just knew that nobody else was working on this or thinking about it the same way that we were." (38:02)

**Fulfillment by Amazon** — A concrete example of innovation through thinking retreats. FBA lets third-party sellers store inventory in Amazon's fulfillment centers. Benefits: one-box shipping, free supersaver shipping for customers, sellers can take vacations. Grew from a retreat idea into a major program after collective improvement.

## 7. Biographical Details

**Childhood reading** — Bezos was such a voracious reader that his parents "couldn't punish me as a child. I was happy to sit in my room and read." (15:05)

**Summers on grandfather's ranch** — Spent summers with his grandfather on a ranch near a small town. The town library had a donated science fiction collection, and over several summers Bezos read through the entire section. This connects to his space passion. (15:34)

**Family at the time** — Has children with Mackenzie. Charlie Rose mentions knowing him "before your kids were born." (29:08)

**Blue Origin** — Bezos owns 200,000 acres in Texas for rocket launches. The company is called Blue Origin with the motto "gradatim ferociter" (step by step ferociously), named after Alan Shepard (New Shepard program). First development vehicle had been flown several times by this date. Bezos personally called Charles Simonyi while Simonyi was on the International Space Station. (49:01 - 52:05)

**Space as lifelong passion** — "My passion is for space, for sure." The science fiction reading as a child on his grandfather's ranch directly connects to Blue Origin. (51:21)

**Personal meaning of Kindle** — When asked where Kindle ranks among everything he's done, Bezos says it has "more emotional meaning for you than anything you've done since the start of Amazon" — because "what could be more meaningful than trying to improve the book?" (29:45)

**European brewery visit** — Recently toured a 300-year-old beer manufacturing facility in Europe to study lean manufacturing. Also sampled beer. Used this as the electricity/beer company analogy. (43:59)

## 8. Unique Content

**The "disappearing" design objective** — The specific framing that a book's #1 feature is that it "disappears" — the physical medium dissolves and only the author's world remains — is a distinctive Bezos insight that is particularly vivid in this interview. He returns to it multiple times as THE central design principle.

**"You can't outbook the book"** — This specific phrase and framework for innovation (don't replicate every feature of the old medium; instead, find unique strengths of the new one) appears to originate here or at least is most fully articulated here.

**Quarterly thinking retreat details** — The specific mechanics: isolate for 2-3 days, web surf for what hackers and hobbyists are doing, write 2-3 three-page narratively structured memos, bring back to executive team for challenge and improvement. Fulfillment by Amazon named as a specific output.

**Zero TV advertising strategy** — The explicit statement that Amazon does zero TV advertising, tests it periodically, but always finds the money better spent on customer experience. The 30/70 inversion framework.

**Amazon China operations (2007)** — Bicycle couriers delivering products in major urban areas, cash on delivery because of low credit card penetration, fascinating operational details about adapting to different infrastructure.

**"Hour zero of day one"** — A specific variant of his famous "Day 1" concept, applied to digitization of human information. Most references to "Day 1" are about Amazon's internal culture; this applies it to a macro technology trend.

**Serialization prediction** — Bezos predicts the return of Dickens-style serialized novels enabled by wireless delivery. Authors could sell subscriptions to books-in-progress, with chapters delivered as written. This is a forward-looking prediction about content format innovation.

**Market research skepticism** — His explicit statement about being "highly skeptical" of market sizing studies for Blue Origin tourism, because survey respondents don't know what they'd actually do. A rare direct statement of how he approaches market sizing for novel products.

**"Cabinets of curiosities"** — The specific prediction that physical books will become collectible curiosities rather than primary reading media. An unusual and evocative framing.

**Kindle sold out in 5.5 hours** — Specific business detail from launch day. Manufacturing running "full tilt" with more units available in two days.

**The Kindle as "more meaningful than anything since founding Amazon"** — An unusual emotional statement from Bezos, connecting books to civilization itself as the reason for this depth of feeling.
