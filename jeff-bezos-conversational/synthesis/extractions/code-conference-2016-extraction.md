# Extraction: Code Conference 2016

- **Source:** Jeff Bezos — Code Conference (2016)
- **Type:** Interview (with audience Q&A)
- **Date:** 2016-06-01
- **Duration:** ~80 minutes
- **Interviewer:** Walt Mossberg
- **Context:** Wide-ranging interview at Recode's Code Conference. Bezos covers AI/Alexa, retail logistics, Washington Post strategy, Amazon culture, Blue Origin/space vision, free speech, Amazon Prime Video, physical bookstores, Career Choice program, and responding to critics. Includes audience Q&A on China/India strategy, giving up on ideas, Trump/press freedom, and going public.

## 1. Key Themes

- **AI as a civilizational shift.** Bezos frames natural language understanding and machine learning as among the biggest societal impacts of the next 20 years — driven by better algorithms, more compute power, and massive training data. Emphasizes this is "the first inning... might even be the first guys up at bat."
- **Customer obsession as identity.** Amazon doesn't think of itself as tied to any technology or skill set — it's tied to its customers, working backwards from their needs. This is why a company born online can open physical bookstores without irony.
- **Infrastructure enables entrepreneurial dynamism.** The internet's explosive growth was possible because credit cards, postal services, telecom fiber, and PCs already existed. Space lacks this infrastructure — which is why Blue Origin exists: to build the "heavy lifting infrastructure" so the next generation can have entrepreneurial dynamism in space.
- **Stability of customer needs.** The things customers want (selection, price, speed) are stable over decades. Build strategy around what won't change, not what will.
- **Free speech as cultural norm, not just law.** The Constitution is "just a piece of paper" without cultural norms protecting ugly speech. This applies to press freedom, billionaire lawsuits against media, and presidential candidates trying to chill scrutiny.
- **Wandering and exploration.** Bezos explicitly values wandering, loose agendas, and going down blind alleys. "Thinking you know exactly where you're going is a kind of lack of humility that doesn't let you invent."
- **Work-life harmony over balance.** Balance implies a strict tradeoff; harmony recognizes that energy from work and home feed each other.
- **Flywheel economics.** Prime Video doesn't need to be a standalone profit center — it drives shoe sales, free trial conversions, and membership renewals. The flywheel means each component accelerates the others.

## 2. Principles & Beliefs

1. **Customer needs are the North Star.** "We never think of ourselves as tied to any particular technology or skill set. We think of ourselves as tied to our customers, and we're trying to work backwards from their needs. And we'll learn whatever skills we need to service our customers." [17:37]

2. **Build strategy around stable truths.** "Think about what are the things that you know will be true even 10 years from now... the customer needs, if you find the right ones, will tend to be stable in time." Selection, price, and delivery speed will never go out of style. [1:09:48]

3. **Stubborn on vision, flexible on details.** "You want always to be very flexible on the tactics and the details, but very stubborn on the vision and the strategy." [~1:00:00]

4. **Defying conventional wisdom is required for greatness.** "Every single important thing we've ever done, the most important things have always seemed dumb to industry experts at the beginning. So to get really good results, you have to defy conventional wisdom. And the problem, of course, is that conventional wisdom is usually correct." [~59:30]

5. **Develop a thick skin.** "The best defense to speech that you don't like about yourself as a public figure is to develop a thick skin. It's really the only effective defense... If you absolutely can't tolerate critics, then don't do anything new or interesting." [25:44]

6. **Seek revenge, dig two graves.** Even for legitimate wrongs, the best use of time and energy is to move forward and do amazing things, not to pursue vengeance. [23:40]

7. **Transparency beats fine print.** On data/privacy: "You have to figure out ways to be kind of obviously clear." Amazon greets you by name so you know you're not anonymous — clearer than any terms of service. [9:43]

8. **Ugly speech needs protection, beautiful speech doesn't.** "The most important thing to remember about that is that beautiful speech doesn't need protection. It's ugly speech that needs protection." [27:52]

9. **You don't choose your passions, they choose you.** On Blue Origin: "I've been passionate about space and rockets since I was a 5-year-old boy." [44:00]

10. **Don't give up too easily, but know when to fold.** Keep funding things as long as one "high judgment champion" still believes. Only stop when the last champion folds — or when you start wondering if you're still high-judgment yourself. [~1:00:30]

11. **Happy people create great customer experiences.** "The only way to do that is with happy people. You can't do it with a set of miserable people watching the clock all day." [30:32]

12. **Wandering is essential to invention.** "I like things where wandering matters... Thinking you know exactly where you're going is a kind of lack of humility that doesn't let you invent." [53:59–54:09]

13. **Local customization matters.** The #1 meta-lesson from China vs. India: "We have done much more local market customization in India than we did in China." [1:13:03]

## 3. Decision Frameworks

### When to Give Up on an Idea
- Plant the seed. Let great teams work on it for multiple years.
- Keep going as long as **one high-judgment champion** still wants to try.
- Most companies give up too soon — bias toward persistence.
- The signal to fold: the last high-judgment champion folds their cards.
- "Sometimes I'm the last high judgment champion, and then eventually I start to wonder if I'm high judgment. That's another good time to fold the cards."

### When to Enter a New Market
- Not about finding the biggest market — "the landscape is gigantic, and we will ever only enter into the tiniest little portion."
- Avoid industries that are "extremely well served by really smart people" with enormous barriers to entry (e.g., oil exploration).
- Look for where Amazon's specific capabilities (data, logistics, customer relationships, technology) create a differentiated edge.
- Don't listen to experts who say "this is stupid" at the beginning — but do ask "what are we doing differently?"

### Stubborn on Vision, Flexible on Details
- The vision (what you're trying to achieve for customers) should be nearly immovable.
- The tactics, details, and execution path should be highly adaptable.
- At some point, even the vision may need to fold — but this should be the exception, not the rule.

### Privacy Transparency Test
- When collecting data: can you make the customer obviously aware (not just legally covered)?
- Combine transparency with a clear explanation of consumer benefit.
- The "Amazon greeting" test: is the customer's awareness of data use as clear as seeing "Welcome back, [Name]"?

## 4. Mental Models & Metaphors

### The Infrastructure Foundation Model
The internet didn't explode because of the internet — it exploded because credit cards, postal services, long-distance phone networks, and PCs already existed. These were built for other purposes but became the foundation for e-commerce. Space lacks this foundation, which is why it's stagnant (~40 launches/year, down from the peak). Blue Origin's job: build the heavy-lifting infrastructure so future entrepreneurs can have "a solar system as dynamic and interesting and varied as what we see on the internet today."

### Seeds and Trees (Pillar Development)
Amazon has three "big pillars" (Marketplace, Prime, AWS) and plants "little seeds" (Amazon Studios, Alexa/Echo, others) that may or may not become big trees. You don't know which seeds will grow — ask again in 10 years.

### The Flywheel
Prime Video winning Golden Globes → drives Prime membership → members shop more → sells more shoes → funds more content. "It's hard when you're talking about a circle to say which one is pushing the other, because they're pushing each other." The flywheel creates a unique monetization advantage: Amazon can charge less for premium content because of the downstream retail effects.

### The Street Corner Test (for handling criticism)
"Go stand on a street corner in a crowded urban area, and watch all the people walk by, and think about what they're thinking about. I bet you none of those people are thinking about you." Most people are thinking about dinner, an argument with an employee, etc. This reframes the scale of any negative press or criticism.

### Two Graves (Confucius)
"Seek revenge and you should dig two graves, one for yourself." On the Peter Thiel/Gawker situation — even legitimate wrongs aren't worth spending your time and energy on revenge.

### Plan A, Not Plan B
Bezos rejects the "Plan B for Earth" framing of space colonization. "I want Plan B to make sure Plan A works." Space is about saving Earth — moving heavy industry off-planet so Earth can be "zoned residential and light industrial."

### The 50 Watts Comparison
Humans operate on ~50 watts of power. AlphaGo beats the world Go champion but uses vastly more energy. "If AlphaGo had to be limited to 50 watts, he'd have creamed it." Humans are fundamentally more data-efficient and power-efficient than current AI — suggesting current ML approaches are doing "something fundamentally different."

### Work-Life Harmony (Not Balance)
"Balance implies a strict trade, whereas I find that when I am happy at work, I come home more energized. I'm a better husband, a better dad. And when I'm happy at home, I come in a better boss and better colleague." Energy flows between domains rather than being zero-sum.

## 5. Management & Leadership

### Culture and Innovation
- Amazon's culture is a "gold standard culture for innovation and pioneering work."
- People are "missionaries for what they do" — not mercenaries.
- Happy employees are a prerequisite: "If you're giving great customer experience, the only way to do that is with happy people."
- The culture embraces change: "for somebody who hated change, I imagine high tech would be a pretty bad career."

### Meeting Philosophy
- "I make sure all of my meetings do not have tight agendas."
- Exception: operational reviews (weekly business review, metrics deck) should have tight agendas.
- "Most meetings should be used for a kind of mild brainstorming... we were really doing a lot of wandering."
- Tight agendas "assume you know where you're going" — which suppresses invention.

### Teaching Leadership
- Bezos personally teaches 3 leadership classes per year at Amazon.
- Uses these sessions to talk about work-life harmony, culture, and principles.

### India Team Empowerment (Cowboys vs. Computer Scientists)
- "I asked them to be fast-acting cowboys instead of calm, clear-headed computer scientists, and they took those instructions."
- Gave the India team their own software engineers for autonomy and speed.
- Local adaptation: "Just Do It Award" uses a cowboy hat in India instead of a Nike shoe.
- Result: massive local market customization, dramatically better outcomes than China.

### Career Choice Program
- For fulfillment center employees: prepaid training in in-demand fields (airplane mechanic, nursing, commercial truck driving).
- Key insight: **prepay tuition** — even with reimbursement, entry-level employees can't front $4,000–$6,000.
- Key insight: **glass-walled classrooms on the plant floor** — co-workers see peers learning, creating aspirational pull. Added after year one, massively increased enrollment.
- Only trains for in-demand fields (uses Bureau of Labor Statistics data) — no paying for fields without job demand.
- Philosophy: "The last thing that any enlightened company wants is for any employee to feel trapped in that job."
- 7,000 employees engaged at time of interview. Open-sourcing the program for other companies to adopt.
- Done in coordination with local community colleges near fulfillment centers.

### Don't Listen to Experts at the Start
- "If you're being stubborn on the vision and flexible on the details... you want always to be very flexible on the tactics... but very stubborn on the vision and the strategy."
- AWS example: "Let's go talk to experts and ask them if we should do Amazon Web Services. That would be a huge failure. They would all tell you, this is stupid."
- You can't let expert skepticism kill ideas early — but you do need to articulate what you're doing differently.

## 8. Unique Content

### Washington Post Strategy (Detailed Business Model Transformation)
- Old model: "relatively large amount of money per reader with a relatively small number of readers" (local paper).
- New model: "relatively small amount of money per reader, but on a very large number of readers" (national/global).
- The internet destroyed the old model but gifted "almost zero-cost global distribution."
- Goal: become a national and eventually global newspaper for people interested in the US capital.
- English-speaking world is the priority before translation/truly global.
- Explicitly frames WaPo as "the paper best situated because of its geography to cover the capital city of the most important country in the world."
- More optimistic than when he bought it 2 years prior.

### Prime as a "Physical-Digital Hybrid Membership Program"
- Unique in the market: "There's nothing quite like Prime."
- Three pillars: Marketplace (3P sellers), Amazon Prime, AWS.
- Video doesn't compete with Netflix on demand side — "people are going to subscribe to both." Compete on supply side (bidding for content).
- Key metrics: free trial conversion rate and annual renewal rate. Both improve when members use Prime Video.

### Amazon Physical Bookstores (Data-Driven Curation)
- ~5,000 titles, all face-out, highly curated using Amazon.com data.
- Almost every book has 5 stars (plus the top 100 bestsellers regardless).
- Not designed for people who know what they want (Amazon.com does that). Designed for browsing, discovery, and serendipity.
- A new kind of serendipity: "that kind of serendipity that you get from just wandering around bookshelves looking at book covers is a yet another kind of serendipity."

### Space Vision (Earth Zoned Residential)
- Earth's energy needs compound at 3% annually → in a few hundred years, you'd need to cover all landmass with solar cells.
- Solution: move heavy industry to space (orbiting, not on other planets, for proximity to Earth).
- "Earth will be zoned residential and light industrial."
- Build chip factories in space, send the chips down.
- Space gets 24/7 solar (no Earth self-shading).
- Transit time to Mars is too long for heavy industry — keep it in Earth orbit.
- Mars settlement is "cool" but it's one of many goals, not the singular focus (contrast with Musk).

### AI Data Efficiency Gap
- Humans learn in fundamentally different ways from current ML.
- Humans are "unbelievably data efficient" — you don't drive a million miles to learn to drive.
- Humans operate on ~50 watts vs. AlphaGo's massive energy consumption.
- Implication: current ML approaches are a stepping stone, not the endpoint.

### On Going Public vs. Staying Private
- Case-specific, no universal answer.
- Large private companies "probably could go public with no — it wouldn't hurt their freedom of movement."
- Private capital markets have gotten large enough to fund most needs.
- Going public is better for employee liquidity — private liquidity markets "don't do it as well."
- Acknowledges potential misalignment: what's best for major shareholders may differ from what's best for employees.

### Katharine Graham as a Role Model
- On press freedom and standing up to political pressure: "With Kay Graham as my role model, I'm very willing to let any of my body parts go through a big fat ringer if need be."
- References Nixon/Haldeman threatening Graham during Watergate, including attempts to rescind broadcast licenses.
- Frames political intimidation of press as "completely un-American."

### The "Just Do It" Award
- Amazon gives a Nike shoe to people who invented things nobody asked them to do.
- In India, they give a cowboy hat instead — reflecting the "fast-acting cowboys" culture Bezos asked for.

### Supplementing, Not Replacing, Logistics Partners
- Amazon isn't trying to replace FedEx, UPS, or USPS.
- Driven by capacity constraints, especially at peak (holiday season).
- UK: ~50% Amazon last-mile delivery (Royal Mail ran out of capacity).
- US: much smaller percentage, growing.
- "We will take all of the capacity that the U.S. Postal Service can give us and that UPS can give us, and we still need to supplement it."
